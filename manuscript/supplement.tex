\section{The initial exploratory study (harness v1)}

\subsection{Updating is regime-dependent and sometimes harmful (initial study)}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig1_regime_spectrum.png}
\caption{Readaptation value is regime-dependent and sometimes harmful: best detector-triggered gain over no-adaptation per dataset and attack regime.}
\label{fig:1}
\end{figure}
\input{tables/table1_regime_taxonomy.tex}
Under progressive drift, an unadapted classifier degrades and triggered readaptation can recover much of the loss --- on CICIDS2017. Averaged over 30 seeds and 100 post-drift windows, the best detector-triggered strategy recovers +19.5 balanced-accuracy (BA) points over no-adaptation in the Friday-DDoS regime, +11.9 in Wednesday, +7.6 in WebAttacks and +6.2 in PortScan (Table~\ref{tab:table1_regime_taxonomy}, Fig.~\ref{fig:1}). This is the familiar picture that motivates adaptive NIDS. (The per-regime \emph{best} detector is descriptive, not a tuned choice: with a single pre-specified detector, KS-max, the taxonomy is unchanged --- +5.5 to +18.6 BA points across the four CICIDS regimes, +1.5 and +0.2 on UNSW-NB15, $-$2.4 on ToN-IoT.)


The picture does not survive to independent data. On UNSW-NB15 the best recovery is marginal (+1.4 points for DoS, +0.5 for Reconnaissance), and on ToN-IoT Scanning it becomes \emph{negative} for the SVC-RBF downstream used here: no detector-triggered strategy matches a no-adaptation baseline (best adaptive $-$0.5 points; worst $-$4.6), so the correct decision there is \textbf{not to adapt} (Fig.~\ref{fig:1}). This net-harm is downstream-dependent --- it does not appear for more robust classifiers --- while the degradation regularity that tracks it is not (Supplementary \S S1.7). The effect is not a balanced-accuracy artifact: under attack-F1 the CICIDS benefits grow (e.g. +32.3 F1 points for DDoS) and ToN-IoT remains harmful for every detector (Energy $-$0.2, KS-max $-$4.6 F1 points). We stress the claim is not "adaptation usually hurts" --- it is that the \emph{sign} of the payoff is regime- and dataset-dependent, spanning +19.5 to $-$4.6 BA points, which is precisely what an adaptive system must anticipate.


% (fig:9 kept in the main body)
Why does adaptation help enormously in one regime and hurt in another? In the evaluated fixed-generator settings the state of the deployed model was consistently more predictive than the amount of drift, and we establish that with two facts --- stated carefully, because the naive statistic invites a coupling objection. First, \textbf{retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level}: across the seven SVC-RBF regimes, post-adaptation BA varies far less than the deployed-model BA it replaces ($\sigma$ = 3.5 vs.\ 7.4 points; regressing the gain on deployed BA gives slope $-$0.87). Under a fixed candidate generator, the benefit of adapting is therefore largely the deployed model's \emph{headroom} to that restored level: r = $-$0.89 between deployed-model BA and gain (descriptive scatter in Fig.~\ref{fig:2}), r = $-$0.81 (bootstrap CI95 [$-$0.94, $-$0.59], 16 model$\times$regime points) pooled across all four downstream classifiers (Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}). We deliberately do not rest the argument on the correlation coefficient itself: since the gain subtracts deployed BA, r is mathematically coupled to it, and an exhaustive re-pairing null holding both observed marginals yields a median r of $-$0.91 --- the observed value is no more extreme (p = 0.80; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). The substance is the compression of the restored level, not the coefficient --- and the evidence we treat as central is the \emph{per-trigger} version of this fact, which shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at individual triggered decisions under the hardened harness, the incumbent's degradation over the five preceding windows predicts the candidate-minus-incumbent value over the five following windows, while the detector's score at the same decisions shows no consistent association (per-trigger figure, clustered CIs and hierarchical model in \S5.3 of the main paper). Second, and free of any coupling, \textbf{the detector's own score carries almost no information about that headroom within a deployment}: over the 30 streams of each regime, the correlation between a detector's score and the gain from its own triggered adaptations has median +0.06 (maximum 0.13) across regimes, whereas deployed-model BA tracks the gain within every regime (median $-$0.68). Across regimes, classical detector scores do co-vary with the benefit (r = 0.75--0.94) --- larger drifts both fire detectors and degrade models --- but that cross-regime association is unavailable to a deployed detector calibrated on its own reference, and the persistence policies built on those scores fail to prevent harm (\S S1.3). The regularity itself is robust to disaggregation: at individual-stream level r = $-$0.91 (210 regime$\times$seed points), stable under seed resampling (CI95 [$-$0.90, $-$0.88]) and under removing any one regime (r between $-$0.72 and $-$0.92) or dataset --- with the expected range-restriction attenuation, not inversion, when all four CICIDS benefit regimes are removed at once (r = $-$0.52; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). Where the deployed model has already collapsed (DDoS: 69.8\% without adaptation), retraining on a fresh window is a large gain; where it remains strong (ToN-IoT: 92.2\%), replacing a well-generalized model with a narrowly-fit current-window model is a net loss.


This exposes the core problem. Drift detectors answer \emph{"has the distribution changed?"}, but an adaptive NIDS needs \emph{"will retraining improve the classifier?"} --- and these are different questions. We quantify the gap with the per-stream policy oracle of \S3.6 of the main paper (holding the detector fixed); being a policy-selection bound, it understates the headroom of perfect per-trigger decisions. The resulting \emph{regret} of naive triggering is essentially zero where adaptation always helps (Wednesday, DDoS: 0.00 points, adaptation hurt on 0\% of streams) and largest where it hurts (ToN-IoT: 3.53 points for QK-ZZ, CI95 [1.76, 5.54], adaptation hurt on 67\% of streams), scaling monotonically with harm frequency (Table~\ref{tab:table4_oracle_regret}, Fig.~\ref{fig:3}). The headroom left by naive triggering is real and concentrated exactly where the deployed model is already good.


\subsection{Detector sensitivity does not resolve the decision}

\input{tables/table2_phase2_gate_summary.tex}
Because drift detectors are blind to model degradation, improving the detector cannot solve the problem --- and it does not. Quantum-kernel MMD monitors do define distinct \emph{operating points} on CICIDS2017 (QK-MMD ZZ triggers 1.8--2.1 fewer readaptations than Energy distance at equivalent accuracy in Wednesday/DDoS, and inverts in PortScan), but at a ~114$\times$ monitoring-runtime cost, and these profiles do not replicate externally. Critically, the regime taxonomy and the harmful-adaptation outcome are identical for classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:3}, Tables~\ref{tab:table1_regime_taxonomy} and~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}): on ToN-IoT, naive triggering is net-harmful whether the trigger is KS-max ($-$1.4 points) or QK-ZZ ($-$3.7 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). Among the monitors evaluated, the operative lever is the adapt/no-adapt decision, not the similarity measure that fires the trigger.


\subsection{Simple confirmation and cooldown policies do not prevent harm}


If the decision is the lever, the natural fix is a smarter trigger policy. We pre-specified a grid of alarm-confirmation (k-of-n) and cooldown policies and evaluated them against fixed success criteria (preserve benefit in PortScan; avoid harm in ToN-IoT; do not degrade UNSW). None passed: in the harmful regime the tested policies remained far below no-adaptation and did not reduce adaptations relative to a conservative legacy baseline (Table~\ref{tab:table5_phase1_negative}). Confirmation and cooldown act on the \emph{persistence} of distributional change --- a signal that, within the evaluated triggered decisions, carried no consistent information about whether adaptation helps. This negative result motivates a policy that acts on the right quantity.


\subsection{A label-efficient validate-before-commit gate resolves the problem}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig4_phase2_gate.png}
\caption{The validate-before-commit gate preserves benefit and avoids harm, with the same sign pattern for a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (magnitudes differ; see text).}
\label{fig:4}
\end{figure}
We introduce a decision gate that acts directly on estimated model improvement. On each drift trigger, a candidate model is retrained as usual, but it is \textbf{deployed only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe} drawn from the current window (budget 32 or 64 flows); otherwise the incumbent is kept. We evaluate the gate against the same pre-specified criteria (30 seeds, 100 windows, legacy trigger fixed so the gate is the only designed difference) across the benefit (PortScan), mixed (UNSW Reconnaissance) and harmful (ToN-IoT) regimes, for both a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}, Fig.~\ref{fig:4}).

\input{tables/table3_phase2_paired_ci.tex}
The gate passes every criterion for both detectors. In the harmful regime it turns net-harmful naive triggering into net benefit: ToN-IoT gain moves from $-$1.36 (KS-max) and $-$3.69 (QK-ZZ) points to +0.93 and +1.06, \textbf{significantly above naive triggering} (+2.30 points, CI95 [1.15, 3.63] for KS-max; +4.74, [2.47, 7.69] for QK-ZZ; Table~\ref{tab:table3_phase2_paired_ci}) and, notably, \textbf{significantly above no-adaptation itself} (+0.93, [0.53, 1.36]; +1.06, [0.77, 1.40]) --- the gate does not merely stop adapting, it commits the minority of beneficial adaptations while rejecting the harmful majority. In the benefit regime it preserves the full gain (PortScan +8.3/+8.4 points, statistically indistinguishable from naive; formally, benefit preservation was pre-specified as \emph{non-inferiority} at a 0.3-point margin, and the paired gate$-$naive CI95 lower bounds --- $\ge$ $-$0.12 across both detectors and both budgets --- clear it), and it does not degrade the mixed regime. The label cost is small: roughly 100--160 labeled flows over a 100-window stream at budget 32.


The gate's power comes from labels, not from the confirmation heuristic: a zero-label variant that commits on deployed-vs-candidate prediction \emph{disagreement} fails, remaining significantly below no-adaptation in the harmful regime ($-$4.95 and $-$5.26 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). A handful of target-labeled flows per confirmed drift therefore converts net-harmful naive updating into net benefit across the evaluated benefit, marginal and harm regimes --- without giving up the benefit, and matching what \S S1.5 shows even state-of-the-art label-free estimators do not. We are precise about ``safe'': the point rule is not a formal guarantee (\S5.3 of the main paper reports its residual harmful-commit rate, 15--32\% of commits, and the risk-controlled gates that bound it); the claim here is average harm-avoidance at this operating point, not a certified safety property. Because the gate behaves the same way (same sign pattern) for the classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:4}), the contribution is a detector-agnostic decision mechanism.


\subsection{Gate robustness (post-registration)}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig5_label_budget_curve.png}
\caption{Label-efficiency frontier: gain over no-adaptation versus probe budget; a small probe suffices to avoid harm.}
\label{fig:5}
\end{figure}
Ten extensions beyond the pre-specified design (explicitly labeled as robustness, not part of the confirmatory claim) probe the gate's limits. \emph{Label efficiency:} sweeping the probe budget from 8 to 128 flows (KS-max, 30 seeds; Fig.~\ref{fig:5}) shows the gate is remarkably label-cheap --- in this initial study even the smallest budget avoided harm, ~8 labeled flows per confirmed drift ($\approx$23 per stream) lifting ToN-IoT gain from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.38, plateauing near +1.1 by budget 32--64 (the hardened-harness re-run sharpens this: at $b{=}8$ harm is reduced but not converted to net benefit, fixing the operating point at $b{=}32$; \S5.3 of the main paper); benefit-regime gain is flat at every budget. A few dozen labels, not hundreds, suffice in this balanced-probe setting. \emph{Benefit breadth:} beyond PortScan the gate preserves --- and slightly improves --- the benefit in the two strongest benefit regimes, Wednesday (naive +15.39 $\to$ +16.32) and DDoS (naive +24.81 $\to$ +25.27), preserving benefit in all three benefit regimes tested --- the exception being the deep-benefit chronological replays, where a stale probe imposes a measured, metric-dependent premium (\S5.3 of the main paper).


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig8_probe_poison.png}
\caption{The gate remains harm-avoiding under randomly corrupted validation labels (up to 40\% flipped, initial study; replicated on the hardened harness in \S5.3 of the main paper).}
\label{fig:8}
\end{figure}
\emph{Label latency:} the probe need not be current. Drawing it from traffic labeled up to 20 windows in the past (Fig.~\ref{fig:7}) leaves the gate safe and effective at every lag --- in the harmful regime the gate holds at +0.9 to +1.1 (vs naive $-$1.36) for lags of 5, 10 and 20 windows, and the benefit regimes are essentially unchanged --- so the method tolerates realistic labeling delay rather than requiring instantaneous labels. \emph{Harm breadth:} the net-harm of naive triggering (with the fragile SVC-RBF downstream) is not confined to one ToN-IoT regime --- it also appears in ToN-IoT DDoS (naive \textbf{$-$16.81}) and Injection ($-$1.21), and the gate converts all three to net-positive (+1.26, +0.80, +0.93). \emph{Cost knob:} raising the commit margin $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed adaptations by ~30\% (e.g. PortScan 3.1$\to$2.2 commits) at equal downstream gain, giving a simple operating characteristic that trades update frequency for benefit. \emph{Randomly corrupted validation labels:} because the gate trusts a labeled probe, we test a faulty labeler (or non-adaptive attacker) that flips a uniformly random fraction of the probe labels --- the random variant of the label-flipping poisoning threat against SVM-family learners \cite{biggio2012poisoning} (Fig.~\ref{fig:8}). The gate avoids harm under this corruption process: even with 40\% of validation labels corrupted --- near the point where the probe is pure noise --- it still matches-or-beats naive triggering in every regime (ToN-IoT degrades gracefully from +0.93 to +0.32 but never goes negative; benefit and mixed regimes are essentially unchanged). Label noise perturbs the deployed and candidate estimates symmetrically, so it moves the gate along the naive-to-oracle line without pushing it below the safe baseline. (A candidate-aware adversary that chooses \emph{which} labels to flip is a strictly stronger threat we do not model; \S7 of the main paper.) \emph{Label-free estimators head-to-head:} the zero-label variant of \S S1.4 is deliberately naive, so we also instantiate the two standard label-free accuracy estimators --- ATC \cite{garg2022atc} and DoC \cite{guillory2021doc} --- as gates in the same harness: each model estimates its target-window accuracy from its own confidences plus a labeled validation sample drawn at its training time, using zero target-window labels. Both avoid harm --- on the fragile SVC-RBF downstream, ToN-IoT moves from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.40 (ATC) and +1.16 (DoC) --- but both pay for safety with benefit: on PortScan they lose 24\% (ATC) and 60\% (DoC) of the naive gain ($-$1.86 [$-$3.48, $-$0.17] and $-$4.64 [$-$6.86, $-$2.26] vs naive, CI95), and they remain significantly below naive even for random forest with native predicted probabilities. The labeled probe is thus the only gate on the benefit--safety Pareto front \emph{among those evaluated}. The mechanism mirrors the degradation law: confidence-based estimates are trustworthy only while the incumbent is healthy --- exactly the regime where the gate is least needed --- and misrank incumbent against candidate once the incumbent has degraded, which is where the benefit lives; related estimators share the same calibration premise \cite{jiang2022disagreement,baek2022agreement,rosenfeld2023dis2,deng2021labels}. \emph{Replay retraining:} the harmful baseline retrains on a narrow current window and fully replaces the incumbent, so we also test the standard label-free mitigation --- retrain-current-plus-replay, augmenting each candidate's training set with an equal-size sample from the reference regime (50/50) --- under pre-fixed criteria (KS-max, SVC-RBF, 30 seeds). The tested fixed 50/50 replay does not rescue naive triggering: in the harm regime it remains significantly net-harmful ($-$4.54, CI95 [$-$7.06, $-$2.34] vs no-adaptation; worse than full replacement's $-$1.36, since under a still-advancing ramp a replay-anchored candidate misfits later windows even more), and in the benefit regime it \emph{loses} part of the gain (+6.59 vs +7.79 naive). The gate, by contrast, is orthogonal to the update rule: placed on top of replay it restores safety and benefit in every regime --- ToN-IoT +0.59, significantly above both no-adaptation ([0.23, 0.97]) and replay-naive (+5.13 [2.96, 7.60]); PortScan +8.26, recovering the benefit replay-naive loses (+1.66 [0.52, 3.04]). Harmful adaptation is therefore not an artifact of full-replacement retraining, and the gate's value composes with the update strategy. \emph{Probe prevalence:} the probes above are class-balanced, which presumes an enrichment mechanism. An initial low-prevalence experiment (conditioned on the probe containing at least one attack) suggested the gate was indifferent down to $\pi=0.01$; that conditioning turned out to force a minority-class flow into every probe and the claim was an artifact. \S5.3 of the main paper reports the corrected experiment, in which the attack count is drawn honestly as $\mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$: the gate tolerates genuinely random inspection down to $\pi\approx0.05$, but its harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi=0.01$ (where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all). We report the corrected bound and withdraw the stronger one. \emph{Candidate size and dimensionality:} two controls address the concern that the harm is a pipeline artifact. Size-matching \emph{under this study's frozen transformer and advancing full-drift ramp}: training every candidate on 2{,}000 flows per class --- the same budget as the initial model, removing the 4$\times$ size asymmetry --- \emph{deepens} the harm (ToN-IoT naive $-$5.34, CI95 [$-$8.73, $-$2.49], vs $-$1.36 at 512/class): a larger candidate fits the current mixture better and mis-specializes harder while the ramp keeps advancing, the opposite of what a small-sample explanation predicts in that configuration (the preregistered own-transformer zero-drift control of \S5.3 of the main paper and \S S8 shows that with self-contained preprocessing and no drift, size matching \emph{removes} the mean harm --- the two settings answer different questions and both are reported); the gate remains safe and beneficial throughout (+0.58 / +9.33 / +3.37 across the three regimes), and candidate size does shift the marginal regime's magnitude (UNSW +3.31 vs +0.92), so magnitudes are pipeline-dependent even where signs are not. Dimensionality: with the PCA bottleneck removed (full feature space, classical detector), the benefit grows (PortScan +16.2) and the harm regime's point estimate remains negative ($-$0.65 [$-$3.03, +1.44], not significant at 30 seeds) --- neither the candidate's sample size nor the 8-dimensional projection manufactures the phenomenon, though full dimensionality attenuates the harm and we report that plainly. \emph{Significance of the safety claim.} Paired bootstrap CIs in the harm regime confirm the guarantee is not noise: the gate significantly outperforms naive triggering under every label-latency and every probe-poison level tested (CI95 excludes zero), and in no robustness condition --- across downstream models, latencies or poison levels --- is it significantly \emph{worse} than either naive triggering or no-adaptation. Where the gate is not significantly better than naive (the robust downstream models, where naive is already non-harmful), it is statistically indistinguishable from it, i.e. never worse.


\subsection{Generalization across downstream classifiers}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig6_downstream_generalization.png}
\caption{Generalization across four downstream classifiers: net-harm is specific to SVC-RBF; the gate is safe for all.}
\label{fig:6}
\end{figure}
\input{tables/table6_downstream_generalization.tex}
To test whether the phenomenon and the solution depend on the downstream model, we repeat the three regimes with the KS-max trigger for four downstream classifiers --- SVC-RBF, random forest, logistic regression and MLP (20 seeds; Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}, Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). Three findings stand out, and we report them plainly because they refine the headline.


First, \textbf{the degradation--headroom regularity generalizes.} Across all four classifiers the benefit of adaptation tracks deployed-model degradation: where the frozen model is weak the gain is large (e.g. random forest and MLP on PortScan start at 69--70\% no-adaptation BA and gain +27 points; logistic regression on ToN-IoT starts at 84\% and gains +6.7), and where the frozen model is already strong the gain is small (random forest on ToN-IoT starts at 97\% and gains only +1.9). Pooled across all four classifiers the degradation--benefit correlation remains strong (r = $-$0.81, \S S1.1; Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}): the relationship is a property of the adaptation problem, not of SVC.


Second, \textbf{whether the effect crosses into net harm is downstream-specific.} The negative-gain result --- naive triggering below no-adaptation --- occurs only for SVC-RBF on ToN-IoT ($-$1.36 points); random forest (+1.88), logistic regression (+6.70) and MLP (+0.24) all remain non-negative there (Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). SVC-RBF is fragile enough on this regime that retraining on a narrow current window loses more than it gains; more robust downstream models do not. We therefore do not claim that naive readaptation is universally harmful --- only that it can be, for fragile models, exactly where the regularity predicts the smallest headroom.


Third, and most important for deployment, \textbf{the gate avoids material harm in all twelve settings of this study.} Across all four downstream classifiers and all three regimes (twelve settings), the labeled-probe gate shows no material deficit against no-adaptation or naive triggering; the largest observed mean deficit vs.\ naive anywhere is 0.11 BA points (random forest on UNSW Reconnaissance), within seed noise (Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}). (The hardened-harness replication puts sharper bounds on this claim --- including one statistically resolvable but immaterial 0.01-point deficit, and a real chronological stream where the premium is material; \S5.3 of the main paper.) This empirical safety property holds even in the settings where naive triggering is already fine (the gate simply commits), and rescues the one setting where it is harmful. The solution generalizes across downstream models even though the harm does not.


\subsection{Controls}


The findings are robust to the evaluation metric (the taxonomy and harmful-adaptation result hold under attack-F1; \S S1.1). Quantum monitors do not reduce nuisance triggers on benign drift (QK-MMD ZZ 1.40 vs Energy 1.03 false triggers), so their value cannot be framed as cleaner benign-drift filtering; and an expensive Random-Forest downstream shows that quantum monitoring runtime can dominate total operational cost. A pre-specified feature-map sensitivity study finds no deeper map that unlocks a stronger quantum advantage. (Full tables for both: reproducibility artifact \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact}.) These controls reinforce, rather than qualify, the central conclusion: distribution-shift magnitude alone is not a sufficient commit signal.




\section{Registered-extension details relocated from the main paper}
The subsections below are verbatim accounts of registered extensions whose conclusions are summarized in \S5.5--\S5.7 of the main paper; they are relocated here for compactness, not because their status is exploratory --- each was locked in a registered amendment note before its runs.

\subsection{Canonical performance-aware monitors: reference-implementation validation and budget sweep}

\textbf{Canonical monitors, validated against reference implementations.} Amendment 003 added the canonical performance-aware triggers (800 monitoring labels/stream): our batch DDM captures much of the benefit (PortScan +6.81) but is itself \textbf{net-harmful in the harm regime} ($-$2.59 [$-$5.90, $-$0.34]) --- monitoring the model's own error still commits harmful updates once a transient fires the trigger --- and the gate rescues it (+0.19). Because bespoke implementations invite doubt, amendment 004 cross-checks both monitors against the \texttt{river} reference implementations. Our DDM agrees with river's on synthetic Bernoulli step streams (94\% fire/no-fire agreement, comparable delays), and \emph{in-harness river-DDM replicates the key result}: fed the same 8 labels/window as individual outcomes, it remains net-harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.23 [$-$5.29, $-$0.05]) and benefit-lossy in UNSW (+0.14 vs naive +1.21). Our window-rate ADWIN variant, by contrast, proved insensitive (57\% agreement --- it under-fires), so we report the reference ADWIN instead: at these monitoring rates it never fires in two regimes and fires rarely in PortScan, forfeiting most of the benefit (+1.77 vs +8.25). The direction of the amendment-003 claim survives under reference implementations; the specific "never fires" wording was an artifact of our variant, and we correct it. Because a single budget invites the objection that the monitors were starved, a registered sweep raised the monitoring budget to 3{,}200 and 8{,}000 labels per stream (4$\times$ and 10$\times$): no budget fixes the pattern --- reference DDM stays net-harmful or useless in ToN-IoT ($-$1.04 [$-$1.87, $-$0.37] at 4$\times$; $-$0.45 at 10$\times$) and loses benefit elsewhere as its budget grows (PortScan +7.08 at 10$\times$ vs naive's +8.25), while reference ADWIN stays silent in ToN-IoT at every budget. The claim is therefore budget-conditional but robust across the two orders of magnitude evaluated: under these monitoring configurations, labels spent on the commit decision bought a better accuracy--cost trade-off than labels spent monitoring.

\subsection{The v1 robustness envelope on the hardened harness}

\textbf{The v1 robustness envelope, re-established (and partly corrected) on v2.} The initial study's three operational robustness claims were re-run on the hardened harness (descriptive, same pristine seeds, paired against the shared naive arm). \emph{Budget:} the gate's benefit is stable for $b \ge 32$ (ToN +0.79; $b{=}64$: +0.92; $b{=}128$: +1.01), but the initial study's ``8 labels suffice'' does \emph{not} fully replicate: at $b{=}8$ the gate still reduces harm (+1.22 [0.46, 2.15] above naive) yet is no longer net-positive against no-adaptation ($-$0.42 [$-$1.15, 0.23]), and $b{=}16$ is borderline (+0.29 [$-$0.27, 0.77]). We therefore revise the recommended operating point to $b{=}32$. \emph{Label latency:} at 20 windows of staleness the harm regime holds fully (+0.95 [0.76, 1.19]) and the benefit regimes pay only a small, real cost (PortScan $-$0.47 [$-$0.74, $-$0.22] vs fresh lp32). \emph{Random label corruption:} harm \emph{avoidance} survives 40\% flipped probe labels (still +1.60 [0.79, 2.55] above naive), but net benefit over no-adaptation survives only to 25\% (+0.57 [0.25, 0.85]; at 40\%: $-$0.04 [$-$0.62, 0.49]) --- the corrected, sharper version of the initial claim.

\subsection{Natural-prevalence probes and evaluation: corrections and a retraction}

\textbf{Honest natural-prevalence probes: a claim we retract.} The initial study reported that the probe ``need not be balanced'' and retained full safety and benefit even at $\pi{=}0.01$. That test drew the probe with a forced minority-class flow (\texttt{max(1,\,$\lfloor b\pi \rceil$)}), so it was really an \emph{attack-enriched} probe containing exactly one attack --- not random inspection at $\pi$. A registered rerun samples the attack count as $\mathrm{Binomial}(b, \pi)$, allowing zero-attack probes, and the picture changes. At $\pi{=}0.10$ (2--3\% of probes contain no attack) the gate is unaffected. At $\pi{=}0.05$ (15--23\% zero-attack) it still holds (ToN +0.91 [0.67, 1.17]), at a small real cost in the benefit regime ($-$0.44 [$-$0.79, $-$0.12] vs the balanced probe). At $\pi{=}0.01$, where \emph{two-thirds} of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all, the harm-regime guarantee dissolves: +0.21 [$-$1.39, 1.15] --- no longer resolvable from zero. The corrected claim is therefore narrower and, we think, more useful: the gate tolerates genuinely random inspection down to roughly $\pi \approx 0.05$; below that, an operator must \emph{enrich} the probe (alert-guided or active sampling, $\approx 1/\pi$ inspections to find one attack) or accept that the commit decision is being made on benign traffic alone.

Separately, on natural-prevalence \emph{evaluation} streams, the corrected experiment --- with the detector also \emph{calibrated} at operating prevalence --- is reported in \S5.3 of the main paper, where the binding constraint turns out to be the starved monitor, not the gate. An earlier variant that calibrated on balanced windows but evaluated on imbalanced streams overstated trigger rates and gains (ToN naive $-$2.63, gated +0.80, lower FPR and alert volume under the gate) and is retained as exploratory.

\subsection{The two-stage health reference, recalibrated}

\textbf{The two-stage health reference, recalibrated.} The review noted that the two-stage gate compared each incumbent's health against the \emph{initial} model's reference, which stops being meaningful after the first commit. Resetting the reference on every commit to the new incumbent's accuracy on the (already labeled) commit half leaves the conclusions unchanged (ToN +0.11 [$-$0.27, 0.45] vs +0.15 [$-$0.15, 0.46] static; PortScan +7.88 vs +7.87), so the reported savings are not an artifact of a stale reference --- but the corrected variant is the one we recommend.

\subsection{Cumulative-generator controls under zero drift}

\textbf{Cumulative controls (amendment 011): the harm is not an artifact of duplication or regularization.} Review asked whether the cumulative generator's harm is confounded by with-replacement duplication (its training set is only 76--83\% unique rows), by omitting the incumbent's initial training set, or by holding the SVC penalty $C$ fixed as $n$ grows. We ran all three registered controls under zero drift. \emph{Deduplicating} the training rows (100\% unique) does \emph{not} remove the harm --- it \emph{deepens} it (ToN $-$9.46 $\to$ $-$10.74, PortScan $-$7.04 $\to$ $-$11.36), because more distinct current-regime rows mis-specialize the replacement harder. \emph{$n$-scaling} $C$ with the corrected $C\propto 1/n$ rule (stronger regularization as the set grows) likewise leaves it --- indeed slightly deepens it (ToN $-$10.62, PortScan $-$9.82, vs $-$9.46 / $-$7.04 at fixed $C{=}1$). \emph{Adding the initial training set} attenuates but does not eliminate it (ToN $-$8.27, PortScan $-$5.14) and flips the marginal UNSW case positive ($-$0.07 $\to$ +0.53) --- an honest boundary: where the healthy incumbent had almost nothing to lose, re-anchoring the candidate to the original data recovers a small gain. None of the controls that a ``just use more data / de-duplicate / re-regularize'' reading would predict to help actually removes the harm (the $n$-scaled control uses the corrected $C\propto 1/n$ regularization --- \emph{stronger} as the set grows --- and the harm persists there too); so the effect is not duplication or under-regularization. The symmetric-A/B control (\S5.3 of the main paper) then identifies what it \emph{is}: not re-estimation variance but the incumbent's frozen, incumbent-fit transformer, which disadvantages any challenger that inherits it --- the harm vanishes under an independent transformer and reappears under an incumbent-fit one.

\subsection{Chronological replays: the full per-replay account}

\textbf{Chronologically ordered subsampled replays: the gate's honest boundary, measured properly.} A first Friday replay (seeds 134--163) contained two implementation flaws, caught in external review: a stride mismatch let candidates train on ${\sim}$28k rows the stream never presented as evaluation windows, and its summary averaged plain accuracy (single-class windows) with balanced accuracy. We corrected the runner, registered the correction (amendment 004), and re-ran on fresh seeds with two additional CICIDS streams; a further registered amendment (005) added an \emph{external} chronological stream --- the raw UNSW-NB15 captures, 2.54M flows sorted by flow start time, incumbent trained on the first 30\% of the timeline (temporal-streams table in the main paper). The corrected picture is stronger in both directions, and the fourth stream completes it. The phenomenon is real: Tuesday-trained CICIDS incumbents collapse on all three days (BA 53--72\%) and drift-triggered retraining recovers +13.6 to +27.6 points --- deep-benefit territory, confirming the ramps did not manufacture the recovery. The gate's boundary is sharper than the flawed run suggested: on Friday the gate surrenders 6.3 of naive's 13.6 recovered BA points ($-$6.34 [$-$6.83, $-$5.90]) and on Thursday 5.0 of 22.7, while on Wednesday it pays nothing (+0.40 [0.09, 0.74]). \emph{And on the external stream the account's central prediction holds}: the UNSW incumbent stays healthy (82.3\% BA, no collapse; 163/200 two-class windows, ${\sim}$21 triggers/stream), updating helps moderately (+7.33 [6.80, 7.85]), and the gate pays \emph{no premium at all} (+0.16 [$-$0.31, 0.63] vs naive on BA; +0.21 on accuracy) --- the insurance is free precisely where the incumbent's health makes the commit decision genuinely uncertain, which is where the gate's value concentrates. A fifth registered replay attempted to engineer chronological harm --- train on Wednesday (DoS-rich) and replay Thursday (sparse WebAttacks $\to$ Infiltration), hoping for a healthy incumbent facing weak candidates --- but the Wednesday-trained model collapses on Thursday too (48.7\% BA), producing another deep-benefit stream (naive +33.66, gate +18.58; premium $-$15.08 on BA, $-$0.08 [$-$0.30, 0.15] on accuracy --- the same metric-dependent pattern, at its most extreme). A sixth registered replay (amendment 009) targeted the healthy-incumbent corner directly --- a purely \emph{intra-day} CICIDS stream (Tuesday, train on the first 30\% of the timeline, stream the rest) so the incumbent faces the same day it trained on --- but it collapses there too (no-adaptation BA 49.5\%, naive +43.8, gate premium $-$5.66 [$-$8.62, $-$3.85]), because Tuesday's brute-force phase switches attack sub-type over the day (the training window contains only FTP-Patator, the stream only SSH-Patator), so a new variant appears that the incumbent never saw: deep benefit again, not a healthy incumbent. \emph{Chronological net-harm is therefore unobserved across these six replays, and within this first set the only healthy-incumbent chronological stream is the external UNSW-NB15 timeline (the pre-enumerated matrix of \S5.5 of the main paper adds two more)}: every CICIDS incumbent collapses on a later window --- even within a single day --- which is exactly the deep-benefit corner where the account says the gate has least to offer. This is a real limit on external validity for the harm result, and \S7 of the main paper states it --- though the mild-drift prediction test in the main paper shows that the harm is not confined to one dataset once the incumbent is allowed to stay healthy.

These replays are \emph{chronologically ordered subsampled replays}, not full-capture replays: each processes 200 windows of 256 flows, strided to span the day, so a fraction of the capture is evaluated. The ordering, the labels and the causal availability of every probe are real; the sampling density is not. The premium is \emph{metric-dependent}: on overall accuracy the gate \emph{beats} naive on the same streams (Friday +4.19 [3.85, 4.52]; Thursday +1.23) while losing balanced accuracy on attack-mixed windows. Our first diagnosis --- that the gate inherits the benign-dominated \emph{class composition} of a natural-composition probe --- was put to a registered causal test (amendment 005): a stratified probe (16 per class wherever the past window contains both classes) leaves the premium unchanged (stratified$-$plain: $-$0.05 [$-$0.49, 0.41] on Friday), so the composition account is \emph{not supported} where it is testable --- with the caveat that roughly two-thirds of past windows are single-class, where stratification cannot act. The surviving explanation is probe \emph{staleness}: on a stream whose regime changes within a few windows, a nine-window-old probe --- whatever its composition --- misranks candidates whose relative merits have already shifted, and it systematically misranks them on the minority class first (which is why balanced accuracy pays and overall accuracy does not). We report this as a hypothesis consistent with the data, not a demonstrated mechanism; on streams known to sit deep in the benefit regime, naive triggering is already adequate. This is the one registered check where the gate costs real benefit, reported as such.

\subsection{How the leakage-free observed-data arm was reached (three registered iterations)}

\textbf{Each iteration closed a flaw that external review or our own assertions surfaced},
and the harm-regime conclusion survived all of them. Two earlier ones preceded the three
listed below. Amendment 006's arm drew the candidate and probe causally but still

and because the gate and the naive loop commit at different times, that oracle state
interacts with the policy instead of cancelling between arms; amendment 007 rebuilt the

\subsection{Superseded risk-gate implementations: the Robbins CS and the Bonferroni sequential probe}

Two risk-controlled rules preceded the empirical-Bernstein machinery the paper now recommends,
and both are dominated by it. We record them here so the frontier's endpoints are documented.

\textbf{Robbins normal-mixture confidence sequence.} Commits only when a normal-mixture lower
confidence sequence on the per-flow correctness difference exceeds zero, bounding the
per-proposal probability of falsely establishing positive expected paired correctness on the
probe distribution by $\alpha$, uniformly over the inspection schedule (\S3.5 of the main
paper states the guarantee and its scope). Because the bound must budget for the worst-case
sub-Gaussian variance ($\sigma^2 \le 1$) rather than the observed one, at probe budgets
$\le 64$ flows it essentially never commits (gain $+0.00$ across all mild- and zero-drift
regimes). That is correct behaviour where there is no headroom, and it is the
maximally risk-averse endpoint of the point/LCB/McNemar/sequential frontier --- but it forgoes
benefit wherever headroom exists, which is exactly what the empirical-Bernstein sequence
recovers by using the empirical variance of a mostly-tied difference sequence.

\textbf{Bonferroni sequential probe.} Draws 16 flows, decides as soon as the evidence resolves
the sign, and buys more (to 64) only while the decision is in doubt, spending $\alpha/K$ at
each of $K{=}4$ looks so that stopping is valid. (A first heuristic version inspected a
fixed-sample interval at each look, which inflates the error rate under optional stopping;
external review noted this and the registered rule replaced it.) It spends 58--61 labels per
decision and is deliberately conservative: CICIDS-PortScan $+8.04$, i.e.\ $-1.08$
[$-1.38$, $-0.79$] below the fixed 32-label probe; UNSW-Recon $+1.00$; ToN-IoT $+0.16$. It is
also the rule that recovers essentially all of the zero-drift loss by refusing to commit
without evidence. The confidence sequences of \S3.5 subsume it: they are valid at every sample
size rather than at four pre-declared looks, and the empirical-Bernstein version is
strictly tighter at the same $\alpha$.

\subsection{Decision-quality of the lp32 gate on the logged triggers (relocated from main \S5.5)}

Using the logged five-window lookahead as ground truth (never available to the policy), the
lp32 gate's per-trigger operating point is: in the harm regime, rejection is almost always
right (95\% of rejections blocked a non-beneficial update; recall of beneficial updates 0.95)
and the gate cuts \emph{local per-decision regret} --- commit vs.\ reject on the gate policy's
own trigger states, against an always-commit rule on those identical states --- from 0.0452
to 0.0020, ${\sim}22\times$; in the benefit regime it still reduces this regret five-fold
(0.0028 vs 0.0140). Pooled across 308 triggers: precision 0.74, recall 0.87, regret 0.0030
vs 0.0212; horizon reruns at 1/3/5/10 leave the reductions essentially unchanged, and the
marginal regime is honestly a wash at every horizon. The gate does commit some harmful
updates (23\% of its commits have negative realized value, mostly small) --- it is a cheap
statistical filter, not an oracle.

\section{Proof of Proposition 1 (conditional validity of the commit e-process)}
\label{sec:s4proof}

\noindent\textbf{Setting.} Per class, the gate observes per-flow correctness differences
$d_1, d_2, \dots \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ in adjudication order, mapped to $x_i = (d_i+1)/2 \in [0,1]$.
The implementation maintains, before observing $x_i$, the predictable plug-ins
$\hat\mu_{i-1} = (1/2 + \sum_{j<i} x_j)/i$ and
$\hat\sigma^2_{i-1} = (1/4 + \sum_{j<i} (x_j - \hat\mu_{j-1})^2)/i$, and the predictable betting
fraction $\lambda_i = \min\{3/4,\ \sqrt{2\log(1/\alpha) / (\hat\sigma^2_{i-1}\, i \log(i+1))}\}$.
With $\psi_E(\lambda) = -\log(1-\lambda) - \lambda$, define the wealth process
$$W_n \;=\; \exp\Big(\sum_{i\le n} \lambda_i \big(x_i - \tfrac12\big) \;-\; \sum_{i\le n} \psi_E(\lambda_i)\,\big(x_i - \hat\mu_{i-1}\big)^2\Big), \qquad W_0 = 1 .$$
The gate's commit condition --- the empirical-Bernstein lower bound on $\mathbb{E}[d]$
exceeding $0$, i.e.\ the lower bound on $\mathbb{E}[x]$ exceeding $1/2$ --- rearranges exactly to
$\sum_i \lambda_i (x_i - \tfrac12) - \sum_i \psi_E(\lambda_i)(x_i - \hat\mu_{i-1})^2 > \log(1/\alpha)$,
i.e.\ the event $\{W_n > 1/\alpha\}$: the confidence-sequence inversion and the e-process
threshold are the same object.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Claim.} If $\mathbb{E}[x_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] \le 1/2$ for every $i$ (the weak
null: the challenger is at no time conditionally better), with no stationarity or identical-distribution
assumption, then $(W_n)$ is a nonnegative supermartingale and
$\Pr(\exists n : W_n > 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Proof.} Write $\mu_i = \mathbb{E}[x_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] \le 1/2$ and
$y_i = x_i - \hat\mu_{i-1}$. Since $x_i \in [0,1]$ and $\hat\mu_{i-1} \in (0,1)$ is
$\mathcal F_{i-1}$-measurable, $y_i > -1$; and $\lambda_i \in [0, 3/4] \subset [0,1)$ is
$\mathcal F_{i-1}$-measurable. Fan's inequality states that for all $y \ge -1$ and
$\lambda \in [0,1)$,
$\exp\big(\lambda y - \psi_E(\lambda)\, y^2\big) \le 1 + \lambda y$.
Multiplying both sides by the $\mathcal F_{i-1}$-measurable factor
$\exp\big(\lambda_i(\hat\mu_{i-1} - \tfrac12)\big)$ and using
$\lambda_i(x_i - \tfrac12) = \lambda_i y_i + \lambda_i(\hat\mu_{i-1} - \tfrac12)$,
$$\exp\Big(\lambda_i \big(x_i - \tfrac12\big) - \psi_E(\lambda_i)\, y_i^2\Big) \;\le\; \big(1 + \lambda_i y_i\big)\, e^{\lambda_i (\hat\mu_{i-1} - 1/2)} .$$
Taking $\mathbb{E}[\,\cdot \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}]$ of the right-hand side and using
$\mathbb{E}[y_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] = \mu_i - \hat\mu_{i-1}$ together with $1 + u \le e^u$:
$$\big(1 + \lambda_i(\mu_i - \hat\mu_{i-1})\big)\, e^{\lambda_i(\hat\mu_{i-1} - 1/2)} \;\le\; e^{\lambda_i(\mu_i - \hat\mu_{i-1})}\, e^{\lambda_i(\hat\mu_{i-1} - 1/2)} \;=\; e^{\lambda_i(\mu_i - 1/2)} \;\le\; 1,$$
the last step because $\mu_i \le 1/2$ and $\lambda_i \ge 0$. Hence
$\mathbb{E}[W_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] \le W_{i-1}$: $(W_n)$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with
$W_0 = 1$, and Ville's inequality yields $\Pr(\exists n: W_n \ge 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$. $\square$

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Scope.} The argument is the standard testing-by-betting supermartingale bound
\cite{waudbysmith2023betting,howard2021timeuniform}; we claim only the correspondence to this
implementation. Rejecting the weak null establishes that the challenger was conditionally better
at \emph{some} point of the adjudication stream --- not that it is better at commit time, which is
why the cohort and refresh continuation modes of \S3.5 of the main paper exist. The stratified
gates run one such process per class at $\alpha/2$ (or $\alpha/4$ with a futility side) and
Bonferroni preserves the level. A registered null simulation with time-varying conditional means
(all $\le 0$) and time-varying tie probabilities verifies the bound empirically
(\texttt{test\_weak\_null\_drifting\_means} in the artifact).

\section{Multiplicity: every family and its adjustment}
\label{sec:s5multiplicity}

The main paper states the policy; this is the table behind it. Families are defined by
evidence tier, not by convenience, and a block is treated as a family only where several
inferential statements are made together.

\textbf{Confirmatory core (Holm).} The six registered gate-versus-naive contrasts --- the
primary $b{=}32$ gate against always-deploying, on the benefit, marginal and harm regimes,
under both the classical and the quantum-kernel detector. Three are nominally significant
(ToN-IoT under both detectors, and CICIDS-PortScan under QK-ZZ) and \emph{all three survive}
Holm step-down control at $\alpha{=}0.05$. The three that do not reach significance are the
benefit- and marginal-regime cells where the paper's claim is \emph{preservation}, not
superiority --- non-inferiority against a pre-registered margin, which is the criterion those
cells were registered under and which Table~1 of the main paper reports.

\textbf{Registered follow-ups (Benjamini--Hochberg within block).} The budget frontier
(the fifteen pre-declared cells --- 3 policies $\times$ 5 caps --- on CICIDS-PortScan at
full drift under the Bonferroni schedule, including the two configurations that never
commit) and the chronological matrix (the strict gate on seven pre-enumerated replays) each
form a within-block family fixed in advance of the results. Thirteen of the fifteen
frontier cells and all seven replays survive BH control of the false-discovery rate; the
two non-survivors are exactly the never-committing cells, whose paired gain is identically
zero.

\textbf{Descriptive tier (uncorrected, and labelled).} Ablations, sensitivity sweeps and
auxiliary negative results are reported without correction and without confirmatory language.

\emph{The point of the table is what it does not change.} Every nominally significant contrast
in this paper survives its adjustment, so no conclusion rests on an uncorrected comparison;
the correction is reported because a reader is entitled to check that, not because it rescues
anything. Multiplicity-adjusted $p$-values are computed directly from the paired per-seed
contrasts using a \emph{deterministic centered paired bootstrap test}: with $d$ the seed-level
paired differences (each contrast is a mean over 30 seeds, the unit of inference), the
observed statistic is $|\bar d|$, resampling is performed on $d - \bar d$ (imposing the null),
$B = 100{,}000$, and the two-sided Monte-Carlo--corrected $p$ is
$(\#\{|\bar d^{*}_0| \ge |\bar d|\} + 1)/(B+1)$, with per-contrast RNG seeds derived from the
contrast label (bit-reproducible, order-independent); this is a bootstrap approximation of
the null distribution, not an exact test. Holm controls the six-test confirmatory family, and
Benjamini--Hochberg is applied separately to the pre-declared 15-cell budget-frontier block
(3 policies $\times$ 5 caps on PortScan-full under the Bonferroni schedule, \emph{including}
the two configurations that never commit --- the family is fixed in advance and does not
condition on observed outcomes) and to the seven-replay chronological block. Two pre-declared
sensitivities (paired $t$-test and Wilcoxon signed-rank on $d$) are reported alongside in the
table and agree with the primary analysis; they never replace it. Survival under adjustment:
3 of the 6 core contrasts are nominally significant and all 3 survive Holm; 13 of the 15
frontier cells survive BH (the two non-survivors are exactly the never-committing
configurations, whose paired gain is identically zero); all 7 chronological replays survive
BH. The full machine-readable table is
\texttt{results/tables/paper2\_final\_q1/multiplicity.csv} in the artifact.
