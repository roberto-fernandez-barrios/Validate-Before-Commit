\section{The initial exploratory study (harness v1)}

\subsection{Updating is regime-dependent and sometimes harmful (initial study)}


% (fig:9 kept in the main body)
Why does adaptation help enormously in one regime and hurt in another? In the evaluated fixed-generator settings the state of the deployed model was consistently more predictive than the amount of drift, and we establish that with two facts --- stated carefully, because the naive statistic invites a coupling objection. First, \textbf{retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level}: across the seven SVC-RBF regimes, post-adaptation BA varies far less than the deployed-model BA it replaces ($\sigma$ = 3.5 vs.\ 7.4 points; regressing the gain on deployed BA gives slope $-$0.87). Under a fixed candidate generator, the benefit of adapting is therefore largely the deployed model's \emph{headroom} to that restored level: r = $-$0.89 between deployed-model BA and gain (descriptive scatter in the appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:2}), r = $-$0.81 (bootstrap CI95 [$-$0.94, $-$0.59], 16 model$\times$regime points) pooled across all four downstream classifiers (Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}). We deliberately do not rest the argument on the correlation coefficient itself: since the gain subtracts deployed BA, r is mathematically coupled to it, and an exhaustive re-pairing null holding both observed marginals yields a median r of $-$0.91 --- the observed value is no more extreme (p = 0.80; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). The substance is the compression of the restored level, not the coefficient --- and the evidence we treat as central is the \emph{per-trigger} version of this fact, which shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at individual triggered decisions under the hardened harness, the incumbent's degradation over the five preceding windows predicts the candidate-minus-incumbent value over the five following windows, while the detector's score at the same decisions shows no consistent association (Fig.~\ref{fig:9}; clustered CIs and a hierarchical model in \S\ref{sec:v2}). Second, and free of any coupling, \textbf{the detector's own score carries almost no information about that headroom within a deployment}: over the 30 streams of each regime, the correlation between a detector's score and the gain from its own triggered adaptations has median +0.06 (maximum 0.13) across regimes, whereas deployed-model BA tracks the gain within every regime (median $-$0.68). Across regimes, classical detector scores do co-vary with the benefit (r = 0.75--0.94) --- larger drifts both fire detectors and degrade models --- but that cross-regime association is unavailable to a deployed detector calibrated on its own reference, and the persistence policies built on those scores fail to prevent harm (\S5.5). The regularity itself is robust to disaggregation: at individual-stream level r = $-$0.91 (210 regime$\times$seed points), stable under seed resampling (CI95 [$-$0.90, $-$0.88]) and under removing any one regime (r between $-$0.72 and $-$0.92) or dataset --- with the expected range-restriction attenuation, not inversion, when all four CICIDS benefit regimes are removed at once (r = $-$0.52; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). Where the deployed model has already collapsed (DDoS: 69.8\% without adaptation), retraining on a fresh window is a large gain; where it remains strong (ToN-IoT: 92.2\%), replacing a well-generalized model with a narrowly-fit current-window model is a net loss.


This exposes the core problem. Drift detectors answer \emph{"has the distribution changed?"}, but an adaptive NIDS needs \emph{"will retraining improve the classifier?"} --- and these are different questions. We quantify the gap with the per-stream policy oracle of \S3.5 (holding the detector fixed); being a policy-selection bound, it understates the headroom of perfect per-trigger decisions. The resulting \emph{regret} of naive triggering is essentially zero where adaptation always helps (Wednesday, DDoS: 0.00 points, adaptation hurt on 0\% of streams) and largest where it hurts (ToN-IoT: 3.53 points for QK-ZZ, CI95 [1.76, 5.54], adaptation hurt on 67\% of streams), scaling monotonically with harm frequency (Table~\ref{tab:table4_oracle_regret}, Fig.~\ref{fig:3}). The headroom left by naive triggering is real and concentrated exactly where the deployed model is already good.


\subsection{Detector sensitivity does not resolve the decision}

\input{tables/table2_phase2_gate_summary.tex}
Because drift detectors are blind to model degradation, improving the detector cannot solve the problem --- and it does not. Quantum-kernel MMD monitors do define distinct \emph{operating points} on CICIDS2017 (QK-MMD ZZ triggers 1.8--2.1 fewer readaptations than Energy distance at equivalent accuracy in Wednesday/DDoS, and inverts in PortScan), but at a ~114$\times$ monitoring-runtime cost, and these profiles do not replicate externally. Critically, the regime taxonomy and the harmful-adaptation outcome are identical for classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:3}, Tables 1--2): on ToN-IoT, naive triggering is net-harmful whether the trigger is KS-max ($-$1.4 points) or QK-ZZ ($-$3.7 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). Among the monitors evaluated, the operative lever is the adapt/no-adapt decision, not the similarity measure that fires the trigger.


\subsection{Simple confirmation and cooldown policies do not prevent harm}


If the decision is the lever, the natural fix is a smarter trigger policy. We pre-specified a grid of alarm-confirmation (k-of-n) and cooldown policies and evaluated them against fixed success criteria (preserve benefit in PortScan; avoid harm in ToN-IoT; do not degrade UNSW). None passed: in the harmful regime the tested policies remained far below no-adaptation and did not reduce adaptations relative to a conservative legacy baseline (Table~\ref{tab:table5_phase1_negative}, Appendix). Confirmation and cooldown act on the \emph{persistence} of distributional change, which is exactly the signal that is uninformative about whether adaptation helps. This negative result motivates a policy that acts on the right quantity.


\subsection{A label-efficient validate-before-commit gate resolves the problem}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig4_phase2_gate.png}
\caption{The validate-before-commit gate preserves benefit and avoids harm, with the same sign pattern for a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (magnitudes differ; see text).}
\label{fig:4}
\end{figure}
We introduce a decision gate that acts directly on estimated model improvement. On each drift trigger, a candidate model is retrained as usual, but it is \textbf{deployed only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe} drawn from the current window (budget 32 or 64 flows); otherwise the incumbent is kept. We evaluate the gate against the same pre-specified criteria (30 seeds, 100 windows, legacy trigger fixed so the gate is the only designed difference) across the benefit (PortScan), mixed (UNSW Reconnaissance) and harmful (ToN-IoT) regimes, for both a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}, Fig.~\ref{fig:4}).

\input{tables/table3_phase2_paired_ci.tex}
The gate passes every criterion for both detectors. In the harmful regime it turns net-harmful naive triggering into net benefit: ToN-IoT gain moves from $-$1.36 (KS-max) and $-$3.69 (QK-ZZ) points to +0.93 and +1.06, \textbf{significantly above naive triggering} (+2.30 points, CI95 [1.15, 3.63] for KS-max; +4.74, [2.47, 7.69] for QK-ZZ; Table~\ref{tab:table3_phase2_paired_ci}) and, notably, \textbf{significantly above no-adaptation itself} (+0.93, [0.53, 1.36]; +1.06, [0.77, 1.40]) --- the gate does not merely stop adapting, it commits the minority of beneficial adaptations while rejecting the harmful majority. In the benefit regime it preserves the full gain (PortScan +8.3/+8.4 points, statistically indistinguishable from naive; formally, benefit preservation was pre-specified as \emph{non-inferiority} at a 0.3-point margin, and the paired gate$-$naive CI95 lower bounds --- $\ge$ $-$0.12 across both detectors and both budgets --- clear it), and it does not degrade the mixed regime. The label cost is small: roughly 100--160 labeled flows over a 100-window stream at budget 32.


The gate's power comes from labels, not from the confirmation heuristic: a zero-label variant that commits on deployed-vs-candidate prediction \emph{disagreement} fails, remaining significantly below no-adaptation in the harmful regime ($-$4.95 and $-$5.26 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). A handful of target-labeled flows per confirmed drift therefore converts net-harmful naive updating into net benefit across the evaluated benefit, marginal and harm regimes --- without giving up the benefit, and matching what \S5.7 shows even state-of-the-art label-free estimators do not. We are precise about ``safe'': the point rule is not a formal guarantee (\S\ref{sec:v2} reports its residual harmful-commit rate, 15--32\% of commits, and the risk-controlled gates that bound it); the claim here is average harm-avoidance at this operating point, not a certified safety property. Because the gate behaves the same way (same sign pattern) for the classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:4}), the contribution is a detector-agnostic decision mechanism.


\subsection{Gate robustness (post-registration)}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig5_label_budget_curve.png}
\caption{Label-efficiency frontier: gain over no-adaptation versus probe budget; a small probe suffices to avoid harm.}
\label{fig:5}
\end{figure}
Ten extensions beyond the pre-specified design (explicitly labeled as robustness, not part of the confirmatory claim) probe the gate's limits. \emph{Label efficiency:} sweeping the probe budget from 8 to 128 flows (KS-max, 30 seeds; Fig.~\ref{fig:5}) shows the gate is remarkably label-cheap --- in this initial study even the smallest budget avoided harm, ~8 labeled flows per confirmed drift ($\approx$23 per stream) lifting ToN-IoT gain from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.38, plateauing near +1.1 by budget 32--64 (the hardened-harness re-run sharpens this: at $b{=}8$ harm is reduced but not converted to net benefit, fixing the operating point at $b{=}32$; \S\ref{sec:v2}); benefit-regime gain is flat at every budget. A few dozen labels, not hundreds, suffice. \emph{Benefit breadth:} beyond PortScan the gate preserves --- and slightly improves --- the benefit in the two strongest benefit regimes, Wednesday (naive +15.39 $\to$ +16.32) and DDoS (naive +24.81 $\to$ +25.27), preserving benefit in all three benefit regimes tested --- the exception being the deep-benefit chronological replays, where a stale probe imposes a measured, metric-dependent premium (\S\ref{sec:v2}).


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig8_probe_poison.png}
\caption{The gate remains harm-avoiding under randomly corrupted validation labels (up to 40\% flipped, initial study; replicated on the hardened harness in \S\ref{sec:v2}).}
\label{fig:8}
\end{figure}
\emph{Label latency:} the probe need not be current. Drawing it from traffic labeled up to 20 windows in the past (Fig.~\ref{fig:7}) leaves the gate safe and effective at every lag --- in the harmful regime the gate holds at +0.9 to +1.1 (vs naive $-$1.36) for lags of 5, 10 and 20 windows, and the benefit regimes are essentially unchanged --- so the method tolerates realistic labeling delay rather than requiring instantaneous labels. \emph{Harm breadth:} the net-harm of naive triggering (with the fragile SVC-RBF downstream) is not confined to one ToN-IoT regime --- it also appears in ToN-IoT DDoS (naive \textbf{$-$16.81}) and Injection ($-$1.21), and the gate converts all three to net-positive (+1.26, +0.80, +0.93). \emph{Cost knob:} raising the commit margin $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed adaptations by ~30\% (e.g. PortScan 3.1$\to$2.2 commits) at equal downstream gain, giving a simple operating characteristic that trades update frequency for benefit. \emph{Randomly corrupted validation labels:} because the gate trusts a labeled probe, we test a faulty labeler (or non-adaptive attacker) that flips a uniformly random fraction of the probe labels --- the random variant of the label-flipping poisoning threat against SVM-family learners \cite{biggio2012poisoning} (Fig.~\ref{fig:8}). The gate avoids harm under this corruption process: even with 40\% of validation labels corrupted --- near the point where the probe is pure noise --- it still matches-or-beats naive triggering in every regime (ToN-IoT degrades gracefully from +0.93 to +0.32 but never goes negative; benefit and mixed regimes are essentially unchanged). Label noise perturbs the deployed and candidate estimates symmetrically, so it moves the gate along the naive-to-oracle line without pushing it below the safe baseline. (A candidate-aware adversary that chooses \emph{which} labels to flip is a strictly stronger threat we do not model; \S7.) \emph{Label-free estimators head-to-head:} the zero-label variant of \S5.6 is deliberately naive, so we also instantiate the two standard label-free accuracy estimators --- ATC \cite{garg2022atc} and DoC \cite{guillory2021doc} --- as gates in the same harness: each model estimates its target-window accuracy from its own confidences plus a labeled validation sample drawn at its training time, using zero target-window labels. Both avoid harm --- on the fragile SVC-RBF downstream, ToN-IoT moves from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.40 (ATC) and +1.16 (DoC) --- but both pay for safety with benefit: on PortScan they lose 24\% (ATC) and 60\% (DoC) of the naive gain ($-$1.86 [$-$3.48, $-$0.17] and $-$4.64 [$-$6.86, $-$2.26] vs naive, CI95), and they remain significantly below naive even for random forest with native predicted probabilities. The labeled probe is thus the only gate on the benefit--safety Pareto front \emph{among those evaluated}. The mechanism mirrors the degradation law: confidence-based estimates are trustworthy only while the incumbent is healthy --- exactly the regime where the gate is least needed --- and misrank incumbent against candidate once the incumbent has degraded, which is where the benefit lives; related estimators share the same calibration premise \cite{jiang2022disagreement,baek2022agreement,rosenfeld2023dis2,deng2021labels}. \emph{Replay retraining:} the harmful baseline retrains on a narrow current window and fully replaces the incumbent, so we also test the standard label-free mitigation --- retrain-current-plus-replay, augmenting each candidate's training set with an equal-size sample from the reference regime (50/50) --- under pre-fixed criteria (KS-max, SVC-RBF, 30 seeds). The tested fixed 50/50 replay does not rescue naive triggering: in the harm regime it remains significantly net-harmful ($-$4.54, CI95 [$-$7.06, $-$2.34] vs no-adaptation; worse than full replacement's $-$1.36, since under a still-advancing ramp a replay-anchored candidate misfits later windows even more), and in the benefit regime it \emph{loses} part of the gain (+6.59 vs +7.79 naive). The gate, by contrast, is orthogonal to the update rule: placed on top of replay it restores safety and benefit in every regime --- ToN-IoT +0.59, significantly above both no-adaptation ([0.23, 0.97]) and replay-naive (+5.13 [2.96, 7.60]); PortScan +8.26, recovering the benefit replay-naive loses (+1.66 [0.52, 3.04]). Harmful adaptation is therefore not an artifact of full-replacement retraining, and the gate's value composes with the update strategy. \emph{Probe prevalence:} the probes above are class-balanced, which presumes an enrichment mechanism. An initial low-prevalence experiment (conditioned on the probe containing at least one attack) suggested the gate was indifferent down to $\pi=0.01$; that conditioning turned out to force a minority-class flow into every probe and the claim was an artifact. \S\ref{sec:v2} reports the corrected experiment, in which the attack count is drawn honestly as $\mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$: the gate tolerates genuinely random inspection down to $\pi\approx0.05$, but its harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi=0.01$ (where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all). We report the corrected bound and withdraw the stronger one. \emph{Candidate size and dimensionality:} two controls address the concern that the harm is a pipeline artifact. Size-matching: training every candidate on 2{,}000 flows per class --- the same budget as the initial model, removing the 4$\times$ size asymmetry --- \emph{deepens} the harm (ToN-IoT naive $-$5.34, CI95 [$-$8.73, $-$2.49], vs $-$1.36 at 512/class): a larger candidate fits the current mixture better and mis-specializes harder while the ramp keeps advancing, the opposite of what a small-sample explanation predicts; the gate remains safe and beneficial throughout (+0.58 / +9.33 / +3.37 across the three regimes), and candidate size does shift the marginal regime's magnitude (UNSW +3.31 vs +0.92), so magnitudes are pipeline-dependent even where signs are not. Dimensionality: with the PCA bottleneck removed (full feature space, classical detector), the benefit grows (PortScan +16.2) and the harm regime's point estimate remains negative ($-$0.65 [$-$3.03, +1.44], not significant at 30 seeds) --- neither the candidate's sample size nor the 8-dimensional projection manufactures the phenomenon, though full dimensionality attenuates the harm and we report that plainly. \emph{Significance of the safety claim.} Paired bootstrap CIs in the harm regime confirm the guarantee is not noise: the gate significantly outperforms naive triggering under every label-latency and every probe-poison level tested (CI95 excludes zero), and in no robustness condition --- across downstream models, latencies or poison levels --- is it significantly \emph{worse} than either naive triggering or no-adaptation. Where the gate is not significantly better than naive (the robust downstream models, where naive is already non-harmful), it is statistically indistinguishable from it, i.e. never worse.


\subsection{Generalization across downstream classifiers}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig6_downstream_generalization.png}
\caption{Generalization across four downstream classifiers: net-harm is specific to SVC-RBF; the gate is safe for all.}
\label{fig:6}
\end{figure}
\input{tables/table6_downstream_generalization.tex}
To test whether the phenomenon and the solution depend on the downstream model, we repeat the three regimes with the KS-max trigger for four downstream classifiers --- SVC-RBF, random forest, logistic regression and MLP (20 seeds; Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}, Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). Three findings stand out, and we report them plainly because they refine the headline.


First, \textbf{the degradation--headroom regularity generalizes.} Across all four classifiers the benefit of adaptation tracks deployed-model degradation: where the frozen model is weak the gain is large (e.g. random forest and MLP on PortScan start at 69--70\% no-adaptation BA and gain +27 points; logistic regression on ToN-IoT starts at 84\% and gains +6.7), and where the frozen model is already strong the gain is small (random forest on ToN-IoT starts at 97\% and gains only +1.9). Pooled across all four classifiers the degradation--benefit correlation remains strong (r = $-$0.81, \S5.3; Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}): the relationship is a property of the adaptation problem, not of SVC.


Second, \textbf{whether the effect crosses into net harm is downstream-specific.} The negative-gain result --- naive triggering below no-adaptation --- occurs only for SVC-RBF on ToN-IoT ($-$1.36 points); random forest (+1.88), logistic regression (+6.70) and MLP (+0.24) all remain non-negative there (Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). SVC-RBF is fragile enough on this regime that retraining on a narrow current window loses more than it gains; more robust downstream models do not. We therefore do not claim that naive readaptation is universally harmful --- only that it can be, for fragile models, exactly where the regularity predicts the smallest headroom.


Third, and most important for deployment, \textbf{the gate avoids material harm in all twelve settings of this study.} Across all four downstream classifiers and all three regimes (twelve settings), the labeled-probe gate shows no material deficit against no-adaptation or naive triggering; the largest observed mean deficit vs.\ naive anywhere is 0.11 BA points (random forest on UNSW Reconnaissance), within seed noise (Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}). (The hardened-harness replication puts sharper bounds on this claim --- including one statistically resolvable but immaterial 0.01-point deficit, and a real chronological stream where the premium is material; \S\ref{sec:v2}.) This empirical safety property holds even in the settings where naive triggering is already fine (the gate simply commits), and rescues the one setting where it is harmful. The solution generalizes across downstream models even though the harm does not.


\subsection{Controls}


The findings are robust to the evaluation metric (the taxonomy and harmful-adaptation result hold under attack-F1; \S5.2). Quantum monitors do not reduce nuisance triggers on benign drift (QK-MMD ZZ 1.40 vs Energy 1.03 false triggers), so their value cannot be framed as cleaner benign-drift filtering; and an expensive Random-Forest downstream shows that quantum monitoring runtime can dominate total operational cost. A pre-specified feature-map sensitivity study finds no deeper map that unlocks a stronger quantum advantage. (Full tables for both: reproducibility artifact \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact}.) These controls reinforce, rather than qualify, the central conclusion: distribution-shift magnitude alone is not a sufficient commit signal.
