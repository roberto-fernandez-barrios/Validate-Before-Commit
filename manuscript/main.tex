\begin{abstract}
Adaptive intrusion detectors retrain when a drift monitor fires, but a drift alarm \emph{proposes} a challenger --- it does not establish that the challenger beats the deployed incumbent. In our original experiments, where one scaler and PCA fitted at deployment served every later challenger, always-deploying drift-triggered updates was net-harmful under full drift. A preregistered replication with self-contained pipelines removed that mean harm, but a residual zero-drift harm remained when 512-per-class challengers faced a 2{,}000-per-class incumbent ($-1.70$, $-0.65$ and $-0.24$ balanced-accuracy points on CICIDS2017-PortScan, UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT). A final preregistered control isolates the challenger's nominal training size under zero drift (random proposals, balanced pools, self-contained SVC-RBF pipelines): at nominal 2{,}000-per-class sample-size parity, always-deploy is equivalent to never-adapt in all three benchmarks within the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point CI90 margin ($+0.19$, $-0.02$, $-0.01$ points; PortScan is margin-sensitive at $\pm0.2$), the candidate-size effect is Holm-significant everywhere ($+1.89$, $+0.63$, $+0.23$), and point/strict gates provide no measurable gain under this control (all effects $<0.14$ points, none significant). The registered taxonomy returned ATTENUATION because its sign-based future-value criterion remained inconclusive despite the near-zero mean. Pipeline construction and evidence conditions therefore precede promotion testing, and validation gates are conditional tools for asymmetric or uncertain candidates --- not a universal requirement. Thirteen chronological replays show no net harm, an acquisition-yield simulation prices operational labeling, and results reproduce from a public artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


Machine-learning network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) are trained on historical traffic \cite{nguyen2023tsids}, yet the traffic they monitor evolves: new services, shifting user behaviour, reconfigured infrastructure and novel attacks all move the input distribution away from the training distribution, a phenomenon known as concept drift \cite{shyaa2024evolving,wahab2022intrusion,roshan2018adaptive}. As the distribution shifts, a frozen classifier loses accuracy, so operational NIDS periodically \textbf{retrain} their downstream classifier on recent traffic \cite{wahab2022intrusion,camarda2025managing,constantinides2019novel}. Retraining is not free --- it consumes labeled data and compute and destabilizes a deployed model --- so the central operational question is \emph{when} to retrain.


The dominant answer frames this as a \textbf{drift-detection} problem: run a statistical monitor that compares recent traffic to a reference window, and trigger retraining when the monitor signals a change \cite{gama2004learning,rabanser2019failing,lukats2024benchmark}. A large literature accordingly competes to build more sensitive monitors, from univariate change detectors to multivariate two-sample tests such as maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) and energy distance \cite{gretton2012kernel,szekely2013energy}, and more recently to quantum-kernel similarity measures whose feature-map geometry may respond differently to distributional change \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,kaissar2026enhancing}. The implicit assumption is that a better change detector yields a better retraining policy.


This paper challenges that assumption with a controlled decomposition of the adaptation loop. Adaptive systems routinely conflate the \emph{trigger} with the \emph{promotion}: an alarm (or a schedule, or a false alarm) \emph{proposes} a challenger, and the quality of that proposal depends on how the challenger is constructed and evidenced --- not on how far the distribution moved. Across three public benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT) we establish two facts about that gap. First, whether an update helps tracks not the drift magnitude but how much the deployed model has already degraded: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so under a fixed candidate generator the benefit of an update is largely the deployed model's headroom to that level (r $\approx$ $-$0.8 to $-$0.9 across four downstream classifiers, with a coupling-aware analysis in Supplementary \S S1.1 and a per-decision hierarchical model in \S\ref{sec:v2}); a drift monitor, which measures distributional change, does not track this quantity --- within triggered decisions under the evaluated detectors, score magnitude provided no consistent incremental information about the gain, and this holds even when the detector is a quantum-kernel MMD monitor rather than a classical two-sample test. Second --- the paper's final, controlled finding --- \textbf{evaluations that are not symmetric between incumbent and challenger can produce apparent promotion harm}: deploying candidates whose construction or evidence differs materially from the incumbent can be harmful, and a trigger alone does not reveal whether that asymmetry matters. In our controlled experiments the harm we originally reported traces to two such asymmetries --- challengers scored inside a frozen, incumbent-owned representation (the harm we observed under full drift, including a ToN-IoT regime where \emph{no adaptation} beat every triggered strategy), and challengers trained on one quarter of the incumbent's per-class evidence (the residual harm under zero drift). Candidate validation, on this account, should be evaluated only \emph{after} comparability is established --- which is exactly the sequence of controls this paper runs.


We therefore argue that adaptive model updating should be recast from a \emph{detection} problem to a cost-aware \emph{decision} --- in fact two decisions: whether to train a candidate at all, and, once a candidate exists, whether to \emph{deploy} it. This paper is chiefly about the second decision, where the detection-centric loop silently answers "always". We evaluate a \textbf{validate-before-commit} gate that, on each triggered drift, retrains a candidate model but deploys it only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe drawn from current traffic (in the core harness a pool sample at the current mixture --- an idealization; the observed-data arm of \S\ref{sec:v2} uses only past observed windows); a two-stage variant additionally spends a disjoint half of the same budget \emph{before} training, to skip candidates the incumbent's health does not warrant. Across benefit, mixed and harmful regimes, the gate (pre-specified criteria, 30 seeds) preserves the gains of updating where it helps, avoids harm where it does not, significantly outperforms naive in the harm regime, and exceeds never-adapting across the confirmatory cells; its robustness envelope (label staleness to 20 windows, up to 40\% corrupted validation labels, two detectors, four downstream classifiers) is established in \S\ref{sec:v2}. In the controlled balanced-probe confirmatory setting the commit decision is label-efficient in a strictly bounded sense: a 32-label point gate converts average harm into average benefit, but candidates themselves consume 1{,}024 labeled flows per trigger whether or not the gate commits --- the probe is the incremental cost of the \emph{decision}, not of the pipeline, and both are accounted throughout --- while formal risk control, severe imbalance and stratified deployment-long guarantees require larger or differently acquired samples. Label-free alternatives --- from naive disagreement to state-of-the-art accuracy estimators (ATC, DoC) --- either fail outright or sacrifice much of the benefit: in this controlled setting, target labels spent on the decision rather than the detector are what buys harm avoidance without giving up the gains. The program's final verdict, however, bounds the gate itself: its measured value concentrates exactly where the challenger's construction or evidence is asymmetric, and in the evaluated zero-drift control, once challengers are self-contained and trained at matched nominal per-class size, it adds nothing measurable (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}) --- validate-before-commit is a conditional control, not a universal requirement.


Our contributions are three. \textbf{(1) A decomposition of candidate promotion} that separates the loop's stages --- \emph{trigger} $\to$ \emph{candidate construction} $\to$ \emph{evidence comparability} $\to$ \emph{candidate validation} $\to$ \emph{promotion} --- and shows each answers a distinct question: an alarm proposes; construction determines whether the challenger \emph{can} compete fairly; evidence comparability determines whether the comparison is informative; validation estimates the sign of the specific proposal; and promotion is the decision a healthy incumbent can win. \textbf{(2) Controlled identification of two candidate-construction asymmetries}, each by a preregistered replication on identical raw streams with fresh seeds, frozen margins, equivalence testing and attack-recall/FPR guardrails. \emph{Transformer ownership} (frozen vs.\ self-contained, seeds 3001--3030): scoring challengers inside the incumbent's frozen scaler/PCA explains the full-drift difference --- the mean full-drift harm does not persist once each challenger owns its preprocessing, and a role-randomized experiment localizes the mechanism to feature scaling (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}, \S\ref{sec:risk}). \emph{Candidate evidence} (512 vs.\ 2{,}000 per class, seeds 4001--4030, nested candidate batches): training challengers on one quarter of the incumbent's per-class sample size explains the residual zero-drift mean harm --- under that zero-drift control, self-contained challengers at nominal 2{,}000-per-class sample-size parity are mean-equivalent to never adapting in all three benchmarks within the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point margin (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}). \textbf{(3) The conditional value of commit gates}: point/strict validation materially helps when the challenger's construction or evidence is asymmetric (frozen preprocessing, or a 4$\times$ nominal-evidence disadvantage), and provides no measurable benefit in the evaluated zero-drift control once pipelines and nominal per-class sizes are matched; the sequential VBC-SG family and its accuracy--labels--risk frontier formalize the gate for settings where one \emph{does} validate, with guarantees and costs evaluated under the historical frozen policy --- candidate validation emerges as a conditional control, not a universal necessity (\S\ref{sec:risk}). An acquisition-yield simulation and thirteen chronological replays (no net harm observed) bound the evidence operationally and externally (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}). The paper proceeds: related work (\S2), the decision and the gate (\S3), protocol (\S4), results (\S5), implications and limits (\S6--\S8).


\section{Related work}


\textbf{Concept drift and adaptive intrusion detection.} Concept drift degrades ML-based NIDS, and a body of work adapts models online or on a schedule to compensate \cite{gama2014survey,lu2019learning,shyaa2024evolving}. Most such systems assume adaptation is beneficial and focus on \emph{how} to adapt (incremental learning, windowing, ensembles) \cite{constantinides2019novel,roshan2018adaptive,shyaa2023enhanced}. We instead study \emph{whether and when} adaptation helps, and show it can be net-harmful --- connecting to, but distinct from, work on negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting in continual learning \cite{mccloskey1989catastrophic,kirkpatrick2017overcoming,zhang2023survey}, which we observe operationally in a security setting.


\textbf{Drift detection and two-sample monitors.} Drift is commonly detected with change detectors \cite{bifet2007learning,gama2004learning,baena2006early,raab2020reactive}, statistical-test ensembles over batch streams \cite{komorniczak2022ensemble}, or multivariate two-sample tests --- MMD, energy distance, Kolmogorov--Smirnov and Jensen--Shannon divergence --- used as retraining or alarm triggers \cite{gretton2012kernel,rizzo2016energy,lin1991divergence,darling1957ks}; how drift manifests (type, locality, severity) itself varies widely across streams \cite{aguiar2024locality}. Our classical baselines are drawn from this family. A complementary family of \emph{performance-aware} detectors monitors the model's error signal directly, presupposing continuous labeled feedback; Bayram et al. \cite{bayram2022degradation} survey them and formalize the distinction between distributional change and model degradation that our degradation--headroom analysis (Supplementary \S S1.1) quantifies empirically. We use distribution-level monitors but argue that improving them does not improve the retraining decision, because they are blind to model degradation --- and our gate concentrates the label budget at the update decision instead of spending it on continuous performance monitoring.


\textbf{Quantum kernels for drift monitoring.} Quantum-kernel methods embed data via parameterized quantum feature maps and have been proposed for classification and similarity estimation \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,huang2021power}, including exploratory use in cybersecurity \cite{gouveia2020towards,nicesio2023quantum,bellante2025evaluating}. In a companion study \cite{fernandez_paper1} we found that fidelity-based quantum kernels can improve out-of-distribution robustness under shift, the operative property being the kernel's geometry rather than its quantumness --- which motivated evaluating quantum-kernel MMD as a drift monitor here. The present paper reaches the analogous conclusion one level up: for the \emph{update decision}, changing the detector family --- quantum or classical --- did not change the outcome in any regime we evaluated --- consistent with mounting evidence that quantum-kernel advantage claims require careful classical baselining and face concentration limits \cite{schnabel2025quantum,thanasilp2024concentration}.


\textbf{Drift, rejection and evaluation discipline in ML security.} A parallel security literature asks when a deployed classifier should no longer be trusted. Transcend \cite{jordaney2017transcend} and its revision Transcendent \cite{barbero2022transcendent} use conformal evaluation to reject individual predictions whose low credibility signals model aging; CADE \cite{yang2021cade} detects and explains drifting samples via contrastive representations; INSOMNIA \cite{andresini2021insomnia} updates a NIDS continuously with semi-supervision as drift accrues. These systems abstain on individual samples or adapt continuously; none evaluates whether a specific retrained candidate should replace the incumbent. Our gate acts at exactly that decision: it compares candidate against incumbent on current traffic and can conclude that the correct action is \emph{not to update at all} --- the case Supplementary \S S1.1 shows is real. TESSERACT \cite{pendlebury2019tesseract} and the ``dos and don'ts'' of Arp et al. \cite{arp2022dodonts} document how temporal and experimental bias inflate security-ML results; our pre-specified protocol with temporally ordered progressive windows and paired within-run baselines (\S4) follows that discipline.


\textbf{When to retrain: cost-aware and validation-gated updates.} Beyond detection, some work weighs the cost of adaptation or validates a candidate before deployment, e.g. in MLOps model-update pipelines and shadow/champion--challenger evaluation \cite{nath2007champion,sculley2015hidden,kreuzberger2023mlops,guissouma2023continuous}; that a well-intentioned update can be net-harmful is documented beyond security \cite{bansal2019updates}. Our gate instantiates this idea for streaming NIDS, quantifies its label cost, and --- unlike prior work --- delimits \emph{when} the validation layer earns its keep: under construction or evidence asymmetry, not universally. Its reliance on a small labeled probe connects to active and label-efficient learning for data streams \cite{cacciarelli2024active,krawczyk2019adaptive,liu2021comprehensive}. A complementary line estimates a model's accuracy under distribution shift without target labels \cite{garg2022atc,guillory2021doc,deng2021labels,baek2022agreement,jiang2022disagreement,rosenfeld2023dis2}; rather than argue past it, we instantiate its two standard estimators (ATC, DoC) as gates in our own harness and find they trade benefit for safety (Supplementary \S S1.5).


\section{Method}


\subsection{Adaptive readaptation as a sequential decision}


We consider a deployed binary classifier $h_0$ (benign vs. attack) monitoring a stream of traffic windows $W_1, W_2, \dots$ Each window $W_t$ is a set of flows with (at evaluation time) ground-truth labels; the deployed model degrades as the traffic distribution drifts. A drift monitor $D$ produces a score $s_t = D(W_t)$ measuring divergence of $W_t$ from a reference. The standard adaptive-NIDS loop is: raise an alarm when $s_t$ exceeds a threshold, and upon a confirmed alarm \textbf{retrain} the classifier on recent data. We make explicit that this loop conflates two questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{detection:} \emph{has the distribution changed?} --- answered by $D$;
  \item \textbf{decision:} \emph{will replacing $h$ with a model retrained on the current window improve accuracy?} --- which $D$ does not answer.
\end{itemize}


Let $h$ be the incumbent model and $h'$ a candidate retrained on the current regime. Adaptation is beneficial at $t$ iff $\mathrm{acc}(h', W_t) > \mathrm{acc}(h, W_t)$. Because $D$ measures distributional change rather than the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h',\cdot)-\mathrm{acc}(h,\cdot)$, a detector-only policy commits every confirmed change, including changes for which retraining does not help. Our method (\S3.4) inserts a decision step that estimates this sign before committing.


\subsection{Drift monitors}


We use two-sample drift monitors comparing a reference window (from the pre-drift regime) to the current window. Classical baselines: \textbf{Energy distance}, \textbf{RBF-MMD}, per-feature \textbf{Kolmogorov--Smirnov} (max reduction) and histogram \textbf{Jensen--Shannon} divergence. Quantum monitors: \textbf{quantum-kernel MMD} with \textbf{ZZ} and \textbf{Pauli-XZ} feature maps ($q_{\text{reps}}=1$, angle encoding with standardized \texttt{atan} scaling), the MMD being computed in the quantum-kernel-induced RKHS. All monitors expose a scalar score; thresholds are calibrated per detector on held-out calibration windows at a fixed quantile (0.95). The paper's claims are detector-agnostic; the quantum monitors are included as instruments and to test whether a more expressive kernel changes the decision (it does not, Supplementary \S S1.2).


\subsection{Progressive-drift readaptation protocol}


Each stream has 100 post-drift windows. Window $t$ is a class-balanced sample in which each class is a mixture of the reference and current pools with mixing weight $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ ("severity"), ramped linearly from 0 to 1 over the first 80 windows and held at 1 thereafter, so drift arrives gradually. On a committed adaptation, the candidate is trained on a fresh balanced labeled sample from the current regime, and the detector reference is reset to the adapted regime. The trigger policy (alarm confirmation and cooldown) is held fixed across all gate conditions, so the decision gate is the only \emph{designed} difference between arms. On the confirmatory harness (v2) every arm additionally processes \emph{bit-identical} streams, so contrasts are exactly paired; only the initial exploratory study drew a separate pseudo-random realization per arm (identical in distribution, not in realization; \S4).


\subsection{The validate-before-commit gate}


Our contribution is a decision step interposed between the trigger and the model swap: a \emph{deployment} (commit) gate, not a retraining gate. On each confirmed trigger the loop retrains a candidate $h'$ as usual --- consuming its training labels and compute regardless of the outcome --- and the gate then decides only whether $h'$ replaces the incumbent $h$; otherwise we keep $h$ (and keep the detector reference, so a later window can re-propose once an update becomes worthwhile). \S\ref{sec:v2} additionally evaluates a two-stage variant that moves the same probe \emph{before} candidate training, gating the training decision itself. We evaluate three commit gates:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{none} (naive):} always commit --- the standard loop.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{labeled\_probe}$(b,\varepsilon)$:} draw a small balanced labeled probe $P$ of $b$ flows from the current window; commit iff $\mathrm{BA}(h',P) \ge \mathrm{BA}(h,P) + \varepsilon$. The probe is the method's incremental labeling cost; we report $b\in\{32,64\}$, $\varepsilon=0$.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{unsup\_disagree}$(\tau)$:} commit iff the fraction of flows on which $h$ and $h'$ disagree on the \emph{unlabeled} current window is $\ge \tau$ (a zero-label proxy for "adaptation would change decisions"); we report $\tau=0.15$.
\end{itemize}

Two variants are evaluated and we state both, because they differ in exactly the information they are allowed to use. Algorithm 1A is the \emph{core simulation} gate: the environment supplies candidate batches and probes from its class pools at the current mixture severity --- convenient, and the source of the paper's main tables, but an oracle a deployment lacks. Algorithm 1B is the \emph{observed-data} gate: every object it touches --- the candidate's training rows, the probe, and even the detector's post-commit reference --- comes from windows the system has actually seen, and nothing reads $\mathrm{sev}(t)$. \S\ref{sec:v2} reports 1B as a registered replication of 1A.

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm 1A: Gated update, core simulation harness (one stream)
  h <- h0 ; D <- fit_reference(pools, sev=0) ; alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- 0
  for t = 1..T:
      W_t <- environment.stream[t]              # pre-generated, balanced, labeled
      record BA(h, W_t)
      s_t <- D.score(W_t) ; alarms.append(s_t > threshold)
      trigger <- policy(alarms) and cooldown == 0
      if trigger:
          h' <- train(pools.train, sev(t))      # candidate: current mixture (oracle)
          P   <- sample(pools.probe, sev(t))    # probe: balanced, b flows (oracle)
          commit <- [ BA(h',P) >= BA(h,P) + eps ]                  # Sec 3.4
          if commit:
              h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(pools, sev(t))          # deploy + reset
          alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- C                             # applied either way
      else: cooldown <- max(0, cooldown - 1)
  return {BA(h, W_t)}_t

Algorithm 1B: Gated update, observed-data harness (no sev(t), no pools)
  ... identical control flow, but at a trigger at window t:
      h' <- train(rows of the last 8 OBSERVED windows)             # candidate
      P  <- 32 rows of the OBSERVED window t-9, natural composition,
            excluding by row identity any row used to train h'     # probe
      commit <- [ acc(h',P) >= acc(h,P) + eps ]
      if commit: h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(last 8 OBSERVED windows),
                 threshold <- quantile_0.95(scores of preceding OBSERVED windows)
\end{verbatim}


The \texttt{labeled\_probe} gate directly targets the quantity \S3.1 identifies (the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$), estimated from $b$ labels; the \texttt{unsup\_disagree} gate tests whether that estimate can be obtained without labels.


\textbf{A cost-sensitive reading: two decisions, two thresholds.} The update loop makes two sequential decisions with different cost structures, and it matters not to conflate them. Let each error cost $\lambda_e$ per flow, let deploying an update cost $\lambda_U$ (deployment risk, churn), let training a candidate cost $\lambda_C$ (compute plus its $2m$ training labels at $\lambda_L$ each), and let probe labels cost $\lambda_L$ each. \emph{At commit time the candidate already exists}: $\lambda_C$ and the probe are sunk, and the only decision-relevant cost of committing is $\lambda_U$. Over a horizon of $N$ post-decision flows, the myopic commit rule is therefore
$$\mathrm{commit} \iff \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h',P) - \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h,P) \ge \varepsilon^{\ast}, \qquad \varepsilon^{\ast} = \frac{\lambda_U}{\lambda_e N},$$
so the margin $\varepsilon$ is not a heuristic knob but the \emph{deployment} cost expressed in accuracy points over the remaining horizon --- $\lambda_C$ does not belong in $\varepsilon^{\ast}$, because rejecting the candidate cannot recover it. Training costs enter one decision earlier: whether to build a candidate at all, which pays off only if the \emph{expected} committed gain exceeds $(\lambda_C + \lambda_U)/(\lambda_e N)$. The two-stage gate of \S\ref{sec:v2} instantiates exactly this structure, spending the probe on the incumbent \emph{before} training and skipping candidate construction when the incumbent shows no measurable degradation --- saving all candidate labels and compute at those triggers. The probe budget $b$ trades $\lambda_L b$ against estimator variance, and a risk-averse operator replaces the point estimate with a lower confidence bound (the LCB variant of \S\ref{sec:v2}). The margin sweep of Supplementary \S S1.5 is the empirical operating characteristic of the commit rule: raising $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed updates by ${\sim}30\%$ at equal downstream gain, which is exactly the behaviour an operator with non-trivial $\lambda_U/\lambda_e$ should choose. Asymmetric error costs ($\lambda_{FN} \ne \lambda_{FP}$) substitute a weighted accuracy for BA in the same rule.


\subsection{Risk-controlled commit gates and the scope of their guarantee}
\label{sec:riskgates}

The point gate above ($\varepsilon{=}0$) \emph{maximizes the probe point-estimate} --- not future accuracy, as the strict-comparison baseline below shows a more conservative rule can win downstream: it commits on any observed probe advantage and carries no formal error control (about a quarter of its commits have negative realized value; \S\ref{sec:v2}). We separately study \emph{risk-controlled} gates that commit only on statistical evidence. Let the probe be $b$ flows drawn from a distribution $\mathcal P$, and for each flow let
$$d_i = \mathbf 1[h'\text{ correct on }i] - \mathbf 1[h\text{ correct on }i]\in\{-1,0,+1\}.$$
Three rules: (i) \textbf{exact McNemar} --- commit iff a one-sided exact test on the discordant pairs rejects $H_0:\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0$ at level $\alpha$ (ties never commit); (ii) \textbf{Robbins CS} --- a normal-mixture (sub-Gaussian, $\sigma^2\le1$) lower confidence sequence \cite{howard2021timeuniform} on $\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]$, commit at the first look whose lower bound exceeds $0$; (iii) \textbf{empirical-Bernstein CS} --- a predictable-plug-in betting confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} using the empirical variance of $d_i$ (far tighter, since the $d_i$ are mostly ties). The CS gates inspect in blocks (16 flows) up to a budget $b$; if the lower bound never clears $0$ they do \emph{not} commit (the risk-averse default), so at the maximum budget an undecided proposal is rejected. Because the probe is drawn with \emph{fixed class quotas} ($b/2$ per class) rather than i.i.d.\ from one distribution, the pooled tests above match the sampling only approximately; \emph{stratified} variants close that gap by lower-bounding each class's mean correctness difference at $\alpha/2$ (Bonferroni over the two classes) and committing iff $\tfrac12(L_{\mathrm{ben}}+L_{\mathrm{att}})>0$, which bounds the false-superiority probability for the \emph{balanced-accuracy} difference under the stratified design actually used. We evaluate two: a \emph{fixed-sample} version (per-class one-sided normal LCB --- an approximation at $16$ samples per class, carrying no sequential validity) and the \emph{anytime-valid} version, in which each class's bound is an empirical-Bernstein confidence sequence inspected at the same sequential looks as the pooled gate; only the latter supports the anytime-valid claim, and the text is explicit about which variant each number comes from. \textbf{Levels used.} The confidence sequences run at $\alpha=0.10$; the exact-McNemar gate runs at its registered $\alpha=0.05$ (a stricter level, so its zero-drift behaviour is if anything \emph{more} conservative than the $0.10$ CS gates, not less). To rule out that the McNemar gate's near-total abstention is an artifact of the stricter level, we also run it at $\alpha=0.10$ (\S\ref{sec:risk}): the zero-drift conclusion is unchanged (still $\approx 0$ commits). The effective per-arm $\alpha$ is recorded in the run manifest.

\textbf{VBC-SG: the assembled policy.} The rules above form a family --- a sequential decision-support instantiation for settings in which validation is chosen --- and the paper's algorithmic contribution is that family together with the frontier it spans --- \emph{not} a claim that any one member dominates, and not the source of the paper's final verdict. We name its maximally-guaranteed member, the \emph{Validate-Before-Commit Sequential Gate}, because it is the configuration that carries every layer of control at once; \S\ref{sec:risk} then shows what each layer costs, and that a \emph{less} stratified sibling and even the no-additional-label strict rule are the better buy at some operating points. VBC-SG assembles the pieces as follows: per-class empirical-Bernstein confidence sequences (the stratified anytime-valid bounds above) drive a three-action decision --- \textsc{commit} when the balanced-accuracy lower bound clears zero, \textsc{reject} when the upper bound falls below zero (futility), and \textsc{defer} otherwise: the candidate is retained and evidence continues at subsequent windows, up to a deferral cap, rejecting at the cap. Deferral raises a question the first version of this gate left implicit: once the stream drifts \emph{between} windows, what does a sequence that keeps acquiring labels at later windows estimate? We make the answer explicit through three registered continuation modes. \emph{Cohort-sim} (fixed-target): additional labels are resampled from the proposal-time target distribution available to the simulator (the mixture the proposal was raised on), so the sequence estimates one fixed mean and the per-proposal guarantee is the clean fixed-target one. Cohort-sim does not model retention and delayed adjudication of a literal production cohort, so this acquisition scheme is not claimed to be directly deployable. \emph{Refresh}: each deferral window opens a \emph{fresh} evidence set tested at $\alpha_j/(1+D)$ (Bonferroni over the at most $1+D$ sets a proposal with deferral cap $D$ may open), so observations from temporally different targets are never mixed. \emph{Accumulate} (the behaviour of the earlier amendments, retained as an ablation): the same sequences continue on labels drawn at each current window; by Proposition~1 below this mode still bounds the probability of a false commit, but only under the \emph{weak conditional null} that the challenger is at no time conditionally better on the probe stream --- rejecting that null establishes that the challenger was conditionally better at \emph{some} window, not necessarily at commit time, which is why cohort and refresh are the interpretable variants under drift. A deployment-long risk budget allocates each proposal's level $\alpha_j$ by a declared spending schedule (Bonferroni $\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}/M$, or the $p$-series $\alpha_j = 6\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}/(\pi^2 j^2)$, which needs no horizon $M$); the effective $\alpha_j$ of every proposal is logged. Four properties, stated at the strength we can support: (i) \emph{per-proposal}: the probability of falsely establishing positive expected balanced-accuracy difference on the probe distribution is at most $\alpha_j$, uniformly over the sampling schedule --- under within-class i.i.d.\ sampling from the proposal's target for the cohort/refresh modes, and under the weak conditional null of Proposition~1 for the accumulate mode; (ii) \emph{deployment-long}: by the union bound over the spending schedule, the probability of \emph{ever} making such a false commit over the deployment is at most $\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}$, under the same assumptions; (iii) \emph{label cost}: labels per proposal are bounded by the per-window budget times $(1 + D)$, and the observed cost concentrates far below the bound because most proposals resolve early; (iv) \emph{transfer}: none of (i)--(iii) bounds future deployed accuracy unless the probe is representative of the evaluation horizon --- the stale-probe and dependence results quantify exactly where that premise fails. We prove nothing stronger, and the empirical sections report the price of each guarantee.

\textbf{Proposition 1 (conditional validity of the commit e-process).} Map each per-flow correctness difference to $x_i = (d_i+1)/2 \in [0,1]$ and let the gate's wealth process be $W_n = \exp\!\big(\sum_{i\le n} \lambda_i (x_i - \tfrac12) - \sum_{i\le n} \psi_E(\lambda_i)\,(x_i - \hat\mu_{i-1})^2\big)$, where $\psi_E(\lambda) = -\log(1-\lambda)-\lambda$ and the betting fractions $\lambda_i \in [0, \tfrac34]$ and plug-in means $\hat\mu_{i-1}$ are predictable (computed from observations before $i$; this is exactly the empirical-Bernstein sequence implemented in the artifact, whose commit condition $\mathrm{LCB}_n > 0$ is algebraically the event $W_n > 1/\alpha$). If $\mathbb{E}[d_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] \le 0$ for every $i$ --- the weak null; no stationarity or identical distribution is assumed --- then $W_n$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with $W_0 = 1$, and Ville's inequality gives $\Pr(\exists n : W_n > 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$: the probability of \emph{ever} committing under the weak null is at most $\alpha$. The proof (Supplementary \S S4) is the standard testing-by-betting argument \cite{waudbysmith2023betting,howard2021timeuniform} via Fan's inequality $e^{\lambda y - \psi_E(\lambda) y^2} \le 1 + \lambda y$ for $y \ge -1$; we claim no novelty, only the correspondence to this implementation, which a registered null simulation with time-varying conditional means and variances verifies empirically.

\textbf{What is and is not guaranteed.} The confidence-sequence gates satisfy, for a single proposal and \emph{under the probe distribution} $\mathcal P$,
$$\Pr\!\big(\exists n\le b:\ \text{LCB}_n>0 \ \big|\ \mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0\big)\ \le\ \alpha ,$$
i.e.\ they control the probability of \emph{falsely establishing positive expected paired correctness on the probe distribution}, uniformly over the (data-dependent) stopping time. This is \emph{not} the same as bounding the probability that the committed candidate lowers future balanced accuracy: the two coincide only when the probe is representative of the windows the candidate will be evaluated on, and the paper documents where that fails --- a probe up to nine windows stale, a fast-evolving stream, and accuracy vs balanced accuracy ranking candidates differently on class-imbalanced windows (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}). We therefore claim a bound on \emph{false probe-superiority}, not on future deployment regret, and treat probe representativeness as a separate, empirically-scoped assumption. Without a spending schedule the guarantee is also \emph{per proposal}: the process restarts for each challenger, so over a stream with $M$ committed-or-tested proposals the elementary union bound on ever making a false commit is $M\alpha$; a lifetime bound requires alpha-spending across proposals or a single deployment-long e-process --- exactly what the VBC-SG risk budget above supplies (property (ii)). The plain per-proposal gates carry no such budget, and \S\ref{sec:risk} reports both the empirical per-stream harmful-commit rate and the label price of the lifetime schedules.

\subsection{Self-contained candidate-pipeline replication}
\label{sec:symmetric_method}

Every experiment above inherits a convention from the original deployment story: scaler and PCA are fitted once, on the initial training set, and \emph{frozen} --- every later challenger is trained and scored inside that initial representation (\texttt{frozen\_initial\_transformer}). The role-randomized control of \S\ref{sec:risk} shows this convention is not neutral for SVC-RBF, so the decisive question is dynamic: does the harmful-update result survive when each challenger is a \emph{self-contained pipeline}? A preregistered replication answers it; protocol, estimands, margins and interpretation rules were frozen before any confirmatory seed ran (in the artifact).

\textbf{Design.} The environment emits a \emph{raw} stream: per seed, the same windows are served to every arm, hash-verified bit-identical, with no global transformation. Each model is a \texttt{ModelPipeline} (scaler + PCA + classifier + provenance metadata) that receives raw flows and transforms internally; the drift monitor is a separate \texttt{DetectorPipeline} whose representation stays the initial transformer under \emph{both} policies, so candidate-construction policy is never confounded with monitoring policy. The two transformer policies differ in exactly one intervention: \texttt{frozen\_initial\_transformer} reuses the initial scaler/PCA for every candidate (reproducing the historical harness bit-for-bit against the published v1.20.2 outputs), while \texttt{own\_transformer\_per\_model} fits each challenger's scaler on its own candidate batch only and its PCA on the scaled batch only --- same dimension, solver and classifier hyperparameters; raw candidate batches are bit-identical across policies, the probe is served raw to both pipelines, and neither probe nor future windows enter any fit. A commit deploys the \emph{complete bundle}, serving from window $t{+}1$; scaler/PCA parameter hashes and the SVC's fitted effective $\gamma$ are recorded per candidate.

\textbf{Estimands and inference.} Fresh confirmatory seeds 3001--3030 (never used before; smoke and parity used disjoint seeds), six scenarios (PortScan, UNSW-Recon, ToN-IoT $\times$ full and zero drift), KS-max only, policies never/naive/point/strict. The registered estimands are $\Delta_{\text{naive,own}} = \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,own}} - \mathrm{BA}_{\text{never}}$ (does harm persist?), $\Delta_{\text{transformer}} = \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,own}} - \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,frozen}}$ (the ownership interaction --- the estimand that answers the objection directly), and $\Delta_{\text{point--naive,own}}$, $\Delta_{\text{strict--naive,own}}$ (does validation retain value?). Four frozen families (harm under own/full; transformer interaction/full; gate value under own/full; a 12-contrast zero-drift secondary family) are tested by the same deterministic centered paired bootstrap as the rest of the paper, Holm-corrected within family; the inferential unit is the \emph{seed} (trajectory means paired through the shared raw stream) --- windows, triggers and commits are never treated as independent units. Margins were frozen in advance: $\pm0.5$ BA points for equivalence (CI90 fully inside; $\pm0.2/\pm1.0$ sensitivities), and non-inferiority guardrails of $-1.0$ recall points and $+0.5$ FPR points (one-sided 95\% bounds) that restrict safety \emph{language} --- balanced accuracy alone determines the registered A/B/C interpretation.

\subsection{Size-matched self-contained challenger control}
\label{sec:sizematched_method}

The replication above trains every challenger on 512 flows per class while the incumbent was trained on 2{,}000 per class --- the historical convention, inherited by every arm of this paper. Its residual zero-drift harm therefore admits one last mundane explanation: the challenger simply sees a quarter of the incumbent's evidence. A final preregistered control --- the paper's last experiment --- isolates that variable: does harmful candidate promotion under zero drift persist when self-contained challengers are trained with the \emph{same} per-class sample size as the initial incumbent? Protocol, config, seeds, margins, families and machine-evaluable outcome rules were committed before any confirmatory seed ran (in the artifact).

\textbf{Design.} Zero drift only (where the residual harm lives), with the identical v1.21.0 scenario definitions: random proposal trigger ($p{=}0.05$, drift-independent), severity fixed at zero, \texttt{own\_transformer\_per\_model} exclusively, KS-max machinery, same windows, duration, evaluation and statistics. Per benchmark, seven arms --- never-adapt, and \{naive, point, strict\} $\times$ candidate size $\in \{512, 2000\}$/class --- give a 21-arm matrix on 30 fresh confirmatory seeds (4001--4030, verified virgin against every prior ledger). The only new variable is the challenger's training size; trigger, probe (32 labels), gate margins, hyperparameters (SVC $C{=}1.0$, $\gamma{=}$\texttt{scale}, PCA dim 8), temporal semantics and sampling policy are unchanged, and the incumbent keeps its 2{,}000/class training set.

\textbf{Nested candidate batches.} The two size conditions are not independently sampled challengers: at every proposal the canonical draw first produces the \emph{unchanged} historical 512-per-class batch (bit-identical to the v1.21.0 arms --- verified by a bit-level parity run), then extends it with 1{,}488 further samples per class from the same per-trigger RNG stream, so the 512 batch \emph{is} the first 512 rows per class of the 2{,}000 batch (same rows, labels, order, hash; 999 proposal-level pairs verified at confirmatory scale). The size contrast thus adds information to an otherwise identical candidate rather than comparing independently sampled challengers. Each challenger's scaler/PCA/classifier still fit on its own batch only, with full per-candidate provenance recorded. This is a controlled pool-based construction --- not an observed-data or deployment-realistic arm. What it matches is \emph{nominal} per-class sample size: matching nominal per-class sample size isolates training-set size, but it does not establish equivalence in temporal coverage, diversity, subtype support, label quality, duplication, prevalence or effective sample size.

\textbf{Estimands, families and outcome rules (all frozen).} Primary estimands, in BA points per benchmark: E1 size-matched damage ($\mathrm{naive}_{2000}-\mathrm{never}$), E2 candidate-size effect ($\mathrm{naive}_{2000}-\mathrm{naive}_{512}$), E3/E4 gate value at matched size, E5 (secondary) the gate$\times$size interaction. Four Holm-corrected families (F1/F2/F3/F4: 3/3/6/6 contrasts), the same deterministic centered paired bootstrap, CI95 for harm/superiority, CI90 for equivalence at the reused $\pm0.5$-point margin, recall/FPR guardrails as before; the inferential unit remains the seed. Three machine-evaluable outcomes were frozen in advance --- PERSISTENCE (material Holm-significant damage at 2{,}000 plus a guardrail-clean gate win), ELIMINATION (equivalence of all three damage contrasts \emph{and} all six gate contrasts, plus no contradicting harmful-future-value signal), ATTENUATION (anything else) --- with the harmful-future-value criterion keyed, by preregistration, to the \emph{sign rate} of committed proposals' five-window future value. \S\ref{sec:sizematched} applies these rules literally.

\subsection{Metrics}


Per window we record balanced accuracy (BA) and attack-class F1; per stream we average over the 100 windows, and per condition over seeds. We report \textbf{gain vs. no-adaptation} (the frozen $h_0$ baseline), \textbf{committed adaptations} and \textbf{labels used}. To quantify the headroom of the decision we define a per-stream \textbf{policy oracle}, $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}=\mathbb{E}_{\text{seed}}\max(\mathrm{BA}_{\text{no-adapt}},\mathrm{BA}_{\text{detector}})$ --- a conservative lower bound on the value of perfect per-trigger decisions --- the \textbf{regret} of naive triggering $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}-\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive}}$, and the \textbf{harm frequency} (fraction of streams on which adapting scored below no-adaptation). All confirmatory comparisons are \textbf{truly paired}: on the v2 harness (\S4) every policy processes bit-identical pre-generated streams, and contrasts use cluster (per-seed) bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals; numbers from the initial exploratory study use seed-matched bootstrap CIs (conservative rather than fully paired; \S4).


\section{Experimental protocol}


\textbf{Datasets and regimes.} CICIDS2017 \cite{sharafaldin2018toward} (Tuesday reference $\to$ Wednesday, Friday-DDoS, Friday-PortScan, Thursday-WebAttacks), UNSW-NB15 \cite{moustafa2015unsw} (DoS, Reconnaissance) and ToN-IoT \cite{alsaedi2020toniot} (Scanning), spanning benefit, marginal/mixed and harmful readaptation. Features are numeric, standardized and PCA-reduced to 8 dimensions; windows contain 128 flows. To rule out label leakage, identifier and label-derived columns are removed at staging (UNSW: \texttt{id}, binary \texttt{label}, \texttt{attack\_cat}; ToN-IoT: \texttt{label}, \texttt{type}, timestamps and IP/uid identifiers; the CICIDS variant used ships no flow identifiers), and we verified that no remaining single feature separates the classes beyond AUC $\approx$ 0.81 --- far from the near-perfect separation a leaked label would produce. CICIDS2017 has documented traffic-generation and labeling artifacts \cite{engelen2021troubleshooting}; we therefore treat it as the benefit end of a three-benchmark spectrum rather than as sole evidence --- the paper's central negative results (the marginal and harmful regimes) come from the independent UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT benchmarks.


\textbf{The confirmatory harness (v2) --- the methodology behind every headline claim.} All confirmatory results (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory} and \S\ref{sec:v2}) come from a harness built to make the paired contrasts exact. (i) \emph{Common streams:} every evaluation stream and calibration draw is pre-generated by an environment generator that depends on the seed alone, before any policy runs --- all arms process bit-identical windows in the same order, so gate$-$naive contrasts are truly paired and policy actions cannot perturb the environment. (ii) \emph{Role-disjoint partitions:} each per-class pool is split once per seed (50/30/20) into window/train/probe partitions; evaluation windows draw only from the first, initial models, candidates and detector references only from the second, probes only from the third --- train/probe/evaluation overlap is impossible by construction. (iii) \emph{Separate generators:} environment, initial training, detector calibration, candidate training, probe draws and post-commit recalibration each use their own RNG, seeded by (seed) or (seed, $t$) only, never advanced by policy actions. (iv) \emph{Per-trigger logging:} at every trigger the harness records pre-trigger incumbent degradation, the detector score, and incumbent-vs-candidate BA over the following 1/3/5/10 windows --- lookahead used for logging only, never by any policy. The replication protocol (criteria, margins, one-pass stopping rule) was committed and publicly tagged (\texttt{harness-v2-protocol}) before any confirmatory seed ran, with pre-run registered amendments covering seed hygiene, deduplication, baselines and analysis specifications; the confirmatory window is the 30 pristine seeds 104--133, untouched by any smoke test.


\textbf{Shared protocol constants.} The initial model trains on 2{,}000 flows per class from the reference regime; the scaler and PCA are fit on that training set only and frozen thereafter. Every candidate trains on a balanced sample of 512 flows per class drawn \emph{at the current mixture severity} $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ --- never from the final regime pool --- so the candidate sees the population the deployed model faces at the trigger, not the future (a simulator convenience a production system lacks; \S7 bounds the word ``deployable'' accordingly, and the sliding-window and chronological-stream experiments provide the observed-data counterpoints). Detector references use 256 flows per class; thresholds are the 0.95 quantile of detector scores over 30 pre-drift calibration windows; the trigger is 3 consecutive alarms with a 10-window cooldown throughout; windows contain 128 flows. The probe is the gate's \emph{incremental} label cost on top of the labeled retraining data (512 per class per candidate) that any labeled-retraining loop --- gated or not --- already consumes: we claim label efficiency of the \emph{commit decision}, not of the retraining pipeline, and \S\ref{sec:v2} accounts for both.


\textbf{Label and information latency: exact semantics.} Several arms model the delay between a flow being observed and its label being available, and Table~\ref{tab:latency} fixes them, read directly off the artifact. The probe knob ($L_p$) draws the probe from the stream as of window $t-L_p$; the candidate knob ($L_c$) shifts the candidate's \emph{whole} training batch --- features and labels together, as one adjudicated block: under the pool generator the batch is sampled at the mixture of window $t-L_c$, and under the sliding-window generator it is the eight observed windows ending at $t-L_c$. The observed-data probe is inherently nine windows old. Training completion is instantaneous in the confirmatory, observed-data and chronological arms; the pool-based operational sensitivity (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}) explicitly evaluates a five-window deployment delay, during which the incumbent keeps serving. While a deferred VBC-SG proposal is pending, the incumbent remains deployed and \emph{new proposals are blocked} --- a fresh alarm during a pending decision is absorbed exactly like a cooldown window, so a proposal is never silently replaced.

\subsection{Symmetric-pipeline dynamic replication: the first asymmetry}
\label{sec:symmetric}

Table~\ref{tab:symmetric_pipeline} is the replication of \S\ref{sec:symmetric_method}; its registered verdict was Scenario~A --- \emph{persistence with a shifted locus}: preprocessing asymmetry amplified harmful promotion, while a residual zero-drift harm survived for self-contained challengers. All of its challengers train on 512 flows per class against a 2{,}000/class incumbent --- the historical convention whose consequences the size-matched control of \S\ref{sec:sizematched} then isolates. Four findings, in the order the frozen families test them.

\textbf{(1) The mean full-drift harm does not persist under self-contained pipelines.} With each challenger owning its preprocessing, always-deploy is \emph{beneficial} in all three full-drift regimes: PortScan $+7.21$ [4.89, 9.52], UNSW-Recon $+2.55$ [2.34, 2.75], and --- the regime that anchored the original harm claim --- ToN-IoT $+1.03$ [0.55, 1.53], all Holm-significant. \textbf{(2) Preprocessing ownership is a major amplifier --- not a complete explanation of candidate-promotion risk.} The registered interaction $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,own}} - \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,frozen}}$, on bit-identical raw streams and candidate batches, is positive everywhere it was harmful: $+5.98$ [3.82, 8.26] on ToN-IoT full, $+0.90$ and $+1.15$ on PortScan and UNSW full, and $+5.49$/$+2.36$ on the ToN/PortScan zero-drift streams --- the frozen convention had been handicapping every challenger, most severely exactly where the harm was reported. Switching to self-contained preprocessing removes the observed mean full-drift harm and accounts for the large frozen-versus-own difference; findings (3)--(4) are what it does not account for. \textbf{(3) But ownership does not explain all of the risk: zero-drift promotion harm persists at the historical candidate size.} With no drift at all, self-contained 512-per-class challengers still hurt: naive$-$never is $-1.74$ [$-2.35$, $-0.96$] on PortScan and $-0.65$ [$-0.77$, $-0.55$] on UNSW-Recon --- both beyond the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point materiality margin, Holm-significant --- and $-0.38$ [$-0.53$, $-0.25$] on ToN-IoT, sub-material but significant and \emph{not} equivalent to zero (its CI90 is not inside $\pm0.5$). At this stage the result read as ``replacing a healthy model with a freshly re-estimated pipeline remains net-negative even when the pipeline is built correctly''; \S\ref{sec:sizematched} shows the accurate reading is narrower --- these challengers were also trained on a quarter of the incumbent's evidence, and that asymmetry, not re-estimation per se, accounts for the mean effect. \textbf{(4) Validation retains material value exactly there.} Point and strict validation improve over naive in all six zero-drift comparisons (Holm-significant), recovering much or nearly all of the loss depending on the cell: strict gains $+1.68$ [1.23, 2.13] over naive on PortScan, $+0.51$ [0.38, 0.64] on UNSW, $+0.34$ [0.20, 0.49] on ToN (point $+0.75/+0.19/+0.11$). ``Recover'' means improvement \emph{over always-deploy}: strict returns to within $0.06$--$0.14$ points of never-adapt in all three regimes, the point gate still sits $0.27$--$0.99$ points below it, and no zero-drift cell converts to a net benefit over never adapting --- nor should it where there is nothing to gain. At full drift the point gate still filters harmful candidates on ToN-IoT ($+0.64$ [0.26, 1.06]); the costs are equally visible: the gates add nothing material on PortScan ($+0.19$/$+0.05$, n.s.) and the point gate has a small resolved \emph{cost} on UNSW-Recon ($-0.21$ [$-0.36$, $-0.06$]) --- where updating is already clearly beneficial, validation's marginal value can be zero or slightly negative (full contrasts in Supplementary \S S7).

\subsection{Size-matched self-contained challengers: the decisive zero-drift control}
\label{sec:sizematched}

Table~\ref{tab:size_matched} reports the registered control of \S\ref{sec:sizematched_method}, and it settles the residual question. Three results, one per frozen family.

\textbf{(1) The 512 harm replicates; the size-matched harm does not exist in the mean.} On thirty entirely fresh seeds, 512-per-class challengers reproduce the replication's residual harm almost exactly (naive$_{512}-$never: $-1.70$ [$-2.05$, $-1.36$] PortScan, $-0.65$ [$-0.77$, $-0.55$] UNSW, $-0.24$ [$-0.39$, $-0.10$] ToN --- descriptive, uncorrected). Give the same challengers the incumbent's 2{,}000 flows per class --- as pure extension of the identical nested batch --- and the mean damage vanishes: naive$_{2000}-$never is $+0.19$ [$-0.15$, $+0.55$], $-0.02$ [$-0.10$, $+0.06$] and $-0.01$ [$-0.05$, $+0.04$], with the CI90 fully inside the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point equivalence margin in \emph{all three} benchmarks (PortScan's upper bound sits at $0.49$, inside by $0.006$ points --- we report the margin's tightness rather than hide it). The conclusion is margin-dependent, and the preregistered sensitivities make it explicit: at the stricter $\pm0.2$-point margin PortScan is \emph{not} equivalent while UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT remain so, and all three benchmarks pass at $\pm1.0$; equivalence within a margin does not imply equality of predictions or of class-conditional behaviour. None of the F1 damage contrasts approaches Holm significance. \textbf{(2) Candidate size is the driver, and it is Holm-significant everywhere.} The paired size effect naive$_{2000}-$naive$_{512}$ is $+1.89$ [$+1.54$, $+2.23$] on PortScan, $+0.63$ [$+0.53$, $+0.73$] on UNSW and $+0.23$ [$+0.10$, $+0.38$] on ToN --- essentially the mirror image of the 512 harm, all three Holm-significant. \textbf{(3) Under this zero-drift control, validation gates have nothing left to recover at the matched nominal size.} All six F3 gate contrasts are tiny ($+0.01$ to $+0.13$ points, every one below $0.14$), none Holm-significant, and every one CI90-equivalent to naive within $\pm0.5$; the secondary F4 interactions are uniformly negative and Holm-significant (e.g.\ strict on PortScan $-1.54$ [$-1.89$, $-1.20$]) --- the gates' value at 512 was, to the extent the interaction measures it, compensation for the challenger's evidence disadvantage (full tables in Supplementary \S S8).

\textbf{Registered outcome: ATTENUATION --- and what it does and does not mean.} The frozen rules classify this result mechanically. PERSISTENCE fails because there is no material mean damage at the matched size (no F1 contrast is $\le -0.5$ with CI95 below zero). ELIMINATION fails on exactly one criterion: the preregistered harmful-future-value check keys on the \emph{sign rate} of committed proposals' five-window value, and at the matched size that rate is $\approx$48--52\% in two benchmarks --- so the registered outcome is ATTENUATION, and we keep it. Substantively, the same logs show the mean five-window future value of committed 2{,}000-challengers is $\approx 0$ in every benchmark ($-0.06$ to $+0.03$ points, against $-0.13$ to $-1.03$ at 512): a near-50\% negative-sign rate around a null mean is proposal-level variability, not systematic net harm, and it must not be read as a deployment-risk probability. The formal classification and the substantive reading are both true, and the paper keeps them distinct.

\textbf{Guardrails at the matched size.} The security side closes cleanly: all six matched-size gate cells pass both preregistered non-inferiority margins vs.\ naive$_{2000}$ (paired $\Delta$recall one-sided lower bounds all $>-1.0$; $\Delta$FPR upper bounds all $<+0.5$), while at 512 the zero-drift UNSW strict cell again fails recall non-inferiority --- replicating the earlier trade-off on fresh seeds (full guardrail table in Supplementary \S S8). There is no security trade-off at the matched size; there is also no BA gain, and the absence of a trade-off is not evidence of utility.

\textbf{Evidence versus validation: what each nominal label buys.} The same sealed outputs answer the operator's question: invest in more candidate-training evidence, or keep the challenger small and spend a small validation probe? Table~\ref{tab:evidence_validation_tradeoff} compares, per benchmark, the 512-per-class policies against naive at nominal 2{,}000-per-class parity. In nominal adjudication counts, moving from 512 to 2{,}000 samples per class requires $2\times1{,}488 = 2{,}976$ additional candidate-training labels per proposal, whereas the evaluated point/strict decision uses a 32-label probe. At 512, the strict gate recovers most of the mean loss (PortScan: naive$_{512}$ $-1.70$ vs strict$_{512}$ $-0.03$ against never, descriptive); naive$_{2000}$ reaches equivalence without any probe. This establishes no economic dominance in either direction: labels are counted, not priced, and the inspected-flow cost of acquiring balanced attack labels is not modeled --- under operational imbalance candidate-training and probe labels need not cost the same to obtain.

\subsection{Confirmatory and observed-data results}
\label{sec:v2}

We rebuilt the harness as described in \S4 and replicated the confirmatory study under it, with the protocol publicly tagged before any confirmatory seed ran \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact} --- an internal, not external, replication. \textbf{Every criterion replicates} (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory}). Naive triggering remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$1.64, CI95 [$-$2.72, $-$0.64]); the gate converts it to +0.79 [0.53, 1.07], significantly above naive (+2.43 [1.53, 3.43]), preserves PortScan (+9.12 vs naive +8.25) and UNSW (+1.35 vs +1.21), and the pattern is detector-invariant (QK-ZZ: $-$2.91 naive $\to$ +0.72 gated, +3.63 [1.66, 6.38] vs naive). Two pre-declared extensions sharpen the contribution. First, a \emph{holdout} variant carves the probe out of the candidate's retraining batch, deduplicated by row identity --- zero labels beyond those any labeled-retraining loop already uses. It removes most of the harm (ToN $-$0.77 vs naive $-$1.64; PortScan +9.08) but, unlike the fresh probe, does not beat no-adaptation in the harm regime: zero incremental cost buys harm reduction, and the 32-label fresh probe remains the recommended gate. A conservative lower-confidence-bound rule (commit only if the 90\% LCB of the per-flow correctness difference exceeds zero) is harm-avoiding in the evaluated runs and slightly benefit-lossy (ToN +0.34, PortScan +8.51). Second, per-trigger logging provides the mechanism test that shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at each of 250 triggers we measured the incumbent's degradation over the five \emph{preceding} windows and the candidate-minus-incumbent BA over the five \emph{following} windows. Pre-trigger degradation predicts the future value of committing in every regime (r = $-$0.67 to $-$0.70; cluster bootstrap over seeds keeps every regime's CI95 well away from zero, e.g.\ [$-$0.76, $-$0.66] in PortScan; pooled $-$0.57, stable under leave-one-regime-out, $-$0.52 to $-$0.67), while the detector's score at the same triggers shows no consistent association (r = $-$0.01 to +0.05, every clustered CI95 straddling zero). Triggers are nested in seeds and regimes and the incumbent's past and future performance are autocorrelated, so we read this as strong \emph{predictive} support for the degradation--headroom account at the level of individual decisions --- on disjoint past/future windows, under the hardened harness --- not as a causal proof (Fig.~\ref{fig:9}).

\textbf{A hierarchical model sharpens the per-trigger claim --- for both detectors.} Because raw correlations control for nothing, we fit a mixed-effects model of the future update value $\Delta_5$ on pre-trigger degradation, detector score, current severity and stream time (random intercepts over seeds; the pooled model clusters on regime$\times$seed). Degradation is the only predictor whose CI95 consistently excludes zero --- pooled $\beta_{\mathrm{deg}} = -1.02$ [$-$1.61, $-$0.43] per BA point (robust to a random slope: $-$0.92 [$-$1.30, $-$0.55]), per-regime $-$1.10/$-$0.43/$-$1.15, every CI excluding zero. Severity and stream time are structurally collinear on a ramp (VIFs 60--230) and not separately interpretable, but degradation and score are clean (VIF $\le$ 2.4). The whole analysis replicates on the QK-ZZ trigger logs (311 triggers: $\beta_{\mathrm{deg}}$ $-$0.38 to $-$1.07, every CI excluding zero; the raw score--value correlation straddles zero in all six regime$\times$detector cells; the single exception, a small positive QK-score coefficient in PortScan's benefit regime, is reported as such) --- at triggered decisions the evaluated detectors' scores carry no \emph{consistent} incremental signal, not provably none. Leave-one-regime-out prediction transfers directionally but not in scale (held-out $R^2$ 0.18/0.36, collapsing to ${\le}0$ without the degradation term; UNSW fails on its much smaller value scale): a robust ranking signal, not a calibrated universal law, and we claim only the former.

\textbf{Decision-quality of the gate, on the same logged triggers.} Against the logged five-window lookahead (never available to the policy), the lp32 gate cuts local per-decision regret ${\sim}22\times$ in the harm regime and five-fold in the benefit regime (pooled over 308 triggers: precision 0.74, recall 0.87; stable across horizons 1--10, with the marginal regime a wash). It does commit some harmful updates (23\% of its commits have negative realized value, mostly small) --- a cheap statistical filter, not an oracle; full operating-point accounting in Supplementary \S S2.10.

\textbf{Model-agnosticism moves onto the hardened harness}: for random forest, logistic regression and MLP (KS-max, same pristine seeds) the fresh-probe gate is never materially below naive retraining (largest deficit 0.02 points), never significantly below no-adaptation, and it rescues the one non-SVC cell where naive dips negative (MLP on ToN-IoT: $-$0.16 naive $\to$ +0.25 gated, +0.41 [0.17, 0.69]). Two registered extras complete the picture. A \emph{performance-aware trigger} (DDM-style, 8 labels/window, 3$\sigma$ excursion) avoids harm where the incumbent stays healthy --- it never fires in ToN-IoT --- but is benefit-lossy (PortScan +5.40 vs +7.94 naive) and spends 800 monitoring labels per stream against zero for the holdout gate. On \emph{natural-prevalence evaluation streams} ($\pi=0.05$), with the detector also calibrated at operating prevalence, the benign mass dilutes the drift signal: KS-max rarely or never fires and gate and naive coincide almost everywhere --- retraining policy matters little when the monitor is starved, an open operational problem for detector design under imbalance. \textbf{Canonical monitors, cross-validated against reference implementations} (Supplementary \S S2.1): batch DDM --- ours and the \texttt{river} reference, same 8 labels/window --- remains \emph{net-harmful in the harm regime} (river-DDM ToN-IoT $-$2.23 [$-$5.29, $-$0.05]); the reference ADWIN under-fires and forfeits most of the benefit (+1.77 vs naive +8.25 in PortScan); raising the monitoring budget $4\times$/$10\times$ changes neither conclusion. Under every monitoring configuration evaluated, labels spent on the commit decision bought a better accuracy--cost trade-off than labels spent monitoring.

\textbf{Adaptive update rules, including a properly calibrated ensemble.} Sliding-window retraining on the last eight observed windows consumes those windows' ${\sim}1{,}024$ labels per candidate and remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.44 [$-$4.23, $-$0.91]). Re-run with Platt-calibrated members and probabilistic nesting (amendment 004), the soft incumbent+candidate ensemble \emph{improves}: it avoids the harm (ToN +0.56 [0.26, 0.87], +2.20 above naive), preserves PortScan (+8.55), and beats both naive and the gate in the marginal regime (UNSW +1.72) --- the strongest label-free update rule we evaluated. The gate is ahead in the two decisive regimes (paired gate$-$ensemble: ToN +0.23 [0.04, 0.41], PortScan +0.57 [0.10, 1.11]), can \emph{decline} an update --- the ensemble commits every trigger, incurring churn and unboundedly nested models --- and emits an auditable per-decision signal. Table~\ref{tab:policy_frontier} presents the honest conclusion of the baseline program: \emph{no policy dominates the frontier}. The plain gate maximizes downstream accuracy; the calibrated ensemble is the best probe-free rule; the split two-stage gate is the cheapest harm-reducer; under four illustrative a-priori cost scenarios the cheap policies win the utility in almost every cell. Policy choice is a cost decision, and \S3.4's utility calculus is the instrument for making it.

\input{tables/table_policy_frontier.tex}

\textbf{The v1 robustness envelope, re-established (and partly corrected) on v2} (full numbers in Supplementary \S S2.2). The gate's benefit is stable for $b \ge 32$; the initial study's ``8 labels suffice'' does \emph{not} fully replicate --- at $b{=}8$ the gate still reduces harm (+1.22 above naive) but is no longer net-positive against no-adaptation --- so we revise the recommended operating point to $b{=}32$. Harm avoidance survives 20 windows of label staleness in full and 40\% random probe-label corruption; net benefit over no-adaptation survives corruption only to 25\%.

\textbf{The two-stage gate: gating training, not just deployment.} Instantiating \S3.4's two-decision structure, the two-stage gate spends labeled flows on the incumbent \emph{first} and only builds a candidate when incumbent accuracy has fallen $\ge \delta$ below its healthy reference; the probe is split per class into a health half and a commit half (8+8; no sample feeds both decisions --- reuse induces selection optimism; registered correction history in Supplementary \S S2.4). At $\delta{=}0.05$: the split gate trains 69\% fewer candidates than the plain gate (1.07 vs 3.43 per stream), cuts total labels to roughly half of \emph{naive's} (1{,}341 vs 2{,}594; Supplementary \S S5), remains significantly above naive (+1.79 [0.69, 2.92]) --- but its net gain over never adapting is no longer resolved at 30 seeds (+0.15 [$-$0.15, 0.46]). The $\delta$ sweep is a clean operating characteristic (details in \S S2.4). The menu is explicit: the plain commit gate maximizes downstream accuracy; the split two-stage gate buys harm \emph{reduction} at the lowest total cost; full harm-to-benefit conversion still requires validating a trained candidate.

\textbf{Total cost accounting.} Supplementary \S S5 makes the full ledger explicit for the harm regime (all regimes in the artifact): candidates dominate every policy's label bill, the gate's \emph{total} cost exceeds naive's (rejected candidates are trained and discarded; rejections also keep the detector reference, producing more triggers), and the probe is ${\sim}3\%$ of the gate's total. ``Label-efficient'' therefore means --- and only means --- that the \emph{decision} adds tens of labels to a pipeline that already spends thousands; the two-stage variant is the configuration that reduces the pipeline itself.

\textbf{The gate does not need the simulator: a leakage-free observed-data replication (Algorithm 1B).} The strongest objection to everything above is that the core harness hands the candidate and the probe a sample drawn from the environment's pools \emph{at the true current severity} --- an oracle-quality snapshot no deployment can obtain. Answering it took three registered iterations, each closing an oracle privilege or leakage path; Supplementary \S S2.7 gives the per-iteration account. \textbf{With every oracle privilege removed, the observed-data result stands} (Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}, the final arm of amendments 011--013): the stream is drawn without replacement from a globally value-deduplicated pool (candidate-training/future-evaluation collisions exactly zero), the gate rejects when no observed probe exists yet (zero unvalidated commits), and all three datasets run at the same 64-flow windows (the 128-flow sensitivity agrees; registered threshold-minimum sweep unchanged). Naive updating on observed candidates remains net-harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.85 [$-$5.29, $-$0.74] full; $-$4.75 [$-$8.80, $-$1.49] mild) and the observed-data gates convert it to a non-negative outcome (point gate $+$1.20 full / $+$0.08 mild; \textbf{+4.05 [2.01, 6.51]} and \textbf{+4.83 [1.59, 8.81]} above naive), with the no-additional-label strict gate on par ($+$3.86 / $+$4.78 above naive); PortScan's benefit is preserved by both gates ($+$9.4--9.5 vs naive $+$8.85); UNSW sits below no-adaptation in both arms at this window size --- the marginal-regime boundary --- with both gates still significantly ahead of naive ($+$0.51--0.64), reported as-is. The mild-drift prediction test thus replicates inside the same leakage-free pipeline; the harm-regime contrast was stable through every iteration, while PortScan's small benefit-regime premium proved partly leakage (per-iteration numbers in Supplementary \S S2.7). This arm predates the size-matched control and retains the historical 512/class candidate budget.

\subsection{Risk-controlled candidate governance}
\label{sec:risk}

\textbf{A registered prediction test: net harm is a property of incumbent health, not of one dataset.} If the value of updating is the incumbent's headroom, then \emph{lowering the drift severity} --- keeping the incumbent healthy --- should turn updating harmful even in regimes we had labelled ``benefit''. We locked this prediction publicly before running it (amendment 006), and \textbf{it holds} (Table~\ref{tab:harm_generality}): at mild drift the mean gain of naive always-deploy is negative in all three benchmarks ($-$0.46 [$-$1.57, 0.42], $-$0.15 [$-$0.21, $-$0.09], $-$0.65 [$-$1.28, $-$0.16]) --- statistically resolved in UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT, unresolved in CICIDS --- and the gate meets its registered $-$0.2-point tolerance in all three ($+$0.38, $-$0.09, $-$0.04) while beating naive significantly in each (in UNSW it sits a resolved but immaterial 0.09 points below no-adaptation). The harm also broadens at full drift: naive updating is net-harmful in two further ToN-IoT regimes (DDoS $-$15.24, Injection $-$2.24), and the gate rescues both. The regime label ``benefit'' or ``harm'' is a property of the operating point, not of the dataset --- exactly what the degradation--headroom account predicts. (All arms in this subsection use the historical frozen transformer and 512/class candidates.)

\input{tables/table_harm_generality.tex}

\textbf{The zero-drift control (frozen transformer): the effect is not drift.} The sharpest objection to the mild-drift result is that it might have nothing to do with drift: replacing a healthy model with a finite re-estimate introduces training variance, and that alone could explain a small negative mean. The registered control --- triggers at a fixed rate \emph{independent of any drift signal}, on streams with \textbf{no drift at all} --- reshapes the claim (Table~\ref{tab:zero_drift}). Four findings. (i) The objection is partly right and the effect is real: with zero drift, always-deploy is significantly net-harmful in all three benchmarks ($-$2.76, $-$0.75, $-$4.75). (ii) Under the \emph{frozen-transformer convention of this arm}, it is not a small-candidate artifact --- size-matching each candidate to the incumbent's 2{,}000 flows/class leaves the harm in place and usually deeper ($-$4.81, $-$0.13, $-$5.76); the preregistered own-transformer control of \S\ref{sec:sizematched} later shows this persistence is itself bound to the frozen representation: once challengers own their preprocessing \emph{and} match the incumbent's size, the mean zero-drift harm is equivalent to zero in all three benchmarks --- the two construction asymmetries compound, and both must be removed. (iii) The plain $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate reduces this harm but does not eliminate it ($-$1.11, $-$0.56, $-$0.25): it commits on ties, and under zero drift ties dominate. Simply \emph{rejecting ties} (amendment 012) recovers most of the loss at zero extra cost (PortScan $-$1.11$\to-$0.13, ToN $-$0.25$\to-$0.07, UNSW $-$0.56$\to-$0.28), holds outside zero drift too (amendment 013) --- reject-ties is a genuine no-additional-label Pareto point --- and a \emph{risk-controlled} gate closes the residual: requiring statistical evidence of superiority (exact McNemar, or the anytime-valid sequential test below) makes the gate almost never commit when there is no signal, recovering essentially all of the loss (McNemar: exactly $+$0.00 in all three). When there is genuinely nothing to gain, a properly calibrated validator does nothing. (iv) With the \emph{real} KS-max detector at zero drift the trigger fires only ${\sim}0.1$ times per stream, so a well-specified monitor mostly avoids the harm by not firing. The synthesis at this stage was \emph{candidate governance}: an alarm --- or a false alarm, or a scheduled retrain --- is a proposal, and an update is a hypothesis; the size-matched control of \S\ref{sec:sizematched} later sharpens \emph{which} proposals actually put the mean at risk.

\input{tables/table_zero_drift.tex}

\textbf{The zero-drift harm across classifiers: partly general, but size-matched only for SVC-RBF.} A load-bearing objection to the harm result is that its magnitude was read off one fragile pipeline --- an SVC-RBF on an 8-dimensional PCA projection. A registered sweep shows that, \emph{at the default candidate budget}, under zero drift naive always-deploy is net-harmful for every downstream model --- random forest, logistic regression and MLP alike --- and that removing the PCA bottleneck (SVC-RBF, full feature space) does not help; Table~\ref{tab:amendment009} carries all twelve cells. \textbf{But a size-matched control (amendment 012) qualifies this sharply, and we report it plainly.} Those default arms train the candidate on 512 flows/class against a 2{,}000/class incumbent; re-running size-matched (candidate also 2{,}000/class) under zero drift \emph{removes} the harm for random forest, logistic regression and MLP --- all within $\pm0.1$ of zero in every regime --- while for SVC-RBF, \emph{under this arm's frozen transformer}, it persists and deepens ($-$4.81 / $-$0.13 / $-$5.76; Supplementary \S S1.5). So for the robust learners the default-budget harm was largely the 4$\times$ candidate-size asymmetry, not a replacement effect; for SVC-RBF the apparent size-robustness is specific to the frozen convention --- the preregistered own-transformer size-matched control (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}) shows that with self-contained preprocessing, size matching removes the mean SVC-RBF zero-drift harm as well. The honest claim is therefore narrower than ``generalizes across four classifiers'': under the default asymmetric budget the harmful \emph{sign} appears in all four, but size-matching localizes the robust replacement harm to SVC-RBF. Where the harm is present, the risk-controlled McNemar gate recovers essentially all of it ($+0.00$) by declining to commit without resolved evidence. We separate sign from magnitude: these zero-drift losses span the operationally trivial (logistic regression on UNSW $-$0.08) to the severe (SVC on ToN-IoT $-$4.76; cumulative generator $-$9.46); the robust claim is the \emph{consistency of the sign once the incumbent is healthy}, and the magnitude is pipeline-dependent. Two nuances. First, at \emph{mild} and full drift the net-harm sign remains SVC-specific --- robust models resist it (Supplementary \S S1.6) --- so the SVC caveat survives for drift-driven harm; what generalizes (for SVC-RBF, at the default budget) is the \emph{zero-drift replacement} harm, for which the pre-declared A/B control (Table~\ref{tab:ab_equivalence}; disjoint, globally value-deduplicated blocks, incumbent role randomized per seed) identifies a \emph{representation-ownership} interaction as the dominant mechanism in the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline --- not pure re-estimation variance. In the harm-regime dataset, with an \emph{independent} transformer the challenger$-$incumbent gap is unresolved from zero ($-1.78$ [$-7.45$, $+3.92$]; equivalence to zero at the registered $\pm1.0$-point margin is \emph{not} established at this precision); fitting the transformer on the \emph{incumbent's} data --- the standard freeze-the-pipeline convention --- produces a large systematic incumbent advantage ($-12.35$ [$-19.33$, $-6.45$]); fitting it on the \emph{challenger's} data \emph{inverts} the effect ($+17.77$ [$+11.00$, $+24.75$]); and giving each model its \emph{own} transformer leaves a tightly-bounded near-zero gap ($+0.05$ [$-0.11$, $+0.21$]). Random forest shows none of this anywhere. The four pre-declared \emph{sign} criteria hold, and the inversion is the load-bearing evidence: whichever model owns the preprocessing wins, the effect is learner-specific to SVC-RBF, and per-challenger preprocessing is the mitigation --- while the gate correctly catches the disadvantaged challenger in the meantime.

\textbf{Equivalence, not just non-significance --- and \emph{which} part of the pipeline owns the effect.} A registered follow-up supplies two missing pieces (Table~\ref{tab:ab_equivalence}). First, ``the independent-transformer gap is zero'' had been argued from a CI that merely \emph{contains} zero. Re-run on 100 fresh confirmatory seeds (2001--2100; the original 30 re-labelled a pilot), with CI90-inside-margin equivalence at a pre-fixed $\pm1.0$-point margin (interval-inclusion TOST), the verdict is mixed and reported as such: giving each model its \emph{own} transformer is \textbf{equivalent to zero} on all three benchmarks (ToN-IoT $-0.01$ [$-0.09$, $+0.07$], passing even at $\pm0.2$), as is the independent transformer on UNSW ($+0.00$ [$-0.05$, $+0.06$]); on the two high-variance SVC benchmarks the independent-transformer gap remains \emph{unresolved} rather than demonstrably null (ToN-IoT $-1.43$ [$-4.47$, $+1.63$]; PortScan $-0.30$ [$-3.88$, $+3.28$]). The claim the data support: the \emph{inversion} across ownership conditions identifies the interaction, and per-model preprocessing demonstrably removes it. Second, a registered decomposition separates standardization from PCA: fitting only the \emph{standardizer} on the incumbent's data reproduces essentially the entire ownership advantage ($-12.17$ [$-15.26$, $-9.23$] vs $-12.35$ full), fitting only the \emph{PCA} does not ($+0.16$ [$-2.90$, $+3.08$]). The mechanism is feature \emph{scaling}, not projection --- exactly what an RBF kernel with a variance-scaled bandwidth predicts: whoever's data sets the feature variances sets the kernel geometry the challenger is scored in. The symmetric-pipeline replication closes the loop at trajectory scale (interaction up to $+5.98$ points on ToN-IoT); scaling is the dominant identified amplifier in this pipeline, with the residual zero-drift effect traced by \S\ref{sec:sizematched} to the nominal evidence asymmetry rather than re-estimation per se.

\input{tables/table_ab_equivalence.tex} Second, the harm tracks the \emph{update generator}, not full replacement specifically: under zero drift, sliding-window, replay 50/50 and --- most tellingly --- a \emph{cumulative} candidate trained on \emph{all} observed windows (the deepest harm of any generator, $-$9.46 on ToN-IoT) are all net-harmful; only the calibrated ensemble, which never fully discards the incumbent, escapes, and only on PortScan ($+0.84$), which the risk-averse gate forgoes (Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}). That the tested cumulative generator --- retrained from scratch on all observed windows --- is the \emph{worst} is evidence against a simple ``just use more data'' reading: repeatedly replacing a well-fitted incumbent with a fresh re-estimate injects variance a healthy model has little to gain from. Three registered controls --- row-identity deduplication, re-anchoring to the incumbent's initial training set, and corrected $n$-scaled SVC regularization --- all fail to remove the zero-drift cumulative harm, two deepening it: neither duplication nor under-regularization explains it (Supplementary \S S2.5). The McNemar gate recovers the loss for every generator, and neutralizes the residual mild-drift harm of the cumulative and replay generators too.  This is candidate governance in its strongest form: the object that needs validating is the \emph{proposal}, whatever produced it.

\textbf{Where the harm hides, and an anytime-valid gate that bounds it.} Two risk-oriented additions close the account. \emph{The mean understates the tail.} In the ToN-IoT harm regime the naive arm's \emph{worst-window} gap below no-adaptation is $-$5.24 [$-$8.39, $-$2.34] and its 10th-percentile and CVaR$@$10\% gaps are $-$4.48 and $-$4.74 --- roughly $3$--$4\times$ the $-$1.38 mean gap --- whereas the gate keeps the entire lower tail non-negative (worst-window $+$0.72, CVaR $+$0.78): risk, not just the average, is what the gate controls. \emph{An anytime-valid confidence sequence, and what it bounds.} Because the correctness differences are mostly ties, a predictable-plug-in empirical-Bernstein confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} --- $\alpha$-uniform harmful-commit bound with empirical rather than worst-case variance --- is far tighter than the Robbins normal-mixture gate it dominates (Supplementary \S S2.9). It captures benefit where the benefit is large (deep-benefit PortScan at full drift: $+5.17$ [3.64, 6.70], vs the fixed 32-probe gate's $+8.70$), commits nothing under mild or zero drift, and still avoids the ToN-IoT full-drift harm ($+0.00$); at $b{=}64$ it cannot resolve 1-point benefits, and a registered budget sweep confirms this is a \emph{budget}, not a rule, limit (128/256-flow probes recover the missed benefits monotonically; Supplementary \S S2.8). A Monte-Carlo coverage check backs the guarantee under i.i.d.\ and without-replacement probes (false-commit rate $\le 0.002$ at nominal $\alpha{=}0.10$) but shows the sequence \emph{exceeds} nominal under strong within-probe autocorrelation ($0.18$ vs $0.10$) --- the probe-representativeness caveat of \S\ref{sec:riskgates}, stated wherever we recommend it. The \emph{empirical} harmful-commit accounting makes the per-proposal-vs-lifetime distinction concrete (descriptive within-sweep fractions; commits cluster within shared seeds and are not independent trials): the $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate commits a harmful update on 0.15--0.32 of its resolved commits, whereas the risk-controlled gates of the budget frontier commit \emph{zero observed} harmful updates over a complete accounting --- of their 520 commits across the sweep (each scored from the window where it took effect), 506 are $H{=}5$-evaluable and 14 right-censored, and \emph{zero of the 506 were harmful} --- against 39\% for always-deploy, 26\% for the point gate and 9\% for the strict rule on the same streams. These commits cluster within shared seeds, scenarios and policy grids, so they are not treated as 506 independent Bernoulli trials and no binomial population-rate bound is inferred; ``zero observed harmful commits'' is not ``eliminates'' --- the per-proposal bound is what the confidence sequence formally provides. The lesson: a pooled sequential gate provides useful empirical risk control at tens of adjudicated labels and captures the \emph{large} benefits; the formally aligned stratified guarantee requires substantially larger balanced-probe budgets.

\textbf{VBC-SG in practice: what each guarantee layer costs.} The assembled policy of \S\ref{sec:riskgates} was evaluated layer by layer (all cells: 30 seeds, probe budget 64 in blocks of 16, deferral cap 5). Under \emph{zero drift} every risk-controlled configuration commits nothing (gain $+0.00$, all three regimes). At \emph{full drift}, where PortScan offers ${\sim}$9 points of benefit, the layers price themselves out one by one: pooled EB-CS with deferral captures $+6.76$ [5.10, 8.36] ($\approx$900 labels/stream); the full \textbf{VBC-SG} --- stratified per-class bounds \emph{and} deferral at per-proposal $\alpha{=}0.10$ --- captures $+3.02$ [2.09, 4.00]; a Bonferroni lifetime budget reduces the pooled gate to $+1.01$; and the maximally-stacked configuration (stratified + deferral + $p$-series spending) commits nothing \emph{at this budget}, as does the fixed exact stratified baseline (per-class McNemar, 114--241 labels) --- power, not validity, is what the labels buy.

\textbf{The deployment-long guarantee is non-vacuous within the evaluated adjudication budget: a registered budget frontier.} Because $b{=}64$ alone cannot distinguish ``the rule is too conservative'' from ``the budget is too small'', a registered budget frontier (pristine seeds 501--530; probe cap $\in\{64,\dots,1024\}$ $\times$ spending schedule $\times$ \{pooled EB-CS with deferral, VBC-SG-Cohort, VBC-SG-Refresh\}, with point/strict as no-additional-label anchors) pre-declared non-vacuity criteria; \textbf{twelve configurations meet them} (Table~\ref{tab:budget_frontier}). The lifetime-budgeted pooled EB-CS with deferral recovers $52\%$ of naive's benefit at a cap of 64 and rises monotonically to $+6.73$ [4.76, 8.66] at cap 512 ($\approx$578 adjudicated labels per proposal) --- \textbf{93\% of the benefit of always-deploying under the approximate pooled analysis}; the fully stratified \textbf{VBC-SG-Cohort} (Cohort-sim), which carries the formal deployment-long false-superiority guarantee, reaches $81\%$ at the same cap, and Refresh $68\%$. Safety does not degrade anywhere on this frontier: under zero drift every lifetime configuration at every cap commits nothing (0 commits from 91 proposals), and no configuration committed a single harmful update --- the complete, cluster-caveated 520-commit accounting above. The deployment-long stratified guarantee thus becomes non-vacuous from $b{=}256$ onward within the evaluated balanced-probe adjudication budget; its inspected-flow acquisition cost under operational imbalance is not measured. The price is visible: abstention 74--86\%, ${\sim}4$ windows of deferral --- and the no-additional-label strict gate still buys most of the practical safety (zero drift: 0.13 commits per stream), which is why we present the frontier as a menu rather than a winner.

\input{tables/table_budget_frontier.tex} \emph{Operational-prevalence sensitivity (lite).} A separate sensitivity inside the pool-based v2 harness --- distinct from the acquisition-yield arm of Table~\ref{tab:operational_e2e} --- places the evaluation stream and the Binomial probe at operating prevalence ($\pi \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10\}$) with candidate and probe labels lagged 5 windows (lag 20 as sensitivity); its detector calibration and candidate training batch remain balanced per class, and inspected-flow acquisition costs are not modeled. The binding constraint is again upstream of the gate: at $\pi{=}0.01$ the KS detector fires 0.03--2.3 times per stream (ToN effectively never), so naive and gated arms differ little where the monitor is starved; at $\pi{=}0.10$ PortScan recovers $+4.8$ (naive) with the gate close behind ($+4.0$), and UNSW shows the gate slightly \emph{ahead} of naive at every prevalence and latency tested (e.g.\ $+0.37$ vs $-0.02$ at $\pi{=}0.05$, lag 20). The claim this sensitivity supports is deliberately narrow: once a trigger fires and labels arrive, the 32-flow probe adds little adjudication burden relative to the 1{,}024-label candidate batch and continues to reject harmful candidates; making the \emph{trigger} operate under extreme imbalance remains the open upstream problem the paper has flagged since \S\ref{sec:v2}.

\textbf{The risk axis: exact tests and a sequential probe.} Our recommended rule commits on a point comparison ($\varepsilon{=}0$), which is not a statistical guarantee --- 23\% of its commits have negative realized value. The registered risk-controlled variants price the guarantee explicitly. The exact one-sided McNemar gate ($\alpha{=}0.05$, ties never commit) is maximally harm-averse and essentially never harmful (ToN +0.00), but at $b{=}32$ it has almost no power (0.03 commits per stream in the harm regime) and forfeits most of the benefit (PortScan +5.45, $-$3.67 [$-$4.61, $-$2.74] vs the plain gate; doubling to $b{=}64$ recovers part of it, +7.67, still $-$1.45 below). A Bonferroni sequential probe that buys labels only while the decision is in doubt spends 58--61 labels per decision, likewise gives up some benefit for its guarantee, and likewise recovers essentially all of the zero-drift loss by refusing to commit without evidence; the confidence sequences above subsume it, and its numbers are in Supplementary \S S2.9. The lesson is not that exact tests are wrong but that \emph{the label budget, not the decision rule, is the binding constraint}: with 32 labels one cannot statistically resolve a one-point accuracy difference, so demanding significance means almost never updating. Point comparison, LCB, exact McNemar and the anytime-valid sequential tests span the risk axis of the frontier; an operator who fears a bad commit more than a missed gain --- or who can pay for more labels --- moves along it. The frontier's role in the final account is deliberately bounded: it formalizes and prices the commit gate for settings where one \emph{decides} to validate --- asymmetric or uncertain candidate evidence, limited labels, formal risk requirements --- and all of its numbers are evidence under the historical frozen-transformer policy. It was not re-evaluated under size-matched self-contained pipelines, where \S\ref{sec:sizematched} shows plain promotion is already mean-equivalent to never adapting; nothing here shows VBC-SG to be \emph{necessary} in that setting.

\subsection{Operational and chronological boundaries}
\label{sec:boundaries}

\textbf{Honest natural-prevalence probes: a claim we retract} (corrected experiment in Supplementary \S S2.3). The initial study's ``the probe need not be balanced'' rested on a probe conditioned to contain one attack flow. Under genuinely random inspection (attack count $\sim \mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$) the gate holds down to $\pi \approx 0.05$ (ToN +0.91 [0.67, 1.17]) but the harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi{=}0.01$ (+0.21 [$-$1.39, 1.15]), where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack; below that prevalence the operator must enrich the probe or accept a decision made on benign traffic alone.


\textbf{Chronologically ordered subsampled replays: the gate's honest boundary.} Six initial registered replays on real, chronologically ordered streams (200 windows of 256 flows, strided to span each capture: ordering, labels and the temporal availability of every probe are real; the sampling density is not) map where the gate costs benefit (per-replay details in Supplementary \S S2.6). The phenomenon the ramps model is real and deep: every CICIDS incumbent collapses on a later window (no-adaptation BA 49--72\%) as each stream presents an unseen attack sub-type, and drift-triggered retraining recovers +13.6 to +43.8 BA points. On these deep-benefit streams the gate pays a real, metric-dependent insurance premium ($-$5.0 to $-$15.1 of the recovered balanced-accuracy points on three streams; nothing on Wednesday, +0.40 [0.09, 0.74]) --- while on \emph{overall accuracy} it \emph{beats} naive on the same streams (Friday +4.19; Thursday +1.23), because a stale probe misranks candidates on the minority class first. A registered interventional test rules out probe class composition as the cause (a stratified probe leaves the premium unchanged); the surviving explanation is probe \emph{staleness}, reported as a hypothesis consistent with the data, not a demonstrated mechanism. \emph{On the external stream the account's central prediction holds}: the raw UNSW-NB15 timeline keeps its incumbent healthy (82.3\% BA), updating helps moderately (+7.33), and the gate pays \emph{no premium at all} (+0.16 [$-$0.31, 0.63] vs naive) --- the insurance is free precisely where incumbent health makes the commit decision genuinely uncertain. \emph{Chronological net harm is unobserved across these six replays}; every CICIDS stream lands in deep-benefit territory, the only healthy incumbent among them is the external UNSW timeline, and \S7 states the resulting external-validity limit. This is the registered check where the gate costs real benefit, reported as such.

\textbf{The full chronological matrix, enumerated in advance.} To close the forking-paths objection, we pre-enumerated \emph{every} chronological stream this data supports --- each train$\to$future day pair of CICIDS2017 whose training day contains attacks, the two remaining intra-day splits, and the UNSW-NB15 timeline at two further training fractions --- and ran all seven new ones under the same protocol with the obligatory strict baseline and VBC-SG added (seeds 601--630; ToN-IoT ships no timestamps). Table~\ref{tab:chronological_q1} reports all of them. \textbf{Net harm remains unobserved} --- on no chronological stream, old or new, does always-deploying lose to never adapting --- so the harmful-update finding stays a controlled-condition result, and this is the paper's principal limit on external validity. What the matrix does add is the account's \emph{conditional} prediction, tested where it is falsifiable. Incumbent health, not the calendar, sorts the outcomes: on the streams where the incumbent collapses (no-adaptation balanced accuracy 49--59\%) always-deploying is excellent and the gates pay a real premium (Tue$\to$Wed+Thu+Fri: $+11.5$ gated vs $+14.8$ naive; Wed$\to$Fri: $+27.6$ vs $+35.3$), whereas on the two UNSW timelines, where the incumbent stays healthy (82.9\% and 84.2\%), \emph{the point and strict gates beat always-deploying on both} --- the no-additional-label strict rule by $+2.78$ and $+1.34$ points, on non-overlapping intervals, while committing 2.5 and 3.6 times per stream against naive's 13.0 and 17.3. VBC-SG joins them on the first timeline ($+10.76$ vs naive's $+8.35$) but not on the second ($+6.92$ vs $+7.64$), which is the behaviour its extra conservatism predicts and which we report rather than average away. We do not round this into a law: the Wednesday intra-day split also keeps a healthy incumbent (84.7\%) yet its gates sit a statistically unresolved $0.5$--$0.7$ points \emph{below} naive, so the honest summary is that the chronological premium disappears --- and can reverse --- once the incumbent has little headroom to lose, without being guaranteed to reverse.

\textbf{Attack-label acquisition yield at operating prevalence.} Every label count quoted so far is an \emph{adjudicated} label; an operator pays in \emph{inspected flows}, and at realistic prevalence finding an \emph{attack} label costs two orders of magnitude more than adjudicating a flow. Inspection policies read only model \emph{predictions}, never ground truth, and the adjudication budget is split by construction into an auxiliary \textbf{discovery} half (selected by the acquisition policy, used only to measure attack-finding cost) and an independent uniform \textbf{validation} half at operating prevalence --- the only sample the commit rule is ever shown (32 adjudications per decision, plain accuracy; no balanced-accuracy guarantee at extreme prevalence). Delay degrades gracefully: quadrupling label latency to twenty windows takes CICIDS-PortScan from $+5.83$ to $+4.81$, and a five-window training-completion delay costs a comparable $0.5$ point ($+5.37$).

\section{Discussion}


The controlled program --- frozen diagnostic, symmetric-pipeline replication, size-matched control --- reorganizes what this paper teaches into three findings.

\textbf{Finding 1: pipeline comparability precedes promotion testing.} A challenger cannot be fairly evaluated if it inherits an incumbent-owned representation or receives substantially less training evidence. Both asymmetries were latent in the historical convention, and each was isolated by a preregistered control: frozen incumbent-owned preprocessing manufactured the mean full-drift harm (the ownership interaction reaches $+6.0$ points where the harm was reported), and the challengers' 4$\times$ nominal per-class evidence disadvantage accounts for the residual zero-drift mean harm (size effects $+1.89$/$+0.63$/$+0.23$, all Holm-significant; under the zero-drift control, equivalence to never-adapt at the matched nominal size within $\pm0.5$ points in 3/3). The control identifies a nominal sample-size effect in the evaluated pool-based setting; broader evidence comparability --- temporal coverage, diversity, subtype support, label quality, effective sample size --- remains an operational property, not a consequence of equal row counts alone. In the evaluated setting, what looked like a property of drift-triggered promotion was, in its mean, a property of how the candidates were built and evidenced. The general lesson survives in its precise form: a drift alarm does not establish challenger superiority --- but nothing here shows that a fairly constructed, fairly evidenced proposal is intrinsically dangerous.

\textbf{Finding 2: the apparent need for gating is conditional.} Each configuration tells the same story from a different angle: under the frozen transformer, the gate was partly compensating a representation bias; at own-transformer 512, it compensated a nominal-evidence disadvantage (its value there is real --- up to $+1.68$ points --- and guardrail-clean in all but one cell); at own-transformer 2{,}000, under the zero-drift control, it has no measurable value left to add (all six contrasts $<0.14$ points, none significant, F4 interactions uniformly negative). The zero-drift control thus shows that the measured value of the gate disappears when the evaluated representation and nominal sample-size asymmetries are removed. The recommendation is therefore \emph{not} ``gate every adaptation'': first construct a comparable challenger pipeline; then use a commit gate when candidate construction or evidence conditions remain asymmetric, limited, noisy or operationally uncertain --- in the evaluated zero-drift control, with both asymmetries removed, it added nothing measurable. The frontier of \S\ref{sec:risk} prices the gate for exactly those asymmetric settings; the size-matched control tells the operator when the evaluated asymmetries are absent.

\textbf{Finding 3: negative results improve adaptive-system evaluation.} The trajectory of this paper is itself a result. The original experiments detected harmful promotion; successive preregistered controls did not defend that finding but decomposed it --- ownership first, evidence size second --- until the mean effect was fully attributed to construction asymmetries in the evaluated setting. The final claim is not that candidate governance is always necessary; it is that adaptation benchmarks must control challenger representation and training evidence \emph{before} attributing harm (or benefit) to the promotion policy, because asymmetric construction can manufacture either. We offer the three-stage control sequence --- construction, comparability, then validation --- as a template for evaluating adaptive pipelines beyond intrusion detection.

\textbf{Detection is not decision.} A detector reports that the distribution moved; an adaptive NIDS needs to know whether retraining will help, and these come apart precisely when the deployed model is still good: the same distributional shift is worth +19 balanced-accuracy points in one regime and $-$4 in another, while the detector's score shows no consistent association with which (within-deployment r $\approx$ +0.06; adaptation benefit is essentially deployed-model headroom, Supplementary \S S1.1). None of the evaluated distribution-level monitors distinguishes these cases from window statistics alone --- which is why classical and quantum-kernel monitors, and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them, all fail to prevent harm (Supplementary \S S1.2--S1.3).


\textbf{Why retraining can hurt, and why it is model-dependent.} In our protocol the candidate is retrained on a balanced, correctly-labeled sample of the current regime --- near-ideal conditions. When it still degraded accuracy against a strong incumbent, the controlled program traced the mean effect to two concrete disadvantages rather than to replacement itself: the challenger was scored in a representation fitted on someone else's data (the frozen convention), or fitted to a quarter of the incumbent's evidence (the historical 512/class budget) --- a model built on less information replaces one built on more, and the generalization loss outweighs the gain from recency. With both matched, the mean effect is gone in the evaluated setting (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}). Whether this loss actually pushes the outcome below no-adaptation depends on the downstream model's robustness: the SVC-RBF classifier is fragile enough on ToN-IoT to go net-negative, whereas random forest, logistic regression and MLP retain enough generalization to stay non-negative (Supplementary \S S1.6). This is not a contradiction of the regularity --- all four obey the degradation--headroom relationship --- but a refinement: net harm is the fragile-model tail of a general phenomenon. It is precisely why a decision layer that estimates \emph{actual} candidate-vs-incumbent improvement, rather than distributional change, is the right abstraction: the gate is model-agnostic by construction and, empirically, not materially worse than either baseline across the four downstream models (its residual harmful-commit rate and the chronological premium are the measured exceptions, \S\ref{sec:v2}).


\textbf{Why the gate works, and what labels it actually needs.} Where it earns its keep, the gate succeeds because it estimates the one quantity the mechanism identifies --- the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ --- directly, from a small labeled probe, and acts on it; in the frozen harmful regime it \emph{beats} no-adaptation by committing the minority of genuinely beneficial updates while rejecting the harmful majority (Supplementary \S S1.4). The label-free alternatives are informative rather than disappointing: naive disagreement measures how much retraining would change decisions, not whether the changes are correct, and fails outright; ATC and DoC estimate accuracy from the incumbent's own confidences, whose trustworthiness collapses precisely when the incumbent is degraded, so they buy safety with much of the benefit (Supplementary \S S1.5). The labeled probe sidesteps this circularity: its validity does not depend on the model being judged. The label bill is layered: retraining labels are unavoidable for any labeled-retraining loop; carving the probe from that same (deduplicated) batch costs nothing extra and buys most of the harm reduction; conversion of average harm into average benefit in the controlled confirmatory harm regime costs a fresh probe of $\approx$32 flows per confirmed drift --- adjudication counts compatible with analyst-assisted workflows, though the inspected-flow acquisition cost of the balanced candidate batch and the formal stratified probes was not evaluated. Two registered extensions sharpen the picture (\S\ref{sec:v2}): the calibrated soft ensemble shows label-free harm avoidance is achievable (at a modest cost in the decisive regimes, with no ability to decline an update), and the two-stage gate shows the same 32 labels, spent \emph{before} training, also gate the pipeline's dominant cost, the candidate itself.


\textbf{Practitioner guidance.} The results invert the usual deployment recipe. Rather than first choosing the most sensitive (or most expensive, e.g. quantum) detector and then wiring it to automatic retraining, a practitioner should (i) construct every challenger as a self-contained pipeline that fits its own preprocessing on its own training batch --- never score a challenger inside the incumbent's frozen representation (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}); (ii) match the challenger's nominal training evidence to the incumbent's, or treat any shortfall as a reason to validate (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}); and (iii) when candidate construction or evidence conditions remain asymmetric, limited, noisy or operationally uncertain, gate the commit on a small labeled validation probe --- in the evaluated zero-drift control, where the representation and nominal-size asymmetries were removed, the gate added nothing measurable. Under this recipe, and conditional on a comparable proposal being generated, the detector acts mainly as a scheduler; at extreme class imbalance, detector calibration and alarm starvation determine whether any of this is invoked at all. A 114$\times$ quantum monitoring overhead buys nothing once the decision, not the detection, is the bottleneck.


\textbf{Scope: a deployment-decision discipline, not a NIDS-specific fix.} Nothing in the control sequence is specific to network traffic: it instantiates a general champion--challenger discipline \cite{nath2007champion,guissouma2023continuous} for drift-triggered retraining --- build a comparable challenger, then estimate the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ on a small labeled probe when comparability is uncertain. The mechanism is equally generic: update value tracks incumbent degradation, which distribution-level monitors do not measure (Supplementary \S S1.1) and confidence-based self-assessment mismeasures exactly when it matters (Supplementary \S S1.5). We expect the pattern to transfer to other streaming-classification deployments where labels are scarce but not absent --- fraud, spam and abuse filtering, predictive maintenance --- while stressing that our evidence is confined to network-security benchmarks.


\textbf{Relation to quantum-kernel drift monitoring.} This work began as an evaluation of quantum-kernel MMD as a superior drift trigger; that hypothesis is not supported (QK-MMD defines regime-dependent operating points on one dataset that do not replicate externally, at a ${\sim}114\times$ simulated monitoring cost), and the quantum monitors serve in this paper as a detector-invariance check: the harm, the gate's rescue and the per-trigger mechanism all replicate under a quantum-kernel detector (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory}, \S\ref{sec:v2}), which is part of the evidence that the decision layer, not the similarity measure, is what matters (details in Supplementary \S S1.2).


\section{Limitations}


\textbf{External validity.} The strongest positive (benefit) evidence is concentrated in CICIDS2017; the external benchmarks provide the marginal and harmful ends of the spectrum, which is what the argument requires, but broader benefit-regime replication is future work; three datasets do not constitute broad external validation. The core drift trajectories are gradual mixing ramps between real regime pools --- covariate/regime drift; we do not isolate a $p(y\,|\,x)$ change. Thirteen real chronological replays (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}) add abrupt real drift: most CICIDS streams land in deep-benefit territory, whereas the UNSW-NB15 timelines and the Wednesday intra-day split keep their incumbents healthy; on the two UNSW timelines the point and strict gates beat always-deploying, while the Wednesday split is an unresolved counterexample. What no chronological stream has produced is \emph{net harm} --- a regime where always-deploy loses to never-adapt --- so the harmful-update finding remains demonstrated only on controlled constructions; its absence across thirteen pre-enumerated replays is the honest bound on how often the phenomenon should be expected in deployment. Recurrent and $p(y\,|\,x)$-isolated drift remain untested. Quantum kernels are classically simulated, so the reported 114$\times$ monitoring overhead is a simulation figure.

\textbf{Learner and update scope.} At full drift the net-harm result is specific to a fragile downstream classifier (SVC-RBF) and does not appear for more robust classifiers (Supplementary \S S1.6) --- but it is no longer confined to one dataset: with a healthy incumbent (mild or zero drift) naive updating is net-harmful in all three benchmarks (Tables~\ref{tab:harm_generality} and \ref{tab:zero_drift}), and at full drift in three ToN-IoT regimes. The zero-drift replacement harm appears for random forest, logistic regression, MLP and full-feature SVC at the default candidate budget, and size matching removes it for the robust learners (a finite-sample candidate-size effect); for SVC-RBF the frozen-transformer size-matched arm left it in place, but the preregistered own-transformer size-matched control (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}) shows that persistence was bound to the frozen representation --- with self-contained preprocessing \emph{and} matched size, the mean harm is equivalent to zero there too. The PCA-8 pipeline, chosen for quantum-simulation tractability, attenuates but does not create the harm. The harm holds across replacement-based update generators for SVC-RBF (cumulative --- the worst --- sliding-window and replay; Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}), and the standard label-free mitigation, retrain-current-plus-replay, does not rescue it; incremental or weighted online learners with no discrete replacement step remain untested, as do deployment settings beyond the four classifiers and regimes evaluated. The symmetric A/B experiment (\S\ref{sec:risk}) shows the dominant driver in this pipeline is not pure estimation variance but a representation-ownership interaction: fitting the transformer on the incumbent's data produces a systematic incumbent advantage, fitting it on the challenger's data \emph{inverts} the effect, per-model transformers leave a tightly-bounded near-zero gap, and with an independent transformer the gap is unresolved from zero (equivalence at the registered margin not established). This is a real effect of the freeze-the-pipeline convention in the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline, and the two preregistered replications now quantify it dynamically: per-challenger preprocessing removes the \emph{mean} full-drift harm entirely (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}), and matching the challenger's per-class training size removes the residual zero-drift mean harm (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}) --- so neither the frozen-policy full-drift harm nor the 512-challenger zero-drift harm should be quoted as a general property of drift-triggered updating. We therefore frame harm as what happens when a healthy model is replaced by a challenger whose construction or evidence is materially asymmetric --- a condition on the proposal, not on the dataset --- with a magnitude that depends on learner fragility, preprocessing ownership and the evidence gap.

\textbf{Prevalence and acquisition cost.} The gate assumes a small labeled probe at decision time; we report its size ($\approx$32--64 adjudicated flows per confirmed drift) as the incremental adjudication count under the evaluated protocol --- not an estimate of inspected-flow acquisition cost in an operationally imbalanced deployment. Evaluation windows are class-balanced; alert volumes and false-positive costs under extreme operational imbalance are untested. The probe's tolerance to imbalance is bounded, and we correct an earlier overclaim: under \emph{genuinely random} inspection (attack count $\sim \mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$, zero-attack probes allowed; \S\ref{sec:boundaries}) the gate holds down to $\pi \approx 0.05$ but its harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi = 0.01$, where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack; below that prevalence the operator must enrich the probe (${\approx}1/\pi$ inspections per attack flow) or accept a decision made on benign traffic alone --- the initial study's stronger claim rested on a probe conditioned to contain one attack, and is withdrawn. The inspected-flow cost of the balanced candidate batch, of the formal stratified probes, and of the deployment pipeline end to end is not modeled.

\textbf{Probe representativeness and information availability.} In the core harness, candidate batches and probes are sampled from the simulator's mixture at the true current severity --- information a production system lacks. That caveat is discharged rather than merely disclosed: the registered leakage-free observed-data arm (\S\ref{sec:v2}, Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}) rebuilds both from observed past traffic only, with no access to $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ or the pools, and reproduces the harmful-regime rescue and the gate-over-naive ordering, while exposing negative marginal and mild-drift cells relative to no-adaptation; equivalence to the oracle-probe arm was not tested, and the core-harness tables retain the convenience (with-replacement sampling and its small, symmetric collision count), which the observed-data arm's without-replacement, value-deduplicated stream mode removes exactly. The gate is evaluated at fixed per-window severity. Label latency is modeled for the probe (up to 20 windows stale) and the candidate batch (5--20 windows; Table~\ref{tab:latency} fixes every knob's semantics); training completion is instantaneous except in the operational sensitivity (five-window delay); asynchronous or partial labeling is not modeled, and the probe-corruption experiment flips labels uniformly at random --- a candidate-aware adversary is strictly stronger and untested. The label-free estimators of Supplementary \S S1.5 are instantiated in their standard form; estimator-specific tuning could narrow the gap we report. The confidence-sequence gates control the per-proposal probability of falsely establishing superiority \emph{on the probe distribution}; this equals a bound on future-deployment harm only when the probe is representative --- an assumption our own stale-probe and coverage results show can fail (the empirical-Bernstein sequence exceeds nominal $\alpha$ under strong within-probe autocorrelation). The plain sequences are per proposal; VBC-SG's lifetime budget supplies the deployment-long bound by alpha-spending across proposals, at a label cost the frontier shows is prohibitive in its maximally-stacked stratified form at $b{=}64$.

\textbf{Scope of the symmetric-pipeline replication and the size-matched control.} Both replications target the primary KS-max point/strict question; the quantum detector and the full sequential frontier remain evaluated under the historical frozen-transformer policy. Concretely: QK-ZZ detection, VBC-SG and the budget frontier, and the mild-drift matrix were not re-run with self-contained pipelines --- and VBC-SG was not re-run under size-matched own pipelines --- so none of their numbers should be assumed to transfer; their evidence is scoped to the configuration in which it was produced. The size-matched control itself is deliberately narrow: size matching was tested \emph{only under zero drift} (full-drift size-matched own-transformer arms were not run); its zero-drift trigger is a random proposal rate, not a real alarm process; candidate and incumbent still draw from balanced pools (the own $+$ size-matched $+$ observed-data combination was not evaluated); and it is a controlled pool-based construction, not an operational arm. Within both replications: evaluation streams, candidate batches and probes remain class-balanced; the inspected-flow acquisition cost is not modeled; balanced accuracy is the registered primary endpoint and does not replace operational security metrics (attack recall and FPR are preregistered guardrails; one 512-size cell fails recall non-inferiority); the future-negative sign percentages (65\%/61\% at 512, 48--52\% at the matched size, at $H{=}5$ under zero drift) are descriptive within-trajectory fractions over seed-clustered commits, not population prevalences or deployment probabilities; mean equivalence at the matched size does not imply that no individual committed candidate can be worse than the incumbent, nor identity of per-class outcomes; and VBC-SG's guarantee --- where it applies --- controls false probe-superiority, not guaranteed future harm. The main evidence remains concentrated on the SVC-RBF pipeline.

\textbf{Statistical granularity and multiplicity.} Seeds quantify within-regime sampling variability, not new environments: the external units are three datasets and seven regimes, and confidence intervals should be read at that granularity (Supplementary \S S3 reports leave-one-out behaviour). Two v1 harness caveats --- seed-matched but unpaired stream realizations, and with-replacement pool sampling --- apply to the initial study only: the registered replication re-runs the confirmatory design on a common-stream, partition-disjoint harness with fresh seeds and reproduces every criterion. Multiplicity-adjusted $p$-values are computed directly from the paired per-seed contrasts using a deterministic centered paired bootstrap test, with Holm controlling the six-test confirmatory family, Holm within each of the four preregistered symmetric-replication families (F1--F3 full-drift, 3/3/6 contrasts; F4 zero-drift, 12 contrasts; Supplementary \S S7), Holm within each of the four preregistered size-matched families (F1--F4: 3/3/6/6 contrasts; Supplementary \S S8), and Benjamini--Hochberg applied separately to the pre-declared 15-cell budget-frontier block and the seven-replay chronological block (Supplementary \S S5); other point/VBC-SG comparisons, the frozen-side cells of Table~\ref{tab:symmetric_pipeline} and the 512-vs-never column of Table~\ref{tab:size_matched} are descriptive and uncorrected. \emph{No conclusion depends on the correction}: every nominally significant contrast survives its adjustment, in every family.


\section{Conclusion}


Drift detection does not establish candidate superiority, but neither does every proposal require a validation gate. In the evaluated setting, frozen incumbent-owned preprocessing generated the original full-drift harm, and a fourfold nominal candidate-sample-size disadvantage generated the remaining zero-drift harm. Under the preregistered zero-drift, random-proposal control with balanced pools, self-contained challengers trained at nominal 2{,}000-per-class sample-size parity were equivalent to never-adapt within the $\pm0.5$-point margin, and under that same control point/strict validation offered no measurable gain. Candidate construction, nominal evidence parity and promotion testing should therefore be treated as sequential controls: validate the challenger when its construction or evidence conditions remain asymmetric or uncertain, rather than treating a gate as a substitute for building a fair challenger --- the measured value of the gate disappeared when the evaluated representation and nominal sample-size asymmetries were removed. The registered outcome of the final control is ATTENUATION, its preregistered sign-based future-value criterion remaining inconclusive around the near-zero mean; the substantive result --- size matching neutralizes the mean zero-drift harm in all three benchmarks --- and the formal classification are both reported and kept distinct. The frozen-policy frontier prices validation where it earns its keep, thirteen chronological replays without observed net harm bound the claims where the evidence stops, and everything reproduces from the public artifact.
