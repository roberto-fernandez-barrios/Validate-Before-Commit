\begin{abstract}
Machine-learning intrusion detectors are retrained for distribution drift, and the update decision is usually delegated to a drift detector. We show drift magnitude is not a sufficient commit signal: under a fixed update generator, whether an update helps tracks how degraded the model already is, because retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level. A registered prediction test confirms its sharpest consequence: under mild drift, where the incumbent stays healthy, always-deploy turns negative in all three benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT), resolved in two. We recast updating as two decisions --- train a candidate, then commit it --- and evaluate a \emph{validate-before-commit} gate: deploy the candidate only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe (tens of flows per decision; candidate training itself consumes ${\sim}$1,024 labels, accounted). A replication registered on a hardened harness (bit-identical streams, disjoint partitions, pristine seeds) confirms every criterion for two detectors and, via registered extensions, four classifiers; a causal arm --- candidates, probes and recalibration from observed traffic only --- reproduces it. A zero-drift control reframes the thesis toward \emph{candidate governance}: forcing updates on a healthy model is net-harmful \emph{even with no drift at all}. The harmful sign appears for four downstream classifiers at the default candidate budget, but a size-matched control localizes the \emph{size-robust} replacement harm to the fragile SVC-RBF learner (it vanishes for random forest, logistic regression and MLP once the candidate matches the incumbent's sample size); separately, for SVC-RBF it holds across replacement-based generators (a cumulative all-data candidate the worst; a calibrated ensemble the one exception). So a drift alarm is a proposal generator, not deployment evidence: whatever produces a proposal, validate the challenger before it replaces the incumbent. We distinguish two gates: an accuracy-maximizing point rule ($\varepsilon{=}0$), and risk-controlled rules (exact McNemar, and anytime-valid confidence sequences) whose per-proposal guarantee is over the \emph{probe} distribution. No policy dominates the accuracy--labels--updates frontier: we map it and report its boundaries (the point gate reduces but does not eliminate the zero-drift replacement cost; the risk-controlled gates eliminate it but forgo small benefits at tens of labels; and the gate pays a premium on deep-benefit chronological replays). Results reproduce from a public artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


Machine-learning network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) are trained on historical traffic \cite{nguyen2023tsids}, yet the traffic they monitor evolves: new services, shifting user behaviour, reconfigured infrastructure and novel attacks all move the input distribution away from the training distribution, a phenomenon known as concept drift \cite{shyaa2024evolving,wahab2022intrusion,roshan2018adaptive}. As the distribution shifts, a frozen classifier loses accuracy, so operational NIDS periodically \textbf{retrain} their downstream classifier on recent traffic \cite{wahab2022intrusion,camarda2025managing,constantinides2019novel}. Retraining is not free --- it consumes labeled data and compute and destabilizes a deployed model --- so the central operational question is \emph{when} to retrain.


The dominant answer frames this as a \textbf{drift-detection} problem: run a statistical monitor that compares recent traffic to a reference window, and trigger retraining when the monitor signals a change \cite{gama2004learning,rabanser2019failing,lukats2024benchmark}. A large literature accordingly competes to build more sensitive monitors, from univariate change detectors to multivariate two-sample tests such as maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) and energy distance \cite{gretton2012kernel,szekely2013energy}, and more recently to quantum-kernel similarity measures whose feature-map geometry may respond differently to distributional change \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,kaissar2026enhancing}. The implicit assumption is that a better change detector yields a better retraining policy.


This paper challenges that assumption on empirical grounds. Across three public benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT) and multiple attack regimes we find the premise beneath the whole detection-centric framing is false: \textbf{deploying every drift-triggered update is not always beneficial.} The value of an update ranges from strongly positive to negative --- for a fragile deployed model in a ToN-IoT scanning regime, \emph{no adaptation} outperforms every triggered strategy we test. Whether an update helps tracks not how much the distribution moved but how much the deployed model has already degraded: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so under a fixed candidate generator the benefit of an update is largely the deployed model's headroom to that level (r $\approx$ $-$0.8 to $-$0.9 across four downstream classifiers, with a coupling-aware analysis in \S5.3 and a per-decision hierarchical model in \S\ref{sec:v2}). A drift monitor, which measures distributional change, does not track this quantity --- within a deployment its score is uninformative about the gain, which is precisely why sensitive detectors --- and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them --- fail to prevent harmful updates. This holds even when the detector is a quantum-kernel MMD monitor rather than a classical two-sample test: improving the monitor does not, by itself, improve the update decision.


We therefore argue that adaptive model updating should be recast from a \emph{detection} problem to a cost-aware \emph{decision} --- in fact two decisions: whether to train a candidate at all, and, once a candidate exists, whether to \emph{deploy} it. This paper is chiefly about the second decision, where the detection-centric loop silently answers "always". We evaluate a \textbf{validate-before-commit} gate that, on each triggered drift, retrains a candidate model but deploys it only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe drawn from current traffic; a two-stage variant (\S\ref{sec:v2}) additionally spends a disjoint half of the same label budget \emph{before} training, to skip candidates the incumbent's health does not warrant. Across benefit, mixed and harmful regimes, the gate (pre-specified criteria, 30 seeds) preserves the gains of updating where it helps, avoids harm where it does not, and significantly outperforms both naive triggering and never adapting. The commit decision is cheap and robust: 32 labeled flows per confirmed drift suffice (on the hardened harness even 8 still reduce harm, though 32 is the recommended operating point; \S\ref{sec:v2}); it tolerates validation labels up to 20 windows stale; it remains harm-avoiding under heavy random label corruption (up to 40\% of validation labels flipped); and it generalizes across two drift detectors and four downstream classifiers. To be explicit about total cost: candidates themselves are retrained on labeled data (1{,}024 flows per trigger in our loop, spent whether or not the gate commits), so the probe is the incremental cost of the \emph{decision}, not of the pipeline --- we account for both throughout (\S\ref{sec:v2}). Label-free alternatives --- from naive disagreement to state-of-the-art accuracy estimators (ATC, DoC) --- either fail outright or sacrifice much of the benefit, showing that a few target labels, spent on the decision rather than the detector, are what buys harm avoidance without giving up the gains.


Our contributions are: (1) a regime taxonomy showing the value of drift-triggered updates spans benefit to net-harm across three benchmarks --- and a \emph{registered prediction test} showing that the value of updating turns negative in all three once the drift is mild enough for the incumbent to stay healthy (resolved in two), so harm is a property of the operating point rather than of one dataset; (2) a coupling-aware empirical account: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so update value is deployed-model headroom --- which detector scores do not track within a deployment, an account supported at the level of individual decisions by a hierarchical per-trigger model that controls for severity and time; (3) detector-invariance evidence and an oracle-regret analysis showing the decision, not the detector --- classical or quantum --- is the lever; (4) pre-specified negatives on simple confirmation/cooldown policies, replay retraining, canonical performance monitors (DDM, ADWIN) and adaptive-update rules (ensembling, sliding windows); and (5) a label-efficient commit gate, validated by a replication registered before execution on a hardened harness --- including a fully causal arm in which candidates and probes come only from observed past traffic --- and delimited honestly: it pays a measurable premium on chronological deep-benefit replays, it does not dominate the policy frontier, and its guarantee dissolves when probes are drawn by random inspection below ${\sim}1$--5\% attack prevalence. The remainder of the paper reviews related work (\S2), formalizes the decision and the gate (\S3), details the protocol (\S4), reports results (\S5), and discusses implications and limits (\S6--\S8).


\section{Related work}


\textbf{Concept drift and adaptive intrusion detection.} Concept drift degrades ML-based NIDS, and a body of work adapts models online or on a schedule to compensate \cite{gama2014survey,lu2019learning,shyaa2024evolving}. Most such systems assume adaptation is beneficial and focus on \emph{how} to adapt (incremental learning, windowing, ensembles) \cite{constantinides2019novel,roshan2018adaptive,shyaa2023enhanced}. We instead study \emph{whether and when} adaptation helps, and show it can be net-harmful --- connecting to, but distinct from, work on negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting in continual learning \cite{mccloskey1989catastrophic,kirkpatrick2017overcoming,zhang2023survey}, which we observe operationally in a security setting.


\textbf{Drift detection and two-sample monitors.} Drift is commonly detected with change detectors \cite{bifet2007learning,gama2004learning,baena2006early,raab2020reactive}, statistical-test ensembles over batch streams \cite{komorniczak2022ensemble}, or multivariate two-sample tests --- MMD, energy distance, Kolmogorov--Smirnov and Jensen--Shannon divergence --- used as retraining or alarm triggers \cite{gretton2012kernel,rizzo2016energy,lin1991divergence,darling1957ks}; how drift manifests (type, locality, severity) itself varies widely across streams \cite{aguiar2024locality}. Our classical baselines are drawn from this family. A complementary family of \emph{performance-aware} detectors monitors the model's error signal directly, presupposing continuous labeled feedback; Bayram et al. \cite{bayram2022degradation} survey them and formalize the distinction between distributional change and model degradation that our degradation--headroom analysis (\S5.3) quantifies empirically. We use distribution-level monitors but argue that improving them does not improve the retraining decision, because they are blind to model degradation --- and our gate concentrates the label budget at the update decision instead of spending it on continuous performance monitoring.


\textbf{Quantum kernels for drift monitoring.} Quantum-kernel methods embed data via parameterized quantum feature maps and have been proposed for classification and similarity estimation \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,huang2021power}, including exploratory use in cybersecurity \cite{gouveia2020towards,nicesio2023quantum,bellante2025evaluating}. In a companion study \cite{fernandez_paper1} we found that fidelity-based quantum kernels can improve out-of-distribution robustness under distribution shift, but that the operative property is the kernel's geometry rather than its quantumness --- which motivated evaluating quantum-kernel MMD as a drift monitor here. The present paper reaches the analogous conclusion one level up: for the \emph{update decision}, changing the detector family --- quantum or classical --- did not change the outcome in any regime we evaluated; quantum monitors act as one instrument among several and do not change the adapt/no-adapt outcome --- consistent with mounting evidence that quantum-kernel advantage claims require careful classical baselining and face fundamental concentration limits \cite{schnabel2025quantum,thanasilp2024concentration}.


\textbf{Drift, rejection and evaluation discipline in ML security.} A parallel security literature asks when a deployed classifier should no longer be trusted. Transcend \cite{jordaney2017transcend} and its revision Transcendent \cite{barbero2022transcendent} use conformal evaluation to reject individual predictions whose low credibility signals model aging; CADE \cite{yang2021cade} detects and explains drifting samples via contrastive representations; INSOMNIA \cite{andresini2021insomnia} updates a NIDS continuously with semi-supervision as drift accrues. These systems abstain on individual samples or adapt continuously; none evaluates whether a specific retrained candidate should replace the incumbent. Our gate acts at exactly that decision: it compares candidate against incumbent on current traffic and can conclude that the correct action is \emph{not to update at all} --- the case \S5.2 shows is real. TESSERACT \cite{pendlebury2019tesseract} and the ``dos and don'ts'' of Arp et al. \cite{arp2022dodonts} document how temporal and experimental bias inflate security-ML results; our pre-specified protocol with temporally ordered progressive windows and paired within-run baselines (\S4) follows that discipline.


\textbf{When to retrain: cost-aware and validation-gated updates.} Beyond detection, some work weighs the cost of adaptation or validates a candidate before deployment, e.g. in MLOps model-update pipelines and shadow/champion--challenger evaluation \cite{nath2007champion,sculley2015hidden,kreuzberger2023mlops,guissouma2023continuous}; that a well-intentioned update can be net-harmful is documented beyond security, e.g.\ in human--AI teams, where updates that raise average accuracy can still hurt end-to-end performance \cite{bansal2019updates}. Our gate instantiates this idea for streaming NIDS and, crucially, quantifies its label cost and demonstrates its necessity --- that detection and simple policies are insufficient without it. Its reliance on a small labeled probe connects to active and label-efficient learning for data streams \cite{cacciarelli2024active,krawczyk2019adaptive,liu2021comprehensive}, where labels are scarce and must be spent where they most affect the decision. A complementary line estimates a model's accuracy under distribution shift without target labels \cite{garg2022atc,guillory2021doc,deng2021labels,baek2022agreement,jiang2022disagreement,rosenfeld2023dis2}; rather than argue past it, we instantiate its two standard estimators (ATC, DoC) as gates in our own harness and find they trade benefit for safety (\S5.7).


\section{Method}


\subsection{Adaptive readaptation as a sequential decision}


We consider a deployed binary classifier $h_0$ (benign vs. attack) monitoring a stream of traffic windows $W_1, W_2, \dots$ Each window $W_t$ is a set of flows with (at evaluation time) ground-truth labels; the deployed model degrades as the traffic distribution drifts. A drift monitor $D$ produces a score $s_t = D(W_t)$ measuring divergence of $W_t$ from a reference. The standard adaptive-NIDS loop is: raise an alarm when $s_t$ exceeds a threshold, and upon a confirmed alarm \textbf{retrain} the classifier on recent data. We make explicit that this loop conflates two questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{detection:} \emph{has the distribution changed?} --- answered by $D$;
  \item \textbf{decision:} \emph{will replacing $h$ with a model retrained on the current window improve accuracy?} --- which $D$ does not answer.
\end{itemize}


Let $h$ be the incumbent model and $h'$ a candidate retrained on the current regime. Adaptation is beneficial at $t$ iff $\mathrm{acc}(h', W_t) > \mathrm{acc}(h, W_t)$. Because $D$ measures distributional change rather than the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h',\cdot)-\mathrm{acc}(h,\cdot)$, a detector-only policy commits every confirmed change, including changes for which retraining does not help. Our method (\S3.4) inserts a decision step that estimates this sign before committing.


\subsection{Drift monitors}


We use two-sample drift monitors comparing a reference window (from the pre-drift regime) to the current window. Classical baselines: \textbf{Energy distance}, \textbf{RBF-MMD}, per-feature \textbf{Kolmogorov--Smirnov} (max reduction) and histogram \textbf{Jensen--Shannon} divergence. Quantum monitors: \textbf{quantum-kernel MMD} with \textbf{ZZ} and \textbf{Pauli-XZ} feature maps ($q_{\text{reps}}=1$, angle encoding with standardized \texttt{atan} scaling), the MMD being computed in the quantum-kernel-induced RKHS. All monitors expose a scalar score; thresholds are calibrated per detector on held-out calibration windows at a fixed quantile (0.95). The paper's claims are detector-agnostic; the quantum monitors are included as instruments and to test whether a more expressive kernel changes the decision (it does not, \S5.4).


\subsection{Progressive-drift readaptation protocol}


Each stream has 100 post-drift windows. Window $t$ is a class-balanced sample in which each class is a mixture of the reference and current pools with mixing weight $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ ("severity"), ramped linearly from 0 to 1 over the first 80 windows and held at 1 thereafter, so drift arrives gradually. On a committed adaptation, the candidate is trained on a fresh balanced labeled sample from the current regime, and the detector reference is reset to the adapted regime. The trigger policy (alarm confirmation and cooldown) is held fixed across all gate conditions, so the decision gate is the only \emph{designed} difference between arms. On the confirmatory harness (v2) every arm additionally processes \emph{bit-identical} streams, so contrasts are exactly paired; only the initial exploratory study drew a separate pseudo-random realization per arm (identical in distribution, not in realization; \S4).


\subsection{The validate-before-commit gate}


Our contribution is a decision step interposed between the trigger and the model swap: a \emph{deployment} (commit) gate, not a retraining gate. On each confirmed trigger the loop retrains a candidate $h'$ as usual --- consuming its training labels and compute regardless of the outcome --- and the gate then decides only whether $h'$ replaces the incumbent $h$; otherwise we keep $h$ (and keep the detector reference, so a later window can re-propose once an update becomes worthwhile). \S\ref{sec:v2} additionally evaluates a two-stage variant that moves the same probe \emph{before} candidate training, gating the training decision itself. We evaluate three commit gates:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{none} (naive):} always commit --- the standard loop.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{labeled\_probe}$(b,\varepsilon)$:} draw a small balanced labeled probe $P$ of $b$ flows from the current window; commit iff $\mathrm{BA}(h',P) \ge \mathrm{BA}(h,P) + \varepsilon$. The probe is the method's incremental labeling cost; we report $b\in\{32,64\}$, $\varepsilon=0$.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{unsup\_disagree}$(\tau)$:} commit iff the fraction of flows on which $h$ and $h'$ disagree on the \emph{unlabeled} current window is $\ge \tau$ (a zero-label proxy for "adaptation would change decisions"); we report $\tau=0.15$.
\end{itemize}

Two variants are evaluated and we state both, because they differ in exactly the information they are allowed to use. Algorithm 1A is the \emph{core simulation} gate: the environment supplies candidate batches and probes from its class pools at the current mixture severity --- convenient, and the source of the paper's main tables, but an oracle a deployment lacks. Algorithm 1B is the \emph{observed-data} gate: every object it touches --- the candidate's training rows, the probe, and even the detector's post-commit reference --- comes from windows the system has actually seen, and nothing reads $\mathrm{sev}(t)$. \S\ref{sec:v2} reports 1B as a registered replication of 1A.

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm 1A: Gated update, core simulation harness (one stream)
  h <- h0 ; D <- fit_reference(pools, sev=0) ; alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- 0
  for t = 1..T:
      W_t <- environment.stream[t]              # pre-generated, balanced, labeled
      record BA(h, W_t)
      s_t <- D.score(W_t) ; alarms.append(s_t > threshold)
      trigger <- policy(alarms) and cooldown == 0
      if trigger:
          h' <- train(pools.train, sev(t))      # candidate: current mixture (oracle)
          P   <- sample(pools.probe, sev(t))    # probe: balanced, b flows (oracle)
          commit <- [ BA(h',P) >= BA(h,P) + eps ]                  # Sec 3.4
          if commit:
              h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(pools, sev(t))          # deploy + reset
          alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- C                             # applied either way
      else: cooldown <- max(0, cooldown - 1)
  return {BA(h, W_t)}_t

Algorithm 1B: Gated update, observed-data harness (no sev(t), no pools)
  ... identical control flow, but at a trigger at window t:
      h' <- train(rows of the last 8 OBSERVED windows)             # candidate
      P  <- 32 rows of the OBSERVED window t-9, natural composition,
            excluding by row identity any row used to train h'     # probe
      commit <- [ acc(h',P) >= acc(h,P) + eps ]
      if commit: h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(last 8 OBSERVED windows),
                 threshold <- quantile_0.95(scores of preceding OBSERVED windows)
\end{verbatim}


The \texttt{labeled\_probe} gate directly targets the quantity \S3.1 identifies (the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$), estimated from $b$ labels; the \texttt{unsup\_disagree} gate tests whether that estimate can be obtained without labels.


\textbf{A cost-sensitive reading: two decisions, two thresholds.} The update loop makes two sequential decisions with different cost structures, and it matters not to conflate them. Let each error cost $\lambda_e$ per flow, let deploying an update cost $\lambda_U$ (deployment risk, churn), let training a candidate cost $\lambda_C$ (compute plus its $2m$ training labels at $\lambda_L$ each), and let probe labels cost $\lambda_L$ each. \emph{At commit time the candidate already exists}: $\lambda_C$ and the probe are sunk, and the only decision-relevant cost of committing is $\lambda_U$. Over a horizon of $N$ post-decision flows, the myopic commit rule is therefore
$$\mathrm{commit} \iff \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h',P) - \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h,P) \ge \varepsilon^{\ast}, \qquad \varepsilon^{\ast} = \frac{\lambda_U}{\lambda_e N},$$
so the margin $\varepsilon$ is not a heuristic knob but the \emph{deployment} cost expressed in accuracy points over the remaining horizon --- $\lambda_C$ does not belong in $\varepsilon^{\ast}$, because rejecting the candidate cannot recover it. Training costs enter one decision earlier: whether to build a candidate at all, which pays off only if the \emph{expected} committed gain exceeds $(\lambda_C + \lambda_U)/(\lambda_e N)$. The two-stage gate of \S\ref{sec:v2} instantiates exactly this structure, spending the probe on the incumbent \emph{before} training and skipping candidate construction when the incumbent shows no measurable degradation --- saving all candidate labels and compute at those triggers. The probe budget $b$ trades $\lambda_L b$ against estimator variance, and a risk-averse operator replaces the point estimate with a lower confidence bound (the LCB variant of \S\ref{sec:v2}). The margin sweep of \S5.7 is the empirical operating characteristic of the commit rule: raising $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed updates by ${\sim}30\%$ at equal downstream gain, which is exactly the behaviour an operator with non-trivial $\lambda_U/\lambda_e$ should choose. Asymmetric error costs ($\lambda_{FN} \ne \lambda_{FP}$) substitute a weighted accuracy for BA in the same rule.


\subsection{Risk-controlled commit gates and the scope of their guarantee}
\label{sec:riskgates}

The point gate above ($\varepsilon{=}0$) is \emph{accuracy-maximizing}: it commits on any observed probe advantage and carries no formal error control (about a quarter of its commits have negative realized value; \S\ref{sec:v2}). We separately study \emph{risk-controlled} gates that commit only on statistical evidence. Let the probe be $b$ flows drawn from a distribution $\mathcal P$, and for each flow let
$$d_i = \mathbf 1[h'\text{ correct on }i] - \mathbf 1[h\text{ correct on }i]\in\{-1,0,+1\}.$$
Three rules: (i) \textbf{exact McNemar} --- commit iff a one-sided exact test on the discordant pairs rejects $H_0:\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0$ at level $\alpha$ (ties never commit); (ii) \textbf{Robbins CS} --- a normal-mixture (sub-Gaussian, $\sigma^2\le1$) lower confidence sequence \cite{howard2021timeuniform} on $\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]$, commit at the first look whose lower bound exceeds $0$; (iii) \textbf{empirical-Bernstein CS} --- a predictable-plug-in betting confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} using the empirical variance of $d_i$ (far tighter, since the $d_i$ are mostly ties). The CS gates inspect in blocks (16 flows) up to a budget $b$; if the lower bound never clears $0$ they do \emph{not} commit (the risk-averse default), so at the maximum budget an undecided proposal is rejected. \textbf{Levels used.} The confidence sequences run at $\alpha=0.10$; the exact-McNemar gate runs at its registered $\alpha=0.05$ (a stricter level, so its zero-drift behaviour is if anything \emph{more} conservative than the $0.10$ CS gates, not less). To rule out that the McNemar gate's near-total abstention is an artifact of the stricter level, we also run it at $\alpha=0.10$ (\S\ref{sec:v2}): the zero-drift conclusion is unchanged (still $\approx 0$ commits). The effective per-arm $\alpha$ is recorded in the run manifest.

\textbf{What is and is not guaranteed.} The confidence-sequence gates satisfy, for a single proposal and \emph{under the probe distribution} $\mathcal P$,
$$\Pr\!\big(\exists n\le b:\ \text{LCB}_n>0 \ \big|\ \mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0\big)\ \le\ \alpha ,$$
i.e.\ they control the probability of \emph{falsely establishing positive expected paired correctness on the probe distribution}, uniformly over the (data-dependent) stopping time. This is \emph{not} the same as bounding the probability that the committed candidate lowers future balanced accuracy: the two coincide only when the probe is representative of the windows the candidate will be evaluated on, and the paper documents where that fails --- a probe up to nine windows stale, a fast-evolving stream, and accuracy vs balanced accuracy ranking candidates differently on class-imbaled windows (\S\ref{sec:v2}). We therefore claim a bound on \emph{false probe-superiority}, not on future deployment regret, and treat probe representativeness as a separate, empirically-scoped assumption. The guarantee is also \emph{per proposal}: the process restarts for each challenger, so over a stream with $M$ committed-or-tested proposals the elementary union bound on ever making a false commit is $M\alpha$; a lifetime bound requires alpha-spending across proposals or a single deployment-long e-process, which we do not implement (\S\ref{sec:v2} reports the empirical per-stream harmful-commit rate instead, and \S7 lists the global guarantee as future work).

\subsection{Metrics}


Per window we record balanced accuracy (BA) and attack-class F1; per stream we average over the 100 windows, and per condition over seeds. We report \textbf{gain vs. no-adaptation} (the frozen $h_0$ baseline), \textbf{committed adaptations} and \textbf{labels used}. To quantify the headroom of the decision we define, per stream and detector, a \textbf{policy oracle} that retrospectively selects, per stream, the better of the two complete policies (never adapt vs.\ detector-triggered), $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}=\mathbb{E}_{\text{seed}}\max(\mathrm{BA}_{\text{no-adapt}},\mathrm{BA}_{\text{detector}})$ --- a conservative lower bound on the value of perfect per-trigger decisions, since choosing per trigger can only do better --- and the \textbf{regret} of naive triggering $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}-\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive}}$, together with the \textbf{harm frequency} (fraction of streams on which adapting scored below no-adaptation). All confirmatory comparisons are \textbf{truly paired}: on the v2 harness (\S4) every policy processes bit-identical pre-generated streams, and contrasts use cluster (per-seed) bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals. Numbers quoted from the initial exploratory study use seed-matched bootstrap CIs (arms matched by seed index but drawing separate stream realizations --- conservative rather than fully paired; \S4).


\section{Experimental protocol}


\textbf{Datasets and regimes.} CICIDS2017 \cite{sharafaldin2018toward} (Tuesday reference $\to$ Wednesday, Friday-DDoS, Friday-PortScan, Thursday-WebAttacks), UNSW-NB15 \cite{moustafa2015unsw} (DoS, Reconnaissance) and ToN-IoT \cite{alsaedi2020toniot} (Scanning), spanning benefit, marginal/mixed and harmful readaptation. Features are numeric, standardized and PCA-reduced to 8 dimensions; windows contain 128 flows. To rule out label leakage, identifier and label-derived columns are removed at staging (UNSW: \texttt{id}, binary \texttt{label}, \texttt{attack\_cat}; ToN-IoT: \texttt{label}, \texttt{type}, timestamps and IP/uid identifiers; the CICIDS variant used ships no flow identifiers), and we verified that no remaining single feature separates the classes beyond AUC $\approx$ 0.81 --- far from the near-perfect separation a leaked label would produce. CICIDS2017 has documented traffic-generation and labeling artifacts \cite{engelen2021troubleshooting}; we therefore treat it as the benefit end of a three-benchmark spectrum rather than as sole evidence --- the paper's central negative results (the marginal and harmful regimes) come from the independent UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT benchmarks.


\textbf{The confirmatory harness (v2) --- the methodology behind every headline claim.} All confirmatory results (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory} and \S\ref{sec:v2}) come from a harness built to make the paired contrasts exact. (i) \emph{Common streams:} every evaluation stream and calibration draw is pre-generated by an environment generator that depends on the seed alone, before any policy runs --- all arms process bit-identical windows in the same order, so gate$-$naive contrasts are truly paired and policy actions cannot perturb the environment. (ii) \emph{Role-disjoint partitions:} each per-class pool is split once per seed (50/30/20) into window/train/probe partitions; evaluation windows draw only from the first, initial models, candidates and detector references only from the second, probes only from the third --- train/probe/evaluation overlap is impossible by construction. (iii) \emph{Separate generators:} environment, initial training, detector calibration, candidate training, probe draws and post-commit recalibration each use their own RNG, seeded by (seed) or (seed, $t$) only, never advanced by policy actions. (iv) \emph{Per-trigger logging:} at every trigger the harness records pre-trigger incumbent degradation, the detector score, and incumbent-vs-candidate BA over the following 1/3/5/10 windows --- lookahead used for logging only, never by any policy. The replication protocol (criteria, margins, one-pass stopping rule) was committed and publicly tagged (\texttt{harness-v2-protocol}) before any confirmatory seed ran, with subsequent pre-run registered amendments (002--005) covering seed hygiene, deduplication, baseline additions and analysis specifications; the confirmatory window is the 30 pristine seeds 104--133, untouched by any smoke test.


\textbf{Shared protocol constants.} The initial model trains on 2{,}000 flows per class from the reference regime; the scaler and PCA are fit on that training set only and frozen thereafter. Every candidate trains on a balanced sample of 512 flows per class drawn \emph{at the current mixture severity} $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ --- never from the final regime pool --- so the candidate sees the population the deployed model faces at the trigger, not the future (a simulator convenience a production system lacks; \S7 bounds the word ``deployable'' accordingly, and the sliding-window and chronological-stream experiments provide the observed-data counterpoints). Detector references use 256 flows per class; thresholds are the 0.95 quantile of detector scores over 30 pre-drift calibration windows; the trigger is 3 consecutive alarms with a 10-window cooldown throughout; windows contain 128 flows. The probe is the gate's \emph{incremental} label cost on top of the labeled retraining data (512 per class per candidate) that any labeled-retraining loop --- gated or not --- already consumes: we claim label efficiency of the \emph{commit decision}, not of the retraining pipeline, and \S\ref{sec:v2} accounts for both.


\textbf{The initial exploratory study (harness v1) and its pre-specification.} The hypotheses, the regime taxonomy and the robustness texture of \S5.3--5.9 were generated on an earlier harness in which arms drew separate pseudo-random stream realizations (seed-matched, not paired) and evaluation windows, candidate batches and probes were independent draws with replacement from shared per-class pools (expected probe--training collisions below one flow per 32-flow probe). Both properties are exactly what v2 removes; v1 numbers are therefore reported as exploratory context, and every claim the paper rests on was re-established on v2 with fresh seeds. The v1 gate evaluation was itself pre-specified (gate family, regimes, detectors and success criteria fixed in a repository protocol document before the confirmatory run), with two honesty notes: the protocol fixed 10 confirmatory seeds and was expanded to 30 after all criteria passed at 10 --- a conditional expansion we report as such --- and a repository document's prospective status cannot be verified from the public history alone, which is why the v2 protocol was additionally \emph{publicly tagged} before its runs. A prior pre-specified policy grid (k-of-n confirmation and cooldown, no decision gate) is reported as a negative baseline (Table~\ref{tab:table5_phase1_negative}, Appendix).

\subsection{Main confirmatory result: the registered replication}

The protocol --- criteria, margins and a one-pass stopping rule --- was committed and publicly tagged before any confirmatory seed ran. Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory} is the paper's primary evidence: naive always-deploy updating remains net-harmful in the harm regime and the commit gate converts the harm to a significant net benefit while preserving the benefit and marginal regimes, under both a classical and a quantum-kernel detector. Every pre-registered criterion passes. Section \ref{sec:v2} documents the harness, the per-trigger mechanism test, decision-quality metrics, cost accounting, and the registered extensions --- including a real chronological stream on which the gate pays a measurable insurance premium, the one pre-registered check it fails.

\subsection{Updating is regime-dependent and sometimes harmful (initial study)}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig1_regime_spectrum.png}
\caption{Readaptation value is regime-dependent and sometimes harmful: best detector-triggered gain over no-adaptation per dataset and attack regime.}
\label{fig:1}
\end{figure}
\input{tables/table1_regime_taxonomy.tex}
Under progressive drift, an unadapted classifier degrades and triggered readaptation can recover much of the loss --- on CICIDS2017. Averaged over 30 seeds and 100 post-drift windows, the best detector-triggered strategy recovers +19.5 balanced-accuracy (BA) points over no-adaptation in the Friday-DDoS regime, +11.9 in Wednesday, +7.6 in WebAttacks and +6.2 in PortScan (Table~\ref{tab:table1_regime_taxonomy}, Fig.~\ref{fig:1}). This is the familiar picture that motivates adaptive NIDS. (The per-regime \emph{best} detector is descriptive, not a tuned choice: with a single pre-specified detector, KS-max, the taxonomy is unchanged --- +5.5 to +18.6 BA points across the four CICIDS regimes, +1.5 and +0.2 on UNSW-NB15, $-$2.4 on ToN-IoT.)


The picture does not survive to independent data. On UNSW-NB15 the best recovery is marginal (+1.4 points for DoS, +0.5 for Reconnaissance), and on ToN-IoT Scanning it becomes \emph{negative} for the SVC-RBF downstream used here: no detector-triggered strategy matches a no-adaptation baseline (best adaptive $-$0.5 points; worst $-$4.6), so the correct decision there is \textbf{not to adapt} (Fig.~\ref{fig:1}). This net-harm is downstream-dependent --- it does not appear for more robust classifiers --- while the degradation law that governs it is not (\S5.8). The effect is not a balanced-accuracy artifact: under attack-F1 the CICIDS benefits grow (e.g. +32.3 F1 points for DDoS) and ToN-IoT remains harmful for every detector (Energy $-$0.2, KS-max $-$4.6 F1 points). We stress the claim is not "adaptation usually hurts" --- it is that the \emph{sign} of the payoff is regime- and dataset-dependent, spanning +19.5 to $-$4.6 BA points, which is precisely what an adaptive system must anticipate.


\subsection{Model degradation, not distribution change, predicts the value of updating}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig9_pertrigger.png}
\caption{The central mechanism figure --- the non-coupled per-trigger test (harness v2, pristine seeds): pre-trigger incumbent degradation predicts the future value of committing (left), while the detector score at the same triggers does not (right). Predictor and outcome share no algebraic term (disjoint past/future windows); clustered CIs and a hierarchical model controlling for severity and time in \S\ref{sec:v2}.}
\label{fig:9}
\end{figure}
Why does adaptation help enormously in one regime and hurt in another? The answer is not the amount of drift but the state of the deployed model, and we establish it with two facts --- stated carefully, because the naive statistic invites a coupling objection. First, \textbf{retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level}: across the seven SVC-RBF regimes, post-adaptation BA varies far less than the deployed-model BA it replaces ($\sigma$ = 3.5 vs.\ 7.4 points; regressing the gain on deployed BA gives slope $-$0.87). Under a fixed candidate generator, the benefit of adapting is therefore largely the deployed model's \emph{headroom} to that restored level: r = $-$0.89 between deployed-model BA and gain (descriptive scatter in the appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:2}), r = $-$0.81 (bootstrap CI95 [$-$0.94, $-$0.59], 16 model$\times$regime points) pooled across all four downstream classifiers (Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}). We deliberately do not rest the argument on the correlation coefficient itself: since the gain subtracts deployed BA, r is mathematically coupled to it, and an exhaustive re-pairing null holding both observed marginals yields a median r of $-$0.91 --- the observed value is no more extreme (p = 0.80; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). The substance is the compression of the restored level, not the coefficient --- and the evidence we treat as central is the \emph{per-trigger} version of this fact, which shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at individual triggered decisions under the hardened harness, the incumbent's degradation over the five preceding windows predicts the candidate-minus-incumbent value over the five following windows, while the detector's score at the same decisions shows no consistent association (Fig.~\ref{fig:9}; clustered CIs and a hierarchical model in \S\ref{sec:v2}). Second, and free of any coupling, \textbf{the detector's own score carries almost no information about that headroom within a deployment}: over the 30 streams of each regime, the correlation between a detector's score and the gain from its own triggered adaptations has median +0.06 (maximum 0.13) across regimes, whereas deployed-model BA tracks the gain within every regime (median $-$0.68). Across regimes, classical detector scores do co-vary with the benefit (r = 0.75--0.94) --- larger drifts both fire detectors and degrade models --- but that cross-regime association is unavailable to a deployed detector calibrated on its own reference, and the persistence policies built on those scores fail to prevent harm (\S5.5). The regularity itself is robust to disaggregation: at individual-stream level r = $-$0.91 (210 regime$\times$seed points), stable under seed resampling (CI95 [$-$0.90, $-$0.88]) and under removing any one regime (r between $-$0.72 and $-$0.92) or dataset --- with the expected range-restriction attenuation, not inversion, when all four CICIDS benefit regimes are removed at once (r = $-$0.52; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). Where the deployed model has already collapsed (DDoS: 69.8\% without adaptation), retraining on a fresh window is a large gain; where it remains strong (ToN-IoT: 92.2\%), replacing a well-generalized model with a narrowly-fit current-window model is a net loss.


This exposes the core problem. Drift detectors answer \emph{"has the distribution changed?"}, but an adaptive NIDS needs \emph{"will retraining improve the classifier?"} --- and these are different questions. We quantify the gap with the per-stream policy oracle of \S3.5 (holding the detector fixed); being a policy-selection bound, it understates the headroom of perfect per-trigger decisions. The resulting \emph{regret} of naive triggering is essentially zero where adaptation always helps (Wednesday, DDoS: 0.00 points, adaptation hurt on 0\% of streams) and largest where it hurts (ToN-IoT: 3.53 points for QK-ZZ, CI95 [1.76, 5.54], adaptation hurt on 67\% of streams), scaling monotonically with harm frequency (Table~\ref{tab:table4_oracle_regret}, Fig.~\ref{fig:3}). The headroom left by naive triggering is real and concentrated exactly where the deployed model is already good.


\subsection{Detector sensitivity does not resolve the decision}

\input{tables/table2_phase2_gate_summary.tex}
Because drift detectors are blind to model degradation, improving the detector cannot solve the problem --- and it does not. Quantum-kernel MMD monitors do define distinct \emph{operating points} on CICIDS2017 (QK-MMD ZZ triggers 1.8--2.1 fewer readaptations than Energy distance at equivalent accuracy in Wednesday/DDoS, and inverts in PortScan), but at a ~114$\times$ monitoring-runtime cost, and these profiles do not replicate externally. Critically, the regime taxonomy and the harmful-adaptation outcome are identical for classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:3}, Tables 1--2): on ToN-IoT, naive triggering is net-harmful whether the trigger is KS-max ($-$1.4 points) or QK-ZZ ($-$3.7 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). Among the monitors evaluated, the operative lever is the adapt/no-adapt decision, not the similarity measure that fires the trigger.


\subsection{Simple confirmation and cooldown policies do not prevent harm}


If the decision is the lever, the natural fix is a smarter trigger policy. We pre-specified a grid of alarm-confirmation (k-of-n) and cooldown policies and evaluated them against fixed success criteria (preserve benefit in PortScan; avoid harm in ToN-IoT; do not degrade UNSW). None passed: in the harmful regime the tested policies remained far below no-adaptation and did not reduce adaptations relative to a conservative legacy baseline (Table~\ref{tab:table5_phase1_negative}, Appendix). Confirmation and cooldown act on the \emph{persistence} of distributional change, which is exactly the signal that is uninformative about whether adaptation helps. This negative result motivates a policy that acts on the right quantity.


\subsection{A label-efficient validate-before-commit gate resolves the problem}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig4_phase2_gate.png}
\caption{The validate-before-commit gate preserves benefit and avoids harm, with the same sign pattern for a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (magnitudes differ; see text).}
\label{fig:4}
\end{figure}
We introduce a decision gate that acts directly on estimated model improvement. On each drift trigger, a candidate model is retrained as usual, but it is \textbf{deployed only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe} drawn from the current window (budget 32 or 64 flows); otherwise the incumbent is kept. We evaluate the gate against the same pre-specified criteria (30 seeds, 100 windows, legacy trigger fixed so the gate is the only designed difference) across the benefit (PortScan), mixed (UNSW Reconnaissance) and harmful (ToN-IoT) regimes, for both a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}, Fig.~\ref{fig:4}).

\input{tables/table3_phase2_paired_ci.tex}
The gate passes every criterion for both detectors. In the harmful regime it turns net-harmful naive triggering into net benefit: ToN-IoT gain moves from $-$1.36 (KS-max) and $-$3.69 (QK-ZZ) points to +0.93 and +1.06, \textbf{significantly above naive triggering} (+2.30 points, CI95 [1.15, 3.63] for KS-max; +4.74, [2.47, 7.69] for QK-ZZ; Table~\ref{tab:table3_phase2_paired_ci}) and, notably, \textbf{significantly above no-adaptation itself} (+0.93, [0.53, 1.36]; +1.06, [0.77, 1.40]) --- the gate does not merely stop adapting, it commits the minority of beneficial adaptations while rejecting the harmful majority. In the benefit regime it preserves the full gain (PortScan +8.3/+8.4 points, statistically indistinguishable from naive; formally, benefit preservation was pre-specified as \emph{non-inferiority} at a 0.3-point margin, and the paired gate$-$naive CI95 lower bounds --- $\ge$ $-$0.12 across both detectors and both budgets --- clear it), and it does not degrade the mixed regime. The label cost is small: roughly 100--160 labeled flows over a 100-window stream at budget 32.


The gate's power comes from labels, not from the confirmation heuristic: a zero-label variant that commits on deployed-vs-candidate prediction \emph{disagreement} fails, remaining significantly below no-adaptation in the harmful regime ($-$4.95 and $-$5.26 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). A handful of target-labeled flows per confirmed drift therefore makes readaptation safe without giving up its benefit --- a property we show in \S5.7 that even state-of-the-art label-free estimators do not match. Because the gate behaves the same way (same sign pattern) for the classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:4}), the contribution is a detector-agnostic decision mechanism.


\subsection{Gate robustness (post-registration)}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig5_label_budget_curve.png}
\caption{Label-efficiency frontier: gain over no-adaptation versus probe budget; a small probe suffices to avoid harm.}
\label{fig:5}
\end{figure}
Ten extensions beyond the pre-specified design (explicitly labeled as robustness, not part of the confirmatory claim) probe the gate's limits. \emph{Label efficiency:} sweeping the probe budget from 8 to 128 flows (KS-max, 30 seeds; Fig.~\ref{fig:5}) shows the gate is remarkably label-cheap --- in this initial study even the smallest budget avoided harm, ~8 labeled flows per confirmed drift ($\approx$23 per stream) lifting ToN-IoT gain from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.38, plateauing near +1.1 by budget 32--64 (the hardened-harness re-run sharpens this: at $b{=}8$ harm is reduced but not converted to net benefit, fixing the operating point at $b{=}32$; \S\ref{sec:v2}); benefit-regime gain is flat at every budget. A few dozen labels, not hundreds, suffice. \emph{Benefit breadth:} beyond PortScan the gate preserves --- and slightly improves --- the benefit in the two strongest benefit regimes, Wednesday (naive +15.39 $\to$ +16.32) and DDoS (naive +24.81 $\to$ +25.27), preserving benefit in all three benefit regimes tested --- the exception being the deep-benefit chronological replays, where a stale probe imposes a measured, metric-dependent premium (\S\ref{sec:v2}).


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig8_probe_poison.png}
\caption{The gate remains harm-avoiding under randomly corrupted validation labels (up to 40\% flipped, initial study; replicated on the hardened harness in \S\ref{sec:v2}).}
\label{fig:8}
\end{figure}
\emph{Label latency:} the probe need not be current. Drawing it from traffic labeled up to 20 windows in the past (Fig.~\ref{fig:7}) leaves the gate safe and effective at every lag --- in the harmful regime the gate holds at +0.9 to +1.1 (vs naive $-$1.36) for lags of 5, 10 and 20 windows, and the benefit regimes are essentially unchanged --- so the method tolerates realistic labeling delay rather than requiring instantaneous labels. \emph{Harm breadth:} the net-harm of naive triggering (with the fragile SVC-RBF downstream) is not confined to one ToN-IoT regime --- it also appears in ToN-IoT DDoS (naive \textbf{$-$16.81}) and Injection ($-$1.21), and the gate converts all three to net-positive (+1.26, +0.80, +0.93). \emph{Cost knob:} raising the commit margin $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed adaptations by ~30\% (e.g. PortScan 3.1$\to$2.2 commits) at equal downstream gain, giving a simple operating characteristic that trades update frequency for benefit. \emph{Randomly corrupted validation labels:} because the gate trusts a labeled probe, we test a faulty labeler (or non-adaptive attacker) that flips a uniformly random fraction of the probe labels --- the random variant of the label-flipping poisoning threat against SVM-family learners \cite{biggio2012poisoning} (Fig.~\ref{fig:8}). The gate avoids harm under this corruption process: even with 40\% of validation labels corrupted --- near the point where the probe is pure noise --- it still matches-or-beats naive triggering in every regime (ToN-IoT degrades gracefully from +0.93 to +0.32 but never goes negative; benefit and mixed regimes are essentially unchanged). Label noise perturbs the deployed and candidate estimates symmetrically, so it moves the gate along the naive-to-oracle line without pushing it below the safe baseline. (A candidate-aware adversary that chooses \emph{which} labels to flip is a strictly stronger threat we do not model; \S7.) \emph{Label-free estimators head-to-head:} the zero-label variant of \S5.6 is deliberately naive, so we also instantiate the two standard label-free accuracy estimators --- ATC \cite{garg2022atc} and DoC \cite{guillory2021doc} --- as gates in the same harness: each model estimates its target-window accuracy from its own confidences plus a labeled validation sample drawn at its training time, using zero target-window labels. Both avoid harm --- on the fragile SVC-RBF downstream, ToN-IoT moves from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.40 (ATC) and +1.16 (DoC) --- but both pay for safety with benefit: on PortScan they lose 24\% (ATC) and 60\% (DoC) of the naive gain ($-$1.86 [$-$3.48, $-$0.17] and $-$4.64 [$-$6.86, $-$2.26] vs naive, CI95), and they remain significantly below naive even for random forest with native predicted probabilities. The labeled probe is thus the only gate on the benefit--safety Pareto front \emph{among those evaluated}. The mechanism mirrors the degradation law: confidence-based estimates are trustworthy only while the incumbent is healthy --- exactly the regime where the gate is least needed --- and misrank incumbent against candidate once the incumbent has degraded, which is where the benefit lives; related estimators share the same calibration premise \cite{jiang2022disagreement,baek2022agreement,rosenfeld2023dis2,deng2021labels}. \emph{Replay retraining:} the harmful baseline retrains on a narrow current window and fully replaces the incumbent, so we also test the standard label-free mitigation --- retrain-current-plus-replay, augmenting each candidate's training set with an equal-size sample from the reference regime (50/50) --- under pre-fixed criteria (KS-max, SVC-RBF, 30 seeds). The tested fixed 50/50 replay does not rescue naive triggering: in the harm regime it remains significantly net-harmful ($-$4.54, CI95 [$-$7.06, $-$2.34] vs no-adaptation; worse than full replacement's $-$1.36, since under a still-advancing ramp a replay-anchored candidate misfits later windows even more), and in the benefit regime it \emph{loses} part of the gain (+6.59 vs +7.79 naive). The gate, by contrast, is orthogonal to the update rule: placed on top of replay it restores safety and benefit in every regime --- ToN-IoT +0.59, significantly above both no-adaptation ([0.23, 0.97]) and replay-naive (+5.13 [2.96, 7.60]); PortScan +8.26, recovering the benefit replay-naive loses (+1.66 [0.52, 3.04]). Harmful adaptation is therefore not an artifact of full-replacement retraining, and the gate's value composes with the update strategy. \emph{Probe prevalence:} the probes above are class-balanced, which presumes an enrichment mechanism. An initial low-prevalence experiment (conditioned on the probe containing at least one attack) suggested the gate was indifferent down to $\pi=0.01$; that conditioning turned out to force a minority-class flow into every probe and the claim was an artifact. \S\ref{sec:v2} reports the corrected experiment, in which the attack count is drawn honestly as $\mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$: the gate tolerates genuinely random inspection down to $\pi\approx0.05$, but its harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi=0.01$ (where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all). We report the corrected bound and withdraw the stronger one. \emph{Candidate size and dimensionality:} two controls address the concern that the harm is a pipeline artifact. Size-matching: training every candidate on 2{,}000 flows per class --- the same budget as the initial model, removing the 4$\times$ size asymmetry --- \emph{deepens} the harm (ToN-IoT naive $-$5.34, CI95 [$-$8.73, $-$2.49], vs $-$1.36 at 512/class): a larger candidate fits the current mixture better and mis-specializes harder while the ramp keeps advancing, the opposite of what a small-sample explanation predicts; the gate remains safe and beneficial throughout (+0.58 / +9.33 / +3.37 across the three regimes), and candidate size does shift the marginal regime's magnitude (UNSW +3.31 vs +0.92), so magnitudes are pipeline-dependent even where signs are not. Dimensionality: with the PCA bottleneck removed (full feature space, classical detector), the benefit grows (PortScan +16.2) and the harm regime's point estimate remains negative ($-$0.65 [$-$3.03, +1.44], not significant at 30 seeds) --- neither the candidate's sample size nor the 8-dimensional projection manufactures the phenomenon, though full dimensionality attenuates the harm and we report that plainly. \emph{Significance of the safety claim.} Paired bootstrap CIs in the harm regime confirm the guarantee is not noise: the gate significantly outperforms naive triggering under every label-latency and every probe-poison level tested (CI95 excludes zero), and in no robustness condition --- across downstream models, latencies or poison levels --- is it significantly \emph{worse} than either naive triggering or no-adaptation. Where the gate is not significantly better than naive (the robust downstream models, where naive is already non-harmful), it is statistically indistinguishable from it, i.e. never worse.


\subsection{Generalization across downstream classifiers}


\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{docs/img/fig6_downstream_generalization.png}
\caption{Generalization across four downstream classifiers: net-harm is specific to SVC-RBF; the gate is safe for all.}
\label{fig:6}
\end{figure}
\input{tables/table6_downstream_generalization.tex}
To test whether the phenomenon and the solution depend on the downstream model, we repeat the three regimes with the KS-max trigger for four downstream classifiers --- SVC-RBF, random forest, logistic regression and MLP (20 seeds; Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}, Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). Three findings stand out, and we report them plainly because they refine the headline.


First, \textbf{the degradation--headroom regularity generalizes.} Across all four classifiers the benefit of adaptation tracks deployed-model degradation: where the frozen model is weak the gain is large (e.g. random forest and MLP on PortScan start at 69--70\% no-adaptation BA and gain +27 points; logistic regression on ToN-IoT starts at 84\% and gains +6.7), and where the frozen model is already strong the gain is small (random forest on ToN-IoT starts at 97\% and gains only +1.9). Pooled across all four classifiers the degradation--benefit correlation remains strong (r = $-$0.81, \S5.3; Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}): the relationship is a property of the adaptation problem, not of SVC.


Second, \textbf{whether the effect crosses into net harm is downstream-specific.} The negative-gain result --- naive triggering below no-adaptation --- occurs only for SVC-RBF on ToN-IoT ($-$1.36 points); random forest (+1.88), logistic regression (+6.70) and MLP (+0.24) all remain non-negative there (Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). SVC-RBF is fragile enough on this regime that retraining on a narrow current window loses more than it gains; more robust downstream models do not. We therefore do not claim that naive readaptation is universally harmful --- only that it can be, for fragile models, exactly where the regularity predicts the smallest headroom.


Third, and most important for deployment, \textbf{the gate avoids material harm in all twelve settings of this study.} Across all four downstream classifiers and all three regimes (twelve settings), the labeled-probe gate shows no material deficit against no-adaptation or naive triggering; the largest observed mean deficit vs.\ naive anywhere is 0.11 BA points (random forest on UNSW Reconnaissance), within seed noise (Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}). (The hardened-harness replication puts sharper bounds on this claim --- including one statistically resolvable but immaterial 0.01-point deficit, and a real chronological stream where the premium is material; \S\ref{sec:v2}.) This empirical safety property holds even in the settings where naive triggering is already fine (the gate simply commits), and rescues the one setting where it is harmful. The solution generalizes across downstream models even though the harm does not.


\subsection{Controls}


The findings are robust to the evaluation metric (the taxonomy and harmful-adaptation result hold under attack-F1; \S5.2). Quantum monitors do not reduce nuisance triggers on benign drift (QK-MMD ZZ 1.40 vs Energy 1.03 false triggers), so their value cannot be framed as cleaner benign-drift filtering; and an expensive Random-Forest downstream shows that quantum monitoring runtime can dominate total operational cost. A pre-specified feature-map sensitivity study finds no deeper map that unlocks a stronger quantum advantage. (Full tables for both: reproducibility artifact \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact}.) These controls reinforce, rather than qualify, the central conclusion: distribution-shift magnitude alone is not a sufficient commit signal.


\subsection{The registered replication in detail: harness, mechanism, costs, and boundaries}
\label{sec:v2}





The harness caveats of \S7 (per-arm stream realizations; sampling from shared pools) admit a direct answer: we rebuilt the harness and replicated the confirmatory study under it. Harness v2 pre-generates every evaluation stream from an environment generator that depends on the seed alone --- all arms process bit-identical windows, so contrasts are \emph{truly} paired --- splits every class pool into disjoint window/train/probe partitions (train/probe/evaluation overlap is impossible by construction), and gives environment, training, calibration, candidate, probe and recalibration separate generators. The replication protocol (the original A/B/C margins plus detector invariance, a one-pass stopping rule) was committed and publicly tagged (\texttt{harness-v2-protocol}) \emph{before} any confirmatory seed ran, in the artifact repository \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact}; pre-run registered amendments replaced the three seeds touched by the harness smoke, fixing the confirmatory window at the 30 pristine seeds 104--133, deduplicated the holdout gate's batch by row identity, calibrated natural-prevalence arms at operating prevalence, and (amendment 004) fixed the chronological-stream runner and added the robustness, cost-accounting and baseline-validation program reported below. This is a \emph{pre-registered internal replication under a hardened harness} --- same datasets, same constructed regimes, fresh seeds and a corrected instrument --- not an external replication on new environments. \textbf{Every criterion replicates} (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory}). Naive triggering remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$1.64, CI95 [$-$2.72, $-$0.64]); the gate converts it to +0.79 [0.53, 1.07], significantly above naive (+2.43 [1.53, 3.43]), preserves PortScan (+9.12 vs naive +8.25) and UNSW (+1.35 vs +1.21), and the pattern is detector-invariant (QK-ZZ: $-$2.91 naive $\to$ +0.72 gated, +3.63 [1.66, 6.38] vs naive). Two pre-declared extensions sharpen the contribution. First, a \emph{holdout} variant carves the probe out of the candidate's retraining batch, deduplicated by row identity --- zero labels beyond those any labeled-retraining loop already uses. It buys most of the protection but not all of it: it removes most of the harm (ToN $-$0.77 vs naive $-$1.64; PortScan +9.08) yet, unlike the fresh probe, does not beat no-adaptation in the harm regime --- so zero incremental cost purchases harm reduction, while the 32-label fresh probe remains the recommended gate. (A non-deduplicated holdout had looked equivalent to the fresh probe; enforcing row-identity disjointness removed that equivalence, and we report the corrected result.) A conservative lower-confidence-bound rule (commit only if the 90\% LCB of the per-flow correctness difference exceeds zero) is safe and slightly benefit-lossy (ToN +0.34, PortScan +8.51). Second, per-trigger logging provides the mechanism test that shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at each of 250 triggers we measured the incumbent's degradation over the five \emph{preceding} windows and the candidate-minus-incumbent BA over the five \emph{following} windows. Pre-trigger degradation predicts the future value of committing in every regime (r = $-$0.67 to $-$0.70; cluster bootstrap over seeds keeps every regime's CI95 well away from zero, e.g.\ [$-$0.76, $-$0.66] in PortScan; pooled $-$0.57, stable under leave-one-regime-out, $-$0.52 to $-$0.67), while the detector's score at the same triggers shows no consistent association (r = $-$0.01 to +0.05, every clustered CI95 straddling zero). Triggers are nested in seeds and regimes and the incumbent's past and future performance are autocorrelated, so we read this as strong \emph{predictive} support for the degradation--headroom account at the level of individual decisions --- on disjoint past/future windows, under the hardened harness --- not as a causal proof (Fig.~\ref{fig:9}).

\textbf{A hierarchical model sharpens the per-trigger claim --- for both detectors.} Because raw correlations control for nothing, we fit a mixed-effects model of the future update value $\Delta_5$ on pre-trigger degradation, detector score, current severity and stream time; per-regime models take random intercepts over seeds, and the pooled model clusters on \emph{regime$\times$seed} (a seed number is not one experimental unit across regimes). Degradation is the only predictor whose CI95 consistently excludes zero --- pooled $\beta_{\mathrm{deg}} = -1.02$ [$-$1.61, $-$0.43] per BA point (robust to a random slope on degradation: $-$0.92 [$-$1.30, $-$0.55]), and per-regime $-$1.10 [$-$1.51, $-$0.69] (PortScan), $-$0.43 [$-$0.67, $-$0.20] (UNSW), $-$1.15 [$-$1.37, $-$0.89] (ToN-IoT; cluster-bootstrap CI, the random-effect variance sits on the boundary). Two specification notes. First, severity and stream time are structurally collinear on a ramp (severity is a function of $t$; VIFs 60--230), so their individual coefficients are not separately interpretable --- but degradation and score are clean (VIF $\le$ 2.4 and $\approx$ 1.0), so the headline contrast is unaffected. Second, we repeated the whole analysis on the QK-ZZ trigger logs (311 triggers): degradation replicates ($r_{\mathrm{deg}}$ $-$0.50 to $-$0.72 per regime, pooled $-$0.59 [$-$0.68, $-$0.45]; $\beta_{\mathrm{deg}}$ $-$0.38 to $-$1.07, every CI excluding zero), the raw score--value correlation straddles zero in all six regime$\times$detector cells, and the single exception in the conditional models --- a small positive QK-score coefficient in the benefit regime (5.49 [0.27, 10.72] per score unit, PortScan only) --- we report as such: at triggered decisions the evaluated detectors' scores carry no \emph{consistent} incremental signal, not provably none. Leave-one-regime-out prediction transfers \emph{directionally but not in scale}: the fixed part predicts held-out PortScan ($R^2$ 0.18) and ToN-IoT (0.36), collapses to ${\le}0$ without the degradation term, and fails on held-out UNSW ($R^2$ $-$10.5) whose update values live on a much smaller scale even though its within-regime correlation is $-$0.67 --- the account is a robust ranking signal, not a calibrated universal law, and we claim only the former.

\textbf{Decision-quality of the gate, on the same logged triggers.} Using the logged five-window lookahead as ground truth (never available to the policy), the lp32 gate's per-trigger operating point is: in the harm regime, rejection is almost always right (beneficial-rejection rate 4.9\%, i.e.\ 95\% of rejections blocked a non-beneficial update; recall of beneficial updates 0.95) and the gate cuts \emph{local per-decision regret} --- commit vs.\ reject evaluated on the gate policy's own trigger states, against an always-commit rule on those identical states (this is a per-decision contrast, not the full naive trajectory, whose incumbents and triggers would differ) --- from 0.0452 to 0.0020, a ${\sim}22\times$ reduction. In the benefit regime the gate still reduces this regret five-fold (0.0028 vs 0.0140): even where updating is on average excellent, some individual candidates are bad, and the probe catches them. Pooled across 308 triggers: precision 0.74, recall 0.87, regret 0.0030 vs 0.0212. The five-window horizon is not doing the work: logging reruns at horizons 1/3/5/10 leave the harm-regime reduction at 15--23$\times$ and the benefit regime at 4--5$\times$ throughout, while in the marginal regime the contest is honestly a wash at every horizon (ratios 0.8--1.0 on regrets an order of magnitude smaller). The gate does commit some harmful updates (23\% of its commits have negative realized value, mostly small) --- it is a cheap statistical filter, not an oracle.

\textbf{Model-agnosticism moves onto the hardened harness}: for random forest, logistic regression and MLP (KS-max, same pristine seeds) the fresh-probe gate is never materially below naive retraining (largest deficit 0.02 points; the single boundary CI is random forest/PortScan's $-$0.01 [$-$0.03, $-$0.00]), never significantly below no-adaptation, and it rescues the one non-SVC cell where naive dips negative (MLP on ToN-IoT: $-$0.16 naive $\to$ +0.25 gated, +0.41 [0.17, 0.69]). (These are the fresh-probe runs \texttt{ks\_lp32\_\{rf,logreg,mlp\}}; the aggregation block that produces the published CSV ships in the artifact's aggregator.) Two further registered extras complete the picture. A \emph{performance-aware trigger} (DDM-style: 8 labeled flows per window monitor the incumbent's error; trigger on a 3$\sigma$ excursion) is safe where the incumbent stays healthy --- it never fires in ToN-IoT --- but it is benefit-lossy (PortScan +5.40 vs +7.94 naive; UNSW +0.04 vs +1.22) and spends 800 monitoring labels per stream, versus zero incremental labels for the holdout gate: labels are better spent on the commit decision than on monitoring. On \emph{natural-prevalence evaluation streams} ($\pi=0.05$) an operational subtlety emerges once the detector is also \emph{calibrated} at operating prevalence (per the amendment): the benign mass dilutes the drift signal, KS-max rarely or never fires (ToN-IoT: zero triggers across 30 seeds; PortScan's naive gain shrinks to +1.04 [0.34, 1.90]), and gate and naive coincide almost everywhere. Retraining policy matters little when the monitor is starved --- further evidence that distribution-level scores are a poor control signal, and an open operational problem for detector design under imbalance. (An earlier variant that calibrated on balanced windows but evaluated on imbalanced streams overstated trigger rates and gains --- ToN naive $-$2.63, gated +0.80, lower FPR and alert volume under the gate --- and is retained as exploratory.) \textbf{Canonical monitors, validated against reference implementations.} Amendment 003 added the canonical performance-aware triggers (800 monitoring labels/stream): our batch DDM captures much of the benefit (PortScan +6.81) but is itself \textbf{net-harmful in the harm regime} ($-$2.59 [$-$5.90, $-$0.34]) --- monitoring the model's own error still commits harmful updates once a transient fires the trigger --- and the gate rescues it (+0.19). Because bespoke implementations invite doubt, amendment 004 cross-checks both monitors against the \texttt{river} reference implementations. Our DDM agrees with river's on synthetic Bernoulli step streams (94\% fire/no-fire agreement, comparable delays), and \emph{in-harness river-DDM replicates the key result}: fed the same 8 labels/window as individual outcomes, it remains net-harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.23 [$-$5.29, $-$0.05]) and benefit-lossy in UNSW (+0.14 vs naive +1.21). Our window-rate ADWIN variant, by contrast, proved insensitive (57\% agreement --- it under-fires), so we report the reference ADWIN instead: at these monitoring rates it never fires in two regimes and fires rarely in PortScan, forfeiting most of the benefit (+1.77 vs +8.25). The direction of the amendment-003 claim survives under reference implementations; the specific "never fires" wording was an artifact of our variant, and we correct it. Because a single budget invites the objection that the monitors were starved, a registered sweep raised the monitoring budget to 3{,}200 and 8{,}000 labels per stream (4$\times$ and 10$\times$): no budget fixes the pattern --- reference DDM stays net-harmful or useless in ToN-IoT ($-$1.04 [$-$1.87, $-$0.37] at 4$\times$; $-$0.45 at 10$\times$) and loses benefit elsewhere as its budget grows (PortScan +7.08 at 10$\times$ vs naive's +8.25), while reference ADWIN stays silent in ToN-IoT at every budget. The claim is therefore budget-conditional but robust across the two orders of magnitude evaluated: under these monitoring configurations, labels spent on the commit decision bought a better accuracy--cost trade-off than labels spent monitoring.

\textbf{Adaptive update rules, including a properly calibrated ensemble.} Sliding-window retraining on the last eight observed windows consumes those windows' ${\sim}1{,}024$ labels per candidate and remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.44 [$-$4.23, $-$0.91]). The soft incumbent+candidate ensemble of amendment 003 averaged uncalibrated sigmoid scores; re-run with Platt-calibrated members and probabilistic nesting (amendment 004), it \emph{improves}: it avoids the harm (ToN +0.56 [0.26, 0.87], +2.20 above naive), preserves PortScan (+8.55), and calibration fixes the marginal-regime dilution (UNSW +1.72, significantly \emph{above} naive +0.51 [0.38, 0.66] and above the gate). We report this fairly: the calibrated ensemble is the strongest label-free update rule we evaluated, and in the marginal regime it beats the gate. The gate is ahead in the two decisive regimes (paired gate$-$ensemble: ToN +0.23 [0.04, 0.41], PortScan +0.57 [0.10, 1.11]), can \emph{decline} an update --- the ensemble commits every trigger, incurring deployment churn and unboundedly nested models --- and emits an explicit per-decision signal an operator can audit; the two compose (+0.37/+0.78 in ToN on the amendment-003 runs). Rather than declare a winner, Table~\ref{tab:policy_frontier} presents the honest conclusion of the whole baseline program: \emph{no policy dominates the frontier}. The plain gate maximizes downstream accuracy; the calibrated ensemble is the best probe-free rule; the split two-stage gate is the cheapest harm-reducer; and under four illustrative operational-cost scenarios (chosen a priori, not measured in a deployment; balanced accuracy stands in for the error count, so these are an illustration of the calculus, not a validated cost model) the cheap policies win the utility in almost every cell --- the plain gate's accuracy advantage pays for its label bill only on long streams (beyond ${\sim}46$k evaluated flows in the harm regime at $c_L{=}0.1$ error-equivalents per label). Policy choice is a cost decision, and \S3.4's utility calculus is the instrument for making it.

\textbf{The v1 robustness envelope, re-established (and partly corrected) on v2.} The initial study's three operational robustness claims were re-run on the hardened harness (descriptive, same pristine seeds, paired against the shared naive arm). \emph{Budget:} the gate's benefit is stable for $b \ge 32$ (ToN +0.79; $b{=}64$: +0.92; $b{=}128$: +1.01), but the initial study's ``8 labels suffice'' does \emph{not} fully replicate: at $b{=}8$ the gate still reduces harm (+1.22 [0.46, 2.15] above naive) yet is no longer net-positive against no-adaptation ($-$0.42 [$-$1.15, 0.23]), and $b{=}16$ is borderline (+0.29 [$-$0.27, 0.77]). We therefore revise the recommended operating point to $b{=}32$. \emph{Label latency:} at 20 windows of staleness the harm regime holds fully (+0.95 [0.76, 1.19]) and the benefit regimes pay only a small, real cost (PortScan $-$0.47 [$-$0.74, $-$0.22] vs fresh lp32). \emph{Random label corruption:} harm \emph{avoidance} survives 40\% flipped probe labels (still +1.60 [0.79, 2.55] above naive), but net benefit over no-adaptation survives only to 25\% (+0.57 [0.25, 0.85]; at 40\%: $-$0.04 [$-$0.62, 0.49]) --- the corrected, sharper version of the initial claim.

\textbf{The two-stage gate: gating training, not just deployment.} Instantiating \S3.4's two-decision structure, the two-stage gate spends labeled flows on the incumbent \emph{first} and only builds a candidate when incumbent accuracy has fallen $\ge \delta$ below its healthy reference. Its first implementation reused one 32-flow probe for both the health check and the commit comparison; the external review correctly identified the resulting selection optimism (training fires exactly when the incumbent's probe realization is low, and re-using that realization favors the candidate), so a registered amendment split the draw per class into a health half and a commit half (8+8 each; no sample feeds both decisions) and re-ran the sweep at $\delta \in \{0.03, 0.05, 0.10\}$. The corrected picture ($\delta{=}0.05$): in the harm regime the split gate trains 69\% fewer candidates than the plain gate (1.07 vs 3.43 per stream), cuts total labels to roughly half of \emph{naive's} (1{,}341 vs 2{,}594; Table~\ref{tab:label_cost}), and remains significantly above naive (+1.79 [0.69, 2.92]) --- but its net gain over never adapting is \emph{no longer resolved} at 30 seeds (+0.15 [$-$0.15, 0.46]; the probe-reuse version's +0.16 [0.03, 0.31] was optimistic, exactly as the review predicted). The $\delta$ sweep is a clean operating characteristic: more sensitivity trains more candidates and retains more benefit ($\delta{=}0.03$: ToN +0.34 [$-$0.01, 0.70], PortScan +8.36; $\delta{=}0.10$: ToN +0.04, PortScan +7.33), with the benefit-regime cost vs the plain gate ranging from $-$0.76 to $-$1.80. The menu is explicit: the plain commit gate maximizes downstream accuracy; the split two-stage gate buys harm \emph{reduction} at the lowest total cost, and full harm-to-benefit conversion still requires validating a trained candidate.

\textbf{Total cost accounting.} Table~\ref{tab:label_cost} makes the full ledger explicit for the harm regime (all regimes in the artifact): candidates dominate every policy's label bill, the gate's \emph{total} cost exceeds naive's (rejected candidates are trained and discarded; rejections also keep the detector reference, producing more triggers), and the probe is ${\sim}3\%$ of the gate's total. ``Label-efficient'' therefore means --- and only means --- that the \emph{decision} adds tens of labels to a pipeline that already spends thousands; the two-stage variant is the configuration that reduces the pipeline itself.

\textbf{The gate does not need the simulator: a genuinely causal, observed-data replication (Algorithm 1B).} The strongest objection to everything above is that the core harness hands the candidate and the probe a sample drawn from the environment's pools \emph{at the true current severity} --- an oracle-quality snapshot no deployment can obtain. Our first attempt to answer it (amendment 006) was incomplete, and external review caught it: candidate and probe were causal, but the detector was still \emph{recalibrated} after every commit from the pools at the true severity --- and since gate and naive commit at different times, that oracle state interacts with the policy rather than cancelling between arms. A second registered arm (amendment 007) removes the last of it: the post-commit detector reference is rebuilt from the last eight \emph{observed} windows and its threshold from the preceding observed windows' scores. The same amendment fixed a second flaw we had missed: because pools are sampled with replacement, window-disjoint was not \emph{row}-disjoint, so the probe now excludes by exact row identity every flow that also occurs in the candidate's training windows (27--38 such rows per stream were being shared). \textbf{With every oracle privilege removed, both pre-registered criteria still pass} (Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}): naive updating on observed candidates remains net-harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$3.19 [$-$5.92, $-$0.99]) and the causal gate converts it to a net gain (+0.67 [0.35, 1.00]; \textbf{+3.86 [1.67, 6.73]} above naive), preserves PortScan (+8.57, +0.95 above naive) and holds UNSW (+0.98). The causal gate remains statistically \emph{indistinguishable} from the oracle-probe gate in the harm regime ($-$0.12 [$-$0.45, 0.21]), at a small real cost in the other two ($-$0.55 and $-$0.37). And the mild-drift prediction test replicates \emph{inside the same causal pipeline} (lower half of Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}: naive negative in all three regimes, resolved in UNSW and ToN, gate significantly better in both) --- so the paper's two central defenses now hold in one experiment rather than two. \textbf{The residual train/future-eval overlap, eliminated (amendment 011).} Because the pools are sampled with replacement, a candidate-training row could still recur in a \emph{future} evaluation window (387--1{,}319 such rows per stream, symmetric across arms but not eliminated). We therefore added a stream mode that draws every window \emph{without replacement} within the (value-deduplicated) window pool, so no row --- and no identical feature vector --- can appear in more than one window, and re-ran the causal arm. The audit counter falls from those hundreds to ${\approx}0$ ($0.2$--$1.1$ rows per stream), and \textbf{the central result survives}: the causal gate still converts the ToN-IoT harm, gate$-$naive $+2.95$ [1.53, 4.50] at full drift (vs $+3.86$ leaky) and $+1.40$ [0.29, 2.87] at mild drift. The one honest change is that PortScan's small \emph{benefit}-regime gate premium ($+0.95$ leaky) was partly a leakage artifact and is now indistinguishable from zero ($-0.06$ [$-0.33$, 0.18]) --- expected, since in a pure-benefit regime the gate has nothing to add over naive; the harm-regime rescue, the load-bearing result, is unaffected. \textbf{And a risk gate must not deploy without evidence.} At early triggers ($t<9$) the observed probe does not yet exist; the first implementation let the gate commit anyway (naive-loop fallback). We fixed this (amendment 012): a \texttt{reject} policy keeps the incumbent when no probe is available. Such triggers are rare (0.6--1.0 per stream), and under \texttt{reject} the gate makes \emph{zero} unvalidated commits, with the causal result if anything strengthened (ToN-IoT gate$-$naive $+3.24$ [1.68, 4.93] full, $+1.58$ [0.40, 3.08] mild); every committed causal update is now backed by a probe, so the \S\ref{sec:riskgates} guarantee applies to all of them.

\textbf{The zero-drift control: harmful updating is candidate governance, not drift.} The sharpest objection to the mild-drift result is that it might have nothing to do with drift: replacing a healthy model with a finite re-estimate introduces training variance, and that alone could explain a small negative mean. We registered the decisive control and ran it --- triggers fired at a fixed rate \emph{independent of any drift signal}, on streams with \textbf{no drift at all} --- and it reshapes the paper's claim (Table~\ref{tab:zero_drift}). Four findings, stated plainly. (i) The objection is partly right and the effect is real: with zero drift, always-deploy is significantly net-harmful in all three benchmarks ($-$2.76, $-$0.75, $-$4.75). (ii) But it is \emph{not} a small-candidate artifact --- size-matching each candidate to the incumbent's 2{,}000 flows/class leaves the harm in place and usually deeper ($-$4.81, $-$0.13, $-$5.76), because repeated full replacement accumulates estimation noise regardless of sample size. (iii) The plain $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate reduces this harm but does not eliminate it ($-$1.11, $-$0.56, $-$0.25): it commits on ties, and under zero drift ties are the dominant case. The cheapest fix confirms this diagnosis (amendment 012): simply \emph{rejecting ties} --- committing only on a strict $\widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h')>\widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h)$, no statistical machinery --- recovers most of the loss at zero extra cost (PortScan $-$1.11$\to-$0.13, ToN $-$0.25$\to-$0.07, UNSW $-$0.56$\to-$0.28), but not all of it and with no guarantee; the exact tests close the residual and add the per-proposal bound. So the value of the statistical gates over the trivial strict rule is the last fraction of harm plus the formal control, which an operator can weigh against the labels they cost. A \emph{risk-controlled} gate is the right tool here --- requiring statistical evidence of superiority (exact McNemar, or the anytime-valid sequential test of \S\ref{sec:v2}) makes the gate almost never commit when there is no signal, recovering essentially all of the loss (McNemar: exactly $+$0.00 in all three; +2.76 / +0.75 / +4.75 above naive). This is what ``validate before commit'' means when done correctly: when there is genuinely nothing to gain, a properly calibrated validator does nothing. (iv) Finally, with the \emph{real} KS-max detector at zero drift the trigger fires only ${\sim}0.1$ times per stream, so a well-specified monitor mostly avoids the harm by not firing. The honest synthesis is therefore \emph{candidate governance}, not a claim that detectors are powerless: a drift alarm --- or a false alarm, or a scheduled retrain --- is a \emph{proposal}, and any proposal that reaches the deployment step is a hypothesis that should be tested against the incumbent before it is committed. The case for commit-time validation does not rest on drift; it rests on the fact that an update is a hypothesis.

\textbf{The zero-drift harm across classifiers: partly general, but size-matched only for SVC-RBF.} A load-bearing objection to the harm result is that its magnitude was read off one fragile pipeline --- an SVC-RBF on an 8-dimensional PCA projection. A registered sweep (Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}) shows that, \emph{at the default candidate budget}, under zero drift naive always-deploy is net-harmful for every downstream model --- random forest ($-$0.33 / $-$1.06 / $-$0.41 across PortScan / UNSW / ToN-IoT), logistic regression ($-$0.29 / $-$0.08 / $-$0.18), MLP ($-$0.81 / $-$1.15 / $-$2.15) --- and removing the PCA bottleneck (SVC-RBF, full feature space) does not help ($-$0.90 / $-$1.16 / $-$4.76). \textbf{But a size-matched control (amendment 012) qualifies this sharply, and we report it plainly.} Those default arms train the candidate on 512 flows/class against a 2{,}000/class incumbent; re-running size-matched (candidate also 2{,}000/class) under zero drift \emph{removes} the harm for random forest, logistic regression and MLP --- all within $\pm0.1$ of zero in every regime --- while for SVC-RBF it persists and \emph{deepens} (as at full drift, $-$4.81 / $-$0.13 / $-$5.76; \S5.7). So for the robust learners the default-budget harm was largely the 4$\times$ candidate-size asymmetry, not a replacement effect; only for the fragile SVC-RBF learner is the zero-drift replacement harm genuine and size-robust. The honest claim is therefore narrower than ``generalizes across four classifiers'': under the default asymmetric budget the harmful \emph{sign} appears in all four, but size-matching localizes the robust replacement harm to SVC-RBF. Where the harm is present, the risk-controlled McNemar gate recovers essentially all of it ($+0.00$) by declining to commit without resolved evidence. We are careful to separate sign from magnitude: these zero-drift losses span the operationally trivial (logistic regression on UNSW $-$0.08, statistically resolved but immaterial) to the severe (SVC on ToN-IoT $-$4.76, and the cumulative generator $-$9.46). What the account predicts, and what the data show, is the \emph{consistency of the sign once the incumbent is healthy} --- not a uniform, uniformly-material magnitude; a practitioner should read the sign as the robust claim and the magnitude as pipeline-dependent. Two nuances we state plainly. First, at \emph{mild} and full drift the net-harm sign is still SVC-specific --- robust models resist it (RF/LogReg mild gains $\approx 0$; \S5.8) --- so the SVC caveat survives \emph{for drift-driven} harm; what generalizes across models is the \emph{zero-drift replacement} harm, where the deployed model is healthy and the loss is, we argue, dominated by re-estimation variance (a hypothesis, not a proof --- \S7 notes that the transformer is fit on the incumbent's data, so incumbent and challenger are not perfectly exchangeable, and a fully symmetric-preprocessing control is the decisive future test). Second, the harm tracks the \emph{update generator}, not full replacement specifically: under zero drift, sliding-window ($-$3.99 / $-$0.92 / $-$7.21), replay 50/50 ($-$2.93 / $-$0.75 / $-$4.03) and --- most tellingly --- a \emph{cumulative} candidate trained on \emph{all} observed windows ($-$7.04 / $-$0.07 / $-$9.46, the deepest harm of any generator) are all net-harmful; only the calibrated ensemble, which never fully discards the incumbent, escapes (and only on PortScan, $+0.84$, which the risk-averse gate forgoes). That the tested cumulative generator --- retrained from scratch on all observed windows --- is the \emph{worst} is evidence against a simple ``just use more data'' reading: repeatedly replacing a well-fitted incumbent with a fresh re-estimate injects variance a healthy model has little to gain from. We do not overclaim it: a registered set of controls (\S\ref{sec:v2}) --- row-identity deduplication, adding the incumbent's initial training set, and $n$-scaled SVC regularization --- probes whether duplication or a silently-changed penalty drives the effect, and we report exactly which of them (if any) removes the harm. The McNemar gate recovers the loss for every generator (and neutralizes the residual mild-drift harm of cumulative/replay too: ToN cumulative naive $-$2.57 $\to$ gated $-$0.14, replay $-$0.80 $\to$ $-$0.09). \textbf{Cumulative controls (amendment 011): the harm is not an artifact of duplication or regularization.} Review asked whether the cumulative generator's harm is confounded by with-replacement duplication (its training set is only 76--83\% unique rows), by omitting the incumbent's initial training set, or by holding the SVC penalty $C$ fixed as $n$ grows. We ran all three registered controls under zero drift. \emph{Deduplicating} the training rows (100\% unique) does \emph{not} remove the harm --- it \emph{deepens} it (ToN $-$9.46 $\to$ $-$10.74, PortScan $-$7.04 $\to$ $-$11.36), because more distinct current-regime rows mis-specialize the replacement harder. \emph{$n$-scaling} $C$ with the corrected $C\propto 1/n$ rule (stronger regularization as the set grows) likewise leaves it --- indeed slightly deepens it (ToN $-$10.62, PortScan $-$9.82, vs $-$9.46 / $-$7.04 at fixed $C{=}1$). \emph{Adding the initial training set} attenuates but does not eliminate it (ToN $-$8.27, PortScan $-$5.14) and flips the marginal UNSW case positive ($-$0.07 $\to$ +0.53) --- an honest boundary: where the healthy incumbent had almost nothing to lose, re-anchoring the candidate to the original data recovers a small gain. None of the controls that a ``just use more data / de-duplicate / re-regularize'' reading would predict to help actually removes the harm (the $n$-scaled control uses the corrected $C\propto 1/n$ regularization --- \emph{stronger} as the set grows --- and the harm persists there too); we therefore keep the claim at the tested-implementation scope and read the effect as most consistent with re-estimation variance rather than duplication or under-regularization, while flagging (\S7) that a fully symmetric-preprocessing A/B is needed to call it \emph{purely} variance. This is candidate governance in its strongest form: the object that needs validating is the \emph{proposal}, whatever produced it.

\textbf{Where the harm hides, and an anytime-valid gate that bounds it.} Two risk-oriented additions close the account. \emph{The mean understates the tail.} Averaging balanced accuracy over 100 post-drift windows hides that harmful updates cluster: in the ToN-IoT harm regime the naive arm's \emph{worst-window} gap below no-adaptation is $-$5.24 [$-$8.39, $-$2.34] and its 10th-percentile and CVaR$@$10\% gaps are $-$4.48 and $-$4.74 --- roughly $3$--$4\times$ the $-$1.38 mean gap --- whereas the gate keeps the entire lower tail non-negative (worst-window $+$0.72, CVaR $+$0.78). Risk, not just the average, is what the gate controls. \emph{An anytime-valid confidence sequence, and what it bounds.} The Bonferroni sequential gate above controls error across four looks; a stronger guarantee is a genuine confidence sequence valid at \emph{every} sample size. We add a gate that commits only when a Robbins normal-mixture lower confidence sequence on the per-flow correctness difference exceeds zero, which bounds the per-proposal probability of \emph{falsely establishing positive expected paired correctness on the probe distribution} by $\alpha$, uniformly over the inspection schedule (\S\ref{sec:riskgates} states the guarantee formally and its scope: it is a bound on false probe-superiority, not directly on future $\Delta$BA, and it is per proposal, not stream-wide). At the small probe budgets studied ($\le 64$ flows) the sub-Gaussian Robbins bound is conservative enough that the gate essentially never commits (gain $+0.00$ across all mild and zero-drift regimes) --- the correct behaviour in these low-headroom regimes, and the maximally risk-averse end of the point-/LCB-/McNemar-/sequential frontier, at the cost of forgoing benefit where headroom exists. \emph{A tighter empirical-Bernstein sequence recovers some of that power.} Because the correctness differences are mostly ties, their empirical variance is far below the sub-Gaussian worst case, so a predictable-plug-in empirical-Bernstein confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} --- same $\alpha$-uniform harmful-commit bound, empirical rather than worst-case variance --- is much tighter. It \emph{does} capture benefit where the benefit is large: in the deep-benefit PortScan regime at full drift it commits and gains $+5.17$ [3.64, 6.70] (vs the Robbins CS's $+0.00$ and the fixed 32-probe gate's $+8.70$), while remaining safe everywhere else --- it still avoids the ToN-IoT full-drift harm ($+0.00$, i.e.\ $+1.38$ above naive) and commits nothing under mild or zero drift. But at $b{=}64$ it cannot resolve the 1-point benefits of UNSW-Reconnaissance or full-drift ToN-IoT (both $+0.00$). A registered budget sweep (amendment 011) confirms this is a \emph{budget}, not a rule, limit: raising the probe to 128 and 256 flows lifts the empirical-Bernstein gate monotonically --- PortScan $+5.17\to+7.31\to+8.07$ (approaching the fixed 32-probe gate's $+8.70$), and it now resolves the small benefits it missed (UNSW $+0.00\to+0.12\to+0.78$; ToN full-drift $+0.00\to+0.01\to+0.12$). So \emph{the binding constraint is the label budget, not the decision rule}: with more labels the anytime-valid gate recovers small gains too, at a proportional labeling cost (63/124/239 labels per decision). \emph{Empirical coverage and harmful-commit rate.} Two audits back the guarantee. A Monte-Carlo coverage check (nominal $\alpha{=}0.10$, $b{=}64$) confirms both sequences hold under i.i.d.\ and sampling-without-replacement probes (false-commit rate $\le 0.002$), but shows the tighter empirical-Bernstein sequence \emph{exceeds} nominal under strong within-probe autocorrelation ($0.18$ vs $0.10$) --- a concrete instance of the probe-representativeness assumption of \S\ref{sec:riskgates}, and the reason we recommend it with that caveat stated. And the \emph{empirical} per-stream harmful-commit rate makes the per-proposal-vs-lifetime distinction concrete, reported with exact (Clopper--Pearson) intervals: the accuracy-maximizing $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate commits a harmful update on a resolved fraction of its commits (PortScan 12/81, 0.15 [0.08, 0.24]; UNSW 20/63, 0.32 [0.21, 0.45]; ToN 14/56, 0.25 [0.14, 0.38]), whereas the risk-controlled gates commit \emph{zero observed} harmful updates (McNemar 0 commits under zero drift, upper bound 0.12 over 30 streams; empirical-Bernstein 0/20 harmful among its PortScan commits, upper bound 0.17). We state this as ``zero observed with a bounded rate'', not ``eliminates'': 30 streams cannot certify a probability below ${\sim}0.1$--$0.17$, and the per-proposal guarantee is what the confidence sequence formally provides. The empirical-Bernstein gate is thus the recommended anytime-valid procedure (it dominates the Robbins CS), and it sharpens rather than overturns the frontier's central lesson: a genuine per-decision risk guarantee at tens of labels buys safety and the \emph{large} benefits, but resolving small ones requires more labels.

\textbf{A sequential probe, and the risk--label frontier.} The McNemar result showed that the binding constraint is the label budget, not the decision rule. The natural response is to spend labels adaptively: draw 16 probe flows, decide as soon as the evidence resolves the sign, and buy more (up to 64) only while the decision is in doubt. A first heuristic version inspected a fixed-sample interval at each look, which inflates the error rate under optional stopping (external review noted this); we therefore use an \emph{anytime-valid} rule that spends $\alpha/K$ at each of the $K{=}4$ looks (Bonferroni over looks), so the family-wise error is controlled and stopping is valid. This sequential gate spends 58--61 labels per decision. On the normal streams it is deliberately conservative --- it gives up some benefit for the guarantee (PortScan +8.04, $-$1.08 [$-$1.38, $-$0.79] below the fixed 32-label probe; UNSW +1.00; ToN +0.16) --- but that is the point: it sits at the risk-averse end of the frontier, and it is exactly the rule that recovers essentially all of the zero-drift loss above by refusing to commit without evidence. Point comparison ($\varepsilon{=}0$), LCB, exact McNemar and this anytime-valid sequential test span a risk--label frontier: an operator who can pay for more labels, or who fears a bad commit more than a missed gain, moves toward the conservative end, and the paper provides a valid procedure at each point.

\textbf{A registered prediction test: net harm is a property of incumbent health, not of one dataset.} The account makes a prediction that had never been tested, and that could have sunk it: if the value of updating is the incumbent's headroom, then \emph{lowering the drift severity} --- keeping the incumbent healthy --- should turn updating harmful even in regimes we had labelled ``benefit''. We locked this prediction publicly before running it (amendment 006: at $\mathrm{sev}_{\max}{=}0.25$, PortScan's naive gain falls below +1.0 and at least one regime turns net-harmful while the gate holds at $\ge -0.2$). \textbf{The prediction holds} (Table~\ref{tab:harm_generality}), and we state exactly what the intervals support. At mild drift the mean gain of naive always-deploy is \emph{negative in all three benchmarks} --- CICIDS-PortScan $-$0.46 [$-$1.57, 0.42], UNSW-Reconnaissance $-$0.15 [$-$0.21, $-$0.09], ToN-IoT $-$0.65 [$-$1.28, $-$0.16] --- \textbf{statistically resolved in UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT}, and negative but unresolved in CICIDS. The registered criterion for the gate was a $-$0.2-point tolerance, and the gate meets it in all three ($+$0.38, $-$0.09, $-$0.04) while beating naive significantly in each; it is \emph{not} non-negative everywhere --- in UNSW it sits 0.09 points below no-adaptation [$-$0.14, $-$0.04], a resolved but immaterial deficit inside the registered margin. So the honest statement is: harmful updating is not a curiosity of one constructed ToN-IoT cell --- the same sign appears in all three benchmarks once the incumbent is healthy, resolved in two of them --- and the gate reduces the harm to within a pre-specified tolerance everywhere. The harm also broadens at full drift: on the hardened harness, naive updating is net-harmful in two further ToN-IoT regimes (DDoS $-$15.24, Injection $-$2.24), and the gate rescues both (+16.26 and +2.67 above naive). The regime label ``benefit'' or ``harm'' is thus a property of the operating point, not of the dataset --- exactly what the degradation--headroom account says it should be.

\textbf{Honest natural-prevalence probes: a claim we retract.} The initial study reported that the probe ``need not be balanced'' and retained full safety and benefit even at $\pi{=}0.01$. That test drew the probe with a forced minority-class flow (\texttt{max(1,\,$\lfloor b\pi \rceil$)}), so it was really an \emph{attack-enriched} probe containing exactly one attack --- not random inspection at $\pi$. A registered rerun samples the attack count as $\mathrm{Binomial}(b, \pi)$, allowing zero-attack probes, and the picture changes. At $\pi{=}0.10$ (2--3\% of probes contain no attack) the gate is unaffected. At $\pi{=}0.05$ (15--23\% zero-attack) it still holds (ToN +0.91 [0.67, 1.17]), at a small real cost in the benefit regime ($-$0.44 [$-$0.79, $-$0.12] vs the balanced probe). At $\pi{=}0.01$, where \emph{two-thirds} of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all, the harm-regime guarantee dissolves: +0.21 [$-$1.39, 1.15] --- no longer resolvable from zero. The corrected claim is therefore narrower and, we think, more useful: the gate tolerates genuinely random inspection down to roughly $\pi \approx 0.05$; below that, an operator must \emph{enrich} the probe (alert-guided or active sampling, $\approx 1/\pi$ inspections to find one attack) or accept that the commit decision is being made on benign traffic alone.

\textbf{The risk axis: what an exact test costs at 32 labels.} Our recommended rule commits on a point comparison ($\varepsilon{=}0$), which is not a statistical guarantee --- 23\% of its commits have negative realized value. A registered risk-controlled variant makes the guarantee explicit: commit only if an exact one-sided McNemar test on the probe's discordant pairs rejects at $\alpha{=}0.05$ (ties never commit). It is maximally harm-averse and essentially never harmful (ToN +0.00), but at $b{=}32$ it has almost no power --- it commits 0.03 updates per stream in the harm regime --- and it forfeits most of the benefit (PortScan +5.45, $-$3.67 [$-$4.61, $-$2.74] vs the plain gate); doubling the budget to $b{=}64$ recovers part of it (+7.67, still $-$1.45 below). The lesson is not that exact tests are wrong but that \emph{the label budget, not the decision rule, is the binding constraint}: with 32 labels one cannot statistically resolve a one-point accuracy difference, so demanding significance means almost never updating. The point comparison, the LCB and the exact test span the risk axis of the frontier, and an operator with a larger budget --- or a sequential probe that buys extra labels only near a tie --- can move along it.

\textbf{The two-stage health reference, recalibrated.} The review noted that the two-stage gate compared each incumbent's health against the \emph{initial} model's reference, which stops being meaningful after the first commit. Resetting the reference on every commit to the new incumbent's accuracy on the (already labeled) commit half leaves the conclusions unchanged (ToN +0.11 [$-$0.27, 0.45] vs +0.15 [$-$0.15, 0.46] static; PortScan +7.88 vs +7.87), so the reported savings are not an artifact of a stale reference --- but the corrected variant is the one we recommend.

\textbf{Chronologically ordered subsampled replays: the gate's honest boundary, measured properly.} A first Friday replay (seeds 134--163) contained two implementation flaws, caught in external review: a stride mismatch let candidates train on ${\sim}$28k rows the stream never presented as evaluation windows, and its summary averaged plain accuracy (single-class windows) with balanced accuracy. We corrected the runner, registered the correction (amendment 004), and re-ran on fresh seeds with two additional CICIDS streams; a further registered amendment (005) added an \emph{external} chronological stream --- the raw UNSW-NB15 captures, 2.54M flows sorted by flow start time, incumbent trained on the first 30\% of the timeline (Table~\ref{tab:temporal_streams}). The corrected picture is stronger in both directions, and the fourth stream completes it. The phenomenon is real: Tuesday-trained CICIDS incumbents collapse on all three days (BA 53--72\%) and drift-triggered retraining recovers +13.6 to +27.6 points --- deep-benefit territory, confirming the ramps did not manufacture the recovery. The gate's boundary is sharper than the flawed run suggested: on Friday the gate surrenders 6.3 of naive's 13.6 recovered BA points ($-$6.34 [$-$6.83, $-$5.90]) and on Thursday 5.0 of 22.7, while on Wednesday it pays nothing (+0.40 [0.09, 0.74]). \emph{And on the external stream the account's central prediction holds}: the UNSW incumbent stays healthy (82.3\% BA, no collapse; 163/200 two-class windows, ${\sim}$21 triggers/stream), updating helps moderately (+7.33 [6.80, 7.85]), and the gate pays \emph{no premium at all} (+0.16 [$-$0.31, 0.63] vs naive on BA; +0.21 on accuracy) --- the insurance is free precisely where the incumbent's health makes the commit decision genuinely uncertain, which is where the gate's value concentrates. A fifth registered replay attempted to engineer chronological harm --- train on Wednesday (DoS-rich) and replay Thursday (sparse WebAttacks $\to$ Infiltration), hoping for a healthy incumbent facing weak candidates --- but the Wednesday-trained model collapses on Thursday too (48.7\% BA), producing another deep-benefit stream (naive +33.66, gate +18.58; premium $-$15.08 on BA, $-$0.08 [$-$0.30, 0.15] on accuracy --- the same metric-dependent pattern, at its most extreme). A sixth registered replay (amendment 009) targeted the healthy-incumbent corner directly --- a purely \emph{intra-day} CICIDS stream (Tuesday, train on the first 30\% of the timeline, stream the rest) so the incumbent faces the same day it trained on --- but it collapses there too (no-adaptation BA 49.5\%, naive +43.8, gate premium $-$5.66 [$-$8.62, $-$3.85]), because Tuesday's brute-force phase switches attack sub-type over the day (the training window contains only FTP-Patator, the stream only SSH-Patator), so a new variant appears that the incumbent never saw: deep benefit again, not a healthy incumbent. \emph{Chronological net-harm therefore remains unobserved across six replays, and a healthy-incumbent chronological stream remains confined to the external UNSW-NB15 timeline}: every CICIDS incumbent collapses on a later window --- even within a single day --- which is exactly the deep-benefit corner where the account says the gate has least to offer. This is a real limit on external validity for the harm result, and \S7 states it --- though the mild-drift prediction test above shows that the harm is not confined to one dataset once the incumbent is allowed to stay healthy.

These five replays are \emph{chronologically ordered subsampled replays}, not full-capture replays: each processes 200 windows of 256 flows, strided to span the day, so a fraction of the capture is evaluated. The ordering, the labels and the causal availability of every probe are real; the sampling density is not. The premium is \emph{metric-dependent}: on overall accuracy the gate \emph{beats} naive on the same streams (Friday +4.19 [3.85, 4.52]; Thursday +1.23) while losing balanced accuracy on attack-mixed windows. Our first diagnosis --- that the gate inherits the benign-dominated \emph{class composition} of a natural-composition probe --- was put to a registered causal test (amendment 005): a stratified probe (16 per class wherever the past window contains both classes) leaves the premium unchanged (stratified$-$plain: $-$0.05 [$-$0.49, 0.41] on Friday), so the composition account is \emph{not supported} where it is testable --- with the caveat that roughly two-thirds of past windows are single-class, where stratification cannot act. The surviving explanation is probe \emph{staleness}: on a stream whose regime changes within a few windows, a nine-window-old probe --- whatever its composition --- misranks candidates whose relative merits have already shifted, and it systematically misranks them on the minority class first (which is why balanced accuracy pays and overall accuracy does not). We report this as a hypothesis consistent with the data, not a demonstrated mechanism; on streams known to sit deep in the benefit regime, naive triggering is already adequate. This is the one registered check where the gate costs real benefit, reported as such.

\section{Discussion}


\textbf{Detection is not decision.} The empirical core of this paper is that optimizing a drift \emph{detector} optimizes the wrong object. A detector reports that the distribution moved; an adaptive NIDS needs to know whether retraining will help, and these come apart precisely when the deployed model is still good. Our analysis makes this concrete: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so adaptation benefit is essentially deployed-model headroom (\S5.3) --- and the same distributional shift is worth +19 balanced-accuracy points in one regime and $-$4 in another, while the detector's score shows no consistent association with which (within-deployment r $\approx$ +0.06). None of the evaluated distribution-level monitors distinguishes these cases from window statistics alone --- which is why classical and quantum-kernel monitors, and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them, all fail to prevent harm (\S5.4--5.5).


\textbf{Why retraining can hurt, and why it is model-dependent.} In our protocol the candidate is retrained on a balanced, correctly-labeled sample of the current regime --- near-ideal conditions. It still degrades accuracy when the incumbent is strong, because retraining on a narrow current window replaces a broadly-generalized model with one fit to a shifting slice of traffic; the loss of generalization outweighs the gain from recency. Whether this loss actually pushes the outcome below no-adaptation depends on the downstream model's robustness: the SVC-RBF classifier is fragile enough on ToN-IoT to go net-negative, whereas random forest, logistic regression and MLP retain enough generalization to stay non-negative (\S5.8). This is not a contradiction of the regularity --- all four obey the degradation--headroom relationship --- but a refinement: net harm is the fragile-model tail of a general phenomenon. It is precisely why a decision layer that estimates \emph{actual} candidate-vs-incumbent improvement, rather than distributional change, is the right abstraction: the gate is model-agnostic by construction and, empirically, safe across all four downstream models.


\textbf{Why the gate works, and what labels it actually needs.} The validate-before-commit gate succeeds because it estimates the one quantity the mechanism identifies --- the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ --- directly, from a small labeled probe, and acts on it. It does not merely suppress adaptation: in the harmful regime it \emph{beats} no-adaptation by committing the minority of genuinely beneficial updates while rejecting the harmful majority (\S5.6). The label-free alternatives are informative rather than disappointing. Naive disagreement measures how much retraining would change decisions, not whether the changes are correct, and fails outright. The state-of-the-art estimators (ATC, DoC) fail more subtly: they estimate accuracy from the incumbent's own confidences, whose trustworthiness collapses precisely when the incumbent is degraded --- the regime where adaptation is most valuable --- so they buy safety at the cost of much of the benefit (\S5.7). The labeled probe sidesteps this circularity: its validity does not depend on the model being judged. The label bill is layered: retraining labels are unavoidable for any labeled-retraining loop; carving the probe from that same (deduplicated) batch costs nothing extra and buys most of the harm reduction; and full protection --- harm converted to net gain --- costs a fresh probe of $\approx$32 flows per confirmed drift, tens over a stream. In operational NIDS, where analysts already label a small fraction of flagged traffic, both budgets are realistic. Two registered extensions sharpen the picture (\S\ref{sec:v2}): a properly \emph{calibrated} soft ensemble shows that label-free harm avoidance is achievable --- at a modest cost in the decisive regimes and with no ability to decline an update --- and the two-stage gate shows that the same 32 labels, spent \emph{before} training, also gate the pipeline's dominant cost, the candidate itself.


\textbf{Practitioner guidance.} The results invert the usual deployment recipe. Rather than first choosing the most sensitive (or most expensive, e.g. quantum) detector and then wiring it to automatic retraining, a practitioner should (i) determine, on held-out labeled windows, whether their setting is in a benefit or harm regime --- equivalently, whether the deployed model has headroom to lose --- and (ii) gate every adaptation on a small labeled validation probe. Under this recipe the detector becomes a coarse scheduler whose exact identity is secondary; a 114$\times$ quantum monitoring overhead buys nothing once the decision, not the detection, is the bottleneck.


\textbf{Scope: a deployment-decision rule, not a NIDS-specific fix.} Nothing in the gate is specific to network traffic. It instantiates a general champion--challenger discipline \cite{nath2007champion,guissouma2023continuous} for drift-triggered retraining: train a candidate on each trigger, estimate the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ on a small labeled probe from the current distribution, and commit only on estimated improvement. The mechanism it exploits is equally generic --- update value tracks incumbent degradation, which distribution-level monitors do not measure (\S5.3) and confidence-based self-assessment mismeasures exactly when it matters (\S5.7). We therefore expect the pattern to transfer to other streaming-classification deployments where labels are scarce but not absent --- fraud detection, spam and abuse filtering, predictive maintenance --- while stressing that our evidence is confined to network-security benchmarks; validating the rule beyond security is future work.


\textbf{Relation to quantum-kernel drift monitoring.} This work began as an evaluation of quantum-kernel MMD as a superior drift trigger. That hypothesis is not supported: QK-MMD defines regime-dependent operating points on one dataset that do not replicate externally, and it does not change the adapt/no-adapt outcome. We report this honestly and repurpose the quantum monitors as one instrument among several, whose invariance under the gate is part of the evidence that the decision layer, not the similarity measure, is what matters.


\section{Limitations}


Adaptation on our benchmarks retrains on labeled windows; the validate-before-commit gate accordingly assumes access to a small labeled probe at decision time, and we report its size ($\approx$32--64 flows per confirmed drift) as the realistic cost rather than treating labels as free. The strongest positive (benefit) evidence is concentrated in CICIDS2017; the external benchmarks provide the marginal and harmful ends of the spectrum, which is what the argument requires, but broader benefit-regime replication is future work. Quantum kernels are classically simulated, so the reported 114$\times$ monitoring overhead is a simulation figure; the PCA-8 pipeline (chosen for quantum-simulation tractability) attenuates but does not create the harm (\S5.7). The gate is evaluated at fixed per-window severity; we do model label latency (the probe may be up to 20 windows stale; \S5.7) but not asynchronous or partial labeling. A simulator artifact used to bound the word ``deployable'': in the core harness, candidate batches and probes are \emph{sampled from the simulator's mixture at the true current severity} --- information a production system does not have. That caveat is now discharged rather than merely disclosed: the registered causal arm (\S\ref{sec:v2}, Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}) rebuilds both from observed past traffic only, with no access to $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ or the pools, and reproduces every conclusion (the causal gate is statistically indistinguishable from the oracle-probe gate in the harm and marginal regimes). The core numbers retain the convenience; the conclusions do not depend on it. The label-free estimators of \S5.7 are instantiated in their standard form; estimator-specific tuning could narrow the benefit gap we report. At full drift the net-harm result is specific to a fragile downstream classifier (SVC-RBF) and does not appear for more robust classifiers (\S5.8) --- but it is no longer confined to one dataset: with a healthy incumbent (mild or zero drift) naive updating is net-harmful in all three benchmarks (Tables~\ref{tab:harm_generality} and \ref{tab:zero_drift}), and at full drift in three ToN-IoT regimes. The \emph{zero-drift} replacement harm appears for random forest, logistic regression, MLP and full-feature SVC at the default candidate budget, but a size-matched control (\S\ref{sec:v2}) shows it is size-robust only for SVC-RBF: matching the candidate to the incumbent's 2{,}000 flows/class removes it for the three robust learners (within $\pm0.1$ of zero) and deepens it for SVC-RBF. So the healthy-incumbent replacement harm, like the drift-driven harm, is ultimately specific to the fragile SVC-RBF learner; for robust learners the default-budget zero-drift harm was largely a finite-sample (candidate-size) effect. It does, however, hold across replacement-based update generators for SVC-RBF (cumulative, sliding-window and replay; Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}). The zero-drift control also bounds the interpretation in the other direction: much of that harm appears to be the variance cost of replacing a well-fitted model with a finite re-estimate rather than a drift-specific specialization effect, and we do not claim to have separated those two components beyond the decomposition the control provides. We are careful not to over-attribute it to \emph{pure} estimation variance: the standardizer and PCA are fit once on the incumbent's training data and reused for every challenger, so under zero drift incumbent and challenger are not perfectly exchangeable (the incumbent has a representation tuned to its own sample). Deduplication, adding the initial training set, and corrected $n$-scaled regularization (\S\ref{sec:v2}) each fail to remove the harm, which is consistent with a variance account but does not prove it; a control that fits the transformer on a third independent partition and randomizes which of two symmetric models is the incumbent is the decisive test, and we name it as the next experiment. Whether the same floor exists for a robust learner under mild drift --- where the gains are small in any case --- is the natural next test. We therefore frame harm as what happens when a healthy model is replaced on an alarm --- a condition, not a dataset --- while noting that its magnitude depends on how fragile the downstream learner is, and that the corresponding experiment for a robust learner under mild drift (where gains are small in any case) is the natural next test. Several further boundaries should be read into the claims. Evaluation windows are class-balanced (balanced accuracy is prevalence-neutral), and alert volumes and false-positive costs under extreme operational imbalance are untested; the probe need not be balanced, but its tolerance to imbalance is bounded, and we correct an earlier overclaim here: under \emph{genuinely random} inspection (attack count $\sim \mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$, zero-attack probes allowed; \S\ref{sec:v2}) the gate holds down to $\pi \approx 0.05$ but its harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi = 0.01$, where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all. Below that prevalence the operator must enrich the probe (alert-guided or active sampling, ${\approx}1/\pi$ inspections per attack flow); the initial study's stronger claim rested on a probe that was conditioned to contain one attack, and is withdrawn. Label latency is modeled for the probe but not for the candidate's training labels. The probe-corruption experiment flips labels uniformly at random; a candidate-aware adversary that selects which labels to flip is strictly stronger and untested. The drift trajectories of the core study are gradual mixing ramps between real regime pools --- covariate/regime drift, which is what our streams demonstrably contain; we do not isolate a $p(y\,|\,x)$ change, and use ``drift'' in that bounded sense. Six real chronological replays (\S\ref{sec:v2}) add abrupt real drift: every CICIDS stream --- four cross-day plus an intra-day Tuesday split --- lands in deep-benefit territory (each incumbent collapses on a later window, even within one day, as a new attack sub-type appears), while the external UNSW-NB15 timeline is the only one that keeps its incumbent healthy and shows the gate costing nothing there. What no chronological stream has yet produced is \emph{net-harm} --- a regime where always-deploy loses to never-adapt --- so the harmful-update finding, the paper's most novel, remains demonstrated only on the controlled ToN-IoT construction; a chronological capture exhibiting it is the outstanding external test. Recurrent and $p(y\,|\,x)$-isolated drift remain untested. Seeds quantify within-regime sampling variability, not new environments: the external units are three datasets and seven regimes, and confidence intervals should be read at that granularity (Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness} reports the leave-one-out behaviour). Two harness-level caveats: policy arms matched by seed draw separate pseudo-random stream realizations (identical in distribution, not in realization), so seed-matched contrasts are between equivalent, not identical, streams; and samples are drawn with replacement from shared per-class pools rather than identifier-disjoint partitions (expected probe--training collisions below one flow per probe, \S4). Both caveats apply to the initial study only: the registered replication of \S\ref{sec:v2} re-runs the confirmatory design on a common-stream, partition-disjoint harness with fresh seeds and reproduces every criterion. Post-registration extensions are descriptive robustness checks without multiplicity correction; confirmatory claims rest solely on the registered criteria. The confidence-sequence gates carry an explicit but \emph{bounded} guarantee (\S\ref{sec:riskgates}): they control the per-proposal probability of falsely establishing candidate superiority \emph{on the probe distribution}, which equals a bound on future-deployment harm only when the probe is representative --- an assumption our own stale-probe and coverage results show can fail (the empirical-Bernstein sequence exceeds nominal $\alpha$ under strong within-probe autocorrelation). The guarantee is also per proposal, not stream-wide; a lifetime bound needs alpha-spending across proposals or a deployment-long e-process, which we do not implement (we report the empirical per-stream harmful-commit rate instead). Relatedly, the with-replacement pool sampling that let a candidate-training row recur in a future evaluation window is now removed for the causal arm by an optional without-replacement, value-deduplicated stream mode (collisions ${\to}{\approx}0$; \S\ref{sec:v2}), under which the harm-regime result survives; the core-harness tables retain the original sampling and its small, symmetric collision count. Finally, the harmful baseline is the standard full-replacement retraining loop; the standard label-free mitigation --- retrain-current-plus-replay --- does not rescue it (it remains net-harmful in the harm regime and loses benefit elsewhere, while the gate restores both on top of it; \S5.7), and a registered sweep of richer update generators --- calibrated ensemble, sliding window, cumulative all-data retraining and replay --- shows the zero-drift replacement harm in all of them (the cumulative generator worst of all; Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}), so it is not an artifact of full replacement; incremental or weighted online learners with no discrete replacement step remain untested, as do broader deployment settings beyond the four classifiers and gate regimes tested.


\section{Conclusion}


Adaptive network intrusion detection is usually framed as a detection problem: monitor for drift, retrain and redeploy when it fires. We showed that this framing is incomplete and sometimes harmful --- across three benchmarks the value of a drift-triggered update ranges from strongly beneficial to net-negative, tracking deployed-model degradation rather than distributional change, and invisible to the detector. A registered zero-drift control reframes the point as one of \emph{candidate governance}: when updates are forced on a healthy model --- as a false alarm or a scheduled retrain would --- replacing it is net-harmful in all three benchmarks \emph{even with no drift at all}. A well-specified detector avoids this by not firing (in our zero-drift runs the real KS-max trigger fires 0.1 times per stream), so the monitor is not powerless; but any proposal that does reach the deployment step --- however it was generated --- is a hypothesis, and the reliable safeguard is to test it against the incumbent before committing. The commit gate substantially reduces this replacement harm (though, honestly, it does not eliminate it under zero drift), which no amount of detector sensitivity can do once a proposal exists. Consequently, neither more expressive detectors (including quantum kernels) nor simple confirmation/cooldown policies prevent harmful updates. Recast as a cost-aware decision, the problem admits a simple mitigation at the point the standard loop leaves ungoverned: a validate-before-commit gate that lets the loop retrain its candidate but \emph{deploys} it only if a small labeled probe confirms an improvement --- and, in its two-stage form, skips even the retraining when the incumbent shows no measurable degradation. In the controlled benefit, marginal and harm regimes the gate preserves average benefit, avoids net harm, and significantly outperforms both naive triggering and never adapting --- for a classical and a quantum detector alike --- at a decision cost of tens of labeled flows per confirmed drift; on fast chronological deep-benefit streams, a stale natural-composition probe imposes a real, metric-dependent insurance premium, which we measure, explain, and bound. \emph{When not to deploy a retrained candidate} is as important as detecting drift, and a few labels, spent on the decision rather than the detector, are enough to know the difference where the incumbent's health is genuinely in question.
