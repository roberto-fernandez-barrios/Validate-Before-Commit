\begin{abstract}
Adaptive intrusion detectors retrain under distribution drift, delegating the update decision to a drift detector. We show drift magnitude is not a sufficient commit signal: under a fixed update generator, whether an update helps tracks how degraded the incumbent already is. A registered prediction test confirms the sharpest consequence --- where the incumbent stays healthy, always-deploy turns negative in all three benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT; statistically resolved in two) --- and a zero-drift control shows that replacing a healthy model can hurt with no drift at all, for the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline. This recasts updating as \emph{candidate governance}: an alarm proposes a challenger, it does not justify deploying one. A role-randomized control traces the harm to preprocessing ownership, and per-model preprocessing removes it. We therefore evaluate \emph{validate-before-commit} gates, which deploy a candidate only when a small labeled probe confirms it beats the incumbent, and map an accuracy--labels--risk frontier spanning a point rule, a no-additional-label rule that rejects ties, and VBC-SG, a stratified sequential gate with commit/reject/defer and a deployment-long risk budget. That deployment-long budget proves non-vacuous within the evaluated balanced-probe adjudication budget: at a configured probe cap of 512, corresponding to about 578 adjudicated probe labels per proposal after deferrals, a lifetime-budgeted pooled gate recovers 93\% of always-deploying's benefit under an approximate pooled analysis (81\% for the fully stratified VBC-SG carrying the formal per-class guarantee), while committing nothing under zero drift. Thirteen chronologically ordered replays bound external validity --- net harm never appears there, our principal limit --- yet point and strict gating \emph{outperform} always-deploying on the two UNSW timelines that keep the incumbent healthy, while a third healthy replay remains an unresolved counterexample. Results reproduce from a public artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


Machine-learning network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) are trained on historical traffic \cite{nguyen2023tsids}, yet the traffic they monitor evolves: new services, shifting user behaviour, reconfigured infrastructure and novel attacks all move the input distribution away from the training distribution, a phenomenon known as concept drift \cite{shyaa2024evolving,wahab2022intrusion,roshan2018adaptive}. As the distribution shifts, a frozen classifier loses accuracy, so operational NIDS periodically \textbf{retrain} their downstream classifier on recent traffic \cite{wahab2022intrusion,camarda2025managing,constantinides2019novel}. Retraining is not free --- it consumes labeled data and compute and destabilizes a deployed model --- so the central operational question is \emph{when} to retrain.


The dominant answer frames this as a \textbf{drift-detection} problem: run a statistical monitor that compares recent traffic to a reference window, and trigger retraining when the monitor signals a change \cite{gama2004learning,rabanser2019failing,lukats2024benchmark}. A large literature accordingly competes to build more sensitive monitors, from univariate change detectors to multivariate two-sample tests such as maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) and energy distance \cite{gretton2012kernel,szekely2013energy}, and more recently to quantum-kernel similarity measures whose feature-map geometry may respond differently to distributional change \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,kaissar2026enhancing}. The implicit assumption is that a better change detector yields a better retraining policy.


This paper challenges that assumption on empirical grounds. Across three public benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT) and multiple attack regimes we find the premise beneath the whole detection-centric framing is false: \textbf{deploying every drift-triggered update is not always beneficial.} The value of an update ranges from strongly positive to negative --- for a fragile deployed model in a ToN-IoT scanning regime, \emph{no adaptation} outperforms every triggered strategy we test. Whether an update helps tracks not how much the distribution moved but how much the deployed model has already degraded: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so under a fixed candidate generator the benefit of an update is largely the deployed model's headroom to that level (r $\approx$ $-$0.8 to $-$0.9 across four downstream classifiers, with a coupling-aware analysis in Supplementary \S S1.1 and a per-decision hierarchical model in \S\ref{sec:v2}). A drift monitor, which measures distributional change, does not track this quantity --- within a deployment its score is uninformative about the gain, which is precisely why sensitive detectors --- and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them --- fail to prevent harmful updates. This holds even when the detector is a quantum-kernel MMD monitor rather than a classical two-sample test: improving the monitor does not, by itself, improve the update decision.


We therefore argue that adaptive model updating should be recast from a \emph{detection} problem to a cost-aware \emph{decision} --- in fact two decisions: whether to train a candidate at all, and, once a candidate exists, whether to \emph{deploy} it. This paper is chiefly about the second decision, where the detection-centric loop silently answers "always". We evaluate a \textbf{validate-before-commit} gate that, on each triggered drift, retrains a candidate model but deploys it only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe drawn from current traffic (in the core harness a pool sample at the current mixture --- an idealization; the observed-data arm of \S\ref{sec:v2} uses only past observed windows); a two-stage variant (\S\ref{sec:v2}) additionally spends a disjoint half of the same label budget \emph{before} training, to skip candidates the incumbent's health does not warrant. Across benefit, mixed and harmful regimes, the gate (pre-specified criteria, 30 seeds) preserves the gains of updating where it helps, avoids harm where it does not, significantly outperforms naive in the harm regime, and exceeds never-adapting across the confirmatory cells. In the controlled balanced-probe confirmatory setting the commit decision is label-efficient: a 32-label point gate is sufficient to convert average harm into average benefit (\S\ref{sec:v2}), while formal risk control, severe imbalance and stratified deployment-long guarantees require larger or differently acquired samples. The gate's robustness envelope --- label staleness to 20 windows, up to 40\% corrupted validation labels, two drift detectors, four downstream classifiers --- is established in \S\ref{sec:v2}. To be explicit about total cost: candidates themselves are retrained on labeled data (1{,}024 flows per trigger in our loop, spent whether or not the gate commits), so the probe is the incremental cost of the \emph{decision}, not of the pipeline --- we account for both throughout (\S\ref{sec:v2}). Label-free alternatives --- from naive disagreement to state-of-the-art accuracy estimators (ATC, DoC) --- either fail outright or sacrifice much of the benefit, showing that, in this controlled balanced-probe setting, target labels spent on the decision rather than the detector are what buys harm avoidance without giving up the gains.


Our contributions are four. \textbf{(1) A candidate-governance formulation} of adaptive model updating: an alarm --- or a schedule, or a false alarm --- \emph{proposes} a challenger; deployment is a separate decision. A registered prediction test shows always-deploying turns negative in all three benchmarks once the incumbent stays healthy (resolved in two, for the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline), and a role-randomized A/B control traces the zero-drift harm to preprocessing ownership, localized to feature scaling and removed by per-model preprocessing (\S\ref{sec:risk}). \textbf{(2) A conditional degradation--headroom account}: under fixed candidate generators, recent incumbent health predicts update value more consistently than the evaluated scalar drift scores --- supported per-decision by a hierarchical per-trigger model, detector-invariant, and delimited by pre-specified negatives on confirmation/cooldown policies, replay, canonical monitors and adaptive-update rules. \textbf{(3) VBC-SG}, a stratified sequential commit gate with commit/reject/defer actions, adaptive label acquisition and a deployment-long risk budget, with guarantees stated at the strength the sampling supports and a registered budget frontier showing that guarantee to become non-vacuous within the evaluated balanced-probe adjudication budget (its inspected-flow acquisition cost under operational class imbalance is not measured). \textbf{(4) A leakage-free observed-data arm, an operational simulation, and an explicit policy frontier}: an observed-data arm free of simulator-oracle information and of train/future leakage (zero collisions), an operational acquisition-yield and delay simulation that prices attack-label discovery in inspected flows rather than adjudicated labels, thirteen chronological replays reported honestly (no chronological net harm; point and strict gates outperform always-deploying on the two healthy UNSW timelines, VBC-SG on one of them, one healthy replay an unresolved counterexample), and an accuracy--labels--risk frontier on which no policy dominates. The remainder of the paper reviews related work (\S2), formalizes the decision and the gate (\S3), details the protocol (\S4), reports results (\S5), and discusses implications and limits (\S6--\S8).


\section{Related work}


\textbf{Concept drift and adaptive intrusion detection.} Concept drift degrades ML-based NIDS, and a body of work adapts models online or on a schedule to compensate \cite{gama2014survey,lu2019learning,shyaa2024evolving}. Most such systems assume adaptation is beneficial and focus on \emph{how} to adapt (incremental learning, windowing, ensembles) \cite{constantinides2019novel,roshan2018adaptive,shyaa2023enhanced}. We instead study \emph{whether and when} adaptation helps, and show it can be net-harmful --- connecting to, but distinct from, work on negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting in continual learning \cite{mccloskey1989catastrophic,kirkpatrick2017overcoming,zhang2023survey}, which we observe operationally in a security setting.


\textbf{Drift detection and two-sample monitors.} Drift is commonly detected with change detectors \cite{bifet2007learning,gama2004learning,baena2006early,raab2020reactive}, statistical-test ensembles over batch streams \cite{komorniczak2022ensemble}, or multivariate two-sample tests --- MMD, energy distance, Kolmogorov--Smirnov and Jensen--Shannon divergence --- used as retraining or alarm triggers \cite{gretton2012kernel,rizzo2016energy,lin1991divergence,darling1957ks}; how drift manifests (type, locality, severity) itself varies widely across streams \cite{aguiar2024locality}. Our classical baselines are drawn from this family. A complementary family of \emph{performance-aware} detectors monitors the model's error signal directly, presupposing continuous labeled feedback; Bayram et al. \cite{bayram2022degradation} survey them and formalize the distinction between distributional change and model degradation that our degradation--headroom analysis (Supplementary \S S1.1) quantifies empirically. We use distribution-level monitors but argue that improving them does not improve the retraining decision, because they are blind to model degradation --- and our gate concentrates the label budget at the update decision instead of spending it on continuous performance monitoring.


\textbf{Quantum kernels for drift monitoring.} Quantum-kernel methods embed data via parameterized quantum feature maps and have been proposed for classification and similarity estimation \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,huang2021power}, including exploratory use in cybersecurity \cite{gouveia2020towards,nicesio2023quantum,bellante2025evaluating}. In a companion study \cite{fernandez_paper1} we found that fidelity-based quantum kernels can improve out-of-distribution robustness under distribution shift, but that the operative property is the kernel's geometry rather than its quantumness --- which motivated evaluating quantum-kernel MMD as a drift monitor here. The present paper reaches the analogous conclusion one level up: for the \emph{update decision}, changing the detector family --- quantum or classical --- did not change the outcome in any regime we evaluated; quantum monitors act as one instrument among several and do not change the adapt/no-adapt outcome --- consistent with mounting evidence that quantum-kernel advantage claims require careful classical baselining and face fundamental concentration limits \cite{schnabel2025quantum,thanasilp2024concentration}.


\textbf{Drift, rejection and evaluation discipline in ML security.} A parallel security literature asks when a deployed classifier should no longer be trusted. Transcend \cite{jordaney2017transcend} and its revision Transcendent \cite{barbero2022transcendent} use conformal evaluation to reject individual predictions whose low credibility signals model aging; CADE \cite{yang2021cade} detects and explains drifting samples via contrastive representations; INSOMNIA \cite{andresini2021insomnia} updates a NIDS continuously with semi-supervision as drift accrues. These systems abstain on individual samples or adapt continuously; none evaluates whether a specific retrained candidate should replace the incumbent. Our gate acts at exactly that decision: it compares candidate against incumbent on current traffic and can conclude that the correct action is \emph{not to update at all} --- the case Supplementary \S S1.1 shows is real. TESSERACT \cite{pendlebury2019tesseract} and the ``dos and don'ts'' of Arp et al. \cite{arp2022dodonts} document how temporal and experimental bias inflate security-ML results; our pre-specified protocol with temporally ordered progressive windows and paired within-run baselines (\S4) follows that discipline.


\textbf{When to retrain: cost-aware and validation-gated updates.} Beyond detection, some work weighs the cost of adaptation or validates a candidate before deployment, e.g. in MLOps model-update pipelines and shadow/champion--challenger evaluation \cite{nath2007champion,sculley2015hidden,kreuzberger2023mlops,guissouma2023continuous}; that a well-intentioned update can be net-harmful is documented beyond security, e.g.\ in human--AI teams, where updates that raise average accuracy can still hurt end-to-end performance \cite{bansal2019updates}. Our gate instantiates this idea for streaming NIDS and, crucially, quantifies its label cost and demonstrates its necessity --- that detection and simple policies are insufficient without it. Its reliance on a small labeled probe connects to active and label-efficient learning for data streams \cite{cacciarelli2024active,krawczyk2019adaptive,liu2021comprehensive}, where labels are scarce and must be spent where they most affect the decision. A complementary line estimates a model's accuracy under distribution shift without target labels \cite{garg2022atc,guillory2021doc,deng2021labels,baek2022agreement,jiang2022disagreement,rosenfeld2023dis2}; rather than argue past it, we instantiate its two standard estimators (ATC, DoC) as gates in our own harness and find they trade benefit for safety (Supplementary \S S1.5).


\section{Method}


\subsection{Adaptive readaptation as a sequential decision}


We consider a deployed binary classifier $h_0$ (benign vs. attack) monitoring a stream of traffic windows $W_1, W_2, \dots$ Each window $W_t$ is a set of flows with (at evaluation time) ground-truth labels; the deployed model degrades as the traffic distribution drifts. A drift monitor $D$ produces a score $s_t = D(W_t)$ measuring divergence of $W_t$ from a reference. The standard adaptive-NIDS loop is: raise an alarm when $s_t$ exceeds a threshold, and upon a confirmed alarm \textbf{retrain} the classifier on recent data. We make explicit that this loop conflates two questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{detection:} \emph{has the distribution changed?} --- answered by $D$;
  \item \textbf{decision:} \emph{will replacing $h$ with a model retrained on the current window improve accuracy?} --- which $D$ does not answer.
\end{itemize}


Let $h$ be the incumbent model and $h'$ a candidate retrained on the current regime. Adaptation is beneficial at $t$ iff $\mathrm{acc}(h', W_t) > \mathrm{acc}(h, W_t)$. Because $D$ measures distributional change rather than the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h',\cdot)-\mathrm{acc}(h,\cdot)$, a detector-only policy commits every confirmed change, including changes for which retraining does not help. Our method (\S3.4) inserts a decision step that estimates this sign before committing.


\subsection{Drift monitors}


We use two-sample drift monitors comparing a reference window (from the pre-drift regime) to the current window. Classical baselines: \textbf{Energy distance}, \textbf{RBF-MMD}, per-feature \textbf{Kolmogorov--Smirnov} (max reduction) and histogram \textbf{Jensen--Shannon} divergence. Quantum monitors: \textbf{quantum-kernel MMD} with \textbf{ZZ} and \textbf{Pauli-XZ} feature maps ($q_{\text{reps}}=1$, angle encoding with standardized \texttt{atan} scaling), the MMD being computed in the quantum-kernel-induced RKHS. All monitors expose a scalar score; thresholds are calibrated per detector on held-out calibration windows at a fixed quantile (0.95). The paper's claims are detector-agnostic; the quantum monitors are included as instruments and to test whether a more expressive kernel changes the decision (it does not, Supplementary \S S1.2).


\subsection{Progressive-drift readaptation protocol}


Each stream has 100 post-drift windows. Window $t$ is a class-balanced sample in which each class is a mixture of the reference and current pools with mixing weight $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ ("severity"), ramped linearly from 0 to 1 over the first 80 windows and held at 1 thereafter, so drift arrives gradually. On a committed adaptation, the candidate is trained on a fresh balanced labeled sample from the current regime, and the detector reference is reset to the adapted regime. The trigger policy (alarm confirmation and cooldown) is held fixed across all gate conditions, so the decision gate is the only \emph{designed} difference between arms. On the confirmatory harness (v2) every arm additionally processes \emph{bit-identical} streams, so contrasts are exactly paired; only the initial exploratory study drew a separate pseudo-random realization per arm (identical in distribution, not in realization; \S4).


\subsection{The validate-before-commit gate}


Our contribution is a decision step interposed between the trigger and the model swap: a \emph{deployment} (commit) gate, not a retraining gate. On each confirmed trigger the loop retrains a candidate $h'$ as usual --- consuming its training labels and compute regardless of the outcome --- and the gate then decides only whether $h'$ replaces the incumbent $h$; otherwise we keep $h$ (and keep the detector reference, so a later window can re-propose once an update becomes worthwhile). \S\ref{sec:v2} additionally evaluates a two-stage variant that moves the same probe \emph{before} candidate training, gating the training decision itself. We evaluate three commit gates:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{none} (naive):} always commit --- the standard loop.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{labeled\_probe}$(b,\varepsilon)$:} draw a small balanced labeled probe $P$ of $b$ flows from the current window; commit iff $\mathrm{BA}(h',P) \ge \mathrm{BA}(h,P) + \varepsilon$. The probe is the method's incremental labeling cost; we report $b\in\{32,64\}$, $\varepsilon=0$.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{unsup\_disagree}$(\tau)$:} commit iff the fraction of flows on which $h$ and $h'$ disagree on the \emph{unlabeled} current window is $\ge \tau$ (a zero-label proxy for "adaptation would change decisions"); we report $\tau=0.15$.
\end{itemize}

Two variants are evaluated and we state both, because they differ in exactly the information they are allowed to use. Algorithm 1A is the \emph{core simulation} gate: the environment supplies candidate batches and probes from its class pools at the current mixture severity --- convenient, and the source of the paper's main tables, but an oracle a deployment lacks. Algorithm 1B is the \emph{observed-data} gate: every object it touches --- the candidate's training rows, the probe, and even the detector's post-commit reference --- comes from windows the system has actually seen, and nothing reads $\mathrm{sev}(t)$. \S\ref{sec:v2} reports 1B as a registered replication of 1A.

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm 1A: Gated update, core simulation harness (one stream)
  h <- h0 ; D <- fit_reference(pools, sev=0) ; alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- 0
  for t = 1..T:
      W_t <- environment.stream[t]              # pre-generated, balanced, labeled
      record BA(h, W_t)
      s_t <- D.score(W_t) ; alarms.append(s_t > threshold)
      trigger <- policy(alarms) and cooldown == 0
      if trigger:
          h' <- train(pools.train, sev(t))      # candidate: current mixture (oracle)
          P   <- sample(pools.probe, sev(t))    # probe: balanced, b flows (oracle)
          commit <- [ BA(h',P) >= BA(h,P) + eps ]                  # Sec 3.4
          if commit:
              h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(pools, sev(t))          # deploy + reset
          alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- C                             # applied either way
      else: cooldown <- max(0, cooldown - 1)
  return {BA(h, W_t)}_t

Algorithm 1B: Gated update, observed-data harness (no sev(t), no pools)
  ... identical control flow, but at a trigger at window t:
      h' <- train(rows of the last 8 OBSERVED windows)             # candidate
      P  <- 32 rows of the OBSERVED window t-9, natural composition,
            excluding by row identity any row used to train h'     # probe
      commit <- [ acc(h',P) >= acc(h,P) + eps ]
      if commit: h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(last 8 OBSERVED windows),
                 threshold <- quantile_0.95(scores of preceding OBSERVED windows)
\end{verbatim}


The \texttt{labeled\_probe} gate directly targets the quantity \S3.1 identifies (the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$), estimated from $b$ labels; the \texttt{unsup\_disagree} gate tests whether that estimate can be obtained without labels.


\textbf{A cost-sensitive reading: two decisions, two thresholds.} The update loop makes two sequential decisions with different cost structures, and it matters not to conflate them. Let each error cost $\lambda_e$ per flow, let deploying an update cost $\lambda_U$ (deployment risk, churn), let training a candidate cost $\lambda_C$ (compute plus its $2m$ training labels at $\lambda_L$ each), and let probe labels cost $\lambda_L$ each. \emph{At commit time the candidate already exists}: $\lambda_C$ and the probe are sunk, and the only decision-relevant cost of committing is $\lambda_U$. Over a horizon of $N$ post-decision flows, the myopic commit rule is therefore
$$\mathrm{commit} \iff \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h',P) - \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h,P) \ge \varepsilon^{\ast}, \qquad \varepsilon^{\ast} = \frac{\lambda_U}{\lambda_e N},$$
so the margin $\varepsilon$ is not a heuristic knob but the \emph{deployment} cost expressed in accuracy points over the remaining horizon --- $\lambda_C$ does not belong in $\varepsilon^{\ast}$, because rejecting the candidate cannot recover it. Training costs enter one decision earlier: whether to build a candidate at all, which pays off only if the \emph{expected} committed gain exceeds $(\lambda_C + \lambda_U)/(\lambda_e N)$. The two-stage gate of \S\ref{sec:v2} instantiates exactly this structure, spending the probe on the incumbent \emph{before} training and skipping candidate construction when the incumbent shows no measurable degradation --- saving all candidate labels and compute at those triggers. The probe budget $b$ trades $\lambda_L b$ against estimator variance, and a risk-averse operator replaces the point estimate with a lower confidence bound (the LCB variant of \S\ref{sec:v2}). The margin sweep of Supplementary \S S1.5 is the empirical operating characteristic of the commit rule: raising $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed updates by ${\sim}30\%$ at equal downstream gain, which is exactly the behaviour an operator with non-trivial $\lambda_U/\lambda_e$ should choose. Asymmetric error costs ($\lambda_{FN} \ne \lambda_{FP}$) substitute a weighted accuracy for BA in the same rule.


\subsection{Risk-controlled commit gates and the scope of their guarantee}
\label{sec:riskgates}

The point gate above ($\varepsilon{=}0$) \emph{maximizes the probe point-estimate} --- not future accuracy, as the strict-comparison baseline below shows a more conservative rule can win downstream: it commits on any observed probe advantage and carries no formal error control (about a quarter of its commits have negative realized value; \S\ref{sec:v2}). We separately study \emph{risk-controlled} gates that commit only on statistical evidence. Let the probe be $b$ flows drawn from a distribution $\mathcal P$, and for each flow let
$$d_i = \mathbf 1[h'\text{ correct on }i] - \mathbf 1[h\text{ correct on }i]\in\{-1,0,+1\}.$$
Three rules: (i) \textbf{exact McNemar} --- commit iff a one-sided exact test on the discordant pairs rejects $H_0:\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0$ at level $\alpha$ (ties never commit); (ii) \textbf{Robbins CS} --- a normal-mixture (sub-Gaussian, $\sigma^2\le1$) lower confidence sequence \cite{howard2021timeuniform} on $\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]$, commit at the first look whose lower bound exceeds $0$; (iii) \textbf{empirical-Bernstein CS} --- a predictable-plug-in betting confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} using the empirical variance of $d_i$ (far tighter, since the $d_i$ are mostly ties). The CS gates inspect in blocks (16 flows) up to a budget $b$; if the lower bound never clears $0$ they do \emph{not} commit (the risk-averse default), so at the maximum budget an undecided proposal is rejected. Because the probe is drawn with \emph{fixed class quotas} ($b/2$ per class) rather than i.i.d.\ from one distribution, the pooled tests above match the sampling only approximately; \emph{stratified} variants close that gap by lower-bounding each class's mean correctness difference at $\alpha/2$ (Bonferroni over the two classes) and committing iff $\tfrac12(L_{\mathrm{ben}}+L_{\mathrm{att}})>0$, which bounds the false-superiority probability for the \emph{balanced-accuracy} difference under the stratified design actually used. We evaluate two: a \emph{fixed-sample} version (per-class one-sided normal LCB --- an approximation at $16$ samples per class, carrying no sequential validity) and the \emph{anytime-valid} version, in which each class's bound is an empirical-Bernstein confidence sequence inspected at the same sequential looks as the pooled gate; only the latter supports the anytime-valid claim, and the text is explicit about which variant each number comes from. \textbf{Levels used.} The confidence sequences run at $\alpha=0.10$; the exact-McNemar gate runs at its registered $\alpha=0.05$ (a stricter level, so its zero-drift behaviour is if anything \emph{more} conservative than the $0.10$ CS gates, not less). To rule out that the McNemar gate's near-total abstention is an artifact of the stricter level, we also run it at $\alpha=0.10$ (\S\ref{sec:risk}): the zero-drift conclusion is unchanged (still $\approx 0$ commits). The effective per-arm $\alpha$ is recorded in the run manifest.

\textbf{VBC-SG: the assembled policy.} The rules above form a family, and the paper's algorithmic contribution is that family together with the frontier it spans --- \emph{not} a claim that any one member dominates. We name its maximally-guaranteed member, the \emph{Validate-Before-Commit Sequential Gate}, because it is the configuration that carries every layer of control at once; \S\ref{sec:risk} then shows what each layer costs, and that a \emph{less} stratified sibling and even the no-additional-label strict rule are the better buy at some operating points. VBC-SG assembles the pieces as follows: per-class empirical-Bernstein confidence sequences (the stratified anytime-valid bounds above) drive a three-action decision --- \textsc{commit} when the balanced-accuracy lower bound clears zero, \textsc{reject} when the upper bound falls below zero (futility), and \textsc{defer} otherwise: the candidate is retained and evidence continues at subsequent windows, up to a deferral cap, rejecting at the cap. Deferral raises a question the first version of this gate left implicit: once the stream drifts \emph{between} windows, what does a sequence that keeps acquiring labels at later windows estimate? We make the answer explicit through three registered continuation modes. \emph{Cohort-sim} (fixed-target): additional labels are resampled from the proposal-time target distribution available to the simulator (the mixture the proposal was raised on), so the sequence estimates one fixed mean and the per-proposal guarantee is the clean fixed-target one. Cohort-sim does not model retention and delayed adjudication of a literal production cohort, so this acquisition scheme is not claimed to be directly deployable. \emph{Refresh}: each deferral window opens a \emph{fresh} evidence set tested at $\alpha_j/(1+D)$ (Bonferroni over the at most $1+D$ sets a proposal with deferral cap $D$ may open), so observations from temporally different targets are never mixed. \emph{Accumulate} (the behaviour of the earlier amendments, retained as an ablation): the same sequences continue on labels drawn at each current window; by Proposition~1 below this mode still bounds the probability of a false commit, but only under the \emph{weak conditional null} that the challenger is at no time conditionally better on the probe stream --- rejecting that null establishes that the challenger was conditionally better at \emph{some} window, not necessarily at commit time, which is why cohort and refresh are the interpretable variants under drift. A deployment-long risk budget allocates each proposal's level $\alpha_j$ by a declared spending schedule (Bonferroni $\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}/M$, or the $p$-series $\alpha_j = 6\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}/(\pi^2 j^2)$, which needs no horizon $M$); the effective $\alpha_j$ of every proposal is logged. Four properties, stated at the strength we can support: (i) \emph{per-proposal}: the probability of falsely establishing positive expected balanced-accuracy difference on the probe distribution is at most $\alpha_j$, uniformly over the sampling schedule --- under within-class i.i.d.\ sampling from the proposal's target for the cohort/refresh modes, and under the weak conditional null of Proposition~1 for the accumulate mode; (ii) \emph{deployment-long}: by the union bound over the spending schedule, the probability of \emph{ever} making such a false commit over the deployment is at most $\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}$, under the same assumptions; (iii) \emph{label cost}: labels per proposal are bounded by the per-window budget times $(1 + D)$, and the observed cost concentrates far below the bound because most proposals resolve early; (iv) \emph{transfer}: none of (i)--(iii) bounds future deployed accuracy unless the probe is representative of the evaluation horizon --- the stale-probe and dependence results quantify exactly where that premise fails. We prove nothing stronger, and the empirical sections report the price of each guarantee.

\textbf{Proposition 1 (conditional validity of the commit e-process).} Map each per-flow correctness difference to $x_i = (d_i+1)/2 \in [0,1]$ and let the gate's wealth process be $W_n = \exp\!\big(\sum_{i\le n} \lambda_i (x_i - \tfrac12) - \sum_{i\le n} \psi_E(\lambda_i)\,(x_i - \hat\mu_{i-1})^2\big)$, where $\psi_E(\lambda) = -\log(1-\lambda)-\lambda$ and the betting fractions $\lambda_i \in [0, \tfrac34]$ and plug-in means $\hat\mu_{i-1}$ are predictable (computed from observations before $i$; this is exactly the empirical-Bernstein sequence implemented in the artifact, whose commit condition $\mathrm{LCB}_n > 0$ is algebraically the event $W_n > 1/\alpha$). If $\mathbb{E}[d_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] \le 0$ for every $i$ --- the weak null; no stationarity or identical distribution is assumed --- then $W_n$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with $W_0 = 1$, and Ville's inequality gives $\Pr(\exists n : W_n > 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$: the probability of \emph{ever} committing under the weak null is at most $\alpha$. The proof (Supplementary \S S4) is the standard testing-by-betting argument \cite{waudbysmith2023betting,howard2021timeuniform} via Fan's inequality $e^{\lambda y - \psi_E(\lambda) y^2} \le 1 + \lambda y$ for $y \ge -1$; we claim no novelty, only the correspondence to this implementation, which a registered null simulation with time-varying conditional means and variances verifies empirically.

\textbf{What is and is not guaranteed.} The confidence-sequence gates satisfy, for a single proposal and \emph{under the probe distribution} $\mathcal P$,
$$\Pr\!\big(\exists n\le b:\ \text{LCB}_n>0 \ \big|\ \mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0\big)\ \le\ \alpha ,$$
i.e.\ they control the probability of \emph{falsely establishing positive expected paired correctness on the probe distribution}, uniformly over the (data-dependent) stopping time. This is \emph{not} the same as bounding the probability that the committed candidate lowers future balanced accuracy: the two coincide only when the probe is representative of the windows the candidate will be evaluated on, and the paper documents where that fails --- a probe up to nine windows stale, a fast-evolving stream, and accuracy vs balanced accuracy ranking candidates differently on class-imbalanced windows (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}). We therefore claim a bound on \emph{false probe-superiority}, not on future deployment regret, and treat probe representativeness as a separate, empirically-scoped assumption. Without a spending schedule the guarantee is also \emph{per proposal}: the process restarts for each challenger, so over a stream with $M$ committed-or-tested proposals the elementary union bound on ever making a false commit is $M\alpha$; a lifetime bound requires alpha-spending across proposals or a single deployment-long e-process --- exactly what the VBC-SG risk budget above supplies (property (ii)). The plain per-proposal gates carry no such budget, and \S\ref{sec:risk} reports both the empirical per-stream harmful-commit rate and the label price of the lifetime schedules.

\subsection{Metrics}


Per window we record balanced accuracy (BA) and attack-class F1; per stream we average over the 100 windows, and per condition over seeds. We report \textbf{gain vs. no-adaptation} (the frozen $h_0$ baseline), \textbf{committed adaptations} and \textbf{labels used}. To quantify the headroom of the decision we define, per stream and detector, a \textbf{policy oracle} that retrospectively selects, per stream, the better of the two complete policies (never adapt vs.\ detector-triggered), $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}=\mathbb{E}_{\text{seed}}\max(\mathrm{BA}_{\text{no-adapt}},\mathrm{BA}_{\text{detector}})$ --- a conservative lower bound on the value of perfect per-trigger decisions, since choosing per trigger can only do better --- and the \textbf{regret} of naive triggering $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}-\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive}}$, together with the \textbf{harm frequency} (fraction of streams on which adapting scored below no-adaptation). All confirmatory comparisons are \textbf{truly paired}: on the v2 harness (\S4) every policy processes bit-identical pre-generated streams, and contrasts use cluster (per-seed) bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals. Numbers quoted from the initial exploratory study use seed-matched bootstrap CIs (arms matched by seed index but drawing separate stream realizations --- conservative rather than fully paired; \S4).


\section{Experimental protocol}


\textbf{Datasets and regimes.} CICIDS2017 \cite{sharafaldin2018toward} (Tuesday reference $\to$ Wednesday, Friday-DDoS, Friday-PortScan, Thursday-WebAttacks), UNSW-NB15 \cite{moustafa2015unsw} (DoS, Reconnaissance) and ToN-IoT \cite{alsaedi2020toniot} (Scanning), spanning benefit, marginal/mixed and harmful readaptation. Features are numeric, standardized and PCA-reduced to 8 dimensions; windows contain 128 flows. To rule out label leakage, identifier and label-derived columns are removed at staging (UNSW: \texttt{id}, binary \texttt{label}, \texttt{attack\_cat}; ToN-IoT: \texttt{label}, \texttt{type}, timestamps and IP/uid identifiers; the CICIDS variant used ships no flow identifiers), and we verified that no remaining single feature separates the classes beyond AUC $\approx$ 0.81 --- far from the near-perfect separation a leaked label would produce. CICIDS2017 has documented traffic-generation and labeling artifacts \cite{engelen2021troubleshooting}; we therefore treat it as the benefit end of a three-benchmark spectrum rather than as sole evidence --- the paper's central negative results (the marginal and harmful regimes) come from the independent UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT benchmarks.


\textbf{The confirmatory harness (v2) --- the methodology behind every headline claim.} All confirmatory results (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory} and \S\ref{sec:v2}) come from a harness built to make the paired contrasts exact. (i) \emph{Common streams:} every evaluation stream and calibration draw is pre-generated by an environment generator that depends on the seed alone, before any policy runs --- all arms process bit-identical windows in the same order, so gate$-$naive contrasts are truly paired and policy actions cannot perturb the environment. (ii) \emph{Role-disjoint partitions:} each per-class pool is split once per seed (50/30/20) into window/train/probe partitions; evaluation windows draw only from the first, initial models, candidates and detector references only from the second, probes only from the third --- train/probe/evaluation overlap is impossible by construction. (iii) \emph{Separate generators:} environment, initial training, detector calibration, candidate training, probe draws and post-commit recalibration each use their own RNG, seeded by (seed) or (seed, $t$) only, never advanced by policy actions. (iv) \emph{Per-trigger logging:} at every trigger the harness records pre-trigger incumbent degradation, the detector score, and incumbent-vs-candidate BA over the following 1/3/5/10 windows --- lookahead used for logging only, never by any policy. The replication protocol (criteria, margins, one-pass stopping rule) was committed and publicly tagged (\texttt{harness-v2-protocol}) before any confirmatory seed ran, with pre-run registered amendments covering seed hygiene, deduplication, baselines and analysis specifications; the confirmatory window is the 30 pristine seeds 104--133, untouched by any smoke test.


\textbf{Shared protocol constants.} The initial model trains on 2{,}000 flows per class from the reference regime; the scaler and PCA are fit on that training set only and frozen thereafter. Every candidate trains on a balanced sample of 512 flows per class drawn \emph{at the current mixture severity} $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ --- never from the final regime pool --- so the candidate sees the population the deployed model faces at the trigger, not the future (a simulator convenience a production system lacks; \S7 bounds the word ``deployable'' accordingly, and the sliding-window and chronological-stream experiments provide the observed-data counterpoints). Detector references use 256 flows per class; thresholds are the 0.95 quantile of detector scores over 30 pre-drift calibration windows; the trigger is 3 consecutive alarms with a 10-window cooldown throughout; windows contain 128 flows. The probe is the gate's \emph{incremental} label cost on top of the labeled retraining data (512 per class per candidate) that any labeled-retraining loop --- gated or not --- already consumes: we claim label efficiency of the \emph{commit decision}, not of the retraining pipeline, and \S\ref{sec:v2} accounts for both.


\textbf{Label and information latency: exact semantics.} Several arms model the delay between a flow being observed and its label being available, and Table~\ref{tab:latency} fixes them, read directly off the artifact. The probe knob ($L_p$) draws the probe from the stream as of window $t-L_p$; the candidate knob ($L_c$) shifts the candidate's \emph{whole} training batch --- features and labels together, as one adjudicated block: under the pool generator the batch is sampled at the mixture of window $t-L_c$, and under the sliding-window generator it is the eight observed windows ending at $t-L_c$. The observed-data probe is inherently nine windows old. Training completion is instantaneous in the confirmatory, observed-data and chronological arms; the pool-based operational sensitivity (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}) explicitly evaluates a five-window deployment delay, during which the incumbent keeps serving. While a deferred VBC-SG proposal is pending, the incumbent remains deployed and \emph{new proposals are blocked} --- a fresh alarm during a pending decision is absorbed exactly like a cooldown window, so a proposal is never silently replaced.

\subsection{Confirmatory and observed-data results}
\label{sec:v2}

We rebuilt the harness as described in \S4 and replicated the confirmatory study under it, with the protocol publicly tagged before any confirmatory seed ran \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact} --- an internal, not external, replication. \textbf{Every criterion replicates} (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory}). Naive triggering remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$1.64, CI95 [$-$2.72, $-$0.64]); the gate converts it to +0.79 [0.53, 1.07], significantly above naive (+2.43 [1.53, 3.43]), preserves PortScan (+9.12 vs naive +8.25) and UNSW (+1.35 vs +1.21), and the pattern is detector-invariant (QK-ZZ: $-$2.91 naive $\to$ +0.72 gated, +3.63 [1.66, 6.38] vs naive). Two pre-declared extensions sharpen the contribution. First, a \emph{holdout} variant carves the probe out of the candidate's retraining batch, deduplicated by row identity --- zero labels beyond those any labeled-retraining loop already uses. It buys most of the protection but not all of it: it removes most of the harm (ToN $-$0.77 vs naive $-$1.64; PortScan +9.08) yet, unlike the fresh probe, does not beat no-adaptation in the harm regime --- so zero incremental cost purchases harm reduction, while the 32-label fresh probe remains the recommended gate. A conservative lower-confidence-bound rule (commit only if the 90\% LCB of the per-flow correctness difference exceeds zero) is harm-avoiding in the evaluated runs and slightly benefit-lossy (ToN +0.34, PortScan +8.51). Second, per-trigger logging provides the mechanism test that shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at each of 250 triggers we measured the incumbent's degradation over the five \emph{preceding} windows and the candidate-minus-incumbent BA over the five \emph{following} windows. Pre-trigger degradation predicts the future value of committing in every regime (r = $-$0.67 to $-$0.70; cluster bootstrap over seeds keeps every regime's CI95 well away from zero, e.g.\ [$-$0.76, $-$0.66] in PortScan; pooled $-$0.57, stable under leave-one-regime-out, $-$0.52 to $-$0.67), while the detector's score at the same triggers shows no consistent association (r = $-$0.01 to +0.05, every clustered CI95 straddling zero). Triggers are nested in seeds and regimes and the incumbent's past and future performance are autocorrelated, so we read this as strong \emph{predictive} support for the degradation--headroom account at the level of individual decisions --- on disjoint past/future windows, under the hardened harness --- not as a causal proof (Fig.~\ref{fig:9}).

\textbf{A hierarchical model sharpens the per-trigger claim --- for both detectors.} Because raw correlations control for nothing, we fit a mixed-effects model of the future update value $\Delta_5$ on pre-trigger degradation, detector score, current severity and stream time; per-regime models take random intercepts over seeds, and the pooled model clusters on \emph{regime$\times$seed} (a seed number is not one experimental unit across regimes). Degradation is the only predictor whose CI95 consistently excludes zero --- pooled $\beta_{\mathrm{deg}} = -1.02$ [$-$1.61, $-$0.43] per BA point (robust to a random slope on degradation: $-$0.92 [$-$1.30, $-$0.55]), and per-regime $-$1.10 [$-$1.51, $-$0.69] (PortScan), $-$0.43 [$-$0.67, $-$0.20] (UNSW), $-$1.15 [$-$1.37, $-$0.89] (ToN-IoT; cluster-bootstrap CI, the random-effect variance sits on the boundary). Two specification notes. First, severity and stream time are structurally collinear on a ramp (severity is a function of $t$; VIFs 60--230), so their individual coefficients are not separately interpretable --- but degradation and score are clean (VIF $\le$ 2.4 and $\approx$ 1.0), so the headline contrast is unaffected. Second, we repeated the whole analysis on the QK-ZZ trigger logs (311 triggers): degradation replicates ($r_{\mathrm{deg}}$ $-$0.50 to $-$0.72 per regime, pooled $-$0.59 [$-$0.68, $-$0.45]; $\beta_{\mathrm{deg}}$ $-$0.38 to $-$1.07, every CI excluding zero), the raw score--value correlation straddles zero in all six regime$\times$detector cells, and the single exception in the conditional models --- a small positive QK-score coefficient in the benefit regime (5.49 [0.27, 10.72] per score unit, PortScan only) --- we report as such: at triggered decisions the evaluated detectors' scores carry no \emph{consistent} incremental signal, not provably none. Leave-one-regime-out prediction transfers \emph{directionally but not in scale}: the fixed part predicts held-out PortScan ($R^2$ 0.18) and ToN-IoT (0.36), collapses to ${\le}0$ without the degradation term, and fails on held-out UNSW ($R^2$ $-$10.5) whose update values live on a much smaller scale even though its within-regime correlation is $-$0.67 --- the account is a robust ranking signal, not a calibrated universal law, and we claim only the former.

\textbf{Decision-quality of the gate, on the same logged triggers.} Using the logged five-window lookahead as ground truth (never available to the policy), the lp32 gate's per-trigger operating point is: in the harm regime, rejection is almost always right (beneficial-rejection rate 4.9\%, i.e.\ 95\% of rejections blocked a non-beneficial update; recall of beneficial updates 0.95) and the gate cuts \emph{local per-decision regret} --- commit vs.\ reject evaluated on the gate policy's own trigger states, against an always-commit rule on those identical states (this is a per-decision contrast, not the full naive trajectory, whose incumbents and triggers would differ) --- from 0.0452 to 0.0020, a ${\sim}22\times$ reduction. In the benefit regime the gate still reduces this regret five-fold (0.0028 vs 0.0140): even where updating is on average excellent, some individual candidates are bad, and the probe catches them. Pooled across 308 triggers: precision 0.74, recall 0.87, regret 0.0030 vs 0.0212. The five-window horizon is not doing the work: logging reruns at horizons 1/3/5/10 leave the harm-regime reduction at 15--23$\times$ and the benefit regime at 4--5$\times$ throughout, while in the marginal regime the contest is honestly a wash at every horizon (ratios 0.8--1.0 on regrets an order of magnitude smaller). The gate does commit some harmful updates (23\% of its commits have negative realized value, mostly small) --- it is a cheap statistical filter, not an oracle.

\textbf{Model-agnosticism moves onto the hardened harness}: for random forest, logistic regression and MLP (KS-max, same pristine seeds) the fresh-probe gate is never materially below naive retraining (largest deficit 0.02 points; the single boundary CI is random forest/PortScan's $-$0.01 [$-$0.03, $-$0.00]), never significantly below no-adaptation, and it rescues the one non-SVC cell where naive dips negative (MLP on ToN-IoT: $-$0.16 naive $\to$ +0.25 gated, +0.41 [0.17, 0.69]). Two further registered extras complete the picture. A \emph{performance-aware trigger} (DDM-style: 8 labeled flows per window monitor the incumbent's error; trigger on a 3$\sigma$ excursion) avoids harm where the incumbent stays healthy --- it never fires in ToN-IoT --- but it is benefit-lossy (PortScan +5.40 vs +7.94 naive; UNSW +0.04 vs +1.22) and spends 800 monitoring labels per stream, versus zero incremental labels for the holdout gate: labels are better spent on the commit decision than on monitoring. On \emph{natural-prevalence evaluation streams} ($\pi=0.05$) an operational subtlety emerges once the detector is also \emph{calibrated} at operating prevalence: the benign mass dilutes the drift signal, KS-max rarely or never fires (ToN-IoT: zero triggers across 30 seeds; PortScan's naive gain shrinks to +1.04 [0.34, 1.90]), and gate and naive coincide almost everywhere. Retraining policy matters little when the monitor is starved --- further evidence that distribution-level scores are a poor control signal, and an open operational problem for detector design under imbalance. \textbf{Canonical monitors, cross-validated against reference implementations} (protocol, agreement rates and full numbers in Supplementary \S S2.1): batch DDM --- ours and the \texttt{river} reference, fed the same 8 labels/window --- captures much of the benefit but remains \emph{net-harmful in the harm regime} (river-DDM ToN-IoT $-$2.23 [$-$5.29, $-$0.05]) and benefit-lossy in the marginal one; the reference ADWIN under-fires and forfeits most of the benefit (+1.77 vs naive +8.25 in PortScan); and a registered sweep raising the monitoring budget to $4\times$ and $10\times$ changes neither conclusion. Under every monitoring configuration evaluated, labels spent on the commit decision bought a better accuracy--cost trade-off than labels spent monitoring. 

\textbf{Adaptive update rules, including a properly calibrated ensemble.} Sliding-window retraining on the last eight observed windows consumes those windows' ${\sim}1{,}024$ labels per candidate and remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.44 [$-$4.23, $-$0.91]). The soft incumbent+candidate ensemble of amendment 003 averaged uncalibrated sigmoid scores; re-run with Platt-calibrated members and probabilistic nesting (amendment 004), it \emph{improves}: it avoids the harm (ToN +0.56 [0.26, 0.87], +2.20 above naive), preserves PortScan (+8.55), and calibration fixes the marginal-regime dilution (UNSW +1.72, significantly \emph{above} naive +0.51 [0.38, 0.66] and above the gate). We report this fairly: the calibrated ensemble is the strongest label-free update rule we evaluated, and in the marginal regime it beats the gate. The gate is ahead in the two decisive regimes (paired gate$-$ensemble: ToN +0.23 [0.04, 0.41], PortScan +0.57 [0.10, 1.11]), can \emph{decline} an update --- the ensemble commits every trigger, incurring deployment churn and unboundedly nested models --- and emits an explicit per-decision signal an operator can audit; the two compose (+0.37/+0.78 in ToN on the amendment-003 runs). Rather than declare a winner, Table~\ref{tab:policy_frontier} presents the honest conclusion of the whole baseline program: \emph{no policy dominates the frontier}. The plain gate maximizes downstream accuracy; the calibrated ensemble is the best probe-free rule; the split two-stage gate is the cheapest harm-reducer; and under four illustrative a-priori operational-cost scenarios (an illustration of the calculus, not a validated cost model) the cheap policies win the utility in almost every cell --- the plain gate's accuracy advantage pays for its label bill only on long streams (beyond ${\sim}46$k evaluated flows in the harm regime at $c_L{=}0.1$ error-equivalents per label). Policy choice is a cost decision, and \S3.4's utility calculus is the instrument for making it.

\textbf{The v1 robustness envelope, re-established (and partly corrected) on v2} (full numbers in Supplementary \S S2.2). The gate's benefit is stable for $b \ge 32$; the initial study's ``8 labels suffice'' does \emph{not} fully replicate --- at $b{=}8$ the gate still reduces harm (+1.22 [0.46, 2.15] above naive) but is no longer net-positive against no-adaptation --- so we revise the recommended operating point to $b{=}32$. \textbf{The two-stage gate: gating training, not just deployment.} Instantiating \S3.4's two-decision structure, the two-stage gate spends labeled flows on the incumbent \emph{first} and only builds a candidate when incumbent accuracy has fallen $\ge \delta$ below its healthy reference. The probe is split per class into a health half and a commit half (8+8 each; no sample feeds both decisions), because reusing one draw for both decisions induces selection optimism --- the registered correction history is in Supplementary \S S2.4. At $\delta{=}0.05$: in the harm regime the split gate trains 69\% fewer candidates than the plain gate (1.07 vs 3.43 per stream), cuts total labels to roughly half of \emph{naive's} (1{,}341 vs 2{,}594; Supplementary \S S5), and remains significantly above naive (+1.79 [0.69, 2.92]) --- but its net gain over never adapting is \emph{no longer resolved} at 30 seeds (+0.15 [$-$0.15, 0.46]; the probe-reuse version's +0.16 [0.03, 0.31] was optimistic, as predicted). The $\delta$ sweep is a clean operating characteristic: more sensitivity trains more candidates and retains more benefit ($\delta{=}0.03$: ToN +0.34 [$-$0.01, 0.70], PortScan +8.36; $\delta{=}0.10$: ToN +0.04, PortScan +7.33), with the benefit-regime cost vs the plain gate ranging from $-$0.76 to $-$1.80. The menu is explicit: the plain commit gate maximizes downstream accuracy; the split two-stage gate buys harm \emph{reduction} at the lowest total cost, and full harm-to-benefit conversion still requires validating a trained candidate. (Resetting the health reference on every commit --- the variant we recommend --- leaves every conclusion unchanged; Supplementary \S S2.4.)

\textbf{Total cost accounting.} Supplementary \S S5 makes the full ledger explicit for the harm regime (all regimes in the artifact): candidates dominate every policy's label bill, the gate's \emph{total} cost exceeds naive's (rejected candidates are trained and discarded; rejections also keep the detector reference, producing more triggers), and the probe is ${\sim}3\%$ of the gate's total. ``Label-efficient'' therefore means --- and only means --- that the \emph{decision} adds tens of labels to a pipeline that already spends thousands; the two-stage variant is the configuration that reduces the pipeline itself.

\textbf{The gate does not need the simulator: a leakage-free observed-data replication (Algorithm 1B).} The strongest objection to everything above is that the core harness hands the candidate and the probe a sample drawn from the environment's pools \emph{at the true current severity} --- an oracle-quality snapshot no deployment can obtain. Answering it took three registered iterations, each closing an oracle privilege or leakage path (post-commit recalibration from the pools; row-level probe/candidate overlap; with-replacement stream sampling); Supplementary \S S2.7 gives the per-iteration account. \textbf{With every oracle privilege removed --- free of simulator-oracle information and of train/future leakage --- the observed-data result stands} (Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}). The table reports the \emph{final} arm (amendments 011--013): the stream is drawn without replacement from a globally value-deduplicated pool (candidate-training/future-evaluation collisions \emph{exactly zero}), the gate \emph{rejects} when no observed probe exists yet (zero unvalidated commits), and the observed threshold is recomputed only once 30 score-windows exist (a registered sweep over minima $\{8,16,30\}$ leaves the contrast unchanged, e.g.\ ToN $+5.44$ to $+5.57$). The final matrix (Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}) runs \emph{all three datasets at the same 64-flow windows} (the largest size UNSW's attack pool supports without replacement; the 128-flow sensitivity for PortScan/ToN-IoT gives the same conclusions, e.g.\ ToN gate$-$naive $+5.57$ [3.19, 8.30] at 128 vs $+4.05$ [2.01, 6.51] at 64) and adds the strict reject-ties gate as a policy column. Naive updating on observed candidates remains net-harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$2.85 [$-$5.29, $-$0.74] full; $-$4.75 [$-$8.80, $-$1.49] mild) and the observed-data gates convert it to a non-negative outcome (point gate $+$1.20 full / $+$0.08 mild; \textbf{+4.05 [2.01, 6.51]} and \textbf{+4.83 [1.59, 8.81]} above naive), with the no-additional-label strict gate on par ($+$3.86 / $+$4.78 above naive); PortScan's benefit is preserved by both gates ($+$9.4--9.5 vs naive $+$8.85); UNSW sits below no-adaptation in both arms at this window size --- the marginal-regime boundary --- with both gates still significantly ahead of naive ($+$0.51--0.64), reported as-is. The mild-drift prediction test thus replicates \emph{inside the same leakage-free pipeline} (naive negative in all three regimes, gates non-negative in ToN and PortScan and significantly above naive in five of six cells) --- the paper's two central defenses hold in one experiment, with nothing left of the leakage. The harm-regime contrast was stable through every iteration ($+3.86$ leaky $\to$ $+5.57$ final), while PortScan's small benefit-regime premium proved partly leakage and settles at $+0.41$; superseded intermediate versions are preserved in the registered amendment notes.

\subsection{Risk-controlled candidate governance}
\label{sec:risk}

\textbf{A registered prediction test: net harm is a property of incumbent health, not of one dataset.} The account makes a prediction that had never been tested, and that could have sunk it: if the value of updating is the incumbent's headroom, then \emph{lowering the drift severity} --- keeping the incumbent healthy --- should turn updating harmful even in regimes we had labelled ``benefit''. We locked this prediction publicly before running it (amendment 006: at $\mathrm{sev}_{\max}{=}0.25$, PortScan's naive gain falls below +1.0 and at least one regime turns net-harmful while the gate holds at $\ge -0.2$). \textbf{The prediction holds} (Table~\ref{tab:harm_generality}), and we state exactly what the intervals support. At mild drift the mean gain of naive always-deploy is \emph{negative in all three benchmarks} --- CICIDS-PortScan $-$0.46 [$-$1.57, 0.42], UNSW-Reconnaissance $-$0.15 [$-$0.21, $-$0.09], ToN-IoT $-$0.65 [$-$1.28, $-$0.16] --- \textbf{statistically resolved in UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT}, and negative but unresolved in CICIDS. The registered criterion for the gate was a $-$0.2-point tolerance, and the gate meets it in all three ($+$0.38, $-$0.09, $-$0.04) while beating naive significantly in each; it is \emph{not} non-negative everywhere --- in UNSW it sits 0.09 points below no-adaptation [$-$0.14, $-$0.04], a resolved but immaterial deficit inside the registered margin. So the honest statement is: harmful updating is not a curiosity of one constructed ToN-IoT cell --- the same sign appears in all three benchmarks once the incumbent is healthy, resolved in two of them --- and the gate reduces the harm to within a pre-specified tolerance everywhere. The harm also broadens at full drift: on the hardened harness, naive updating is net-harmful in two further ToN-IoT regimes (DDoS $-$15.24, Injection $-$2.24), and the gate rescues both (+16.26 and +2.67 above naive). The regime label ``benefit'' or ``harm'' is thus a property of the operating point, not of the dataset --- exactly what the degradation--headroom account says it should be.

\textbf{The zero-drift control: harmful updating is candidate governance, not drift.} The sharpest objection to the mild-drift result is that it might have nothing to do with drift: replacing a healthy model with a finite re-estimate introduces training variance, and that alone could explain a small negative mean. We registered the decisive control and ran it --- triggers fired at a fixed rate \emph{independent of any drift signal}, on streams with \textbf{no drift at all} --- and it reshapes the paper's claim (Table~\ref{tab:zero_drift}). Four findings, stated plainly. (i) The objection is partly right and the effect is real: with zero drift, always-deploy is significantly net-harmful in all three benchmarks ($-$2.76, $-$0.75, $-$4.75). (ii) But it is \emph{not} a small-candidate artifact --- size-matching each candidate to the incumbent's 2{,}000 flows/class leaves the harm in place and usually deeper ($-$4.81, $-$0.13, $-$5.76), because repeated full replacement accumulates estimation noise regardless of sample size. (iii) The plain $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate reduces this harm but does not eliminate it ($-$1.11, $-$0.56, $-$0.25): it commits on ties, and under zero drift ties are the dominant case. The cheapest fix confirms this diagnosis (amendment 012): simply \emph{rejecting ties} --- committing only on a strict $\widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h')>\widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h)$, no statistical machinery --- recovers most of the loss at zero extra cost (PortScan $-$1.11$\to-$0.13, ToN $-$0.25$\to-$0.07, UNSW $-$0.56$\to-$0.28), but not all of it and with no guarantee; the exact tests close the residual and add the per-proposal bound. The strict rule also holds \emph{outside} zero drift (amendment 013): at full drift it retains essentially the whole benefit (PortScan $+8.78$ vs the $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate's $+9.12$; UNSW $+1.41$; ToN $+0.60$) and at mild drift it stays non-negative everywhere the $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate did --- so reject-ties is a genuine no-additional-label Pareto point on the frontier, and the statistical gates' added value is precisely the formal control plus the last fraction of harm. The \emph{stratified} per-class gates (\S\ref{sec:riskgates}) behave as constructed: the fixed-sample normal-LCB variant commits nothing under zero drift and captures benefit conservatively at full drift (PortScan $+6.93$), and its \emph{anytime-valid} empirical-Bernstein counterpart --- the variant that actually supports the abstract's claim --- is reported alongside it below, pricing the sequential guarantee against the fixed-sample approximation. A \emph{risk-controlled} gate is the right tool here --- requiring statistical evidence of superiority (exact McNemar, or the anytime-valid sequential test described below) makes the gate almost never commit when there is no signal, recovering essentially all of the loss (McNemar: exactly $+$0.00 in all three; +2.76 / +0.75 / +4.75 above naive). This is what ``validate before commit'' means when done correctly: when there is genuinely nothing to gain, a properly calibrated validator does nothing. (iv) Finally, with the \emph{real} KS-max detector at zero drift the trigger fires only ${\sim}0.1$ times per stream, so a well-specified monitor mostly avoids the harm by not firing. The honest synthesis is \emph{candidate governance}: an alarm --- or a false alarm, or a scheduled retrain --- is a proposal, and the case for commit-time validation rests not on drift but on the fact that an update is a hypothesis.

\textbf{The zero-drift harm across classifiers: partly general, but size-matched only for SVC-RBF.} A load-bearing objection to the harm result is that its magnitude was read off one fragile pipeline --- an SVC-RBF on an 8-dimensional PCA projection. A registered sweep shows that, \emph{at the default candidate budget}, under zero drift naive always-deploy is net-harmful for every downstream model --- random forest, logistic regression and MLP alike --- and that removing the PCA bottleneck (SVC-RBF, full feature space) does not help; Table~\ref{tab:amendment009} carries all twelve cells. \textbf{But a size-matched control (amendment 012) qualifies this sharply, and we report it plainly.} Those default arms train the candidate on 512 flows/class against a 2{,}000/class incumbent; re-running size-matched (candidate also 2{,}000/class) under zero drift \emph{removes} the harm for random forest, logistic regression and MLP --- all within $\pm0.1$ of zero in every regime --- while for SVC-RBF it persists and \emph{deepens} (as at full drift, $-$4.81 / $-$0.13 / $-$5.76; Supplementary \S S1.5). So for the robust learners the default-budget harm was largely the 4$\times$ candidate-size asymmetry, not a replacement effect; only for the fragile SVC-RBF learner is the zero-drift replacement harm genuine and size-robust. The honest claim is therefore narrower than ``generalizes across four classifiers'': under the default asymmetric budget the harmful \emph{sign} appears in all four, but size-matching localizes the robust replacement harm to SVC-RBF. Where the harm is present, the risk-controlled McNemar gate recovers essentially all of it ($+0.00$) by declining to commit without resolved evidence. We are careful to separate sign from magnitude: these zero-drift losses span the operationally trivial (logistic regression on UNSW $-$0.08, statistically resolved but immaterial) to the severe (SVC on ToN-IoT $-$4.76, and the cumulative generator $-$9.46). What the account predicts, and what the data show, is the \emph{consistency of the sign once the incumbent is healthy} --- not a uniform, uniformly-material magnitude; a practitioner should read the sign as the robust claim and the magnitude as pipeline-dependent. Two nuances. First, at \emph{mild} and full drift the net-harm sign remains SVC-specific --- robust models resist it (Supplementary \S S1.6) --- so the SVC caveat survives for drift-driven harm; what generalizes (for SVC-RBF, at the default budget) is the \emph{zero-drift replacement} harm, for which the pre-declared A/B control (Table~\ref{tab:ab_equivalence}; disjoint, globally value-deduplicated blocks, incumbent role randomized per seed) identifies a \emph{representation-ownership} interaction as the dominant mechanism in the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline --- not pure re-estimation variance. In the harm-regime dataset, with an \emph{independent} transformer the challenger$-$incumbent gap is unresolved from zero ($-1.78$ [$-7.45$, $+3.92$]; equivalence to zero at the registered $\pm1.0$-point margin is \emph{not} established at this precision); fitting the transformer on the \emph{incumbent's} data --- the standard freeze-the-pipeline convention --- produces a large systematic incumbent advantage ($-12.35$ [$-19.33$, $-6.45$]); fitting it on the \emph{challenger's} data \emph{inverts} the effect ($+17.77$ [$+11.00$, $+24.75$]); and giving each model its \emph{own} transformer leaves a tightly-bounded near-zero gap ($+0.05$ [$-0.11$, $+0.21$]). Random forest shows none of this anywhere. The four pre-declared \emph{sign} criteria hold, and the inversion is the load-bearing evidence: whichever model owns the preprocessing wins, the effect is learner-specific to SVC-RBF, and per-challenger preprocessing is the mitigation --- while the gate correctly catches the disadvantaged challenger in the meantime.

\textbf{Equivalence, not just non-significance --- and \emph{which} part of the pipeline owns the effect.} Two things were missing from the control above, and a registered follow-up supplies both (Table~\ref{tab:ab_equivalence}). First, ``the independent-transformer gap is zero'' had been argued from a confidence interval that merely \emph{contains} zero, which is not evidence of equivalence. We therefore re-ran the experiment on 100 \emph{fresh} confirmatory seeds (2001--2100), re-labelling the original 30 a pilot, and tested equivalence by a bootstrap CI-based equivalence assessment at a materiality margin fixed in advance ($\pm1.0$ balanced-accuracy point, the threshold below which a deployment cannot tell the two models apart): equivalence is declared iff the 90\% seed-level bootstrap confidence interval of the mean gap lies inside the margin, the interval-inclusion form of TOST. The verdict is mixed, and we report it as such: giving each model its \emph{own} transformer is \textbf{equivalent to zero} on all three benchmarks (ToN-IoT $-0.01$ [$-0.09$, $+0.07$] --- tight enough to pass even at $\pm0.2$), and so is the independent-transformer condition on UNSW ($+0.00$ [$-0.05$, $+0.06$]); but on the two high-variance SVC benchmarks the independent-transformer gap remains \emph{unresolved} rather than demonstrably null (ToN-IoT $-1.43$ [$-4.47$, $+1.63$]; PortScan $-0.30$ [$-3.88$, $+3.28$]), because the seed-level spread there is an order of magnitude larger than the margin. So the claim we make is the one the data support: the \emph{inversion} across ownership conditions identifies the interaction, and per-model preprocessing demonstrably removes it, while ``no effect whatsoever under an independent transformer'' is consistent with our data but not established by them. Second, ``transformer ownership'' bundles standardization with PCA, and a registered decomposition separates them: fitting only the \emph{standardizer} on the incumbent's data (PCA independent) reproduces essentially the entire ownership advantage ($-12.17$ [$-15.26$, $-9.23$], against $-12.35$ for the full condition), whereas fitting only the \emph{PCA} on it does not ($+0.16$ [$-2.90$, $+3.08$]). The mechanism is therefore feature \emph{scaling}, not projection --- which is exactly what an RBF kernel with a variance-scaled bandwidth would predict, since whoever's data sets the feature variances sets the kernel geometry the challenger is then scored in.

\input{tables/table_ab_equivalence.tex} Second, the harm tracks the \emph{update generator}, not full replacement specifically: under zero drift, sliding-window, replay 50/50 and --- most tellingly --- a \emph{cumulative} candidate trained on \emph{all} observed windows (the deepest harm of any generator, $-$9.46 on ToN-IoT) are all net-harmful; only the calibrated ensemble, which never fully discards the incumbent, escapes, and only on PortScan ($+0.84$), which the risk-averse gate forgoes (Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}). That the tested cumulative generator --- retrained from scratch on all observed windows --- is the \emph{worst} is evidence against a simple ``just use more data'' reading: repeatedly replacing a well-fitted incumbent with a fresh re-estimate injects variance a healthy model has little to gain from. We do not overclaim it: three registered controls --- row-identity deduplication, re-anchoring to the incumbent's initial training set, and corrected $n$-scaled SVC regularization --- all fail to remove the zero-drift cumulative harm, two of them deepening it, so the effect is neither duplication nor under-regularization (numbers in Supplementary \S S2.5). The McNemar gate recovers the loss for every generator, and neutralizes the residual mild-drift harm of the cumulative and replay generators too.  This is candidate governance in its strongest form: the object that needs validating is the \emph{proposal}, whatever produced it.

\textbf{Where the harm hides, and an anytime-valid gate that bounds it.} Two risk-oriented additions close the account. \emph{The mean understates the tail.} Averaging balanced accuracy over 100 post-drift windows hides that harmful updates cluster: in the ToN-IoT harm regime the naive arm's \emph{worst-window} gap below no-adaptation is $-$5.24 [$-$8.39, $-$2.34] and its 10th-percentile and CVaR$@$10\% gaps are $-$4.48 and $-$4.74 --- roughly $3$--$4\times$ the $-$1.38 mean gap --- whereas the gate keeps the entire lower tail non-negative (worst-window $+$0.72, CVaR $+$0.78). Risk, not just the average, is what the gate controls. \emph{An anytime-valid confidence sequence, and what it bounds.} A genuine confidence sequence is valid at \emph{every} sample size. A Robbins normal-mixture gate is so conservative at these budgets that it essentially never commits (the risk-averse endpoint of the frontier; Supplementary \S S2.9); because the correctness differences are mostly ties, their empirical variance is far below the sub-Gaussian worst case, so a predictable-plug-in empirical-Bernstein confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} --- same $\alpha$-uniform harmful-commit bound, empirical rather than worst-case variance --- is much tighter. It \emph{does} capture benefit where the benefit is large: in the deep-benefit PortScan regime at full drift it commits and gains $+5.17$ [3.64, 6.70] (vs the Robbins CS's $+0.00$ and the fixed 32-probe gate's $+8.70$), while committing nothing everywhere else --- it still avoids the ToN-IoT full-drift harm ($+0.00$, i.e.\ $+1.38$ above naive) and commits nothing under mild or zero drift. But at $b{=}64$ it cannot resolve the 1-point benefits of UNSW-Reconnaissance or full-drift ToN-IoT (both $+0.00$). A registered budget sweep confirms this is a \emph{budget}, not a rule, limit --- raising the probe to 128 and 256 flows lifts the empirical-Bernstein gate monotonically and recovers the small benefits it missed (numbers in Supplementary \S S2.8) --- and the full budget frontier below settles the question for the lifetime-budgeted policies as well. \emph{Empirical coverage and harmful-commit rate.} Two audits back the guarantee. A Monte-Carlo coverage check (nominal $\alpha{=}0.10$, $b{=}64$) confirms both sequences hold under i.i.d.\ and sampling-without-replacement probes (false-commit rate $\le 0.002$), but shows the tighter empirical-Bernstein sequence \emph{exceeds} nominal under strong within-probe autocorrelation ($0.18$ vs $0.10$) --- a concrete instance of the probe-representativeness assumption of \S\ref{sec:riskgates}, and the reason we recommend it with that caveat stated. And the \emph{empirical} per-stream harmful-commit rate makes the per-proposal-vs-lifetime distinction concrete, reported as descriptive within-sweep fractions (commits are clustered within shared seeds, so these counts are not independent trials): the point-estimate-maximizing $\varepsilon{=}0$ gate commits a harmful update on a resolved fraction of its commits (PortScan 12/81 $\approx$ 0.15; UNSW 20/63 $\approx$ 0.32; ToN 14/56 $\approx$ 0.25), whereas the risk-controlled gates of the budget frontier commit \emph{zero observed} harmful updates over a far larger and --- crucially --- \emph{complete} accounting: of the 520 commits they make across the sweep (340 immediate, 180 taking effect several windows after the proposal was raised), each scored from the window where it actually took effect, 506 are evaluable at horizon $H{=}5$ and 14 are right-censored because their five-window horizon runs past the end of the stream --- and among the 506 $H5$-evaluable commits \emph{zero were harmful}. These commits are clustered within shared seeds, scenarios and policy grids, so they are not treated as 506 independent Bernoulli trials and no binomial population-rate bound is inferred from this count. The accounting is complete by construction --- every commit is scored from its real resolution window or explicitly right-censored and puts a useful gradient behind it, since on the same streams always-deploying is harmful on 39\% of its commits, the point gate on 26\%, and the no-additional-label strict rule on 9\% --- rejecting ties alone removes two thirds of the point gate's harm without buying a single extra label. We still state this as ``zero observed harmful commits'', not ``eliminates'': a finite sweep cannot certify a probability of zero, and the per-proposal bound is what the confidence sequence formally provides. The empirical-Bernstein gate is thus the recommended anytime-valid procedure (it dominates the Robbins CS), and it sharpens rather than overturns the frontier's central lesson: a pooled sequential gate provides useful empirical risk control at tens of adjudicated labels and captures the \emph{large} benefits, whereas the formally aligned stratified guarantee requires substantially larger balanced-probe budgets --- and resolving small benefits requires more labels under either.

\textbf{VBC-SG in practice: what each guarantee layer costs.} The assembled policy of \S\ref{sec:riskgates} was evaluated layer by layer, and the empirical frontier is exactly the story the construction predicts (all cells: 30 seeds, probe budget 64 in blocks of 16, deferral cap 5). Under \emph{zero drift} every risk-controlled configuration commits nothing (gain $+0.00$: stratified EB-CS, exact per-class McNemar, deferral, lifetime budgets --- all of them, in all three regimes). At \emph{full drift}, where PortScan offers ${\sim}$9 points of benefit, the layers price themselves out one by one: pooled EB-CS with deferral captures $+6.76$ [5.10, 8.36] ($\approx$900 labels/stream); the full \textbf{VBC-SG} --- stratified per-class bounds \emph{and} deferral at per-proposal $\alpha{=}0.10$ --- captures $+3.02$ [2.09, 4.00] (0.67 commits, $\approx$930 labels); a Bonferroni lifetime budget ($\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}{=}0.10$ over $M{=}10$) reduces the pooled gate to $+1.01$; and the maximally-stacked configuration (stratified + deferral + $p$-series lifetime spending) commits nothing \emph{at this budget}. The fixed exact stratified baseline (per-class McNemar, 114--241 labels) likewise never commits --- power, not validity, is what the labels buy.

\textbf{The deployment-long guarantee is non-vacuous within the evaluated adjudication budget: a registered budget frontier.} The natural objection to the paragraph above is that a policy which never commits is a vacuous one, and $b{=}64$ alone cannot distinguish ``the rule is too conservative'' from ``the budget is too small''. We therefore ran a registered budget frontier (pristine seeds 501--530; probe cap $\in\{64,128,256,512,1024\}$ $\times$ spending schedule $\in\{$Bonferroni, $p$-series$\}$ $\times$ \{pooled EB-CS with deferral, VBC-SG-Cohort, VBC-SG-Refresh\}, on benefit (PortScan full drift), harm (ToN-IoT full drift) and zero-drift streams, with the point and strict gates as no-additional-label anchors, i.e.\ using the same probe as the point gate with no extra labels), and pre-declared what would count as a non-vacuous operating point: it must commit, recover $\ge 50\%$ of naive's benefit, observe zero harmful commits under zero drift, and spend fewer probe labels per proposal than candidate training already spends (1{,}024). \textbf{Twelve configurations meet it} (Table~\ref{tab:budget_frontier}). The lifetime-budgeted pooled EB-CS with deferral recovers $+3.73$ of naive's $+7.22$ at a cap of only $64$ ($52\%$; 137 labels per proposal) and rises monotonically to $+6.73$ [4.76, 8.66] at a configured per-proposal cap of $512$ --- deferral continuations raise realized consumption to $\approx$578 adjudicated probe labels per proposal (Cohort-sim 580, Refresh 579), so the cap is a per-block ceiling, not the total cost --- \textbf{93\% of the benefit of always-deploying under the approximate pooled analysis}; the fully stratified \textbf{VBC-SG-Cohort} (a proposal-target resampling simulation, Cohort-sim), which matches the fixed-quota per-class target and so carries the formal deployment-long false-superiority guarantee, reaches $81\%$ at the same cap, and Refresh $68\%$. The 578 probe labels are little more than half what training the candidate costs, and the pooled figure approximates rather than exactly matches the per-class design. Crucially, the safety side does not degrade anywhere on this frontier: under zero drift \emph{every} lifetime configuration at \emph{every} cap commits nothing at all (0 commits from 91 proposals), and no configuration in the sweep committed a single harmful update --- this is the complete, cluster-caveated 520-commit accounting reported above (340 immediate, 180 deferred; 506 evaluable at $H{=}5$, 14 right-censored, zero harmful; no population-rate bound inferred). So the earlier ``commits nothing'' is a statement about a budget, not about the rule: the deployment-long stratified guarantee is out of reach at $b{=}64$ and becomes non-vacuous from $b{=}256$ onward within the evaluated balanced-probe adjudication budget --- the first evaluated operating point meeting the pre-declared non-vacuity criteria when a lifetime bound is required. Its inspected-flow acquisition cost under operational class imbalance is not measured. The price is visible and worth stating plainly: abstention stays high (74--86\% of proposals resolve as neither commit nor futility), decisions take ${\sim}4$ windows of deferral, and the no-additional-label strict gate still buys most of the practical safety --- under zero drift it commits 0.13 times per stream and costs $-0.01$ [$-0.02$, $+0.00$] balanced-accuracy points, which is why we present the frontier as a menu rather than a winner.

\input{tables/table_budget_frontier.tex} \emph{Operational-prevalence sensitivity (lite).} A separate sensitivity inside the pool-based v2 harness --- distinct from the acquisition-yield arm of Table~\ref{tab:operational_e2e} --- places the evaluation stream and the Binomial probe at operating prevalence ($\pi \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10\}$) with candidate and probe labels lagged 5 windows (lag 20 as sensitivity); its detector calibration and candidate training batch remain balanced per class, and inspected-flow acquisition costs are not modeled. The binding constraint is again upstream of the gate: at $\pi{=}0.01$ the KS detector fires 0.03--2.3 times per stream (ToN effectively never), so naive and gated arms differ little where the monitor is starved; at $\pi{=}0.10$ PortScan recovers $+4.8$ (naive) with the gate close behind ($+4.0$), and UNSW shows the gate slightly \emph{ahead} of naive at every prevalence and latency tested (e.g.\ $+0.37$ vs $-0.02$ at $\pi{=}0.05$, lag 20). The claim this sensitivity supports is deliberately narrow: once a trigger fires and labels arrive, the 32-flow probe adds little adjudication burden relative to the 1{,}024-label candidate batch and continues to reject harmful candidates; making the \emph{trigger} operate under extreme imbalance remains the open upstream problem the paper has flagged since \S\ref{sec:v2}.

\textbf{The risk axis: exact tests and a sequential probe.} Our recommended rule commits on a point comparison ($\varepsilon{=}0$), which is not a statistical guarantee --- 23\% of its commits have negative realized value. The registered risk-controlled variants price the guarantee explicitly. The exact one-sided McNemar gate ($\alpha{=}0.05$, ties never commit) is maximally harm-averse and essentially never harmful (ToN +0.00), but at $b{=}32$ it has almost no power (0.03 commits per stream in the harm regime) and forfeits most of the benefit (PortScan +5.45, $-$3.67 [$-$4.61, $-$2.74] vs the plain gate; doubling to $b{=}64$ recovers part of it, +7.67, still $-$1.45 below). A Bonferroni sequential probe that buys labels only while the decision is in doubt spends 58--61 labels per decision, likewise gives up some benefit for its guarantee, and likewise recovers essentially all of the zero-drift loss by refusing to commit without evidence; the confidence sequences above subsume it, and its numbers are in Supplementary \S S2.9. The lesson is not that exact tests are wrong but that \emph{the label budget, not the decision rule, is the binding constraint}: with 32 labels one cannot statistically resolve a one-point accuracy difference, so demanding significance means almost never updating. Point comparison, LCB, exact McNemar and the anytime-valid sequential tests span the risk axis of the frontier; an operator who fears a bad commit more than a missed gain --- or who can pay for more labels --- moves along it.

\subsection{Operational and chronological boundaries}
\label{sec:boundaries}

\textbf{Honest natural-prevalence probes: a claim we retract} (corrected experiment in Supplementary \S S2.3). The initial study's ``the probe need not be balanced'' rested on a probe conditioned to contain one attack flow. Under genuinely random inspection (attack count $\sim \mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$, zero-attack probes allowed) the gate holds down to $\pi \approx 0.05$ (ToN +0.91 [0.67, 1.17]) but the harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi{=}0.01$ (+0.21 [$-$1.39, 1.15]), where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack at all. Below that prevalence the operator must enrich the probe (${\approx}1/\pi$ inspections per attack flow) or accept that the commit decision is being made on benign traffic alone.


\textbf{Chronologically ordered subsampled replays: the gate's honest boundary.} Six initial registered replays on real, chronologically ordered streams (200 windows of 256 flows, strided to span each capture: ordering, labels and the temporal availability of every probe are real; the sampling density is not) map where the gate costs benefit; per-replay history and the corrected implementation details are in Supplementary \S S2.6. The phenomenon the ramps model is real and deep: every CICIDS incumbent collapses on a later window (no-adaptation BA 49--72\%) as each stream presents an unseen attack sub-type, and drift-triggered retraining recovers +13.6 to +43.8 BA points. On these deep-benefit streams the gate pays a real, metric-dependent insurance premium ($-$5.0 to $-$15.1 of the recovered balanced-accuracy points on three streams; nothing on Wednesday, +0.40 [0.09, 0.74]) --- while on \emph{overall accuracy} it \emph{beats} naive on the same streams (Friday +4.19; Thursday +1.23), because a stale probe misranks candidates on the minority class first. A registered interventional test rules out probe class composition as the cause (a stratified probe leaves the premium unchanged); the surviving explanation is probe \emph{staleness}, reported as a hypothesis consistent with the data, not a demonstrated mechanism. \emph{On the external stream the account's central prediction holds}: the raw UNSW-NB15 timeline keeps its incumbent healthy (82.3\% BA), updating helps moderately (+7.33), and the gate pays \emph{no premium at all} (+0.16 [$-$0.31, 0.63] vs naive) --- the insurance is free precisely where incumbent health makes the commit decision genuinely uncertain. \emph{Chronological net harm is unobserved across these six replays}; every CICIDS stream lands in deep-benefit territory, the only healthy incumbent among them is the external UNSW timeline, and \S7 states the resulting external-validity limit. This is the registered check where the gate costs real benefit, reported as such.

\textbf{The full chronological matrix, enumerated in advance.} The six replays above were added one at a time, which invites the objection that they are a walk through a garden of forking paths. We therefore pre-enumerated \emph{every} chronological stream this data supports --- each train$\to$future day pair of CICIDS2017 whose training day contains attacks, the two remaining intra-day splits, and the UNSW-NB15 timeline at two further training fractions --- and ran all seven new ones under the same protocol with the obligatory strict baseline and VBC-SG added (seeds 601--630; ToN-IoT is excluded because its distribution ships no timestamps, so no chronological stream can be built from it, and we say so rather than substituting a proxy). Table~\ref{tab:chronological_q1} reports all of them. \textbf{Net harm remains unobserved} --- on no chronological stream, old or new, does always-deploying lose to never adapting --- so the harmful-update finding stays a controlled-condition result, and this is the paper's principal limit on external validity. What the matrix does add is the account's \emph{conditional} prediction, tested where it is falsifiable. Incumbent health, not the calendar, sorts the outcomes: on the streams where the incumbent collapses (no-adaptation balanced accuracy 49--59\%) always-deploying is excellent and the gates pay a real premium (Tue$\to$Wed+Thu+Fri: $+11.5$ gated vs $+14.8$ naive; Wed$\to$Fri: $+27.6$ vs $+35.3$), whereas on the two UNSW timelines, where the incumbent stays healthy (82.9\% and 84.2\%), \emph{the point and strict gates beat always-deploying on both} --- the no-additional-label strict rule by $+2.78$ and $+1.34$ points, on non-overlapping intervals, while committing 2.5 and 3.6 times per stream against naive's 13.0 and 17.3. VBC-SG joins them on the first timeline ($+10.76$ vs naive's $+8.35$) but not on the second ($+6.92$ vs $+7.64$), which is the behaviour its extra conservatism predicts and which we report rather than average away. We do not round this into a law: the Wednesday intra-day split also keeps a healthy incumbent (84.7\%) yet its gates sit a statistically unresolved $0.5$--$0.7$ points \emph{below} naive, so the honest summary is that the chronological premium disappears --- and can reverse --- once the incumbent has little headroom to lose, without being guaranteed to reverse.

\textbf{Attack-label acquisition yield at operating prevalence.} Every label count quoted so far is an \emph{adjudicated} label; an operator pays in \emph{inspected flows}, and at realistic prevalence finding an \emph{attack} label costs two orders of magnitude more than adjudicating a flow. A registered \emph{pool-based operational acquisition-yield and delay simulation} quantifies that search cost: adjudication candidates are drawn at operating prevalence $\pi$, every probe label arrives five windows late (twenty as sensitivity), and a candidate that has been trained is not committable until a configurable training delay has elapsed, during which the incumbent keeps serving and new alarms are absorbed. Its scope is deliberately bounded, and we state the boundary: the evaluation stream and the detector's calibration remain balanced, and the candidate training batch remains balanced per class with its acquisition cost \emph{not} modeled --- so the arm measures what finding attack labels costs; it does not price the commit pipeline end to end. Unlike the observed-data arm of \S\ref{sec:v2}, this simulation still draws its flows from the environment's pools at the current mixture --- what it adds is the operational layer that arm does not model: who gets inspected, when labels arrive, and how long retraining takes. Flows to inspect are chosen by policies that read only model \emph{predictions}, never ground truth, so an analyst can work the queue the detector already produces (Table~\ref{tab:operational_e2e}). Because an enriched sample is \emph{not} a sample from the deployment distribution, the adjudication budget is split in two --- an auxiliary \textbf{discovery} half, selected by the acquisition policy and used only to measure what finding attacks costs, and an independent uniform \textbf{validation} half at operating prevalence, which is the only sample the commit rule is ever shown (32 adjudications per decision, compared by plain accuracy; at extreme prevalence it may contain few or no attacks, so no balanced-accuracy guarantee is claimed there). Discovery yield and decision validity are thus measured on different samples with different purposes, by construction: enrichment improves the former and neither converts the validation sample into an enriched one, reduces its 32 adjudications, nor makes the commit decision itself any cheaper to validate. Random inspection is punishing exactly as arithmetic predicts --- at $\pi{=}0.01$ it costs about 92--109 inspected flows per attack label found --- but \textbf{alert-enriched inspection cuts that by 5--8$\times$ at every prevalence and on both benchmarks} (ToN-IoT at $\pi{=}0.01$: 14.7 flows per attack against random's 92.2), and the hybrid policy by 3--6$\times$. Ranking by candidate--incumbent \emph{disagreement}, which we expected to help, barely does (1.1--1.3$\times$): the two models differ near the decision boundary rather than on the minority class, and we report the negative result as it came out. The claim the paper is entitled to is therefore the bounded one: the commit decision requires its 32 uniform validation adjudications, whose inspection cost is 32 flows by construction; what costs ${\approx}1/\pi$ inspected flows per flow found is acquiring \emph{attack} labels (the discovery objective, relevant to building attack-labeled corpora), and alert-enriched inspection cuts that search cost roughly fivefold. The candidate's balanced training batch is not priced by this arm, and the upstream problem at extreme imbalance remains the starved \emph{trigger}, not the gate. Delay costs something, but not much: quadrupling label latency to twenty windows takes CICIDS-PortScan from $+5.83$ to $+4.81$, and adding a five-window training-completion delay --- during which the incumbent keeps serving --- costs a comparable $0.5$ point ($+5.37$), so the pipeline degrades gracefully rather than falling over when labels and retraining are slow.

\section{Discussion}


\textbf{Detection is not decision.} The empirical core of this paper is that optimizing a drift \emph{detector} optimizes the wrong object. A detector reports that the distribution moved; an adaptive NIDS needs to know whether retraining will help, and these come apart precisely when the deployed model is still good. Our analysis makes this concrete: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so adaptation benefit is essentially deployed-model headroom (Supplementary \S S1.1) --- and the same distributional shift is worth +19 balanced-accuracy points in one regime and $-$4 in another, while the detector's score shows no consistent association with which (within-deployment r $\approx$ +0.06). None of the evaluated distribution-level monitors distinguishes these cases from window statistics alone --- which is why classical and quantum-kernel monitors, and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them, all fail to prevent harm (Supplementary \S S1.2--S1.3).


\textbf{Why retraining can hurt, and why it is model-dependent.} In our protocol the candidate is retrained on a balanced, correctly-labeled sample of the current regime --- near-ideal conditions. It still degrades accuracy when the incumbent is strong, because retraining on a narrow current window replaces a broadly-generalized model with one fit to a shifting slice of traffic; the loss of generalization outweighs the gain from recency. Whether this loss actually pushes the outcome below no-adaptation depends on the downstream model's robustness: the SVC-RBF classifier is fragile enough on ToN-IoT to go net-negative, whereas random forest, logistic regression and MLP retain enough generalization to stay non-negative (Supplementary \S S1.6). This is not a contradiction of the regularity --- all four obey the degradation--headroom relationship --- but a refinement: net harm is the fragile-model tail of a general phenomenon. It is precisely why a decision layer that estimates \emph{actual} candidate-vs-incumbent improvement, rather than distributional change, is the right abstraction: the gate is model-agnostic by construction and, empirically, not materially worse than either baseline in the evaluated average results across all four downstream models (its residual harmful-commit rate and the chronological premium are the measured exceptions, \S\ref{sec:v2}).


\textbf{Why the gate works, and what labels it actually needs.} The validate-before-commit gate succeeds because it estimates the one quantity the mechanism identifies --- the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ --- directly, from a small labeled probe, and acts on it. It does not merely suppress adaptation: in the harmful regime it \emph{beats} no-adaptation by committing the minority of genuinely beneficial updates while rejecting the harmful majority (Supplementary \S S1.4). The label-free alternatives are informative rather than disappointing. Naive disagreement measures how much retraining would change decisions, not whether the changes are correct, and fails outright. The state-of-the-art estimators (ATC, DoC) fail more subtly: they estimate accuracy from the incumbent's own confidences, whose trustworthiness collapses precisely when the incumbent is degraded --- the regime where adaptation is most valuable --- so they buy safety at the cost of much of the benefit (Supplementary \S S1.5). The labeled probe sidesteps this circularity: its validity does not depend on the model being judged. The label bill is layered: retraining labels are unavoidable for any labeled-retraining loop; carving the probe from that same (deduplicated) batch costs nothing extra and buys most of the harm reduction; and conversion of average net harm into average net benefit in the controlled confirmatory harm regime costs a fresh probe of $\approx$32 flows per confirmed drift, tens over a stream. These adjudication counts may be compatible with analyst-assisted workflows, but the inspected-flow acquisition cost of the balanced candidate batch and of the formal stratified probes was not evaluated. Two registered extensions sharpen the picture (\S\ref{sec:v2}): a properly \emph{calibrated} soft ensemble shows that label-free harm avoidance is achievable --- at a modest cost in the decisive regimes and with no ability to decline an update --- and the two-stage gate shows that the same 32 labels, spent \emph{before} training, also gate the pipeline's dominant cost, the candidate itself.


\textbf{Practitioner guidance.} The results invert the usual deployment recipe. Rather than first choosing the most sensitive (or most expensive, e.g. quantum) detector and then wiring it to automatic retraining, a practitioner should (i) determine, on held-out labeled windows, whether their setting is in a benefit or harm regime --- equivalently, whether the deployed model has headroom to lose --- and (ii) gate every adaptation on a small labeled validation probe. Under this recipe, and conditional on a comparable proposal being generated, the detector acts mainly as a scheduler and candidate validation becomes the decisive layer; at extreme class imbalance, however, detector calibration and alarm starvation determine whether the gate is invoked at all. A 114$\times$ quantum monitoring overhead buys nothing once the decision, not the detection, is the bottleneck.


\textbf{Scope: a deployment-decision rule, not a NIDS-specific fix.} Nothing in the gate is specific to network traffic. It instantiates a general champion--challenger discipline \cite{nath2007champion,guissouma2023continuous} for drift-triggered retraining: train a candidate on each trigger, estimate the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ on a small labeled probe from the current distribution, and commit only on estimated improvement. The mechanism it exploits is equally generic --- update value tracks incumbent degradation, which distribution-level monitors do not measure (Supplementary \S S1.1) and confidence-based self-assessment mismeasures exactly when it matters (Supplementary \S S1.5). We therefore expect the pattern to transfer to other streaming-classification deployments where labels are scarce but not absent --- fraud detection, spam and abuse filtering, predictive maintenance --- while stressing that our evidence is confined to network-security benchmarks; validating the rule beyond security is future work.


\textbf{Relation to quantum-kernel drift monitoring.} This work began as an evaluation of quantum-kernel MMD as a superior drift trigger; that hypothesis is not supported (QK-MMD defines regime-dependent operating points on one dataset that do not replicate externally, at a ${\sim}114\times$ simulated monitoring cost), and the quantum monitors serve in this paper as a detector-invariance check: the harm, the gate's rescue and the per-trigger mechanism all replicate under a quantum-kernel detector (Table~\ref{tab:v2_confirmatory}, \S\ref{sec:v2}), which is part of the evidence that the decision layer, not the similarity measure, is what matters (details in Supplementary \S S1.2).


\section{Limitations}


\textbf{External validity.} The strongest positive (benefit) evidence is concentrated in CICIDS2017; the external benchmarks provide the marginal and harmful ends of the spectrum, which is what the argument requires, but broader benefit-regime replication is future work. The core drift trajectories are gradual mixing ramps between real regime pools --- covariate/regime drift; we do not isolate a $p(y\,|\,x)$ change, and use ``drift'' in that bounded sense. Thirteen real chronological replays (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}) add abrupt real drift: most CICIDS streams land in deep-benefit territory, their incumbents collapsing as a new attack sub-type appears, whereas the UNSW-NB15 timelines and the Wednesday intra-day split keep their incumbents healthy; on the two UNSW timelines the point and strict gates beat always-deploying (VBC-SG on the 20\% training split but not the 40\% split), while the Wednesday intra-day split is an unresolved counterexample in which the gates sit slightly below naive. What no chronological stream has yet produced is \emph{net harm} --- a regime where always-deploy loses to never-adapt --- so the harmful-update finding, the paper's most novel, remains demonstrated only on controlled constructions; a chronological capture exhibiting it is the outstanding external test, and its absence across thirteen pre-enumerated replays is the honest bound on how often the phenomenon should be expected in deployment. Recurrent and $p(y\,|\,x)$-isolated drift remain untested. Quantum kernels are classically simulated, so the reported 114$\times$ monitoring overhead is a simulation figure.

\textbf{Learner and update scope.} At full drift the net-harm result is specific to a fragile downstream classifier (SVC-RBF) and does not appear for more robust classifiers (Supplementary \S S1.6) --- but it is no longer confined to one dataset: with a healthy incumbent (mild or zero drift) naive updating is net-harmful in all three benchmarks (Tables~\ref{tab:harm_generality} and \ref{tab:zero_drift}), and at full drift in three ToN-IoT regimes. The zero-drift replacement harm appears for random forest, logistic regression, MLP and full-feature SVC at the default candidate budget, but a size-matched control (\S\ref{sec:risk}) shows it is size-robust only for SVC-RBF; for the robust learners it was largely a finite-sample (candidate-size) effect. The PCA-8 pipeline, chosen for quantum-simulation tractability, attenuates but does not create the harm. The harm holds across replacement-based update generators for SVC-RBF (cumulative --- the worst --- sliding-window and replay; Table~\ref{tab:amendment009}), and the standard label-free mitigation, retrain-current-plus-replay, does not rescue it; incremental or weighted online learners with no discrete replacement step remain untested, as do deployment settings beyond the four classifiers and regimes evaluated. The symmetric A/B experiment (\S\ref{sec:risk}) shows the dominant driver in this pipeline is not pure estimation variance but a representation-ownership interaction: fitting the transformer on the incumbent's data produces a systematic incumbent advantage ($-12.3$ [$-19.3$, $-6.5$]), fitting it on the challenger's data \emph{inverts} the effect ($+17.8$), per-model transformers leave a tightly-bounded near-zero gap ($+0.05$ [$-0.1$, $+0.2$]), and with an independent transformer the gap is unresolved from zero (ToN-IoT $-1.8$ [$-7.5$, $+3.9$]; equivalence at the registered margin not established). This is a real effect of the freeze-the-pipeline convention in the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline, which the gate catches and per-challenger preprocessing would mitigate; the control does not decompose the interaction's components, and we do not claim to have separated re-estimation variance from drift-specific specialization beyond what it provides. We therefore frame harm as what happens when a healthy model is replaced on an alarm --- a condition, not a dataset --- with a magnitude that depends on learner fragility.

\textbf{Prevalence and acquisition cost.} The gate assumes a small labeled probe at decision time; we report its size ($\approx$32--64 adjudicated flows per confirmed drift) as the incremental adjudication count under the evaluated protocol --- not an estimate of inspected-flow acquisition cost in an operationally imbalanced deployment. Evaluation windows are class-balanced; alert volumes and false-positive costs under extreme operational imbalance are untested. The probe's tolerance to imbalance is bounded, and we correct an earlier overclaim: under \emph{genuinely random} inspection (attack count $\sim \mathrm{Binomial}(b,\pi)$, zero-attack probes allowed; \S\ref{sec:boundaries}) the gate holds down to $\pi \approx 0.05$ but its harm-regime guarantee dissolves at $\pi = 0.01$, where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack; below that prevalence the operator must enrich the probe (${\approx}1/\pi$ inspections per attack flow) or accept a decision made on benign traffic alone --- the initial study's stronger claim rested on a probe conditioned to contain one attack, and is withdrawn. The inspected-flow cost of the balanced candidate batch, of the formal stratified probes, and of the deployment pipeline end to end is not modeled.

\textbf{Probe representativeness and information availability.} In the core harness, candidate batches and probes are sampled from the simulator's mixture at the true current severity --- information a production system lacks. That caveat is discharged rather than merely disclosed: the registered leakage-free observed-data arm (\S\ref{sec:v2}, Table~\ref{tab:causal_probe}) rebuilds both from observed past traffic only, with no access to $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ or the pools, and reproduces the harmful-regime rescue and the gate-over-naive ordering, while exposing negative marginal and mild-drift cells relative to no-adaptation; equivalence to the oracle-probe arm was not tested, and the core-harness tables retain the convenience (with-replacement sampling and its small, symmetric collision count), which the observed-data arm's without-replacement, value-deduplicated stream mode removes exactly. The gate is evaluated at fixed per-window severity. Label latency is modeled for the probe (up to 20 windows stale) and the candidate batch (5--20 windows; Table~\ref{tab:latency} fixes every knob's semantics); training completion is instantaneous except in the operational sensitivity (five-window delay); asynchronous or partial labeling is not modeled, and the probe-corruption experiment flips labels uniformly at random --- a candidate-aware adversary is strictly stronger and untested. The label-free estimators of Supplementary \S S1.5 are instantiated in their standard form; estimator-specific tuning could narrow the gap we report. The confidence-sequence gates control the per-proposal probability of falsely establishing superiority \emph{on the probe distribution}; this equals a bound on future-deployment harm only when the probe is representative --- an assumption our own stale-probe and coverage results show can fail (the empirical-Bernstein sequence exceeds nominal $\alpha$ under strong within-probe autocorrelation). The plain sequences are per proposal; VBC-SG's lifetime budget supplies the deployment-long bound by alpha-spending across proposals, at a label cost the frontier shows is prohibitive in its maximally-stacked stratified form at $b{=}64$.

\textbf{Statistical granularity and multiplicity.} Seeds quantify within-regime sampling variability, not new environments: the external units are three datasets and seven regimes, and confidence intervals should be read at that granularity (Supplementary \S S3 reports leave-one-out behaviour). Two v1 harness caveats --- seed-matched but unpaired stream realizations, and with-replacement pool sampling --- apply to the initial study only: the registered replication re-runs the confirmatory design on a common-stream, partition-disjoint harness with fresh seeds and reproduces every criterion. Multiplicity-adjusted $p$-values are computed directly from the paired per-seed contrasts using a deterministic centered paired bootstrap test, with Holm controlling the six-test confirmatory family and Benjamini--Hochberg applied separately to the pre-declared 15-cell budget-frontier block and the seven-replay chronological block (Supplementary \S S5); other point/VBC-SG comparisons are descriptive and uncorrected. \emph{No conclusion depends on the correction}: every nominally significant contrast survives its adjustment, in all three families.


\section{Conclusion}


Adaptive network intrusion detection is usually framed as a detection problem: monitor for drift, retrain and redeploy when it fires. We showed that this framing is incomplete and sometimes harmful: across three benchmarks the value of a drift-triggered update ranges from strongly beneficial to net-negative, tracking deployed-model degradation rather than distributional change, and invisible to the detector. A registered zero-drift control reframes the point as \emph{candidate governance}: forced updates on a healthy model are net-harmful in all three benchmarks even with no drift at all, size-robustly so for the fragile SVC-RBF learner. A well-specified detector avoids this by not firing; but any proposal that reaches the deployment step --- however it was generated --- is a hypothesis, and the direct safeguard is candidate--incumbent validation before committing, with formal false-superiority control supplied only by the stratified sequential variants under their stated sampling and representativeness assumptions. The validate-before-commit gate provides exactly that layer: the loop retrains its candidate but \emph{deploys} it only if a small labeled probe confirms an improvement, and in two-stage form it skips even the retraining when the incumbent shows no measurable degradation. In the controlled benefit, marginal and harm regimes the gate preserves average benefit, avoids net harm, significantly outperforms naive in the harm regime, and exceeds never-adapting across the confirmatory cells --- for a classical and a quantum detector alike. Three findings sharpen the decision layer. A role-randomized control identifies preprocessing ownership, localized to feature scaling, as the dominant mechanism in the evaluated SVC-RBF pipeline; per-model scaling removes it, and the gate is the interim safeguard. A registered budget frontier turns the deployment-long risk bound from an empty formalism at 64 labels into a policy capturing 93\% of always-deploying's benefit at the 512-label cap ($\approx$578 adjudicated labels per proposal after deferrals) under the approximate pooled analysis --- 81\% for the fully stratified variant carrying the formal per-class guarantee --- while committing nothing when there is nothing to gain. And on thirteen chronologically ordered replays net harm never appeared --- our principal limit on external validity, stated as such --- yet point and strict gating beat always-deploying on the two healthy external timelines, with a third healthy replay left standing as an unresolved counterexample. \emph{When not to deploy a retrained candidate} is as important as detecting drift, and in the controlled balanced-probe setting a few dozen adjudicated labels, spent on the decision rather than the detector, are enough to know the difference where the incumbent's health is genuinely in question.
