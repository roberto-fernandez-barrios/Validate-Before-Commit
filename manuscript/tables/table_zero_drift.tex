Replacing a healthy incumbent with a fresh estimate of \emph{the same} distribution is net-harmful in all three benchmarks --- and the commit gate recovers most of the loss in each. This is the limiting case of the degradation--headroom account (zero headroom $\Rightarrow$ the value of an update cannot be positive), and it decomposes the harm the paper reports: most of what naive updating loses under mild drift is the price of replacing a healthy model with a finite re-estimate, not a drift-specific effect. It also removes the detector from the argument entirely: with no drift to detect, no monitor --- however good --- can prevent this loss. Only validating the candidate can.
