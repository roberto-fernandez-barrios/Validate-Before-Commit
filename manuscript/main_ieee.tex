\begin{abstract}
Operational machine-learning intrusion detectors are periodically retrained to cope with concept drift, and the decision of \emph{when} to retrain is usually delegated to a drift detector. We show this is the wrong control variable. Whether retraining helps is governed by how degraded the deployed model already is: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so its benefit is the deployed model's headroom --- a quantity drift detectors cannot observe (detector scores are uninformative about it within a deployment, r $\approx$ 0.06). The detector is not the lever, and drift-triggered retraining ranges, across three public benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT), from strongly beneficial to net-harmful: for a fragile classifier, never adapting wins. We recast readaptation as a cost-aware decision and introduce a deployable, label-efficient \emph{validate-before-commit} gate: a candidate is retrained on each trigger but committed only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe. The gate needs tens of labeled flows (~8 per drift), tolerates validation labels 20 windows stale or drawn at natural attack prevalence, and \emph{fails safe} under random label corruption (40\% flipped). Under pre-specified criteria (30-seed 95\% CIs) it generalizes across two detectors (with SVC) and four classifiers (with KS-max), converting harm to net benefit and preserving the gain where retraining helps. Ablations delimit the method: confirmation/cooldown policies and 50/50 replay retraining fail, and label-free estimators (ATC, DoC) avoid harm but sacrifice up to 60\% of the benefit --- among the gates evaluated, only the labeled probe attains both. Results are reproducible from a public artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


Machine-learning network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) are trained on historical traffic \cite{nguyen2023tsids}, yet the traffic they monitor evolves: new services, shifting user behaviour, reconfigured infrastructure and novel attacks all move the input distribution away from the training distribution, a phenomenon known as concept drift \cite{shyaa2024evolving,wahab2022intrusion,roshan2018adaptive}. As the distribution shifts, a frozen classifier loses accuracy, so operational NIDS periodically \textbf{retrain} their downstream classifier on recent traffic \cite{wahab2022intrusion,camarda2025managing,constantinides2019novel}. Retraining is not free --- it consumes labeled data and compute and briefly destabilizes a deployed model --- so the central operational question is \emph{when} to retrain.


The dominant answer frames this as a \textbf{drift-detection} problem: run a statistical monitor that compares recent traffic to a reference window, and trigger retraining when the monitor signals a change \cite{gama2004learning,rabanser2019failing,lukats2024benchmark}. A large literature accordingly competes to build more sensitive monitors, from univariate change detectors to multivariate two-sample tests such as maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) and energy distance \cite{gretton2012kernel,szekely2013energy}, and more recently to quantum-kernel similarity measures whose feature-map geometry may respond differently to distributional change \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,kaissar2026enhancing}. The implicit assumption is that a better change detector yields a better retraining policy.


This paper challenges that assumption on empirical grounds. Across three public benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT) and multiple attack regimes we find the premise beneath the whole detection-centric framing is false: \textbf{drift-triggered retraining is not always beneficial.} Its value ranges from strongly positive to negative --- for a fragile deployed model in a ToN-IoT scanning regime, \emph{no adaptation} outperforms every triggered strategy we test. Whether retraining helps is governed not by how much the distribution moved but by how much the deployed model has already degraded: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so the benefit of adapting is essentially the deployed model's headroom to that level (r $\approx$ $-$0.8 to $-$0.9 across four downstream classifiers, with a coupling-aware analysis in \S5.2). A drift monitor, which measures distributional change, cannot observe this quantity --- within a deployment its score is uninformative about the gain, which is precisely why sensitive detectors --- and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them --- fail to prevent harmful retraining. This holds even when the detector is a quantum-kernel MMD monitor rather than a classical two-sample test: the detector family is not the lever.


We therefore argue that adaptive retraining should be recast from a \emph{detection} problem to a cost-aware \emph{decision}: not "did the distribution change?" but "will retraining improve the classifier?". This reframing yields a simple, deployable solution. We propose a \textbf{validate-before-commit} gate that, on each triggered drift, retrains a candidate model but deploys it only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe drawn from current traffic. Across benefit, mixed and harmful regimes, the gate (pre-specified criteria, 30 seeds) preserves the gains of retraining where it helps, avoids harm where it does not, and significantly outperforms both naive triggering and never adapting. It is cheap and robust: as few as ~8 labeled flows per confirmed drift suffice; it tolerates validation labels up to 20 windows stale; it \emph{fails safe} under random label corruption (never harmful even with 40\% of validation labels flipped at random); and it generalizes across two drift detectors and four downstream classifiers. Label-free alternatives --- from naive disagreement to state-of-the-art accuracy estimators (ATC, DoC) --- either fail outright or sacrifice much of the benefit, showing that a few target labels, spent on the decision rather than the detector, are what buys safety without giving up the gains.


Our contributions are: (1) a regime taxonomy showing readaptation value spans benefit to net-harm across three benchmarks; (2) a coupling-aware empirical account: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so adaptation value is deployed-model headroom --- which detector scores do not track within a deployment; (3) detector-invariance evidence and an oracle-regret analysis showing the decision, not the detector --- classical or quantum --- is the lever; (4) pre-specified negatives on simple confirmation/cooldown policies and on replay retraining; and (5) a label-efficient validate-before-commit gate that resolves the problem. The remainder of the paper reviews related work (\S2), formalizes the decision and the gate (\S3), details the protocol (\S4), reports results (\S5), and discusses implications and limits (\S6--\S8).


\section{Related work}


\textbf{Concept drift and adaptive intrusion detection.} Concept drift degrades ML-based NIDS, and a body of work adapts models online or on a schedule to compensate \cite{gama2014survey,lu2019learning,shyaa2024evolving}. Most such systems assume adaptation is beneficial and focus on \emph{how} to adapt (incremental learning, windowing, ensembles) \cite{constantinides2019novel,roshan2018adaptive,shyaa2023enhanced}. We instead study \emph{whether and when} adaptation helps, and show it can be net-harmful --- connecting to, but distinct from, work on negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting in continual learning \cite{mccloskey1989catastrophic,kirkpatrick2017overcoming,zhang2023survey}, which we observe operationally in a security setting.


\textbf{Drift detection and two-sample monitors.} Drift is commonly detected with change detectors \cite{bifet2007learning,gama2004learning,baena2006early,raab2020reactive}, statistical-test ensembles over batch streams \cite{komorniczak2022ensemble}, or multivariate two-sample tests --- MMD, energy distance, Kolmogorov--Smirnov and Jensen--Shannon divergence --- used as retraining or alarm triggers \cite{gretton2012kernel,rizzo2016energy,lin1991divergence,darling1957ks}; how drift manifests (type, locality, severity) itself varies widely across streams \cite{aguiar2024locality}. Our classical baselines are drawn from this family. A complementary family of \emph{performance-aware} detectors monitors the model's error signal directly, presupposing continuous labeled feedback; Bayram et al. \cite{bayram2022degradation} survey them and formalize the distinction between distributional change and model degradation that our degradation--headroom analysis (\S5.2) quantifies empirically. We use distribution-level monitors but argue that improving them does not improve the retraining decision, because they are blind to model degradation --- and our gate concentrates the label budget at the update decision instead of spending it on continuous performance monitoring.


\textbf{Quantum kernels for drift monitoring.} Quantum-kernel methods embed data via parameterized quantum feature maps and have been proposed for classification and similarity estimation \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,huang2021power}, including exploratory use in cybersecurity \cite{gouveia2020towards,nicesio2023quantum,bellante2025evaluating}. In a companion study \cite{fernandez_paper1} we found that fidelity-based quantum kernels can improve out-of-distribution robustness under distribution shift, but that the operative property is the kernel's geometry rather than its quantumness --- which motivated evaluating quantum-kernel MMD as a drift monitor here. The present paper reaches the analogous conclusion one level up: for the \emph{retraining decision}, the detector family --- quantum or classical --- is not the lever either; quantum monitors act as one instrument among several and do not change the adapt/no-adapt outcome --- consistent with mounting evidence that quantum-kernel advantage claims require careful classical baselining and face fundamental concentration limits \cite{schnabel2025quantum,thanasilp2024concentration}.


\textbf{Drift, rejection and evaluation discipline in ML security.} A parallel security literature asks when a deployed classifier should no longer be trusted. Transcend \cite{jordaney2017transcend} and its revision Transcendent \cite{barbero2022transcendent} use conformal evaluation to reject individual predictions whose low credibility signals model aging; CADE \cite{yang2021cade} detects and explains drifting samples via contrastive representations; INSOMNIA \cite{andresini2021insomnia} updates a NIDS continuously with semi-supervision as drift accrues. These systems abstain on individual samples or adapt continuously; none evaluates whether a specific retrained candidate should replace the incumbent. Our gate acts at exactly that decision: it compares candidate against incumbent on current traffic and can conclude that the correct action is \emph{not to update at all} --- the case \S5.1 shows is real. TESSERACT \cite{pendlebury2019tesseract} and the ``dos and don'ts'' of Arp et al. \cite{arp2022dodonts} document how temporal and experimental bias inflate security-ML results; our pre-registered protocol with temporally ordered progressive windows and paired within-run baselines (\S4) follows that discipline.


\textbf{When to retrain: cost-aware and validation-gated updates.} Beyond detection, some work weighs the cost of adaptation or validates a candidate before deployment, e.g. in MLOps model-update pipelines and shadow/champion--challenger evaluation \cite{nath2007champion,sculley2015hidden,kreuzberger2023mlops,guissouma2023continuous}; that a well-intentioned update can be net-harmful is documented beyond security, e.g.\ in human--AI teams, where updates that raise average accuracy can still hurt end-to-end performance \cite{bansal2019updates}. Our gate instantiates this idea for streaming NIDS and, crucially, quantifies its label cost and demonstrates its necessity --- that detection and simple policies are insufficient without it. Its reliance on a small labeled probe connects to active and label-efficient learning for data streams \cite{cacciarelli2024active,krawczyk2019adaptive,liu2021comprehensive}, where labels are scarce and must be spent where they most affect the decision. A complementary line estimates a model's accuracy under distribution shift without target labels \cite{garg2022atc,guillory2021doc,deng2021labels,baek2022agreement,jiang2022disagreement,rosenfeld2023dis2}; rather than argue past it, we instantiate its two standard estimators (ATC, DoC) as gates in our own harness and find they trade benefit for safety (\S5.6).


\section{Method}


\subsection{Adaptive readaptation as a sequential decision}


We consider a deployed binary classifier $h_0$ (benign vs. attack) monitoring a stream of traffic windows $W_1, W_2, \dots$ Each window $W_t$ is a set of flows with (at evaluation time) ground-truth labels; the deployed model degrades as the traffic distribution drifts. A drift monitor $D$ produces a score $s_t = D(W_t)$ measuring divergence of $W_t$ from a reference. The standard adaptive-NIDS loop is: raise an alarm when $s_t$ exceeds a threshold, and upon a confirmed alarm \textbf{retrain} the classifier on recent data. We make explicit that this loop conflates two questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{detection:} \emph{has the distribution changed?} --- answered by $D$;
  \item \textbf{decision:} \emph{will replacing $h$ with a model retrained on the current window improve accuracy?} --- which $D$ does not answer.
\end{itemize}


Let $h$ be the incumbent model and $h'$ a candidate retrained on the current regime. Adaptation is beneficial at $t$ iff $\mathrm{acc}(h', W_t) > \mathrm{acc}(h, W_t)$. Because $D$ measures distributional change rather than the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h',\cdot)-\mathrm{acc}(h,\cdot)$, a detector-only policy commits every confirmed change, including changes for which retraining does not help. Our method (\S3.4) inserts a decision step that estimates this sign before committing.


\subsection{Drift monitors}


We use two-sample drift monitors comparing a reference window (from the pre-drift regime) to the current window. Classical baselines: \textbf{Energy distance}, \textbf{RBF-MMD}, per-feature \textbf{Kolmogorov--Smirnov} (max reduction) and histogram \textbf{Jensen--Shannon} divergence. Quantum monitors: \textbf{quantum-kernel MMD} with \textbf{ZZ} and \textbf{Pauli-XZ} feature maps ($q_{\text{reps}}=1$, angle encoding with standardized \texttt{atan} scaling), the MMD being computed in the quantum-kernel-induced RKHS. All monitors expose a scalar score; thresholds are calibrated per detector on held-out calibration windows at a fixed quantile (0.95). The paper's claims are detector-agnostic; the quantum monitors are included as instruments and to test whether a more expressive kernel changes the decision (it does not, \S5.3).


\subsection{Progressive-drift readaptation protocol}


Each stream has 100 post-drift windows. Window $t$ is a class-balanced sample in which each class is a mixture of the reference and current pools with mixing weight $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ ("severity"), ramped linearly from 0 to 1 over the first 80 windows and held at 1 thereafter, so drift arrives gradually. On a committed adaptation, the candidate is trained on a fresh balanced labeled sample from the current regime, and the detector reference is reset to the adapted regime. The trigger policy (alarm confirmation and cooldown) is held fixed across all gate conditions, so the decision gate is the only \emph{designed} difference between arms; in the current harness each arm consumes its own pseudo-random stream realization (identical in distribution, not in realization; \S7).


\subsection{The validate-before-commit gate}


Our contribution is a decision step interposed between the trigger and the model swap. On each confirmed trigger we retrain a candidate $h'$ as usual, but deploy it only if a cheap estimate says it improves on the incumbent $h$; otherwise we keep $h$ (and keep the detector reference, so a later window can re-propose once adaptation becomes worthwhile). We evaluate three gates:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{none} (naive):} always commit --- the standard loop.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{labeled\_probe}$(b,\varepsilon)$:} draw a small balanced labeled probe $P$ of $b$ flows from the current window; commit iff $\mathrm{BA}(h',P) \ge \mathrm{BA}(h,P) + \varepsilon$. The probe is the method's incremental labeling cost; we report $b\in\{32,64\}$, $\varepsilon=0$.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{unsup\_disagree}$(\tau)$:} commit iff the fraction of flows on which $h$ and $h'$ disagree on the \emph{unlabeled} current window is $\ge \tau$ (a zero-label proxy for "adaptation would change decisions"); we report $\tau=0.15$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm 1: Gated progressive readaptation (one stream)
  h <- h0 ; D <- fit_reference(pre-drift) ; alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- 0
  for t = 1..T:
      W_t <- sample_window(sev(t))          # balanced, labeled
      record BA(h, W_t)
      s_t <- D.score(W_t) ; alarms.append(s_t > threshold)
      trigger <- policy(alarms) and cooldown == 0
      if trigger:
          h' <- train(current-regime balanced labeled sample)      # candidate
          commit <- gate(h, h', W_t)                               # Sec 3.4
          if commit:
              h <- h' ; D <- fit_reference(current-regime)         # deploy + reset
          alarms <- [] ; cooldown <- C                             # applied either way
      else:
          cooldown <- max(0, cooldown - 1)
  return {BA(h, W_t)}_t
\end{verbatim}


The \texttt{labeled\_probe} gate directly targets the quantity \S3.1 identifies (the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$), estimated from $b$ labels; the \texttt{unsup\_disagree} gate tests whether that estimate can be obtained without labels.


\subsection{Metrics}


Per window we record balanced accuracy (BA) and attack-class F1; per stream we average over the 100 windows, and per condition over seeds. We report \textbf{gain vs. no-adaptation} (the frozen $h_0$ baseline), \textbf{committed adaptations} and \textbf{labels used}. To quantify the headroom of the decision we define, per stream and detector, a \textbf{policy oracle} that retrospectively selects, per stream, the better of the two complete policies (never adapt vs.\ detector-triggered), $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}=\mathbb{E}_{\text{seed}}\max(\mathrm{BA}_{\text{no-adapt}},\mathrm{BA}_{\text{detector}})$ --- a conservative lower bound on the value of perfect per-trigger decisions, since choosing per trigger can only do better --- and the \textbf{regret} of naive triggering $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}-\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive}}$, together with the \textbf{harm frequency} (fraction of streams on which adapting scored below no-adaptation). All comparisons use seed-matched bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (arms are matched by seed index; \S7 notes that arms draw separate stream realizations, so these are conservative rather than fully paired contrasts).


\section{Experimental protocol}


\textbf{Datasets and regimes.} CICIDS2017 \cite{sharafaldin2018toward} (Tuesday reference $\to$ Wednesday, Friday-DDoS, Friday-PortScan, Thursday-WebAttacks), UNSW-NB15 \cite{moustafa2015unsw} (DoS, Reconnaissance) and ToN-IoT \cite{alsaedi2020toniot} (Scanning), spanning benefit, marginal/mixed and harmful readaptation. Features are numeric, standardized and PCA-reduced to 8 dimensions; windows contain 128 flows. To rule out label leakage, identifier and label-derived columns are removed at staging (UNSW: \texttt{id}, binary \texttt{label}, \texttt{attack\_cat}; ToN-IoT: \texttt{label}, \texttt{type}, timestamps and IP/uid identifiers; the CICIDS variant used ships no flow identifiers), and we verified that no remaining single feature separates the classes beyond AUC $\approx$ 0.81 --- far from the near-perfect separation a leaked label would produce. CICIDS2017 has documented traffic-generation and labeling artifacts \cite{engelen2021troubleshooting}; we therefore treat it as the benefit end of a three-benchmark spectrum rather than as sole evidence --- the paper's central negative results (the marginal and harmful regimes) come from the independent UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT benchmarks.


\textbf{Runs.} The regime taxonomy and detector operating points use 30 seeds per regime. The Phase-2 gate evaluation uses \textbf{30 seeds} across three representative regimes (PortScan/benefit, UNSW-Reconnaissance/mixed, ToN-IoT-Scanning/harm) and two detectors (KS-max, QK-ZZ), with the trigger fixed at legacy confirmation ($k{=}3$ consecutive, cooldown 10). Fresh no-adaptation and naive baselines are generated within every run for seed-matched comparison.


\textbf{Protocol detail.} The initial model trains on 2{,}000 flows per class from the reference regime; the scaler and PCA are fit on that training set only and frozen thereafter. Every candidate trains on a fresh balanced sample of 512 flows per class drawn \emph{at the current mixture severity} $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ --- never from the final regime pool --- so the candidate sees the population the deployed model faces at the trigger, not the future. Evaluation windows, candidate training sets and validation probes are independent draws (with replacement) from per-class pools of $\ge 2\times10^4$ flows --- expected probe--training row collisions are below one flow per 32-flow probe --- rather than identifier-disjoint partitions. Detector references use 256 flows per class; thresholds are the 0.95 quantile of detector scores over 30 pre-drift calibration windows; the trigger is 3 consecutive alarms with a 10-window cooldown throughout. The probe is the gate's \emph{incremental} label cost on top of the labeled retraining data (512 per class per candidate) that any labeled-retraining loop --- gated or not --- already consumes: we claim label efficiency of the \emph{decision}, not of the retraining pipeline.


\textbf{Pre-specified protocol.} The gate family (\texttt{none}, \texttt{labeled\_probe} at $b\in\{32,64\}$, \texttt{unsup\_disagree} at $\tau{=}0.15$), the three regimes, the two detectors and the success criteria (benefit preservation, harm avoidance, mixed non-degradation, label efficiency, detector-invariance) were fixed in a repository protocol document before the confirmatory run; no gates, thresholds, datasets or regimes were added post hoc. Two honesty notes: the protocol fixed 10 confirmatory seeds, and after all criteria passed at 10 every cell was expanded to 30 seeds for the reported estimates --- a conditional expansion we report as such; and the protocol is a repository document rather than an external registry entry, so its prospective status cannot be verified from the public history alone. A prior pre-registered policy grid (k-of-n confirmation and cooldown, no decision gate) is reported as a negative baseline (Table~\ref{tab:table5_phase1_negative}, Appendix).


\section{Results}


We present results in logical, not chronological, order. Section 5.1 establishes that readaptation is beneficial on CICIDS2017 but \emph{not} on the external benchmarks; 5.2 explains the pattern with a coupling-aware degradation--headroom analysis; 5.3 shows the detector --- classical or quantum --- is not the lever; 5.4 shows simple policies do not fix it; 5.5 presents the label-efficient gate that does; 5.6 probes the gate's robustness, 5.7 its generalization across downstream classifiers, 5.8 the remaining controls, and 5.9 a registered replication of the confirmatory results under a hardened harness, including a per-trigger test of the mechanism.

Under progressive drift, an unadapted classifier degrades and triggered readaptation can recover much of the loss --- on CICIDS2017. Averaged over 30 seeds and 100 post-drift windows, the best detector-triggered strategy recovers +19.5 balanced-accuracy (BA) points over no-adaptation in the Friday-DDoS regime, +11.9 in Wednesday, +7.6 in WebAttacks and +6.2 in PortScan (Table~\ref{tab:table1_regime_taxonomy}, Fig.~\ref{fig:1}). This is the familiar picture that motivates adaptive NIDS. (The per-regime \emph{best} detector is descriptive, not a tuned choice: with a single pre-specified detector, KS-max, the taxonomy is unchanged --- +5.5 to +18.6 BA points across the four CICIDS regimes, +1.5 and +0.2 on UNSW-NB15, $-$2.4 on ToN-IoT.)


The picture does not survive to independent data. On UNSW-NB15 the best recovery is marginal (+1.4 points for DoS, +0.5 for Reconnaissance), and on ToN-IoT Scanning it becomes \emph{negative} for the SVC-RBF downstream used here: no detector-triggered strategy matches a no-adaptation baseline (best adaptive $-$0.5 points; worst $-$4.6), so the correct decision there is \textbf{not to adapt} (Fig.~\ref{fig:1}). This net-harm is downstream-dependent --- it does not appear for more robust classifiers --- while the degradation law that governs it is not (\S5.7). The effect is not a balanced-accuracy artifact: under attack-F1 the CICIDS benefits grow (e.g. +32.3 F1 points for DDoS) and ToN-IoT remains harmful for every detector (Energy $-$0.2, KS-max $-$4.6 F1 points). We stress the claim is not "adaptation usually hurts" --- it is that the \emph{sign} of the payoff is regime- and dataset-dependent, spanning +19.5 to $-$4.6 BA points, which is precisely what an adaptive system must anticipate.


\subsection{The value of readaptation is governed by model degradation, not distribution change}


\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{docs/img/fig2_mechanism_law.png}
\caption{The benefit of adaptation tracks deployed-model degradation (SVC-RBF, $r=-0.89$).}
\label{fig:2}
\end{figure*}
Why does adaptation help enormously in one regime and hurt in another? The answer is not the amount of drift but the state of the deployed model, and we establish it with two facts --- stated carefully, because the naive statistic invites a coupling objection. First, \textbf{retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level}: across the seven SVC-RBF regimes, post-adaptation BA varies far less than the deployed-model BA it replaces ($\sigma$ = 3.5 vs.\ 7.4 points; regressing the gain on deployed BA gives slope $-$0.87). The benefit of adapting is therefore essentially the deployed model's \emph{headroom} to that restored level: r = $-$0.89 between deployed-model BA and gain (Fig.~\ref{fig:2}), r = $-$0.81 (bootstrap CI95 [$-$0.94, $-$0.59], 16 model$\times$regime points) pooled across all four downstream classifiers (Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}). We deliberately do not rest the argument on the correlation coefficient itself: since the gain subtracts deployed BA, r is mathematically coupled to it, and an exhaustive re-pairing null holding both observed marginals yields a median r of $-$0.91 --- the observed value is no more extreme (p = 0.80; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). The substance is the compression of the restored level, not the coefficient. Second, and free of any coupling, \textbf{the detector's own score carries almost no information about that headroom within a deployment}: over the 30 streams of each regime, the correlation between a detector's score and the gain from its own triggered adaptations has median +0.06 (maximum 0.13) across regimes, whereas deployed-model BA tracks the gain within every regime (median $-$0.68). Across regimes, classical detector scores do co-vary with the benefit (r = 0.75--0.94) --- larger drifts both fire detectors and degrade models --- but that cross-regime association is unavailable to a deployed detector calibrated on its own reference, and the persistence policies built on those scores fail to prevent harm (\S5.4). The regularity itself is robust to disaggregation: at individual-stream level r = $-$0.91 (210 regime$\times$seed points), stable under seed resampling (CI95 [$-$0.90, $-$0.88]) and under removing any one regime (r between $-$0.72 and $-$0.92) or dataset --- with the expected range-restriction attenuation, not inversion, when all four CICIDS benefit regimes are removed at once (r = $-$0.52; Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness}). Where the deployed model has already collapsed (DDoS: 69.8\% without adaptation), retraining on a fresh window is a large gain; where it remains strong (ToN-IoT: 92.2\%), replacing a well-generalized model with a narrowly-fit current-window model is a net loss.


This exposes the core problem. Drift detectors answer \emph{"has the distribution changed?"}, but an adaptive NIDS needs \emph{"will retraining improve the classifier?"} --- and these are different questions. We quantify the gap with the per-stream policy oracle of \S3.5 (holding the detector fixed); being a policy-selection bound, it understates the headroom of perfect per-trigger decisions. The resulting \emph{regret} of naive triggering is essentially zero where adaptation always helps (Wednesday, DDoS: 0.00 points, adaptation hurt on 0\% of streams) and largest where it hurts (ToN-IoT: 3.53 points for QK-ZZ, CI95 [1.76, 5.54], adaptation hurt on 67\% of streams), scaling monotonically with harm frequency (Table~\ref{tab:table4_oracle_regret}, Fig.~\ref{fig:3}). The headroom left by naive triggering is real and concentrated exactly where the deployed model is already good.


\subsection{The detector is not the lever}

\input{tables_ieee/table2_phase2_gate_summary.tex}
Because drift detectors are blind to model degradation, improving the detector cannot solve the problem --- and it does not. Quantum-kernel MMD monitors do define distinct \emph{operating points} on CICIDS2017 (QK-MMD ZZ triggers 1.8--2.1 fewer readaptations than Energy distance at equivalent accuracy in Wednesday/DDoS, and inverts in PortScan), but at a ~114$\times$ monitoring-runtime cost, and these profiles do not replicate externally. Critically, the regime taxonomy and the harmful-adaptation outcome are identical for classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:3}, Tables 1--2): on ToN-IoT, naive triggering is net-harmful whether the trigger is KS-max ($-$1.4 points) or QK-ZZ ($-$3.7 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). The lever is the adapt/no-adapt decision, not the similarity measure that fires the trigger.


\subsection{Simple confirmation and cooldown policies do not prevent harm}


If the decision is the lever, the natural fix is a smarter trigger policy. We pre-registered a grid of alarm-confirmation (k-of-n) and cooldown policies and evaluated them against fixed success criteria (preserve benefit in PortScan; avoid harm in ToN-IoT; do not degrade UNSW). None passed: in the harmful regime the tested policies remained far below no-adaptation and did not reduce adaptations relative to a conservative legacy baseline (Table~\ref{tab:table5_phase1_negative}, Appendix). Confirmation and cooldown act on the \emph{persistence} of distributional change, which is exactly the signal that is uninformative about whether adaptation helps. This negative result motivates a policy that acts on the right quantity.


\subsection{A label-efficient validate-before-commit gate resolves the problem}


\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{docs/img/fig4_phase2_gate.png}
\caption{The validate-before-commit gate preserves benefit and avoids harm, identically for a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector.}
\label{fig:4}
\end{figure*}
We introduce a decision gate that acts directly on estimated model improvement. On each drift trigger, a candidate model is retrained as usual, but it is \textbf{deployed only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe} drawn from the current window (budget 32 or 64 flows); otherwise the incumbent is kept. We evaluate the gate against the same pre-registered criteria (30 seeds, 100 windows, legacy trigger fixed so the gate is the only designed difference) across the benefit (PortScan), mixed (UNSW Reconnaissance) and harmful (ToN-IoT) regimes, for both a classical (KS-max) and a quantum (QK-ZZ) detector (Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}, Fig.~\ref{fig:4}).

\input{tables_ieee/table3_phase2_paired_ci.tex}
The gate passes every criterion for both detectors. In the harmful regime it turns net-harmful naive triggering into net benefit: ToN-IoT gain moves from $-$1.36 (KS-max) and $-$3.69 (QK-ZZ) points to +0.93 and +1.06, \textbf{significantly above naive triggering} (+2.30 points, CI95 [1.15, 3.63] for KS-max; +4.74, [2.47, 7.69] for QK-ZZ; Table~\ref{tab:table3_phase2_paired_ci}) and, notably, \textbf{significantly above no-adaptation itself} (+0.93, [0.53, 1.36]; +1.06, [0.77, 1.40]) --- the gate does not merely stop adapting, it commits the minority of beneficial adaptations while rejecting the harmful majority. In the benefit regime it preserves the full gain (PortScan +8.3/+8.4 points, statistically indistinguishable from naive; formally, benefit preservation was pre-registered as \emph{non-inferiority} at a 0.3-point margin, and the paired gate$-$naive CI95 lower bounds --- $\ge$ $-$0.12 across both detectors and both budgets --- clear it), and it does not degrade the mixed regime. The label cost is small: roughly 100--160 labeled flows over a 100-window stream at budget 32.


The gate's power comes from labels, not from the confirmation heuristic: a zero-label variant that commits on deployed-vs-candidate prediction \emph{disagreement} fails, remaining significantly below no-adaptation in the harmful regime ($-$4.95 and $-$5.26 points; Table~\ref{tab:table2_phase2_gate_summary}). A handful of target-labeled flows per confirmed drift therefore makes readaptation safe without giving up its benefit --- a property we show in \S5.6 that even state-of-the-art label-free estimators do not match. Because the gate behaves identically for the classical and quantum detectors (Fig.~\ref{fig:4}), the contribution is a detector-agnostic decision mechanism.


\subsection{Gate robustness (post-registration)}


\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{docs/img/fig5_label_budget_curve.png}
\caption{Label-efficiency frontier: gain over no-adaptation versus probe budget; a small probe suffices to avoid harm.}
\label{fig:5}
\end{figure*}
Ten extensions beyond the pre-specified design (explicitly labeled as robustness, not part of the confirmatory claim) probe the gate's limits. \emph{Label efficiency:} sweeping the probe budget from 8 to 128 flows (KS-max, 30 seeds; Fig.~\ref{fig:5}) shows the gate is remarkably label-cheap --- in the harmful regime even the smallest budget avoids harm, ~8 labeled flows per confirmed drift ($\approx$23 per stream) already lifting ToN-IoT gain from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.38, plateauing near +1.1 by budget 32--64; benefit-regime gain is flat at every budget. A few dozen labels, not hundreds, suffice. \emph{Benefit breadth:} beyond PortScan the gate preserves --- and slightly improves --- the benefit in the two strongest benefit regimes, Wednesday (naive +15.39 $\to$ +16.32) and DDoS (naive +24.81 $\to$ +25.27), never sacrificing benefit where adaptation helps, across three benefit regimes.


\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{docs/img/fig8_probe_poison.png}
\caption{The gate fails safe under randomly corrupted validation labels (up to 40\% flipped).}
\label{fig:8}
\end{figure*}
\emph{Label latency:} the probe need not be current. Drawing it from traffic labeled up to 20 windows in the past (Fig.~\ref{fig:7}) leaves the gate safe and effective at every lag --- in the harmful regime the gate holds at +0.9 to +1.1 (vs naive $-$1.36) for lags of 5, 10 and 20 windows, and the benefit regimes are essentially unchanged --- so the method tolerates realistic labeling delay rather than requiring instantaneous labels. \emph{Harm breadth:} the net-harm of naive triggering (with the fragile SVC-RBF downstream) is not confined to one ToN-IoT regime --- it also appears in ToN-IoT DDoS (naive \textbf{$-$16.81}) and Injection ($-$1.21), and the gate converts all three to net-positive (+1.26, +0.80, +0.93). \emph{Cost knob:} raising the commit margin $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed adaptations by ~30\% (e.g. PortScan 3.1$\to$2.2 commits) at equal downstream gain, giving a simple operating characteristic that trades update frequency for benefit. \emph{Randomly corrupted validation labels:} because the gate trusts a labeled probe, we test a faulty labeler (or non-adaptive attacker) that flips a uniformly random fraction of the probe labels --- the random variant of the label-flipping poisoning threat against SVM-family learners \cite{biggio2012poisoning} (Fig.~\ref{fig:8}). The gate fails safe under this corruption process: even with 40\% of validation labels corrupted --- near the point where the probe is pure noise --- it still avoids harm and still matches-or-beats naive triggering in every regime (ToN-IoT degrades gracefully from +0.93 to +0.32 but never goes negative; benefit and mixed regimes are essentially unchanged). Label noise perturbs the deployed and candidate estimates symmetrically, so it moves the gate along the naive-to-oracle line without pushing it below the safe baseline. (A candidate-aware adversary that chooses \emph{which} labels to flip is a strictly stronger threat we do not model; \S7.) \emph{Label-free estimators head-to-head:} the zero-label variant of \S5.5 is deliberately naive, so we also instantiate the two standard label-free accuracy estimators --- ATC \cite{garg2022atc} and DoC \cite{guillory2021doc} --- as gates in the same harness: each model estimates its target-window accuracy from its own confidences plus a labeled validation sample drawn at its training time, using zero target-window labels. Both avoid harm --- on the fragile SVC-RBF downstream, ToN-IoT moves from $-$1.36 (naive) to +0.40 (ATC) and +1.16 (DoC) --- but both pay for safety with benefit: on PortScan they lose 24\% (ATC) and 60\% (DoC) of the naive gain ($-$1.86 [$-$3.48, $-$0.17] and $-$4.64 [$-$6.86, $-$2.26] vs naive, CI95), and they remain significantly below naive even for random forest with native predicted probabilities. The labeled probe is thus the only gate on the benefit--safety Pareto front \emph{among those evaluated}. The mechanism mirrors the degradation law: confidence-based estimates are trustworthy only while the incumbent is healthy --- exactly the regime where the gate is least needed --- and misrank incumbent against candidate once the incumbent has degraded, which is where the benefit lives; related estimators share the same calibration premise \cite{jiang2022disagreement,baek2022agreement,rosenfeld2023dis2,deng2021labels}. \emph{Replay retraining:} the harmful baseline retrains on a narrow current window and fully replaces the incumbent, so we also test the standard label-free mitigation --- retrain-current-plus-replay, augmenting each candidate's training set with an equal-size sample from the reference regime (50/50) --- under pre-fixed criteria (KS-max, SVC-RBF, 30 seeds). Replay does not rescue naive triggering: in the harm regime it remains significantly net-harmful ($-$4.54, CI95 [$-$7.06, $-$2.34] vs no-adaptation; worse than full replacement's $-$1.36, since under a still-advancing ramp a replay-anchored candidate misfits later windows even more), and in the benefit regime it \emph{loses} part of the gain (+6.59 vs +7.79 naive). The gate, by contrast, is orthogonal to the update rule: placed on top of replay it restores safety and benefit in every regime --- ToN-IoT +0.59, significantly above both no-adaptation ([0.23, 0.97]) and replay-naive (+5.13 [2.96, 7.60]); PortScan +8.26, recovering the benefit replay-naive loses (+1.66 [0.52, 3.04]). Harmful adaptation is therefore not an artifact of full-replacement retraining, and the gate's value composes with the update strategy. \emph{Probe prevalence:} the probes above are class-balanced, which presumes an enrichment mechanism, so we re-run the gate with probes whose composition follows the traffic's natural attack prevalence, conditioned on containing at least one attack flow --- an unconditioned 32-flow sample at $\pi=0.01$ contains zero attacks $\approx$72\% of the time, so the conditioning corresponds to labeling until the first attack is found, the $\approx 1/\pi$ inspections noted below (KS-max, SVC-RBF, 30 seeds). The gate is essentially indifferent: at $\pi=0.10$ (three attack flows per 32-flow probe) and even at $\pi=0.01$ (a single attack flow) it retains full safety and benefit --- ToN-IoT +1.00 [0.66, 1.39] and +1.01 [0.69, 1.40] (vs +0.93 balanced), PortScan +7.93 and +7.62 (vs +8.27 balanced; both within the pre-fixed 0.3-point margin of naive's +7.79) --- and a 10$\times$ inspection budget at $\pi=0.01$ changes nothing (+0.68/+8.00). The commit decision is a paired sign comparison on a shared probe, not a precise accuracy estimate, so the mostly-benign probe already ranks incumbent against candidate; the operative requirement is one labeled attack flow per decision ($\approx 1/\pi$ random inspections at low prevalence), not a balanced sample. \emph{Candidate size and dimensionality:} two controls address the concern that the harm is a pipeline artifact. Size-matching: training every candidate on 2{,}000 flows per class --- the same budget as the initial model, removing the 4$\times$ size asymmetry --- \emph{deepens} the harm (ToN-IoT naive $-$5.34, CI95 [$-$8.73, $-$2.49], vs $-$1.36 at 512/class): a larger candidate fits the current mixture better and mis-specializes harder while the ramp keeps advancing, the opposite of what a small-sample explanation predicts; the gate remains safe and beneficial throughout (+0.58 / +9.33 / +3.37 across the three regimes), and candidate size does shift the marginal regime's magnitude (UNSW +3.31 vs +0.92), so magnitudes are pipeline-dependent even where signs are not. Dimensionality: with the PCA bottleneck removed (full feature space, classical detector), the benefit grows (PortScan +16.2) and the harm regime's point estimate remains negative ($-$0.65 [$-$3.03, +1.44], not significant at 30 seeds) --- neither the candidate's sample size nor the 8-dimensional projection manufactures the phenomenon, though full dimensionality attenuates the harm and we report that plainly. \emph{Significance of the safety claim.} Paired bootstrap CIs in the harm regime confirm the guarantee is not noise: the gate significantly outperforms naive triggering under every label-latency and every probe-poison level tested (CI95 excludes zero), and in no robustness condition --- across downstream models, latencies or poison levels --- is it significantly \emph{worse} than either naive triggering or no-adaptation. Where the gate is not significantly better than naive (the robust downstream models, where naive is already non-harmful), it is statistically indistinguishable from it, i.e. never worse.


\subsection{Generalization across downstream classifiers}


\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{docs/img/fig6_downstream_generalization.png}
\caption{Generalization across four downstream classifiers: net-harm is specific to SVC-RBF; the gate is safe for all.}
\label{fig:6}
\end{figure*}
\input{tables_ieee/table6_downstream_generalization.tex}
To test whether the phenomenon and the solution depend on the downstream model, we repeat the three regimes with the KS-max trigger for four downstream classifiers --- SVC-RBF, random forest, logistic regression and MLP (20 seeds; Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}, Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). Three findings stand out, and we report them plainly because they refine the headline.


First, \textbf{the degradation--headroom regularity generalizes.} Across all four classifiers the benefit of adaptation tracks deployed-model degradation: where the frozen model is weak the gain is large (e.g. random forest and MLP on PortScan start at 69--70\% no-adaptation BA and gain +27 points; logistic regression on ToN-IoT starts at 84\% and gains +6.7), and where the frozen model is already strong the gain is small (random forest on ToN-IoT starts at 97\% and gains only +1.9). Pooled across all four classifiers the degradation--benefit correlation remains strong (r = $-$0.81, \S5.2; Fig.~\ref{fig:2b}): the relationship is a property of the adaptation problem, not of SVC.


Second, \textbf{whether the effect crosses into net harm is downstream-specific.} The negative-gain result --- naive triggering below no-adaptation --- occurs only for SVC-RBF on ToN-IoT ($-$1.36 points); random forest (+1.88), logistic regression (+6.70) and MLP (+0.24) all remain non-negative there (Fig.~\ref{fig:6}). SVC-RBF is fragile enough on this regime that retraining on a narrow current window loses more than it gains; more robust downstream models do not. We therefore do not claim that naive readaptation is universally harmful --- only that it can be, for fragile models, exactly where the regularity predicts the smallest headroom.


Third, and most important for deployment, \textbf{the gate is universally safe.} Across all four downstream classifiers and all three regimes (twelve settings), the labeled-probe gate is never significantly worse than no-adaptation or naive triggering; the largest observed mean deficit vs.\ naive anywhere is 0.11 BA points (random forest on UNSW Reconnaissance), within seed noise (Table~\ref{tab:table6_downstream_generalization}). This empirical safety property holds even in the settings where naive triggering is already fine (the gate simply commits), and rescues the one setting where it is harmful. The solution generalizes across downstream models even though the harm does not.


\subsection{Controls}


The findings are robust to the evaluation metric (the taxonomy and harmful-adaptation result hold under attack-F1; \S5.1). Quantum monitors do not reduce nuisance triggers on benign drift (QK-MMD ZZ 1.40 vs Energy 1.03 false triggers), so their value cannot be framed as cleaner benign-drift filtering; and an expensive Random-Forest downstream shows that quantum monitoring runtime can dominate total operational cost. A pre-registered feature-map sensitivity study finds no deeper map that unlocks a stronger quantum advantage. (Full tables for both: reproducibility artifact \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact}.) These controls reinforce, rather than qualify, the central conclusion: the detector is not the lever.


\subsection{Registered replication under a hardened harness}


The harness caveats of \S7 (per-arm stream realizations; sampling from shared pools) admit a direct answer: we rebuilt the harness and replicated the confirmatory study under it. Harness v2 pre-generates every evaluation stream from an environment generator that depends on the seed alone --- all arms process bit-identical windows, so contrasts are \emph{truly} paired --- splits every class pool into disjoint window/train/probe partitions (train/probe/evaluation overlap is impossible by construction), and gives environment, training, calibration, candidate, probe and recalibration separate generators. The replication protocol (fresh seeds 101--130, the original A/B/C margins plus detector invariance, a one-pass stopping rule) was committed and publicly tagged (\texttt{harness-v2-protocol}) \emph{before} any confirmatory seed ran, in the artifact repository \cite{fernandezbarrios2026artifact}. \textbf{Every criterion replicates.} Naive triggering remains harmful in ToN-IoT ($-$1.41, CI95 [$-$2.44, $-$0.47]); the gate converts it to +0.95 [0.62, 1.32], significantly above naive (+2.36 [1.40, 3.39]), preserves PortScan (+8.79 vs naive +7.94) and UNSW (+1.31 vs +1.22), and the pattern is detector-invariant (QK-ZZ: $-$1.58 naive $\to$ +0.91 gated). Two pre-declared extensions sharpen the contribution. First, a \emph{holdout} variant that carves the probe out of the candidate's labeled retraining batch --- consuming \textbf{zero labels beyond those any labeled-retraining loop already uses} --- is indistinguishable from the fresh-probe gate in all three regimes (ToN +0.97, PortScan +8.69, UNSW +1.31; $|\Delta| \le 0.1$), so the gate's incremental label cost can be made nil; a conservative lower-confidence-bound rule (commit only if the 90\% LCB of the per-flow correctness difference exceeds zero) is also safe but trades some benefit (ToN +0.49). Second, per-trigger logging provides the mechanism test that shares no algebraic term between predictor and outcome: at each of 252 triggers we measured the incumbent's degradation over the five \emph{preceding} windows and the candidate-minus-incumbent BA over the five \emph{following} windows. Pre-trigger degradation predicts the future value of committing in every regime (r = $-$0.65 to $-$0.70 per regime; $-$0.56 pooled), while the detector's score at the same triggers predicts nothing (r = $-$0.00 to $-$0.08). The degradation--headroom account is thus established at the level of individual decisions, on disjoint past/future windows, under the hardened harness. Two further pre-registered extras complete the picture. A \emph{performance-aware trigger} (DDM-style: 8 labeled flows per window monitor the incumbent's error; trigger on a 3$\sigma$ excursion) is safe where the incumbent stays healthy --- it never fires in ToN-IoT --- but it is benefit-lossy (PortScan +5.40 vs +7.94 naive; UNSW +0.04 vs +1.22) and spends 800 monitoring labels per stream, versus zero incremental labels for the holdout gate: labels are better spent on the commit decision than on monitoring. And on \emph{natural-prevalence evaluation streams} ($\pi=0.05$, operational metrics logged), the sign pattern replicates --- ToN-IoT naive $-$2.63 [$-$4.07, $-$1.37], gated +0.80 [0.44, 1.20]; PortScan +9.06/+9.41 --- and the gate simultaneously lowers the false-positive rate (0.145 $\to$ 0.099), raises attack recall (0.934 $\to$ 0.957) and cuts alert volume (23.2 $\to$ 17.8 alerts/window) relative to naive retraining in the harm regime.




\section{Discussion}


\textbf{Detection is not decision.} The empirical core of this paper is that optimizing a drift \emph{detector} optimizes the wrong object. A detector reports that the distribution moved; an adaptive NIDS needs to know whether retraining will help, and these come apart precisely when the deployed model is still good. Our analysis makes this concrete: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so adaptation benefit is essentially deployed-model headroom (\S5.2) --- and the same distributional shift is worth +19 balanced-accuracy points in one regime and $-$4 in another, while the detector's score says nothing about which (within-deployment r $\approx$ +0.06). No detector, however sensitive, can distinguish these cases from the window statistics alone --- which is why classical and quantum-kernel monitors, and simple confirmation/cooldown policies built on them, all fail to prevent harm (\S5.3--5.4).


\textbf{Why retraining can hurt, and why it is model-dependent.} In our protocol the candidate is retrained on a balanced, correctly-labeled sample of the current regime --- near-ideal conditions. It still degrades accuracy when the incumbent is strong, because retraining on a narrow current window replaces a broadly-generalized model with one fit to a shifting slice of traffic; the loss of generalization outweighs the gain from recency. Whether this loss actually pushes the outcome below no-adaptation depends on the downstream model's robustness: the SVC-RBF classifier is fragile enough on ToN-IoT to go net-negative, whereas random forest, logistic regression and MLP retain enough generalization to stay non-negative (\S5.7). This is not a contradiction of the regularity --- all four obey the degradation--headroom relationship --- but a refinement: net harm is the fragile-model tail of a general phenomenon. It is precisely why a decision layer that estimates \emph{actual} candidate-vs-incumbent improvement, rather than distributional change, is the right abstraction: the gate is model-agnostic by construction and, empirically, safe across all four downstream models.


\textbf{Why the gate works, and why a few labels are unavoidable.} The validate-before-commit gate succeeds because it estimates the one quantity the mechanism identifies --- the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ --- directly, from a small labeled probe, and acts on it. It does not merely suppress adaptation: in the harmful regime it \emph{beats} no-adaptation by committing the minority of genuinely beneficial updates while rejecting the harmful majority (\S5.5). The label-free alternatives are informative rather than disappointing. Naive disagreement measures how much retraining would change decisions, not whether the changes are correct, and fails outright. The state-of-the-art estimators (ATC, DoC) fail more subtly: they estimate accuracy from the incumbent's own confidences, whose trustworthiness collapses precisely when the incumbent is degraded --- the regime where adaptation is most valuable --- so they buy safety at the cost of much of the benefit (\S5.6). The labeled probe sidesteps this circularity: its validity does not depend on the model being judged. A handful of target labels per confirmed drift --- tens over a stream --- is therefore what safety \emph{without sacrificing benefit} costs; in operational NIDS, where analysts already label a small fraction of flagged traffic, this budget is realistic.


\textbf{Practitioner guidance.} The results invert the usual deployment recipe. Rather than first choosing the most sensitive (or most expensive, e.g. quantum) detector and then wiring it to automatic retraining, a practitioner should (i) determine, on held-out labeled windows, whether their setting is in a benefit or harm regime --- equivalently, whether the deployed model has headroom to lose --- and (ii) gate every adaptation on a small labeled validation probe. Under this recipe the detector becomes a coarse scheduler whose exact identity is secondary; a 114$\times$ quantum monitoring overhead buys nothing once the decision, not the detection, is the bottleneck.


\textbf{Scope: a deployment-decision rule, not a NIDS-specific fix.} Nothing in the gate is specific to network traffic. It instantiates a general champion--challenger discipline \cite{nath2007champion,guissouma2023continuous} for drift-triggered retraining: train a candidate on each trigger, estimate the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ on a small labeled probe from the current distribution, and commit only on estimated improvement. The mechanism it exploits is equally generic --- update value is governed by incumbent degradation, which distribution-level monitors cannot observe (\S5.2) and confidence-based self-assessment mismeasures exactly when it matters (\S5.6). We therefore expect the pattern to transfer to other streaming-classification deployments where labels are scarce but not absent --- fraud detection, spam and abuse filtering, predictive maintenance --- while stressing that our evidence is confined to network-security benchmarks; validating the rule beyond security is future work.


\textbf{Relation to quantum-kernel drift monitoring.} This work began as an evaluation of quantum-kernel MMD as a superior drift trigger. That hypothesis is not supported: QK-MMD defines regime-dependent operating points on one dataset that do not replicate externally, and it does not change the adapt/no-adapt outcome. We report this honestly and repurpose the quantum monitors as one instrument among several, whose invariance under the gate is part of the evidence that the decision layer, not the similarity measure, is what matters.


\section{Limitations}


Adaptation on our benchmarks retrains on labeled windows; the validate-before-commit gate accordingly assumes access to a small labeled probe at decision time, and we report its size ($\approx$32--64 flows per confirmed drift) as the realistic cost rather than treating labels as free. The strongest positive (benefit) evidence is concentrated in CICIDS2017; the external benchmarks provide the marginal and harmful ends of the spectrum, which is what the argument requires, but broader benefit-regime replication is future work. Quantum kernels are classically simulated, so the reported 114$\times$ monitoring overhead is a simulation figure; the PCA-8 pipeline (chosen for quantum-simulation tractability) attenuates but does not create the harm (\S5.6). The gate is evaluated at fixed per-window severity; we do model label latency (the probe may be up to 20 windows stale; \S5.6) but not asynchronous or partial labeling. The label-free estimators of \S5.6 are instantiated in their standard form; estimator-specific tuning could narrow the benefit gap we report. The net-harm result is specific to a fragile downstream classifier (SVC-RBF) on ToN-IoT and does not appear for more robust classifiers (\S5.7); we therefore frame it as the fragile-model tail of a general degradation--headroom regularity rather than a universal harm claim, and note that the gate's empirical safety --- unlike the harm --- generalizes across the four downstream models tested. Several further boundaries should be read into the claims. Evaluation windows are class-balanced (balanced accuracy is prevalence-neutral), and alert volumes and false-positive costs under extreme operational imbalance are untested; the probe, however, need not be balanced --- drawn at natural prevalence ($\pi$ = 0.10 or 0.01, \S5.6) the gate retains full safety and benefit, making the operative requirement one labeled attack flow per decision ($\approx 1/\pi$ random inspections at low prevalence) rather than a balanced sample. Label latency is modeled for the probe but not for the candidate's training labels. The probe-corruption experiment flips labels uniformly at random; a candidate-aware adversary that selects which labels to flip is strictly stronger and untested. The drift trajectories are gradual mixing ramps between real regime pools --- covariate/regime drift; abrupt, recurrent, or $p(y\,|\,x)$-isolated drift is not exercised. Seeds quantify within-regime sampling variability, not new environments: the external units are three datasets and seven regimes, and confidence intervals should be read at that granularity (Table~\ref{tab:table7_mechanism_law_robustness} reports the leave-one-out behaviour). Two harness-level caveats: policy arms matched by seed draw separate pseudo-random stream realizations (identical in distribution, not in realization), so seed-matched contrasts are between equivalent, not identical, streams; and samples are drawn with replacement from shared per-class pools rather than identifier-disjoint partitions (expected probe--training collisions below one flow per probe, \S4). Both caveats apply to the initial study only: the registered replication of \S5.9 re-runs the confirmatory design on a common-stream, partition-disjoint harness with fresh seeds and reproduces every criterion. Post-registration extensions are descriptive robustness checks without multiplicity correction; confirmatory claims rest solely on the pre-registered criteria. Finally, the harmful baseline is the standard full-replacement retraining loop; the standard label-free mitigation --- retrain-current-plus-replay --- does not rescue it (it remains net-harmful in the harm regime and loses benefit elsewhere, while the gate restores both on top of it; \S5.6), but richer update strategies (ensembles, incremental or weighted training, dynamic windows) remain untested, as are broader deployment settings beyond the four classifiers and three gate regimes tested.


\section{Conclusion}


Adaptive network intrusion detection is usually framed as a detection problem: monitor for drift, retrain when it fires. We showed that this framing is incomplete and sometimes harmful --- across three benchmarks the value of readaptation ranges from strongly beneficial to net-negative, governed by deployed-model degradation rather than by distributional change, and invisible to the detector. Consequently, neither more expressive detectors (including quantum kernels) nor simple confirmation/cooldown policies prevent harmful adaptation. Recast as a cost-aware decision, the problem admits a simple, deployable solution: a validate-before-commit gate that retrains a candidate but deploys it only if a small labeled probe confirms an improvement. The gate preserves the benefit of adaptation where it helps, avoids harm where it does not, and significantly outperforms both naive triggering and never adapting --- for a classical and a quantum detector alike --- with roughly one hundred labeled flows. \emph{When not to retrain} is as important as detecting drift, and a few labels, spent on the decision rather than the detector, are enough to know the difference.
