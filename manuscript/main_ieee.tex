\begin{abstract}
Adaptive network intrusion detection systems retrain when a drift monitor fires, yet a drift alarm only \emph{proposes} a challenger --- it does not establish that the challenger should replace the deployed incumbent. We decompose adaptive model updating into drift triggering, candidate construction, evidence comparability, candidate validation, promotion, and future deployment outcome, and evaluate it through staged, preregistered controls on three public benchmarks (CICIDS2017, UNSW-NB15, ToN-IoT). Two candidate-construction factors dominated the apparent value of promotion. First, incumbent-owned frozen preprocessing strongly amplified apparent promotion harm: with self-contained challenger pipelines the previously observed mean full-drift harm does not persist, and always-deploy updating is beneficial in all three benchmarks. Second, candidate evidence size explains the residual zero-drift mean harm: challengers trained on 512 flows per class degrade a healthy incumbent on average, whereas at nominal 2{,}000-per-class sample-size parity always-deploy is mean-equivalent to never adapting within the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point margin, and point/strict validation gates add no detectable average benefit under that zero-drift control (registered outcome: ATTENUATION, its sign-based future-value criterion inconclusive around the near-zero mean). Thirteen chronological replays show no net harm from always deploying and tie the gates' operational trade-off to incumbent health. Validation is therefore a conditional safeguard for asymmetric or uncertain candidates, not a universal requirement; all results reproduce from a public artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


Machine-learning network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) are trained on historical traffic \cite{nguyen2023tsids}, yet the traffic they monitor evolves: new services, shifting user behaviour, reconfigured infrastructure and novel attacks all move the input distribution away from the training distribution, a phenomenon known as concept drift \cite{shyaa2024evolving,wahab2022intrusion,roshan2018adaptive}. As the distribution shifts, a frozen classifier loses accuracy, so operational NIDS periodically \textbf{retrain} their downstream classifier on recent traffic \cite{wahab2022intrusion,camarda2025managing,constantinides2019novel}. The dominant answer to \emph{when} frames this as a \textbf{drift-detection} problem: run a statistical monitor that compares recent traffic to a reference window, and retrain when the monitor signals a change \cite{gama2004learning,rabanser2019failing,lukats2024benchmark}. A large literature accordingly competes to build more sensitive monitors, from univariate change detectors to multivariate two-sample tests such as maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) and energy distance \cite{gretton2012kernel,szekely2013energy}, and more recently to quantum-kernel similarity measures \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,kaissar2026enhancing}. The implicit assumption is that a better change detector yields a better retraining policy.


This paper's central claim is that drift detection and candidate promotion are different decisions, and that the promotion decision cannot be evaluated --- let alone automated --- before two upstream properties of the proposed challenger are audited: how the candidate is \emph{constructed}, and whether the \emph{evidence} behind it is comparable with the incumbent's. An alarm (or a schedule, or a false alarm) \emph{proposes} a challenger; the quality of that proposal depends on the challenger's construction and evidence, not on how far the distribution moved. In our controlled experiments, evaluations that were not symmetric between incumbent and challenger produced apparent promotion harm: challengers scored inside a frozen, incumbent-owned representation were strongly handicapped under full drift, and challengers trained on one quarter of the incumbent's per-class evidence degraded a healthy incumbent under zero drift. Once challenger pipelines were self-contained and candidate training size was matched at nominal per-class parity, no average promotion harm and no average validation benefit was detectable in the evaluated zero-drift control. Validation gates are therefore \emph{conditional} safeguards rather than universal defaults: most useful when challenger quality or incumbent health is uncertain, and potentially a drag on beneficial adaptation when incumbent degradation is already strong. Prior work has likewise framed model updating and retraining as cost--benefit decisions rather than automatic responses to detected change \cite{zliobaite2015cost,mahadevan2024cost,regol2025retrain}. The study combines an initial controlled core with preregistered corrective replications and explicitly classified external and exploratory evidence; the staged design distinguishes each factor with fresh seeds, frozen margins and machine-evaluable outcome rules.


Two empirical regularities organize the evidence. First, whether an update helps tracks not the drift magnitude but how much the deployed model has already degraded: retraining restores accuracy to a nearly regime-invariant level, so under a fixed candidate generator the benefit of an update is largely the deployed model's headroom to that level (r $\approx$ $-$0.8 to $-$0.9 across four downstream classifiers; coupling-aware analysis in Supplementary \S S1.1, per-decision hierarchical model in \S\ref{sec:v2}). A drift monitor, which measures distributional change, does not track this quantity: within triggered decisions under the evaluated detectors, score magnitude provided no consistent incremental information about the gain, and this holds even when the detector is a quantum-kernel MMD monitor rather than a classical two-sample test. Second, construction and evidence asymmetries between incumbent and challenger can dominate the promotion outcome --- the harm we originally reported traces, in its mean, to two such asymmetries, each isolated by a preregistered control (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}, \S\ref{sec:sizematched}).


Within this frame we evaluate \textbf{validate-before-commit} gating as a family of possible mechanisms, not as an axiom: on each triggered proposal, a candidate is retrained as usual and deployed only if it beats the incumbent on a small labeled probe drawn from current traffic (in the core harness a pool sample at the current mixture --- an idealization; the observed-data arm of \S\ref{sec:v2} uses only past observed windows). In the controlled balanced-probe confirmatory setting the commit decision is label-efficient in a strictly bounded sense: a 32-label point gate converts average harm into average benefit under the historical configuration, but candidates consume 1{,}024 labeled flows per trigger whether or not the gate commits --- the probe is the incremental cost of the \emph{decision}, not of the pipeline. Label-free alternatives --- from naive disagreement to state-of-the-art accuracy estimators (ATC, DoC) --- either fail outright or sacrifice much of the benefit (Supplementary \S S1.5). The program's final verdict, however, bounds the gate itself: its measured value concentrates exactly where the challenger's construction or evidence is asymmetric, and in the evaluated zero-drift control, once challengers are self-contained and trained at matched nominal per-class size, it adds nothing measurable (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}) --- validate-before-commit is a conditional control, not a universal requirement.


Building on active-testing and limited-label model-selection research \cite{sawade2012active,kossen2021active,karimi2021online,han2024model,okanovic2025models}, we integrate paired incumbent--challenger evaluation into a drift-triggered NIDS loop and isolate the upstream construction and evidence conditions that determine when validation has value. Our contributions are four. \textbf{(1) A candidate-governance decomposition} that separates the loop's stages --- \emph{drift trigger} $\to$ \emph{candidate construction} $\to$ \emph{evidence comparability} $\to$ \emph{candidate validation} $\to$ \emph{promotion} $\to$ \emph{future deployment outcome} (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}) --- and shows each answers a distinct question: an alarm proposes; construction determines whether the challenger \emph{can} compete under comparable conditions; evidence comparability determines whether the comparison is informative; validation estimates the sign of the specific proposal; and promotion is the decision a healthy incumbent can win. \textbf{(2) A staged controlled study} showing that preprocessing ownership and candidate evidence size can dominate the apparent benefit or harm of adaptive promotion: the mean full-drift harm does not persist once each challenger owns its preprocessing (a role-randomized experiment localizes the mechanism to feature scaling), and training challengers on one quarter of the incumbent's per-class sample size explains the residual zero-drift mean harm --- under that zero-drift control, self-contained challengers at nominal 2{,}000-per-class sample-size parity are mean-equivalent to never adapting in all three benchmarks within the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point margin (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}, \S\ref{sec:sizematched}). \textbf{(3) Evidence that validation gates have conditional rather than universal average value}: point/strict validation materially helps when the challenger's construction or evidence is asymmetric (frozen preprocessing, or a 4$\times$ nominal-evidence disadvantage), and provides no detectable mean benefit in the evaluated symmetric size-matched zero-drift control (\S\ref{sec:gatevalue}). \textbf{(4) Formal, chronological and operational boundary analyses}: the sequential VBC-SG family formalizes probe-level false-superiority control and prices its label, abstention and latency costs under the historical frozen policy; thirteen chronological replays (no net harm observed) tie the operational trade-off to incumbent health; and an acquisition-yield simulation bounds attack-label discovery cost (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}, \S\ref{sec:risk}). The paper proceeds: related work (\S2), the decision pipeline and gates (\S3), experimental design (\S4), results (\S5), implications and limits (\S6--\S8).


\section{Related work}


\textbf{Concept drift and adaptive intrusion detection.} Concept drift degrades ML-based NIDS, and a body of work adapts models online or on a schedule to compensate \cite{gama2014survey,lu2019learning,shyaa2024evolving}. Most such systems assume adaptation is beneficial and focus on \emph{how} to adapt (incremental learning, windowing, ensembles) \cite{constantinides2019novel,roshan2018adaptive,shyaa2023enhanced}. We instead study \emph{whether and when} adaptation helps, and show it can be net-harmful --- connecting to, but distinct from, work on negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting in continual learning \cite{mccloskey1989catastrophic,kirkpatrick2017overcoming,zhang2023survey}, which we observe operationally in a security setting.


\textbf{Drift detection and two-sample monitors.} Drift is commonly detected with change detectors \cite{bifet2007learning,gama2004learning,baena2006early,raab2020reactive}, statistical-test ensembles over batch streams \cite{komorniczak2022ensemble}, or multivariate two-sample tests --- MMD, energy distance, Kolmogorov--Smirnov and Jensen--Shannon divergence --- used as retraining or alarm triggers \cite{gretton2012kernel,rizzo2016energy,lin1991divergence,darling1957ks}; how drift manifests (type, locality, severity) itself varies widely across streams \cite{aguiar2024locality}. Our classical baselines are drawn from this family. A complementary family of \emph{performance-aware} detectors monitors the model's error signal directly, presupposing continuous labeled feedback; Bayram et al. \cite{bayram2022degradation} survey them and formalize the distinction between distributional change and model degradation that our degradation--headroom analysis (Supplementary \S S1.1) quantifies empirically. We use distribution-level monitors and find that improving them did not improve the retraining decision in any regime we evaluated, because they are blind to model degradation --- and our gate concentrates the label budget at the update decision instead of spending it on continuous performance monitoring.


\textbf{Quantum kernels for drift monitoring.} Quantum-kernel methods embed data via parameterized quantum feature maps and have been proposed for classification and similarity estimation \cite{havlicek2019supervised,schuld2019quantum,huang2021power}, including exploratory use in cybersecurity \cite{gouveia2020towards,nicesio2023quantum,bellante2025evaluating}. Evidence that fidelity-based quantum kernels can improve out-of-distribution robustness under shift --- the operative property being the kernel's geometry rather than its quantumness --- motivated evaluating quantum-kernel MMD as a drift monitor here. The present paper reaches the analogous conclusion one level up: for the \emph{update decision}, changing the detector family --- quantum or classical --- did not change the outcome in any regime we evaluated --- consistent with evidence that quantum-kernel advantage claims require careful classical baselining and face concentration limits \cite{schnabel2025quantum,thanasilp2024concentration}.


\textbf{Drift, rejection and evaluation discipline in ML security.} A parallel security literature asks when a deployed classifier should no longer be trusted. Transcend \cite{jordaney2017transcend} and its revision Transcendent \cite{barbero2022transcendent} use conformal evaluation to reject individual predictions whose low credibility signals model aging; CADE \cite{yang2021cade} detects and explains drifting samples via contrastive representations; INSOMNIA \cite{andresini2021insomnia} updates a NIDS continuously with semi-supervision as drift accrues. These systems abstain on individual samples or adapt continuously; none evaluates whether a specific retrained candidate should replace the incumbent. Our gates act at exactly that decision: they compare candidate against incumbent on current traffic and can conclude that the correct action is \emph{not to update at all} --- the case Supplementary \S S1.1 shows is real. TESSERACT \cite{pendlebury2019tesseract} and the ``dos and don'ts'' of Arp et al. \cite{arp2022dodonts} document how temporal and experimental bias inflate security-ML results; our pre-specified protocol with temporally ordered progressive windows and paired within-run baselines (\S4) follows that discipline.


\textbf{When to retrain: cost-aware and validation-gated updates.} Beyond detection, some work weighs the cost of adaptation or validates a candidate before deployment, e.g. in MLOps model-update pipelines and shadow/champion--challenger evaluation \cite{nath2007champion,sculley2015hidden,kreuzberger2023mlops,guissouma2023continuous}; that a well-intentioned update can be net-harmful is documented beyond security \cite{bansal2019updates}. Our gates instantiate this idea for streaming NIDS, quantify its label cost, and delimit \emph{when} the validation layer earns its keep: under construction or evidence asymmetry, not universally. Their reliance on a small labeled probe connects to active and label-efficient learning for data streams \cite{cacciarelli2024active,krawczyk2019adaptive,liu2021comprehensive}. A complementary line estimates a model's accuracy under distribution shift without target labels \cite{garg2022atc,guillory2021doc,deng2021labels,baek2022agreement,jiang2022disagreement,rosenfeld2023dis2}; rather than argue past it, we instantiate its two standard estimators (ATC, DoC) as gates in our own harness and find they trade benefit for safety (Supplementary \S S1.5).

\textbf{Retraining as a decision.} Beyond drift detection, prior work treats model updating as a decision with its own cost and performance trade-offs. Cost-sensitive adaptation, cost-aware retraining and recent retraining-decision methods ask whether and when replacing or rebuilding the deployed model is worthwhile \cite{zliobaite2015cost,mahadevan2024cost,regol2025retrain}. These approaches primarily address adaptation scheduling or retraining utility; our setting starts after an alarm has produced a concrete challenger and asks whether that particular pipeline is sufficiently comparable to, and should replace, the incumbent.

\textbf{Active comparison and limited-label model selection.} A complementary literature studies how scarce labels can support model evaluation and selection. Active comparison and active testing allocate labels to compare predictors efficiently \cite{sawade2012active,kossen2021active}, while online and target-domain model-selection methods identify a model for deployment from limited labeled evidence, including under temporal distribution shift \cite{karimi2021online,han2024model,okanovic2025models}. We build on this logic but study a different systems setting: a newly trained challenger inside a drift-triggered NIDS loop, where preprocessing ownership, candidate evidence and incumbent health determine whether the paired validation decision is meaningful.


\section{Candidate promotion as a decision pipeline}


\subsection{Promotion as a sequential decision}


We consider a deployed binary classifier $h_0$ (benign vs. attack) monitoring a stream of traffic windows $W_1, W_2, \dots$ Each window $W_t$ is a set of flows with (at evaluation time) ground-truth labels; the deployed model degrades as the traffic distribution drifts. A drift monitor $D$ produces a score $s_t = D(W_t)$ measuring divergence of $W_t$ from a reference. The standard adaptive-NIDS loop is: raise an alarm when $s_t$ exceeds a threshold, and upon a confirmed alarm \textbf{retrain} the classifier on recent data. We make explicit that this loop conflates two questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{detection:} \emph{has the distribution changed?} --- answered by $D$;
  \item \textbf{decision:} \emph{will replacing $h$ with a model retrained on the current window improve accuracy?} --- which $D$ does not answer.
\end{itemize}


Let $h$ be the incumbent model and $h'$ a candidate retrained on the current regime. Adaptation is beneficial at $t$ iff $\mathrm{acc}(h', W_t) > \mathrm{acc}(h, W_t)$. Because $D$ measures distributional change rather than the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h',\cdot)-\mathrm{acc}(h,\cdot)$, a detector-only policy commits every confirmed change, including changes for which retraining does not help. The full pipeline this paper decomposes and evaluates (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}) distinguishes the distributional alarm, the candidate proposal, the evidence used to \emph{train} the candidate, the evidence used to \emph{validate} it, and the evidence that determines its \emph{future deployment} value --- five different objects that the detection-centric loop treats as one. The gates of \S\ref{sec:gates} instantiate the validation stage; the controls of \S\ref{sec:construction} instantiate the construction and comparability stages.

\subsection{Drift monitors}


We use two-sample drift monitors comparing a reference window (from the pre-drift regime) to the current window. Classical baselines: \textbf{Energy distance}, \textbf{RBF-MMD}, per-feature \textbf{Kolmogorov--Smirnov} (max reduction) and histogram \textbf{Jensen--Shannon} divergence. Quantum monitors: \textbf{quantum-kernel MMD} with \textbf{ZZ} and \textbf{Pauli-XZ} feature maps ($q_{\text{reps}}=1$, angle encoding with standardized \texttt{atan} scaling), the MMD being computed in the quantum-kernel-induced RKHS. All monitors expose a scalar score; thresholds are calibrated per detector on held-out calibration windows at a fixed quantile (0.95). The paper's claims are detector-agnostic; the quantum monitors are instruments testing whether a more expressive kernel changes the decision (it does not; Supplementary \S S1.2).


\subsection{Progressive-drift readaptation protocol}


Each stream has 100 post-drift windows. Window $t$ is a class-balanced sample in which each class is a mixture of the reference and current pools with mixing weight $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ ("severity"), ramped linearly from 0 to 1 over the first 80 windows and held at 1 thereafter, so drift arrives gradually. On a committed adaptation, the candidate is trained on a fresh balanced labeled sample from the current regime, and the detector reference is reset to the adapted regime. The trigger policy (alarm confirmation and cooldown) is held fixed across all gate conditions, so the decision gate is the only \emph{designed} difference between arms. On the confirmatory harness (v2) every arm additionally processes \emph{bit-identical} streams, so contrasts are exactly paired; only the initial exploratory study drew a separate pseudo-random realization per arm (identical in distribution, not in realization; \S4).


\subsection{The validate-before-commit gate}
\label{sec:gates}


The gate is a decision step interposed between the trigger and the model swap: a \emph{deployment} (commit) gate, not a retraining gate. On each confirmed trigger the loop retrains a candidate $h'$ as usual --- consuming its training labels and compute regardless of the outcome --- and the gate then decides only whether $h'$ replaces the incumbent $h$; otherwise we keep $h$ (and keep the detector reference, so a later window can re-propose once an update becomes worthwhile). \S\ref{sec:v2} additionally evaluates a two-stage variant that moves the same probe \emph{before} candidate training, gating the training decision itself. We evaluate three commit gates:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{none} (naive):} always commit --- the standard loop.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{labeled\_probe}$(b,\varepsilon)$:} draw a small balanced labeled probe $P$ of $b$ flows from the current window; commit iff $\mathrm{BA}(h',P) \ge \mathrm{BA}(h,P) + \varepsilon$. The probe is the method's incremental labeling cost; we report $b\in\{32,64\}$, $\varepsilon=0$.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{unsup\_disagree}$(\tau)$:} commit iff the fraction of flows on which $h$ and $h'$ disagree on the \emph{unlabeled} current window is $\ge \tau$ (a zero-label proxy for "adaptation would change decisions"); we report $\tau=0.15$.
\end{itemize}

We evaluate and state both variants, which differ in exactly the information they are allowed to use. Algorithm 1A is the \emph{core simulation} gate: the environment supplies candidate batches and probes from its class pools at the current mixture severity --- convenient, and the source of the paper's main tables, but an oracle a deployment lacks. Algorithm 1B is the \emph{observed-data} gate: every object it touches --- the candidate's training rows, the probe, and even the detector's post-commit reference --- comes from windows the system has actually seen, and nothing reads $\mathrm{sev}(t)$. \S\ref{sec:v2} reports 1B as a registered replication of 1A.

The \texttt{labeled\_probe} gate directly targets the quantity \S3.1 identifies (the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$), estimated from $b$ labels; the \texttt{unsup\_disagree} gate tests whether that estimate can be obtained without labels.


\textbf{A cost-sensitive reading: two decisions, two thresholds.} The update loop makes two sequential decisions with different cost structures. Let each error cost $\lambda_e$ per flow, deploying an update $\lambda_U$, training a candidate $\lambda_C$ (compute plus training labels), and probe labels $\lambda_L$ each. \emph{At commit time the candidate already exists}: $\lambda_C$ and the probe are sunk, so over a horizon of $N$ post-decision flows the myopic commit rule is
$$\mathrm{commit} \iff \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h',P) - \widehat{\mathrm{BA}}(h,P) \ge \varepsilon^{\ast}, \qquad \varepsilon^{\ast} = \frac{\lambda_U}{\lambda_e N},$$
--- the margin $\varepsilon$ is the \emph{deployment} cost expressed in accuracy points over the remaining horizon, and $\lambda_C$ does not belong in it, because rejecting the candidate cannot recover it. Training costs enter one decision earlier --- whether to build a candidate at all, which pays off only if the \emph{expected} committed gain exceeds $(\lambda_C + \lambda_U)/(\lambda_e N)$; the two-stage gate of \S\ref{sec:v2} instantiates exactly this structure, spending the probe on the incumbent \emph{before} training. A risk-averse operator replaces the point estimate with a lower confidence bound (the LCB variant of \S\ref{sec:v2}); raising $\varepsilon$ from 0 to 0.01--0.02 cuts committed updates by ${\sim}30\%$ at equal downstream gain (Supplementary \S S1.5), and asymmetric error costs substitute a weighted accuracy for BA in the same rule.


\subsection{Risk-controlled commit gates and the scope of their guarantee}
\label{sec:riskgates}

The point gate above ($\varepsilon{=}0$) \emph{maximizes the probe point-estimate} --- not future accuracy, as the strict-comparison baseline below shows a more conservative rule can win downstream: it commits on any observed probe advantage and carries no formal error control (about a quarter of its commits have negative realized value; \S\ref{sec:v2}). We separately study \emph{risk-controlled} gates that commit only on statistical evidence. Let the probe be $b$ flows drawn from a distribution $\mathcal P$, and for each flow let
$$d_i = \mathbf 1[h'\text{ correct on }i] - \mathbf 1[h\text{ correct on }i]\in\{-1,0,+1\}.$$
Three rules: (i) \textbf{exact McNemar} \cite{mcnemar1947note} --- commit iff a one-sided exact test on the discordant pairs rejects $H_0:\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0$ at level $\alpha$ (ties never commit); (ii) \textbf{Robbins CS} --- a normal-mixture (sub-Gaussian, $\sigma^2\le1$) lower confidence sequence \cite{howard2021timeuniform} on $\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]$, commit at the first look whose lower bound exceeds $0$; (iii) \textbf{empirical-Bernstein CS} --- a predictable-plug-in betting confidence sequence \cite{waudbysmith2023betting} using the empirical variance of $d_i$ (far tighter, since the $d_i$ are mostly ties). The CS gates inspect in blocks (16 flows) up to a budget $b$; if the lower bound never clears $0$ they do \emph{not} commit (the risk-averse default), so at the maximum budget an undecided proposal is rejected. Because the probe is drawn with \emph{fixed class quotas} ($b/2$ per class) rather than i.i.d.\ from one distribution, the pooled tests above match the sampling only approximately; \emph{stratified} variants close that gap by lower-bounding each class's mean correctness difference at $\alpha/2$ (Bonferroni over the two classes) and committing iff $\tfrac12(L_{\mathrm{ben}}+L_{\mathrm{att}})>0$, which bounds the false-superiority probability for the \emph{balanced-accuracy} difference under the stratified design actually used. We evaluate a \emph{fixed-sample} version (per-class one-sided normal LCB; no sequential validity) and the \emph{anytime-valid} version, in which each class's bound is an empirical-Bernstein confidence sequence inspected at the same sequential looks as the pooled gate; only the latter supports the anytime-valid claim. \textbf{Levels used.} The confidence sequences run at $\alpha=0.10$; the exact-McNemar gate at its registered $\alpha=0.05$ (stricter, hence if anything \emph{more} conservative at zero drift); re-running McNemar at $\alpha=0.10$ leaves the zero-drift conclusion unchanged ($\approx 0$ commits; \S\ref{sec:risk}). The effective per-arm $\alpha$ is recorded in the run manifest.

\textbf{VBC-SG: a formal option for settings in which validation is chosen.} The rules above form a family --- a sequential decision-support instantiation for settings in which validation is chosen --- and what this paper contributes is that family together with the frontier it spans --- \emph{not} a claim that any one member dominates, not a claim of fundamental mathematical novelty, and not the source of the paper's final verdict. We name its maximally-guaranteed member, the \emph{Validate-Before-Commit Sequential Gate}, because it is the configuration that carries every layer of control at once; \S\ref{sec:risk} then shows what each layer costs, and that a \emph{less} stratified sibling and even the no-additional-label strict rule are the better buy at some operating points. VBC-SG assembles the pieces as follows: per-class empirical-Bernstein confidence sequences (the stratified anytime-valid bounds above) drive a three-action decision --- \textsc{commit} when the balanced-accuracy lower bound clears zero, \textsc{reject} when the upper bound falls below zero (futility), and \textsc{defer} otherwise: the candidate is retained and evidence continues at subsequent windows, up to a deferral cap, rejecting at the cap. Deferral raises a question this gate must answer explicitly: once the stream drifts \emph{between} windows, what does a sequence that keeps acquiring labels at later windows estimate? Three registered continuation modes make the answer precise. \emph{Cohort-sim} (fixed-target): additional labels are resampled from the proposal-time target distribution available to the simulator (the mixture the proposal was raised on), so the sequence estimates one fixed mean and the per-proposal guarantee is the clean fixed-target one. Cohort-sim does not model retention and delayed adjudication of a literal production cohort, so this acquisition scheme is not claimed to be directly deployable. \emph{Refresh}: each deferral window opens a \emph{fresh} evidence set tested at $\alpha_j/(1+D)$ (Bonferroni over the at most $1+D$ sets a proposal with deferral cap $D$ may open), so observations from temporally different targets are never mixed. \emph{Accumulate} (retained as an ablation): the same sequences continue on labels drawn at each current window; by Proposition~1 below this mode still bounds false commits, but only under the \emph{weak conditional null} that the challenger is at no time conditionally better on the probe stream, which is why cohort and refresh are the interpretable variants under drift. A deployment-long risk budget allocates each proposal's level $\alpha_j$ by a declared spending schedule (Bonferroni $\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}/M$, or the $p$-series $\alpha_j = 6\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}/(\pi^2 j^2)$, which needs no horizon $M$); the effective $\alpha_j$ of every proposal is logged. Four properties, stated at the strength we can support: (i) \emph{per-proposal}: the probability of falsely establishing positive expected balanced-accuracy difference on the probe distribution is at most $\alpha_j$, uniformly over the sampling schedule --- under within-class i.i.d.\ sampling from the proposal's target for the cohort/refresh modes, and under the weak conditional null of Proposition~1 for the accumulate mode; (ii) \emph{deployment-long}: by the union bound over the spending schedule, the probability of \emph{ever} making such a false commit over the deployment is at most $\alpha_{\mathrm{life}}$, under the same assumptions; (iii) \emph{label cost}: labels per proposal are bounded by the per-window budget times $(1 + D)$, and the observed cost concentrates far below the bound because most proposals resolve early; (iv) \emph{transfer}: none of (i)--(iii) bounds future deployed accuracy unless the probe is representative of the evaluation horizon --- the stale-probe and dependence results quantify exactly where that premise fails. No stronger guarantee is claimed; the empirical sections report the price of each.

\textbf{Proposition 1 (conditional validity of the commit e-process).} Map each per-flow correctness difference to $x_i = (d_i+1)/2 \in [0,1]$; the empirical-Bernstein sequence implemented in the artifact is a wealth process $W_n$ with predictable betting fractions and plug-in means, whose commit condition $\mathrm{LCB}_n > 0$ is algebraically the event $W_n > 1/\alpha$. If $\mathbb{E}[d_i \mid \mathcal F_{i-1}] \le 0$ for every $i$ --- the weak null; no stationarity or identical distribution is assumed --- then $W_n$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with $W_0 = 1$, and Ville's inequality gives $\Pr(\exists n : W_n > 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$: the probability of \emph{ever} committing under the weak null is at most $\alpha$. The proof (Supplementary \S S4) is the standard testing-by-betting argument \cite{waudbysmith2023betting,howard2021timeuniform}; we claim no novelty, only the correspondence to this implementation, which a registered null simulation with time-varying conditional means and variances verifies empirically.

\textbf{What is and is not guaranteed.} The confidence-sequence gates satisfy, for a single proposal and \emph{under the probe distribution} $\mathcal P$,
$$\Pr\!\big(\exists n\le b:\ \text{LCB}_n>0 \ \big|\ \mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[d]\le 0\big)\ \le\ \alpha ,$$
i.e.\ they control the probability of \emph{falsely establishing positive expected paired correctness on the probe distribution}, uniformly over the (data-dependent) stopping time. This is \emph{not} a bound on the probability that the committed candidate lowers future balanced accuracy: the two coincide only when the probe is representative of the windows the candidate will be evaluated on, and the paper documents where that fails (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}). We therefore claim a bound on \emph{false probe-superiority}, not on future deployment regret. Without a spending schedule the guarantee is also \emph{per proposal} (union bound $M\alpha$ over $M$ tested proposals); a lifetime bound requires alpha-spending across proposals or a single deployment-long e-process --- exactly what the VBC-SG risk budget above supplies (property (ii)). \S\ref{sec:risk} reports both the empirical per-stream harmful-commit rate and the label price of the lifetime schedules.


\section{Experimental design}


\subsection{Datasets and controlled streams}


\textbf{Datasets and regimes.} CICIDS2017 \cite{sharafaldin2018toward} (Tuesday reference $\to$ Wednesday, Friday-DDoS, Friday-PortScan, Thursday-WebAttacks), UNSW-NB15 \cite{moustafa2015unsw} (DoS, Reconnaissance) and ToN-IoT \cite{alsaedi2020toniot} (Scanning), spanning benefit, marginal/mixed and harmful readaptation. Features are numeric, standardized and PCA-reduced to 8 dimensions; windows contain 128 flows. To rule out label leakage, identifier and label-derived columns are removed at staging (UNSW: \texttt{id}, binary \texttt{label}, \texttt{attack\_cat}; ToN-IoT: \texttt{label}, \texttt{type}, timestamps and IP/uid identifiers; the CICIDS variant used ships no flow identifiers), and we verified that no remaining single feature separates the classes beyond AUC $\approx$ 0.81 --- far from the near-perfect separation a leaked label would produce. CICIDS2017 has documented traffic-generation and labeling artifacts \cite{engelen2021troubleshooting}; we therefore treat it as the benefit end of a three-benchmark spectrum rather than as sole evidence --- the paper's central negative results (the marginal and harmful regimes) come from the independent UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT benchmarks.


\textbf{The confirmatory harness --- the methodology behind every headline claim.} All confirmatory results come from a harness built to make the paired contrasts exact. (i) \emph{Common streams:} every evaluation stream and calibration draw is pre-generated by an environment generator that depends on the seed alone, before any policy runs --- all arms process bit-identical windows in the same order, so gate$-$naive contrasts are truly paired and policy actions cannot perturb the environment. (ii) \emph{Role-disjoint partitions:} each per-class pool is split once per seed (50/30/20) into window/train/probe partitions; evaluation windows draw only from the first, initial models, candidates and detector references only from the second, probes only from the third --- train/probe/evaluation overlap is impossible by construction. (iii) \emph{Separate generators:} environment, initial training, detector calibration, candidate training, probe draws and post-commit recalibration each use their own RNG, seeded by (seed) or (seed, $t$) only, never advanced by policy actions. (iv) \emph{Per-trigger logging:} at every trigger the harness records pre-trigger incumbent degradation, the detector score, and incumbent-vs-candidate BA over the following 1/3/5/10 windows --- lookahead used for logging only, never by any policy. The replication protocol (criteria, margins, one-pass stopping rule) was committed and publicly tagged (\texttt{harness-v2-protocol}) before any confirmatory seed ran, with pre-run registered amendments covering seed hygiene, deduplication, baselines and analysis specifications; the confirmatory window is 30 held-out seeds (104--133), untouched by any smoke test.


\textbf{Shared protocol constants.} The initial model trains on 2{,}000 flows per class from the reference regime; the scaler and PCA are fit on that training set only and frozen thereafter. Every candidate trains on a balanced sample of 512 flows per class drawn \emph{at the current mixture severity} $\mathrm{sev}(t)$ --- never from the final regime pool --- so the candidate sees the population the deployed model faces at the trigger, not the future (a simulator convenience a production system lacks; \S7 bounds the word ``deployable'' accordingly, and the sliding-window and chronological-stream experiments provide the observed-data counterpoints). Detector references use 256 flows per class; thresholds are the 0.95 quantile of detector scores over 30 pre-drift calibration windows; the trigger is 3 consecutive alarms with a 10-window cooldown throughout; windows contain 128 flows. The probe is the gate's \emph{incremental} label cost on top of the labeled retraining data (512 per class per candidate) that any labeled-retraining loop --- gated or not --- already consumes: we claim label efficiency of the \emph{commit decision}, not of the retraining pipeline (\S\ref{sec:v2} accounts for both).


\textbf{Label and information latency: exact semantics.} Several arms model the delay between a flow being observed and its label being available, and Table~\ref{tab:latency} fixes them, read directly off the artifact. The probe knob ($L_p$) draws the probe from the stream as of window $t-L_p$; the candidate knob ($L_c$) shifts the candidate's \emph{whole} training batch --- features and labels together, as one adjudicated block: under the pool generator the batch is sampled at the mixture of window $t-L_c$, and under the sliding-window generator it is the eight observed windows ending at $t-L_c$. The observed-data probe is inherently nine windows old. Training completion is instantaneous in the confirmatory, observed-data and chronological arms; the pool-based operational sensitivity (\S\ref{sec:v2}) explicitly evaluates a five-window deployment delay, during which the incumbent keeps serving. While a deferred VBC-SG proposal is pending, the incumbent remains deployed and \emph{new proposals are blocked} --- a fresh alarm during a pending decision is absorbed exactly like a cooldown window, so a proposal is never silently replaced.

\subsection{Candidate construction policies}
\label{sec:construction}

We evaluate how preprocessing ownership and candidate evidence size alter promotion outcomes through staged, preregistered controls: each factor was isolated by a replication whose protocol, estimands, margins and interpretation rules were frozen before any of its confirmatory seeds ran. The controls were designed sequentially --- each registered after the previous one's outcome was known --- and each is preregistered relative to its own seeds; we present them here as the two levels of a construction factor rather than as a chronology.

\subsubsection{Transformer ownership: self-contained candidate pipelines}
\label{sec:symmetric_method}

The historical convention fits scaler and PCA once, on the initial training set, and \emph{freezes} them --- every later challenger is trained and scored inside that initial representation (\texttt{frozen\_initial\_transformer}). The role-randomized control of \S\ref{sec:risk} shows this convention is not neutral for SVC-RBF, so the decisive question is dynamic: does the harmful-update result survive when each challenger is a \emph{self-contained pipeline}? A preregistered replication answers it; protocol, estimands, margins and interpretation rules were frozen before any confirmatory seed ran (in the artifact).

\textbf{Design.} The environment emits a \emph{raw} stream: per seed, the same windows are served to every arm, hash-verified bit-identical, with no global transformation. Each model is a \texttt{ModelPipeline} (scaler + PCA + classifier + provenance metadata) that receives raw flows and transforms internally; the drift monitor is a separate \texttt{DetectorPipeline} whose representation stays the initial transformer under \emph{both} policies, so candidate-construction policy is never confounded with monitoring policy. The two transformer policies differ in exactly one intervention: \texttt{frozen\_initial\_transformer} reuses the initial scaler/PCA for every candidate (reproducing the historical harness bit-for-bit against the published v1.20.2 outputs), while \texttt{own\_transformer\_per\_model} fits each challenger's scaler on its own candidate batch only and its PCA on the scaled batch only --- same dimension, solver and classifier hyperparameters; raw candidate batches are bit-identical across policies, the probe is served raw to both pipelines, and neither probe nor future windows enter any fit. A commit deploys the \emph{complete bundle}, serving from window $t{+}1$; scaler/PCA parameter hashes and the SVC's fitted effective $\gamma$ are recorded per candidate.

\textbf{Estimands and inference.} Fresh confirmatory seeds 3001--3030 (never used before; smoke and parity used disjoint seeds), six scenarios (PortScan, UNSW-Recon, ToN-IoT $\times$ full and zero drift), KS-max only, policies never/naive/point/strict. The registered estimands are $\Delta_{\text{naive,own}} = \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,own}} - \mathrm{BA}_{\text{never}}$ (does harm persist?), $\Delta_{\text{transformer}} = \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,own}} - \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,frozen}}$ (the ownership interaction --- the estimand that isolates the ownership effect directly), and $\Delta_{\text{point--naive,own}}$, $\Delta_{\text{strict--naive,own}}$ (does validation retain value?). Four frozen families (harm under own/full; transformer interaction/full; gate value under own/full; a 12-contrast zero-drift secondary family) are tested by the same deterministic centered paired bootstrap as the rest of the paper, Holm-corrected within family; the inferential unit is the \emph{seed} (trajectory means paired through the shared raw stream) --- windows, triggers and commits are never treated as independent units. Margins were frozen in advance: $\pm0.5$ BA points for equivalence, assessed by inclusion of the CI90 within the margin (the interval-inclusion form of the two one-sided tests \cite{schuirmann1987comparison}; $\pm0.2/\pm1.0$ sensitivities), and non-inferiority guardrails of $-1.0$ recall points and $+0.5$ FPR points (one-sided 95\% bounds) that restrict safety \emph{language} --- balanced accuracy alone determines the registered A/B/C interpretation. The $\pm0.5$-point equivalence margin equals the materiality threshold this program preregistered for its own harm claims --- effects below half a balanced-accuracy point are treated as operationally immaterial throughout, and the smallest harm the paper calls material ($-0.65$) exceeds it --- so CI90-inside equivalence certifies a residual mean effect smaller than anything the program treats as material; the $\pm0.2$/$\pm1.0$ sensitivities bracket each verdict's margin-dependence.

\subsubsection{Candidate evidence size: the size-matched control}
\label{sec:sizematched_method}

The replication above trains every challenger on 512 flows per class while the incumbent was trained on 2{,}000 per class --- the historical convention, inherited by every arm of this paper. Its residual zero-drift harm therefore admits one last mundane explanation: the challenger simply sees a quarter of the incumbent's evidence. A final preregistered control --- the paper's last experiment --- isolates that variable: does harmful candidate promotion under zero drift persist when self-contained challengers are trained with the \emph{same} per-class sample size as the initial incumbent? Protocol, config, seeds, margins, families and machine-evaluable outcome rules were committed before any confirmatory seed ran (in the artifact).

\textbf{Design.} Zero drift only (where the residual harm lives), with the identical v1.21.0 scenario definitions: random proposal trigger ($p{=}0.05$, drift-independent), severity fixed at zero, \texttt{own\_transformer\_per\_model} exclusively, KS-max machinery, same windows, duration, evaluation and statistics. Per benchmark, seven arms --- never-adapt, and \{naive, point, strict\} $\times$ candidate size $\in \{512, 2000\}$/class --- give a 21-arm matrix on 30 fresh confirmatory seeds (4001--4030, verified virgin against every prior ledger). The only new variable is the challenger's training size; trigger, probe (32 labels), gate margins, hyperparameters (SVC $C{=}1.0$, $\gamma{=}$\texttt{scale}, PCA dim 8), temporal semantics and sampling policy are unchanged, and the incumbent keeps its 2{,}000/class training set.

\textbf{Nested candidate batches.} The two size conditions are not independently sampled challengers: at every proposal the canonical draw first produces the \emph{unchanged} historical 512-per-class batch (bit-identical to the v1.21.0 arms --- verified by a bit-level parity run), then extends it with 1{,}488 further samples per class from the same per-trigger RNG stream, so the 512 batch \emph{is} the first 512 rows per class of the 2{,}000 batch (same rows, labels, order, hash; 999 proposal-level pairs verified at confirmatory scale). The size contrast thus adds information to an otherwise identical candidate rather than comparing independently sampled challengers. Each challenger's scaler/PCA/classifier still fit on its own batch only, with full per-candidate provenance recorded. This is a controlled pool-based construction --- not an observed-data or deployment-realistic arm. What it matches is \emph{nominal} per-class sample size: matching nominal per-class sample size isolates training-set size, but it does not establish equivalence in temporal coverage, diversity, subtype support, label quality, duplication, prevalence or effective sample size.

\textbf{Estimands, families and outcome rules (all frozen).} Primary estimands, in BA points per benchmark: E1 size-matched damage ($\mathrm{naive}_{2000}-\mathrm{never}$), E2 candidate-size effect ($\mathrm{naive}_{2000}-\mathrm{naive}_{512}$), E3/E4 gate value at matched size, E5 (secondary) the gate$\times$size interaction. Four Holm-corrected families (F1/F2/F3/F4: 3/3/6/6 contrasts), the same deterministic centered paired bootstrap, CI95 for harm/superiority, CI90 for equivalence at the reused $\pm0.5$-point margin, recall/FPR guardrails as before; the inferential unit remains the seed. Three machine-evaluable outcomes were frozen in advance --- PERSISTENCE (material Holm-significant damage at 2{,}000 plus a guardrail-clean gate win), ELIMINATION (equivalence of all three damage contrasts \emph{and} all six gate contrasts, plus no contradicting harmful-future-value signal), ATTENUATION (anything else) --- with the harmful-future-value criterion keyed, by preregistration, to the \emph{sign rate} of committed proposals' five-window future value. \S\ref{sec:sizematched} applies these rules literally.

\subsection{Promotion policies under evaluation}

The primary policy matrix is common to every arm: \textbf{never-adapt} (the frozen $h_0$), \textbf{always-deploy} (naive: commit every confirmed proposal), and the \textbf{point} and \textbf{strict} labeled-probe gates of \S\ref{sec:gates} (strict additionally rejects probe ties, at no additional labels). The formal sequential alternatives --- exact McNemar, the confidence-sequence gates and the assembled VBC-SG with its deferral and lifetime-spending machinery (\S\ref{sec:riskgates}) --- are evaluated as a separate axis under the historical frozen policy, where their guarantees and label costs are priced (\S\ref{sec:risk}).

\subsection{Outcomes and inference}

Per window we record balanced accuracy (BA) and attack-class F1; per stream we average over the 100 windows, and per condition over seeds. BA is the registered primary endpoint and measures the mean effect; attack recall and false-positive rate are preregistered non-inferiority guardrails that restrict the interpretation (they gate safety \emph{language}, not the registered verdicts); harmful-proposal summaries are descriptive within-trajectory fractions over seed-clustered commits; probe-level guarantees are not future-deployment guarantees. We report \textbf{gain vs. no-adaptation} (the frozen $h_0$ baseline), \textbf{committed adaptations} and \textbf{labels used}. To quantify the headroom of the decision we define a per-stream \textbf{policy oracle}, $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}=\mathbb{E}_{\text{seed}}\max(\mathrm{BA}_{\text{no-adapt}},\mathrm{BA}_{\text{detector}})$ --- a conservative lower bound on the value of perfect per-trigger decisions --- the \textbf{regret} of naive triggering $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{oracle}}-\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive}}$, and the \textbf{harm frequency} (fraction of streams on which adapting scored below no-adaptation). All confirmatory comparisons are \textbf{truly paired}: on the v2 harness every policy processes bit-identical pre-generated streams, and contrasts use cluster (per-seed) bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals; numbers from the initial exploratory study use seed-matched bootstrap CIs (conservative rather than fully paired).

\textbf{Evidence stages.} The study combines an exploratory discovery stage, two internally registered corrective replications with fresh seeds, supporting registered analyses, and chronological boundary evaluations. These stages have different epistemic roles; the primary conclusions rely on the self-contained-pipeline and size-matched controls, while the remaining blocks test mechanisms, costs or boundaries. The full evidence hierarchy, per-block configuration, seeds and provenance are catalogued in Supplementary \S S0.


\section{Results}

Results are ordered by the pipeline of Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}: the two candidate-construction factors first (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}, \S\ref{sec:sizematched}), the conditional value of validation third (\S\ref{sec:gatevalue}), the external chronological boundary fourth (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}), and the mechanism, formal and operational extensions --- each with an explicit question, main finding and scope --- last (\S\ref{sec:v2}).

\subsection{Preprocessing ownership changes the apparent value of promotion}
\label{sec:symmetric}

Table~\ref{tab:symmetric_pipeline} reports the replication of \S\ref{sec:symmetric_method}; its registered verdict was Scenario~A --- \emph{persistence with a shifted locus}: preprocessing asymmetry amplified harmful promotion, while a residual zero-drift harm survived for self-contained challengers. All of its challengers train on 512 flows per class against a 2{,}000/class incumbent --- the historical convention whose consequences the size-matched control of \S\ref{sec:sizematched} then isolates. Three findings here, in the order the frozen families test them; the fourth registered family (gate value) is reported with the rest of the validation evidence in \S\ref{sec:gatevalue}.

\textbf{(1) The mean full-drift harm does not persist under self-contained pipelines.} With each challenger owning its preprocessing, always-deploy is \emph{beneficial} in all three full-drift regimes: PortScan $+7.21$ [4.89, 9.52], UNSW-Recon $+2.55$ [2.34, 2.75], and --- the regime where the harm was originally reported --- ToN-IoT $+1.03$ [0.55, 1.53], all Holm-significant. \textbf{(2) Preprocessing ownership is a major amplifier --- not a complete explanation of candidate-promotion risk.} The registered interaction $\mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,own}} - \mathrm{BA}_{\text{naive,frozen}}$, on bit-identical raw streams and candidate batches, is positive everywhere it was harmful: $+5.98$ [3.82, 8.26] on ToN-IoT full, $+0.90$ and $+1.15$ on PortScan and UNSW full, and $+5.49$/$+2.36$ on the ToN/PortScan zero-drift streams --- the frozen convention had been handicapping every challenger, most severely exactly where the harm was reported. The original full-drift harm was therefore configuration-dependent and strongly amplified by incumbent-owned preprocessing; switching to self-contained preprocessing removes the observed mean full-drift harm and accounts for the large frozen-versus-own difference. Freezing a representation can be a legitimate production policy; the issue is generalizing results obtained under that treatment beyond it. \textbf{(3) But ownership does not explain all of the risk: zero-drift promotion harm persists at the historical candidate size.} With no drift at all, self-contained 512-per-class challengers still hurt: naive$-$never is $-1.74$ [$-2.35$, $-0.96$] on PortScan and $-0.65$ [$-0.77$, $-0.55$] on UNSW-Recon --- both beyond the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point materiality margin, Holm-significant --- and $-0.38$ [$-0.53$, $-0.25$] on ToN-IoT, sub-material but significant and \emph{not} equivalent to zero (its CI90 is not inside $\pm0.5$). These challengers were also trained on a quarter of the incumbent's evidence, and \S\ref{sec:sizematched} shows that asymmetry, not re-estimation per se, accounts for the mean effect.

\subsection{Candidate evidence size explains the residual zero-drift mean harm}
\label{sec:sizematched}

Table~\ref{tab:size_matched} reports the registered control of \S\ref{sec:sizematched_method}, and it settles the residual question. Two findings here, one per frozen damage family; the gate family (F3) is reported in \S\ref{sec:gatevalue}.

\textbf{(1) The 512 harm replicates; the size-matched harm does not exist in the mean.} On thirty entirely fresh seeds, 512-per-class challengers reproduce the replication's residual harm almost exactly (naive$_{512}-$never: $-1.70$ [$-2.05$, $-1.36$] PortScan, $-0.65$ [$-0.77$, $-0.55$] UNSW, $-0.24$ [$-0.39$, $-0.10$] ToN --- descriptive, uncorrected). Give the same challengers the incumbent's 2{,}000 flows per class --- as pure extension of the identical nested batch --- and the mean damage vanishes: naive$_{2000}-$never is $+0.19$ [$-0.15$, $+0.55$], $-0.02$ [$-0.10$, $+0.06$] and $-0.01$ [$-0.05$, $+0.04$], with the CI90 fully inside the preregistered $\pm0.5$-point equivalence margin in \emph{all three} benchmarks (PortScan's upper bound sits at $0.49$, inside by $0.006$ points --- the margin is tight, and this is reported explicitly). The conclusion is margin-dependent, and the preregistered sensitivities make it explicit: PortScan is margin-sensitive at $\pm0.2$ --- \emph{not} equivalent at that stricter margin --- while UNSW-NB15 and ToN-IoT remain so, and all three benchmarks pass at $\pm1.0$; equivalence within a margin does not imply equality of predictions or of class-conditional behaviour. None of the F1 damage contrasts approaches Holm significance. \textbf{(2) Candidate size is the driver, and it is Holm-significant everywhere.} The paired size effect naive$_{2000}-$naive$_{512}$ is $+1.89$ [$+1.54$, $+2.23$] on PortScan, $+0.63$ [$+0.53$, $+0.73$] on UNSW and $+0.23$ [$+0.10$, $+0.38$] on ToN --- essentially the mirror image of the 512 harm, all three Holm-significant. Increasing candidate evidence removed the detectable average harm in the evaluated zero-drift control; it did not, and cannot, establish equivalence in every respect a proposal can differ (full tables in Supplementary \S S8).

\textbf{Registered outcome: ATTENUATION --- and what it does and does not mean.} The frozen rules classify this result mechanically. PERSISTENCE fails because there is no material mean damage at the matched size (no F1 contrast is $\le -0.5$ with CI95 below zero). ELIMINATION fails on exactly one criterion: the preregistered harmful-future-value check keys on the \emph{sign rate} of committed proposals' five-window value, and at the matched size that rate is $\approx$48--52\% in two benchmarks --- so the registered outcome is ATTENUATION, and we keep it. Substantively, the same logs show the mean five-window future value of committed 2{,}000-challengers is $\approx 0$ in every benchmark ($-0.06$ to $+0.03$ points, against $-0.13$ to $-1.03$ at 512): a near-50\% negative-sign rate around a null mean is proposal-level variability, not systematic net harm, and it must not be read as a deployment-risk probability. The formal classification and the substantive reading are both true, and the paper keeps them distinct.

\textbf{Guardrails at the matched size.} The security side closes cleanly: all six matched-size gate cells pass both preregistered non-inferiority margins vs.\ naive$_{2000}$ (paired $\Delta$recall one-sided lower bounds all $>-1.0$; $\Delta$FPR upper bounds all $<+0.5$), while at 512 the zero-drift UNSW strict cell again fails recall non-inferiority --- replicating the earlier trade-off on fresh seeds (full guardrail table in Supplementary \S S8). There is no security trade-off at the matched size; there is also no BA gain, and the absence of a trade-off is not evidence of utility.

\subsection{Validation has conditional average value}
\label{sec:gatevalue}

The registered gate families of both controls, read together, locate where validation earns its keep --- and where it measurably does not.

\textbf{Under a nominal-evidence disadvantage, validation retains material value.} At own-transformer 512/class, point and strict validation improve over naive in all six zero-drift comparisons (Holm-significant), recovering much or nearly all of the loss depending on the cell: strict gains $+1.68$ [1.23, 2.13] over naive on PortScan, $+0.51$ [0.38, 0.64] on UNSW, $+0.34$ [0.20, 0.49] on ToN (point $+0.75/+0.19/+0.11$). ``Recover'' means improvement \emph{over always-deploy}: strict returns to within $0.06$--$0.14$ points of never-adapt in all three regimes, the point gate still sits $0.27$--$0.99$ points below it, and no zero-drift cell converts to a net benefit over never adapting --- nor should it where there is nothing to gain. At full drift the point gate still filters harmful candidates on ToN-IoT ($+0.64$ [0.26, 1.06]); the costs are equally visible: the gates add nothing material on PortScan ($+0.19$/$+0.05$, n.s.) and the point gate has a small resolved \emph{cost} on UNSW-Recon ($-0.21$ [$-0.36$, $-0.06$]) --- where updating is already clearly beneficial, validation's marginal value can be zero or slightly negative (full contrasts in Supplementary \S S7).

\textbf{Under the symmetric size-matched zero-drift control, validation gates have nothing left to recover.} All six F3 gate contrasts at the matched nominal size are tiny ($+0.01$ to $+0.13$ points, every one below $0.14$), none Holm-significant, and every one CI90-equivalent to naive within $\pm0.5$; the secondary F4 interactions are uniformly negative and Holm-significant (e.g.\ strict on PortScan $-1.54$ [$-1.89$, $-1.20$]) --- the gates' value at 512 was, to the extent the interaction measures it, compensation for the challenger's evidence disadvantage (full tables in Supplementary \S S8). No average benefit under this control is not the same as no value: it does not rule out value against individual harmful candidates, tail risk, temporally uncertain streams, or configurations not evaluated here.

\textbf{Evidence versus validation: what each nominal label buys.} The same sealed outputs answer the operator's trade-off: more candidate-training evidence, or a small challenger plus a validation probe? Table~\ref{tab:evidence_validation_tradeoff} compares, per benchmark, the 512-per-class policies against naive at nominal 2{,}000-per-class parity. In nominal adjudication counts, moving from 512 to 2{,}000 samples per class requires $2\times1{,}488 = 2{,}976$ additional candidate-training labels per proposal, whereas the evaluated point/strict decision uses a 32-label probe. At 512, the strict gate recovers most of the mean loss (PortScan: naive$_{512}$ $-1.70$ vs strict$_{512}$ $-0.03$ against never, descriptive); naive$_{2000}$ reaches equivalence without any probe. This establishes no economic dominance in either direction: labels are counted, not priced, and the inspected-flow cost of acquiring balanced attack labels is not modeled --- under operational imbalance candidate-training and probe labels need not cost the same to obtain.

\subsection{Chronological evidence defines the external boundary}
\label{sec:boundaries}

\textbf{Chronologically ordered subsampled replays: the gates' boundary.} Six initial registered replays on real, chronologically ordered streams (200 windows of 256 flows, strided to span each capture: ordering, labels and the temporal availability of every probe are real; the sampling density is not) map where the gate costs benefit (per-replay details in Supplementary \S S2.6). The phenomenon the ramps model is real and deep: every CICIDS incumbent collapses on a later window (no-adaptation BA 49--72\%) as each stream presents an unseen attack sub-type, and drift-triggered retraining recovers +13.6 to +43.8 BA points. On these deep-benefit streams the gate pays a real, metric-dependent insurance premium ($-$5.0 to $-$15.1 of the recovered balanced-accuracy points on three streams; nothing on Wednesday, +0.40 [0.09, 0.74]) --- while on \emph{overall accuracy} it \emph{beats} naive on the same streams (Friday +4.19; Thursday +1.23), because a stale probe misranks candidates on the minority class first. A registered interventional test rules out probe class composition as the cause (a stratified probe leaves the premium unchanged); the surviving explanation is probe \emph{staleness}, reported as a hypothesis consistent with the data, not a demonstrated mechanism. \emph{On the external stream the account's central prediction holds}: the raw UNSW-NB15 timeline keeps its incumbent healthy (82.3\% BA), updating helps moderately (+7.33), and the gate pays \emph{no premium at all} (+0.16 [$-$0.31, 0.63] vs naive) --- the insurance is free precisely where incumbent health makes the commit decision genuinely uncertain. \emph{Chronological net harm is unobserved across these six replays}; every CICIDS stream lands in deep-benefit territory, the only healthy incumbent among them is the external UNSW timeline, and \S7 states the resulting external-validity limit. This is the registered check where the gate costs real benefit.

\textbf{The full chronological matrix, enumerated in advance.} We pre-enumerated \emph{every} chronological stream this data supports --- each train$\to$future day pair of CICIDS2017 whose training day contains attacks, the two remaining intra-day splits, and the UNSW-NB15 timeline at two further training fractions --- and ran all seven new ones under the same protocol with the obligatory strict baseline and VBC-SG added (seeds 601--630; ToN-IoT ships no timestamps). Table~\ref{tab:chronological_q1} reports all of them. \textbf{Net harm remains unobserved} --- on no chronological stream, old or new, does always-deploying lose to never adapting --- so the harmful-update finding stays a controlled-condition result, and this is the paper's principal limit on external validity. What the matrix does add is the account's \emph{conditional} prediction, tested where it is falsifiable. Incumbent health, not the calendar, sorts the outcomes: on the streams where the incumbent collapses (no-adaptation balanced accuracy 49--59\%) always-deploying is excellent and the gates pay a real premium (Tue$\to$Wed+Thu+Fri: $+11.5$ gated vs $+14.8$ naive; Wed$\to$Fri: $+27.6$ vs $+35.3$), whereas on the two UNSW timelines, where the incumbent stays healthy (82.9\% and 84.2\%), \emph{the point and strict gates beat always-deploying on both} --- the no-additional-label strict rule by $+2.78$ and $+1.34$ points, on non-overlapping intervals, while committing 2.5 and 3.6 times per stream against naive's 13.0 and 17.3. VBC-SG joins them on the first timeline ($+10.76$ vs naive's $+8.35$) but not on the second ($+6.92$ vs $+7.64$), which is the behaviour its extra conservatism predicts and which we report rather than average away. We do not round this into a law: the Wednesday intra-day split also keeps a healthy incumbent (84.7\%) yet its gates sit a statistically unresolved $0.5$--$0.7$ points \emph{below} naive, so the summary is that the chronological premium disappears --- and can reverse --- once the incumbent has little headroom to lose, without being guaranteed to reverse.

\textbf{Mechanism: update value tracks incumbent degradation, not detector score.} At each of 250 triggers the harness measured the incumbent's degradation over the five \emph{preceding} windows and the candidate-minus-incumbent BA over the five \emph{following} windows --- predictor and outcome share no algebraic term. Pre-trigger degradation predicts the future value of committing in every regime (r $= -$0.67 to $-$0.70, every clustered CI95 away from zero), while the detector's score at the same triggers shows no consistent association (r $= -$0.01 to $+$0.05, every CI95 straddling zero). A mixed-effects model controlling for severity and stream time confirms it: degradation is the only predictor whose CI95 consistently excludes zero ($\beta_{\mathrm{deg}} = -1.02$ [$-$1.61, $-$0.43] per BA point; collinearity-clean, VIF $\le$ 2.4), and the analysis replicates on the quantum-detector logs --- within triggered decisions the evaluated detectors' scores carry no \emph{consistent} incremental signal, not provably none. Triggers are nested in seeds and regimes and past/future performance is autocorrelated, so this is strong \emph{predictive} support for the degradation--headroom account at the level of individual decisions, not a causal proof (Fig.~\ref{fig:9}; per-decision accounting in Supplementary \S S1.1, \S S2.10).

\textbf{Supporting robustness.} Four registered blocks test whether the conclusions survive perturbation. \emph{Observed-data:} with every oracle privilege and leakage path removed --- candidate, probe and detector recalibration drawn from observed past traffic only, without-replacement from a value-deduplicated pool --- the \textbf{leakage-free observed-data} arm reproduces the harm-regime rescue ($+$4.05 and $+$4.83 above naive on ToN-IoT full and mild drift), the only tier \textbf{free of simulator-oracle information} (Supplementary \S S2.7). \emph{Classifier and generator controls:} at the default 512/class budget the harmful zero-drift \emph{sign} appears for random forest, logistic regression, MLP and full-feature SVC; size-matching removes it for the robust learners, localizing the residual magnitude to the fragile SVC-RBF pipeline (Supplementary \S S1.5--S1.6), and it holds across replacement generators, the from-scratch cumulative generator being the deepest ($-$9.46 on ToN-IoT). \emph{Mild drift:} a prediction locked before running holds --- with a healthy incumbent, naive always-deploy is net-harmful in all three benchmarks ($-$0.46, $-$0.15, $-$0.65), so harm is a property of incumbent health, not of one dataset. \emph{Quantum detector:} the harm, the gate's rescue and the per-trigger mechanism all replicate under a quantum-kernel monitor at a ${\sim}114\times$ simulated cost, a detector-invariance check rather than a quantum-advantage claim (Supplementary \S S1.2).

\textbf{VBC-SG: what validating formally costs.} The sequential family of \S\ref{sec:riskgates} controls the per-proposal probability of falsely establishing probe-superiority; it does not bound future deployment harm. A registered budget frontier prices it: the lifetime-budgeted pooled sequence recovers $93\%$ of always-deploying's benefit at cap 512 (${\approx}578$ adjudicated labels per proposal, deferrals included) \emph{under the approximate pooled analysis}, while the fully stratified VBC-SG-Cohort, which carries the formal deployment-long guarantee, reaches $81\%$; the deployment-long stratified guarantee is thus \textbf{non-vacuous within the evaluated} balanced-probe budget, from $b{=}256$ onward, at an abstention of 74--86\%. Empirically the risk-controlled gates committed \emph{zero observed} harmful updates over a complete 520-commit accounting (506 evaluable) against 39\% for always-deploy; these commits cluster within shared seeds and are \textbf{not treated as 506 independent Bernoulli trials} --- they are \emph{not independent trials}, so no population-rate bound is inferred and ``zero observed'' is not ``eliminates''. The lesson: a \textbf{pooled sequential gate provides useful empirical risk control} at tens of adjudicated labels and captures the large benefits, while the \textbf{formally aligned stratified guarantee} requires substantially larger balanced-probe budgets. All of these numbers are evidence under the historical frozen-transformer policy; the frontier was not re-evaluated under the size-matched self-contained control, where plain promotion is already mean-equivalent to never adapting, and nothing here shows VBC-SG to be \emph{necessary} in that setting. We claim no mathematical novelty for the construction (Supplementary \S S2.8--S2.9).

\textbf{Operational feasibility.} Every count above is an \emph{adjudicated} label; an operator pays in \emph{inspected flows}, and finding an \emph{attack} label at operating prevalence costs about $1/\pi$ inspections. A registered acquisition-yield simulation prices that search: alert-enriched inspection cuts it 5--8$\times$ at every prevalence, while the commit rule itself is shown only an independent uniform validation sample (32 adjudications), so enrichment improves discovery without touching decision validity. The arm is deliberately bounded --- candidate batch and detector calibration remain balanced, and it is not an end-to-end deployment cost. A separate prevalence sensitivity shows the gate holds down to $\pi \approx 0.05$ but its harm-regime protection dissolves at $\pi = 0.01$, where two-thirds of 32-flow probes contain no attack; there the binding constraint is upstream --- a starved \emph{trigger}, not the gate (Supplementary \S S2.3). The hypotheses and regime taxonomy of this paper were generated in an initial exploratory study (Supplementary \S S1) and every claim now rests on the registered numbers above.

\section{Discussion}


The controlled program --- frozen diagnostic, symmetric-pipeline replication, size-matched control, chronological matrix --- answers four questions.

\textbf{Q1: What makes an adaptive challenger comparable?} A challenger cannot be evaluated on comparable terms if it inherits an incumbent-owned representation or receives substantially less training evidence. Both asymmetries were latent in the historical convention, and each was isolated by a preregistered control: frozen incumbent-owned preprocessing accounted for the mean full-drift harm (the ownership interaction reaches $+6.0$ points where the harm was reported), and the challengers' 4$\times$ nominal per-class evidence disadvantage accounts for the residual zero-drift mean harm (all size effects Holm-significant; equivalence to never-adapt at the matched nominal size within $\pm0.5$ points in 3/3 under the zero-drift control). The control identifies a nominal sample-size effect in the evaluated pool-based setting; broader evidence comparability --- temporal coverage, diversity, subtype support, label quality, effective sample size --- remains an operational property, not a consequence of equal row counts alone. The underlying mechanism is concrete: a challenger scored in a representation fitted on someone else's data, or fitted to a quarter of the incumbent's evidence, replaces a model built on more information, and the generalization loss outweighs the gain from recency; whether that loss pushes the outcome below no-adaptation depends on the downstream model's robustness --- SVC-RBF is fragile enough on ToN-IoT to go net-negative, whereas random forest, logistic regression and MLP retain enough generalization to stay non-negative (Supplementary \S S1.6) --- so net harm is the fragile-model tail of a general phenomenon. In the evaluated setting, what looked like a property of drift-triggered promotion was, in its mean, a property of how the candidates were built and evidenced. The general lesson survives in its precise form: a drift alarm does not establish challenger superiority --- but nothing here shows that a comparably constructed, comparably evidenced proposal is intrinsically dangerous.

\textbf{Q2: When does validation add value?} Each configuration tells the same story from a different angle: under the frozen transformer, the gate was partly compensating a representation bias; at own-transformer 512, it compensated a nominal-evidence disadvantage (its value there is real --- up to $+1.68$ points --- and guardrail-clean in all but one cell); at own-transformer 2{,}000, under the zero-drift control, it has no measurable value left to add (all six contrasts $<0.14$ points, none significant, F4 interactions uniformly negative). The zero-drift control thus shows that the measured value of the gate disappears when the evaluated representation and nominal sample-size asymmetries are removed. Where it earns its keep, the gate succeeds because it estimates the one quantity the mechanism identifies --- the sign of $\mathrm{acc}(h')-\mathrm{acc}(h)$ --- directly, from a small labeled probe whose validity does not depend on the model being judged; label-free alternatives fail exactly on that circularity: ATC and DoC estimate accuracy from the incumbent's own confidences, whose trustworthiness collapses precisely when the incumbent is degraded (Supplementary \S S1.5). The label bill is layered: retraining labels are unavoidable for any labeled-retraining loop; carving the probe from that same (deduplicated) batch costs nothing extra and buys most of the harm reduction; conversion of average harm into average benefit in the controlled confirmatory harm regime costs a fresh probe of $\approx$32 flows per confirmed drift --- adjudication counts compatible with analyst-assisted workflows, though the inspected-flow acquisition cost of the balanced candidate batch and the formal stratified probes was not evaluated.

\textbf{Q3: When can validation delay beneficial adaptation?} The chronological matrix answers with the incumbent's health. On the streams where the incumbent collapses, always-deploying recovers +13.6 to +43.8 BA points and every conservative layer costs part of that recovery: the point/strict gates pay a $-$5.0 to $-$15.1-point balanced-accuracy premium on three deep-benefit CICIDS streams, VBC-SG abstains more and pays more, and the maximally-guaranteed configurations commit nothing at small budgets. On the healthy UNSW timelines the same gates cost nothing --- and win. The mild-drift matrix makes the same point in the controlled setting: where degradation is strong and well established, excessive validation can delay beneficial adaptation, and the operator's protection is not more gating but bounded deferral, adequate probe budgets, and rollback capability.

\textbf{Q4: What do the controlled results imply --- and not imply --- for operational NIDS?} First, detection is not decision: a detector reports that the distribution moved, while the update decision needs the sign of the candidate--incumbent difference; the same distributional shift is worth +19 balanced-accuracy points in one regime and $-$4 in another, while the evaluated detectors' scores showed no consistent association with which (within-deployment r $\approx$ +0.06; Supplementary \S S1.1). Improving the detector --- including a ${\sim}114\times$ more expensive quantum-kernel monitor --- did not change any promotion outcome we evaluated; the companion hypothesis that motivated the quantum monitors (a superior quantum drift trigger) is not supported, and they serve here as a detector-invariance check (Supplementary \S S1.2). Second, the controlled harm results do not license prevalence claims about production: net harmful always-deploy behavior was not observed in the chronological replays evaluated, so harmful promotion should be treated as a risk concentrated in identifiable conditions --- asymmetric construction, limited candidate evidence, healthy incumbents --- not as a documented operational regularity. Third, the evaluation discipline generalizes: adaptation benchmarks must control challenger representation and training evidence \emph{before} attributing harm (or benefit) to the promotion policy, because asymmetric construction can produce either; we offer the construction $\to$ comparability $\to$ validation control sequence as a template for evaluating adaptive pipelines beyond intrusion detection --- fraud, spam and abuse filtering, predictive maintenance --- while stressing that our evidence is confined to network-security benchmarks.

\textbf{An operational implication.} The results suggest a conditioned recipe rather than a universal rule; we present it as an implication of the controlled evidence, not as a validated deployment policy:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Audit candidate construction.} Every challenger should be a self-contained pipeline that fits its own preprocessing on its own training batch --- never score a challenger inside the incumbent's frozen representation (\S\ref{sec:symmetric}).
  \item \emph{Audit evidence comparability.} Match the challenger's nominal training evidence to the incumbent's, or treat any shortfall as a reason to validate (\S\ref{sec:sizematched}); nominal parity is necessary for a fair reading, not sufficient for full comparability.
  \item \emph{Assess incumbent health.} Degradation, not detector score, predicts update value (\S\ref{sec:v2}); incumbent health should inform the promotion decision, although the cost and calibration of such monitoring were not evaluated here.
  \item \emph{Condition validation strength on uncertainty.} Validate conservatively when candidate quality or incumbent health is uncertain; when degradation is strong and well established, avoid unnecessary delay --- conservative gates measurably forfeit recovery there (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}).
  \item \emph{Monitor post-promotion outcomes and retain rollback capability.} Mean equivalence is not a per-proposal guarantee; the tail and sign-rate evidence (\S\ref{sec:v2}, \S\ref{sec:sizematched}) is exactly why post-deployment monitoring stays in the loop.
\end{enumerate}
Under this recipe, and conditional on a comparable proposal being generated, the detector acts mainly as a scheduler; at extreme class imbalance, detector calibration and alarm starvation determine whether any of this is invoked at all.


\section{Limitations}


\textbf{External validity.} The strongest positive (benefit) evidence is concentrated in CICIDS2017; the external benchmarks provide the marginal and harmful ends of the spectrum, which is what the argument requires, but three datasets do not constitute broad external validation. The core drift trajectories are gradual mixing ramps between real regime pools --- covariate/regime drift; we do not isolate a $p(y\,|\,x)$ change, and recurrent drift is untested. Thirteen real chronological replays (\S\ref{sec:boundaries}) add abrupt real drift: most CICIDS streams land in deep-benefit territory, whereas the UNSW-NB15 timelines and the Wednesday intra-day split keep their incumbents healthy; on the two UNSW timelines the point and strict gates beat always-deploying, while the Wednesday split is an unresolved counterexample. What no chronological stream has produced is \emph{net harm} --- a regime where always-deploy loses to never-adapt --- so the harmful-update finding remains demonstrated only on controlled constructions; its absence across thirteen pre-enumerated replays bounds how often the phenomenon should be expected in deployment. Quantum kernels are classically simulated, so the reported 114$\times$ monitoring overhead is a simulation figure.

\textbf{Sample-size matching is not full evidence matching.} The size-matched control matches \emph{nominal} per-class sample size only; temporal coverage, diversity, subtype support, label quality, duplication, prevalence and effective sample size are not equated by equal row counts. It was run \emph{only under zero drift} (full-drift size-matched own-transformer arms were not run); its trigger is a random proposal rate, not a real alarm process; candidate and incumbent draw from controlled balanced pools (the own $+$ size-matched $+$ observed-data combination was not evaluated); and mean equivalence at the matched size does not imply that no individual committed candidate can be worse than the incumbent, nor identity of per-class outcomes. The future-negative sign percentages (65\%/61\% at 512, 48--52\% at the matched size, at $H{=}5$ under zero drift) are descriptive within-trajectory fractions over seed-clustered commits, not population prevalences or deployment probabilities.

\textbf{Learner and update scope.} At full drift the net-harm result is specific to a fragile downstream classifier (SVC-RBF) and does not appear for more robust classifiers (Supplementary \S S1.6) --- but it is no longer confined to one dataset: with a healthy incumbent (mild or zero drift) naive updating is net-harmful in all three benchmarks under the historical configuration (mild- and zero-drift matrices, Supplementary \S S2), and at full drift in three ToN-IoT regimes. The classifier-, generator- and size-dependence of the zero-drift replacement harm is summarized in \S\ref{sec:v2}; the PCA-8 pipeline, chosen for quantum-simulation tractability, attenuates but does not create the harm, and incremental or weighted online learners with no discrete replacement step remain untested. Neither the frozen-policy full-drift harm nor the 512-challenger zero-drift harm should be quoted as a general property of drift-triggered updating: harm is what happens when a healthy model is replaced by a challenger whose construction or evidence is materially asymmetric --- a condition on the proposal, not on the dataset --- with a magnitude that depends on learner fragility, preprocessing ownership and the evidence gap.

\textbf{Scope of the two construction controls.} Both replications target the primary KS-max point/strict question; the quantum detector and the full sequential frontier remain evaluated under the historical frozen-transformer policy. Concretely: QK-ZZ detection, VBC-SG and the budget frontier, and the mild-drift matrix were not re-run with self-contained pipelines --- and VBC-SG was not re-run under size-matched own pipelines --- so none of their numbers should be assumed to transfer; their evidence is scoped to the configuration in which it was produced. Within both replications, evaluation streams, candidate batches and probes remain class-balanced; balanced accuracy is the registered primary endpoint and does not replace operational security metrics (attack recall and FPR are preregistered guardrails; one 512-size cell fails recall non-inferiority); and VBC-SG's guarantee --- where it applies --- controls false probe-superiority, not guaranteed future harm. The main evidence remains concentrated on the SVC-RBF pipeline.

\textbf{Prevalence, labels and probe representativeness.} The gate assumes a small labeled probe at decision time; we report its size ($\approx$32--64 adjudicated flows per confirmed drift) as the incremental adjudication count under the evaluated protocol --- not an estimate of inspected-flow acquisition cost in an operationally imbalanced deployment; the inspected-flow cost of the balanced candidate batch, of the formal stratified probes, and of the deployment pipeline end to end is not modeled. Under genuinely random inspection the gate's harm-regime protection dissolves at $\pi = 0.01$ (\S\ref{sec:v2}); the initial study's stronger claim rested on a probe conditioned to contain one attack, and is withdrawn. In the core harness, candidate batches and probes are sampled from the simulator's mixture at the true current severity --- information a production system lacks; that caveat is addressed by the registered leakage-free observed-data arm (\S\ref{sec:v2}; Supplementary \S S2.7), which reproduces the harmful-regime rescue and the gate-over-naive ordering from observed past traffic only, while exposing negative marginal and mild-drift cells relative to no-adaptation; equivalence to the oracle-probe arm was not tested. Label latency is modeled for the probe (up to 20 windows stale) and the candidate batch (5--20 windows; Table~\ref{tab:latency}); training completion is instantaneous except in the operational sensitivity; asynchronous or partial labeling is not modeled, and the probe-corruption experiment flips labels uniformly at random --- a candidate-aware adversary is strictly stronger and untested. The confidence-sequence gates control the per-proposal probability of falsely establishing superiority \emph{on the probe distribution}; this equals a bound on future-deployment harm only when the probe is representative --- an assumption our own stale-probe and coverage results show can fail (the empirical-Bernstein sequence exceeds nominal $\alpha$ under strong within-probe autocorrelation). VBC-SG's lifetime budget supplies the deployment-long bound by alpha-spending across proposals, at a label cost the frontier shows is prohibitive in its maximally-stacked stratified form at $b{=}64$. The evaluated gates use simple balanced probes rather than optimized active-testing acquisition \cite{kossen2021active}; more informative querying could reduce adjudication labels, but was outside the registered design.

\textbf{Statistical granularity and multiplicity.} Seeds quantify within-regime sampling variability, not new environments: the external units are three datasets and seven regimes, and confidence intervals should be read at that granularity (Supplementary \S S3 reports leave-one-out behaviour). Two v1 harness caveats --- seed-matched but unpaired stream realizations, and with-replacement pool sampling --- apply to the initial study only. Multiplicity-adjusted $p$-values are computed directly from the paired per-seed contrasts using a deterministic centered paired bootstrap test, with Holm \cite{holm1979simple} controlling the six-test confirmatory family, Holm within each of the four preregistered symmetric-replication families (F1--F3 full-drift, 3/3/6 contrasts; F4 zero-drift, 12 contrasts; Supplementary \S S7), Holm within each of the four preregistered size-matched families (F1--F4: 3/3/6/6 contrasts; Supplementary \S S8), and Benjamini--Hochberg \cite{benjamini1995controlling} applied separately to the pre-declared 15-cell budget-frontier block and the seven-replay chronological block (Supplementary \S S5); other point/VBC-SG comparisons, the frozen-side cells of Table~\ref{tab:symmetric_pipeline} and the 512-vs-never column of Table~\ref{tab:size_matched} are descriptive and uncorrected. \emph{No conclusion depends on the correction}: every nominally significant contrast survives its adjustment, in every family.


\section{Conclusion}


Adaptive promotion should not be evaluated before candidate construction and evidence comparability are understood. Drift detection does not establish candidate superiority, but neither does every proposal require a validation gate. In the evaluated setting, frozen incumbent-owned preprocessing generated the original full-drift harm, and a fourfold nominal candidate-sample-size disadvantage generated the remaining zero-drift harm. Under the preregistered zero-drift, random-proposal control with balanced pools, self-contained challengers trained at nominal 2{,}000-per-class sample-size parity were equivalent to never-adapt within the $\pm0.5$-point margin, and under that same control point/strict validation offered no measurable gain. Candidate construction, nominal evidence parity and promotion testing should therefore be treated as sequential controls: validate the challenger when its construction or evidence conditions remain asymmetric or uncertain, rather than treating a gate as a substitute for building a comparably constructed, comparably evidenced challenger --- the measured value of the gate disappeared when the evaluated representation and nominal sample-size asymmetries were removed. The registered outcome of the final control is ATTENUATION, its preregistered sign-based future-value criterion remaining inconclusive around the near-zero mean; the substantive result --- size matching neutralizes the mean zero-drift harm in all three benchmarks --- and the formal classification are reported separately. The chronological replays add the operational axis: governance must depend on incumbent health --- conservative validation can protect a healthy incumbent, but can delay recovery when degradation is severe, and thirteen replays without observed net harm bound the claims where the evidence stops. The frozen-policy frontier prices validation where it earns its keep, and everything reproduces from the public artifact.
